# Fair Use Legal Framework Analysis
## Distinguishing Fixed Law from Interpretable Zones

**Date:** Analysis of Supreme Court Fair Use Precedents  
**Purpose:** Identify "core guideposts" (binding law) vs. "bendy parts" (areas for argumentation)  
**Key Cases:** *Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music* (1994), *Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith* (2023)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Supreme Court's fair use framework contains both rigid mandates and flexible interpretation zones. **Warhol** significantly narrowed **Campbell**'s transformation test by refocusing from "new meaning/message" to "purpose of the specific use" in the marketplace. This creates both constraints and opportunities for EchoCraft's defense.

**Critical Distinction:** Courts must analyze the **challenged use** (licensing to Condé Nast for magazine illustration), not the **artistic work itself** (Orange Prince silkscreen). This opens argumentative space to distinguish EchoCraft's educational/critical use from commercial entertainment substitution.

## I. CORE GUIDEPOSTS (Fixed Binding Law)

### A. Mandatory Analytical Framework

#### 1. **Holistic Case-by-Case Analysis Required**
- **Campbell HN1**: "The task is not to be simplified with bright-line rules, for the statute, like the doctrine it recognizes, calls for case-by-case analysis."
- **Campbell HN2**: "Nor may the four statutory factors be treated in isolation, one from another. All are to be explored, and the results weighed together, in light of the purposes of copyright."
- **Warhol HN23**: "The four statutory fair use factors may not 'be treated in isolation, one from another. All are to be explored, and the results weighed together, in light of the purposes of copyright.'"

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** No single factor is dispositive. Courts cannot mechanically apply factors but must weigh all together considering copyright's dual purposes (incentivizing creation + enabling follow-on use).

**IMPLICATION FOR ECHOCF RAFT:** Even if Factor 1 looks weak post-Warhol, strong showings on Factors 2, 3, and 4 can tip the balance. The holistic test remains our pathway to victory.

---

#### 2. **Focus on the SPECIFIC USE, Not the Work Itself**
- **Warhol HN21-22**: "Whether the purpose and character of a use weighs in favor of fair use is, instead, an objective inquiry into what use was made, i.e., what the user does with the original work."
- **Warhol (majority opinion p. 1285)**: "The use is AWF's commercial licensing of Orange Prince to appear on the cover of Condé Nast's special commemorative edition. The purpose of that use is, still, to illustrate a magazine about Prince with a portrait of Prince."
- **Gorsuch concurrence**: "Nothing in the copyright statute calls on judges to speculate about the purpose an artist may have in mind when working on a particular project. Nothing in the law requires judges to try their hand at art criticism and assess the aesthetic character of the resulting work. Instead, the first statutory fair-use factor instructs courts to focus on 'the purpose and character *of the use*.'"
- **Santos v. Kimmel (2d Cir. 2025)**: "Whether a secondary use is transformative turns on what a **reasonable observer thinks**, not the subjective intent of the copyright holder or that of the secondary user." 154 F.4th 30, 34 (confirming objective test)

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** Factor 1 analysis examines the challenged use's market function, not the artistic merit or transformative qualities of the secondary work in abstract. A transformative *work* used for a substitutive *purpose* loses fair use protection.

**CRITICAL INSIGHT:** This is our **biggest opening**. Warhol lost because AWF licensed Orange Prince to Condé Nast for the same purpose Goldsmith licensed photos (magazine illustration of Prince). EchoCraft's use is **structurally different**: we created a critical educational work to correct historical inaccuracies. We're not substituting for Riven's entertainment function; we're fulfilling a distinct educational/critical function.

---

#### 3. **Commercial + Same Purpose = Presumption Against Fair Use**
- **Warhol HN14**: "Where the copier's use is the same as or highly similar to the original's, and the copier's use is commercial in nature, the first factor is likely to weigh against fair use, absent some other justification for copying."
- **Warhol HN15**: "The inquiry focuses on the use of the copyrighted work, which 'requires an examination of the copying work's purpose and the nature of its use,' not an examination of the copying work itself."
- **Warhol HN16**: "Here, because AWF's use shared the same underlying purpose as Goldsmith's photograph—portraying Prince in a magazine—and because that use was of a commercial nature, the first factor does not favor AWF."

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** When a secondary use serves the same market purpose as the original AND is commercial, Factor 1 weighs against fair use unless there's independent justification for copying (like criticism with critical bearing on the original).

**IMPLICATION FOR ECHOCRAF T:** Our nonprofit structure and below-cost licensing counter the "commercial nature" element. But we must prove our purpose differs from Riven's entertainment purpose—we serve education/criticism, not entertainment.

---

#### 4. **New Expression Alone Is Not Dispositive**
- **Warhol HN2**: "Although new expression may be relevant to whether a copying use has a sufficiently distinct purpose or character, it is not, without more, dispositive of the first factor."
- **Warhol (majority opinion p. 1282)**: "The objective meaning or message of 2 Live Crew's song was relevant to this inquiry into the reasons for copying, but any 'new expression, meaning, or message' was not the test."
- **Warhol HN20**: "Whether a work is transformative cannot turn merely on the stated or perceived intent of the artist or the meaning or impression that a critic—or for that matter, a judge—draws from the work."

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** Adding new lyrics, new aesthetic, or new meaning doesn't automatically satisfy Factor 1. The secondary user must justify *why* copying was necessary for the distinct purpose.

**IMPLICATION FOR ECHOCRAF T:** We cannot rely solely on "our version sounds different" or "we added educational context." We must demonstrate that copying Riven's melody was **necessary** to create recognizable criticism that comments *on Riven's work itself*.

---

### B. Critical Bearing Requirement (The Parody/Criticism Distinction)

#### 5. **Criticism Must Have "Critical Bearing" on the Original Work**
- **Campbell HN6**: "Parody needs to mimic an original to make its point, and so has some claim to use the creation of its victim's (or collective victims') imagination, whereas satire can stand on its own two feet and so requires justification for the very act of borrowing."
- **Warhol (majority opinion p. 1286)**: "Because it [AWF's asserted commentary] 'has no critical bearing on' Goldsmith's photograph, the commentary's 'claim to fairness in borrowing from' her work 'diminishes accordingly (if it does not vanish).' The commercial nature of the use, on the other hand, 'loom[s] larger.'"
- **Warhol (majority opinion p. 1286)**: "Like satire that does not target an original work, AWF's asserted commentary 'can stand on its own two feet and so requires justification for the very act of borrowing.'"

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** For criticism/comment to justify copying under Factor 1, the criticism must target the **original work itself** (parody), not use the original to comment on something else (satire). Satire requires independent justification beyond its message.

**TENSION FOR ECHOCRAF T:** This is our **most vulnerable point**. EchoCraft's song criticizes *historical inaccuracies* (general social concern), not necessarily *Riven's artistic choices*. Under Warhol, this looks like satire (can stand on its own feet), not parody (must conjure up the original).

**COUNTERARGUMENT STRATEGY:** We need to reframe our criticism as having "critical bearing" on Riven's work. Possible arguments:
- Riven's song perpetuates harmful historical myths; our criticism targets *this specific perpetuation*
- By using the melody students already know, we create pedagogical tension that highlights the contrast between popular myth and historical truth
- The criticism has "critical bearing" on Riven's *educational impact*, not just general history

---

#### 6. **Parody Must "Conjure Up" the Original to Make Its Point**
- **Campbell HN12**: "Parody's humor, or in any event its comment, necessarily springs from recognizable allusion to its object through distorted imitation. When parody takes aim at a particular original work, the parody must be able to 'conjure up' at least enough of that original to make the object of its critical wit recognizable."
- **Campbell HN13**: "It is significant that 2 Live Crew not only copied the first line of the original, but thereafter departed markedly from the Orbison lyrics for its own ends."

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** If the work qualifies as criticism/parody, it may copy enough to make the original recognizable ("conjure up"), but must depart meaningfully to serve its critical purpose.

**IMPLICATION FOR ECHOCRAF T:** If we can establish "critical bearing," the 87.5% verbatim copying becomes more defensible. We needed the melody students recognize to conjure up the original, then departed in lyrics to serve educational purpose.

---

### C. Market Harm Analysis

#### 7. **No Derivative Market for Criticism**
- **Campbell HN16**: "When a lethal parody, like a scathing theater review, kills demand for the original, it does not produce a harm cognizable under the Copyright Act. The role of the courts is to distinguish between '[b]iting criticism [that merely] suppresses demand [and] copyright infringement[, which] usurps it.'"
- **Campbell HN17-18**: "There is no protectible derivative market for criticism. The market for potential derivative uses includes only those that creators of original works would in general develop or license others to develop."
- **Santos v. Kimmel (2d Cir. 2025)**: "'[T]he unlikelihood that creators of imaginative works will license critical reviews or lampoons of their own productions removes such uses from the very notion of a potential licensing market.'" 154 F.4th at 35 (reaffirming Campbell)

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** If the secondary use qualifies as criticism/parody, any market harm from the criticism's *effectiveness* (destroying demand through devastating critique) is not cognizable under Factor 4. Only *substitution harm* (usurping the original's market by serving as replacement) counts. **This principle was recently reaffirmed by the Second Circuit in 2025.**

**STRONG POINT FOR ECHOCRAF T:** If we establish critical purpose, any harm to Riven's market from our effective criticism is irrelevant. We only face Factor 4 harm if schools choose our version because it's entertaining, not because it's educational.

---

#### 8. **Preliminary Negotiations Without Contracts Insufficient for Factor 4 Harm**
- **Research Finding (TCA Television)**: Preliminary negotiations without executed licensing agreements are insufficient to establish a protected market under Factor 4.

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** Copyright holders must demonstrate an *established* market, not speculative future markets based on exploratory talks.

**STRONG POINT FOR ECHOCRAF T:** Luna's preliminary educational licensing talks (without signed contracts) likely insufficient to establish protected educational derivative market under Factor 4.

---

### D. Educational vs. Commercial Character

#### 9. **Nonprofit Structure + Below-Cost Licensing Supports Educational Characterization**
- **Research Finding (Hachette)**: Courts consider organizational structure, pricing below commercial rates, and nonprofit mission when evaluating "nonprofit educational purposes" under § 107(1).

**FIXED PRINCIPLE:** Factor 1's "commercial vs. nonprofit educational" inquiry looks beyond mere profit motive to examine organizational structure, pricing strategy, and institutional purpose.

**STRONG POINT FOR ECHOCRAF T:** Our nonprofit structure, below-cost licensing to schools, and educational mission weigh in favor of "nonprofit educational purposes" under Factor 1.

## II. BENDY PARTS (Areas for Creative Argumentation)

### A. Defining "Same Purpose" vs. "Same Market Category"

**THE KEY INTERPRETIVE BATTLEGROUND**

#### Warhol's Ambiguity:
The majority held that AWF and Goldsmith shared "substantially the same purpose" because both licensed images "to illustrate a magazine about Prince with a portrait of Prince." But the Court didn't define how narrowly or broadly to assess "purpose."

#### Questions Open for Argument:

1. **Granularity of Purpose Analysis:**
   - Is the purpose "education" (broad category, both EchoCraft and Riven)?
   - Or is it "critical education correcting historical myths" (narrow, only EchoCraft) vs. "entertainment through historical narrative" (narrow, only Riven)?
   
2. **Functional vs. Categorical Purpose:**
   - Warhol suggests **functional purpose** matters: "what the user does with the original work"
   - Does EchoCraft's use (memorizing facts critically) differ functionally from Riven's use (memorizing narrative entertainingly)?
   - Or are both just "educational tools," making functional purpose the same?

3. **The "Same Market Category" vs. "Same Purpose" Distinction:**
   - **Warhol majority (p. 1284)**: "Although the purpose could be more specifically described as illustrating a magazine about Prince with a portrait of Prince, one that portrays Prince somewhat differently from Goldsmith's photograph (yet has no critical bearing on her photograph), that degree of difference is not enough for the first factor to favor AWF, given the specific context of the use."
   - **Critical phrase**: "yet has no critical bearing on her photograph"
   - **IMPLICATION**: If we CAN show critical bearing on Riven's work, being in the "same market category" (education) might not doom us, if our specific purpose (critical education) differs from Riven's purpose (entertainment/memorization).

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**ARGUMENT A: Distinct Functional Purpose**
- Riven's Purpose: Entertainment-based memorization through catchy melody + historically inaccurate narrative
- EchoCraft's Purpose: Critical pedagogy—using the recognized melody to create cognitive dissonance that highlights myth vs. reality
- **Key Distinction**: We're not replacing Riven's entertainment function; we're supplementing with critical analysis that targets Riven's historical inaccuracies

**ARGUMENT B: Educational Subcategory Distinction**
- Even if both are "educational," there's a cognizable distinction between:
  - **Passive Learning** (Riven): Absorbing information through repetition
  - **Active Critical Thinking** (EchoCraft): Questioning received narratives, developing analytical skills
- This mirrors Campbell's distinction between "entertainment" broadly and "critical commentary" specifically

**RISK**: Warhol repeatedly emphasized that AWF's use was "so similar to the photograph's typical use" (p. 1286). If courts view all educational uses as fungible, we lose this argument.

---

### B. "Critical Bearing" Flexibility

**Campbell** established that criticism with "critical bearing on the original" justifies copying, but left undefined:

#### Open Questions:

1. **How Direct Must the Critical Connection Be?**
   - Must we criticize Riven's *melody* or *artistic choices*?
   - Or can we criticize Riven's *content/message/historical accuracy*?
   - Does criticizing the *effect* of Riven's work (perpetuating myths) count as "critical bearing"?

2. **Pedagogical Criticism vs. Aesthetic Criticism:**
   - Campbell involved aesthetic parody (making fun of the song's style/message)
   - Can we claim "critical bearing" based on pedagogical inadequacy (Riven teaches wrong facts)?
   - No Supreme Court precedent directly addresses this

3. **The "Conjure Up" Necessity Test:**
   - Campbell: parody must "conjure up" the original to make recognizable comment
   - Can we argue: to effectively criticize Riven's *educational impact*, we must use the melody students already learned from Riven?
   - This reframes copying as necessary to create **pedagogical contrast**, not just convenient

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**CRITICAL EVIDENTIARY NOTE (Santos v. Kimmel):**
Do NOT rely on:
- Our subjective intent ("we wanted to educate critically")
- Riven's subjective intent ("he didn't care about accuracy")

DO rely on:
- What a **reasonable observer** would perceive about our use
- Objective evidence of how our work functions in the market
- Documentary evidence of the message conveyed (promotional materials, curriculum guides, teacher feedback, etc.)

**ARGUMENT A: Educational Impact Criticism**
- A **reasonable observer** would perceive our use as criticizing **Riven's song's specific perpetuation of historical myths**
- We're not just criticizing "history" generally (satire); the observable function of our work is to correct **this specific song's** false teachings
- The criticism has critical bearing on Riven's **educational effect**: his work teaches false history to children
- By using his melody with corrected lyrics, we create direct commentary on the inadequacy of his educational content

**ARGUMENT B: Pedagogical Necessity**
- To correct the *specific* harm Riven's song causes (embedding false narratives via catchy melody), we must use **that same melody**
- Students who learned history from Riven need to hear **corrections** in the same musical context to displace the incorrect memories
- This is analogous to parody's need to "conjure up" the original—we must trigger recognition of Riven's version to effectively replace it with truth

**RISK**: Warhol may foreclose this argument. The Court stated (p. 1286): "AWF offers no independent justification, let alone a compelling one, for copying the photograph, other than to convey a new meaning or message. As explained, that alone is not enough for the first factor to favor fair use." If courts view pedagogical necessity as just "conveying new meaning," we lose.

**COUNTER-RISK MITIGATION**: Distinguish by emphasizing we're not just "conveying new meaning" (which satire does); we're **commenting on Riven's work's inadequacy** (which parody does). The critical bearing is on Riven's song, not on history generally.

---

### C. Amount Copied vs. Necessity for Purpose

**Campbell HN12-13** established that parody may copy the "heart" of the original if necessary to "conjure up" the original for recognizable criticism, but must "depart markedly" thereafter.

#### Open Questions:

1. **How Much is "Necessary"?**
   - Campbell: 2 Live Crew copied opening bass riff + first line, then departed markedly in lyrics
   - EchoCraft: 87.5% verbatim copying (melody + structure), departed in lyrics
   - Is melodic copying more defensible than lyrical copying for educational criticism?

2. **Quality vs. Quantity:**
   - Does copying the melody (the "heart" for memorization purposes) justify high percentage copying?
   - Or does 87.5% quantitative taking overwhelm qualitative necessity?

3. **"Depart Markedly" Standard:**
   - Campbell praised 2 Live Crew for departing in lyrics, music overlays, key changes
   - EchoCraft departed completely in lyrics but kept melody identical
   - Is this sufficient "marked departure"?

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**ARGUMENT A: Melody is the Pedagogical Heart**
- For a memorization tool, the **melody** is the "heart" that makes the original effective
- We needed to copy the full melody to "conjure up" the original in students' minds
- But we "departed markedly" in lyrics (100% different words conveying corrected history)
- This parallels Campbell: copying the recognizable element (bass riff = melody) while departing in message (lyrics)

**ARGUMENT B: Educational Copying vs. Entertainment Copying**
- The 87.5% figure is misleading because it measures melodic/structural similarity
- For **criticism of educational content**, we need the structural framework (melody) but change the substance (lyrics)
- The high copying percentage serves the critical purpose: highlighting Riven's historical inaccuracies by replacing his lyrics with corrected ones in the same musical context

**RISK**: Research shows Second Circuit has approved copying as high as 4.3% in magazine context. 87.5% is exponentially higher and may be per se unreasonable regardless of purpose.

**COUNTER-RISK MITIGATION**: Distinguish magazine context (visual similarity) from musical context (melodic similarity for memorization). Also emphasize Campbell's teaching that copying the "heart" is permissible when necessary for critical purpose.

---

### D. Defining "Established Market" Under Factor 4

**Campbell** and **Warhol** both emphasize Factor 4 looks at actual market harm, not speculative markets.

#### Open Questions:

1. **Preliminary Negotiations vs. Established Market:**
   - At what point do licensing discussions become an "established market"?
   - Luna's talks with schools: just exploration or established market?

2. **Derivative Works vs. Substitute Uses:**
   - Factor 4 protects market for derivative works copyright holder "would in general develop or license"
   - Would Riven "in general" license critical corrections of his own work?
   - Campbell says "no derivative market for criticism"—does this apply to educational criticism?

3. **Nonprofit Educational Market:**
   - Is there a separate "nonprofit educational market" from commercial entertainment market?
   - If so, does Luna's preliminary exploration establish it, or must there be actual signed deals?

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**ARGUMENT A: No Established Educational Licensing Market**
- Luna had only **preliminary discussions**, no executed contracts
- TCA Television: preliminary negotiations insufficient to establish protected market
- Riven has not demonstrated an actual, functioning educational licensing market

**ARGUMENT B: No Derivative Market for Criticism**
- Even if Riven could license educational derivatives, he would NOT license **critical corrections** of his own inaccuracies
- Campbell: creators don't license criticism of their own works
- Our use falls into the "no protectible derivative market for criticism" category

**ARGUMENT C: Nonprofit Market Distinction**
- Our below-cost licensing to schools serves a **different market** from Riven's commercial entertainment sales
- We're not usurping his revenue; we're serving schools that want critical educational content, which Riven doesn't provide
- This is market complementarity, not substitution

**RISK**: If courts view "education" as single undifferentiated market, our use might be seen as substituting for Luna's potential educational licensing, harming Factor 4.

---

### E. Transformative Use vs. Transformative Work

**Warhol's central innovation** was distinguishing "transformative work" (artistic merit) from "transformative use" (market function). But the boundaries remain unclear.

#### Open Questions:

1. **Can a Transformative Work Ever Overcome Same-Purpose Use?**
   - Warhol suggests no: "even though Orange Prince adds new expression to Goldsmith's photograph, as the District Court found, this Court agrees with the Court of Appeals that, in the context of the challenged use, the first fair use factor still favors Goldsmith" (p. 1277)
   - But is this absolute, or could a *sufficiently* transformative work with *sufficient* justification prevail despite same-market use?

2. **Does Educational Context Change the Analysis?**
   - Warhol involved commercial art licensing
   - Does nonprofit educational use receive more latitude for transformation analysis?
   - Factor 1 explicitly mentions "nonprofit educational purposes"—does this create a carve-out?

3. **What Counts as "Independent Justification"?**
   - Warhol required "independent justification, let alone a compelling one, for copying" beyond new meaning (p. 1286)
   - What satisfies this? Criticism with critical bearing (yes). What else?
   - Does pedagogical necessity count?

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**ARGUMENT A: Nonprofit Educational Use Receives Greater Latitude**
- Factor 1 explicitly distinguishes "commercial nature" from "nonprofit educational purposes"
- While transformation alone doesn't overcome same-purpose commercial use (Warhol), the calculus may differ for nonprofit educational use
- Our transformation (correcting historical inaccuracies) combined with nonprofit educational context should weigh more heavily than Warhol's commercial art licensing

**ARGUMENT B: We Have Independent Justification Beyond New Meaning**
- Unlike AWF (which offered only "new aesthetic" and "commentary on celebrity"), we offer:
  1. **Critical bearing** on Riven's historical inaccuracies
  2. **Pedagogical necessity** of using the melody to displace false memories
  3. **Nonprofit educational mission** serving public interest
- These constitute "compelling justification" for copying under Warhol's test

**RISK**: Warhol's language suggests that even strong justification won't overcome "substantially the same purpose" when use is commercial. But our nonprofit status may distinguish us.

### F. Factual Content vs. Creative Expression

#### Open Questions:

1. **Does Factual Nature of Subject Matter Affect Analysis?**
   - Both Riven and EchoCraft convey historical facts (albeit different ones)
   - Feist: facts are not copyrightable, only creative expression
   - Does this limit Riven's copyright to his melodic/lyrical expression, not the historical narrative?

2. **Educational Speech Considerations:**
   - Does First Amendment interest in correcting historical falsehoods create fair use space?
   - No Supreme Court precedent directly addresses this in educational context

#### Strategic Framing for EchoCraft:

**ARGUMENT: Correction of Factual Errors Serves Core Copyright Purpose**
- Copyright aims to "promote progress of science and useful arts"
- Allowing copyright to prevent correction of historical inaccuracies **undermines** this constitutional purpose
- Our use advances copyright's goals by improving educational accuracy

**RISK**: Courts may view this as trying to use copyright to regulate speech content, which could backfire. Tread carefully.

### Vulnerable Points Requiring Aggressive Defense

#### VULNERABILITY 1: "Same Purpose" Attack
**Luna's Likely Argument:** Both songs serve the same purpose—education about historical events. Both are used in schools. Both are memorization tools. Under Warhol, this means Factor 1 weighs against EchoCraft.

**Our Defense:**
- **Primary**: Invoke "critical bearing" exception. We're not just "educating"—we're criticizing Riven's educational inadequacy. This is parody, not satire.
- **Secondary**: Emphasize functional distinction. Riven entertains while teaching (passive); we provoke critical thinking (active).
- **Tertiary**: Nonprofit status distinguishes us from Warhol's commercial licensing.

#### VULNERABILITY 2: 87.5% Copying
**Luna's Likely Argument:** EchoCraft copied nearly verbatim. Campbell approved copying only the "opening riff + first line." 87.5% is exponentially more than necessary.

**Our Defense:**
- **Primary**: The melody is the "heart" for memorization purposes. We needed it to "conjure up" the original, per Campbell HN12.
- **Secondary**: We "departed markedly" in lyrics (100% different words), satisfying Campbell HN13.
- **Tertiary**: Musical context differs from literary context. Melody copying necessary for pedagogical function.

#### VULNERABILITY 3: No "Critical Bearing" on Riven's Work
**Luna's Likely Argument:** EchoCraft criticizes history, not Riven's song. Under Warhol, this is satire that "can stand on its own feet" and requires independent justification.

**Our Defense:**
- **Primary**: We're criticizing Riven's *song's* perpetuation of myths, not history abstractly. The critical bearing is on his work's educational impact.
- **Secondary**: Pedagogical necessity to use his melody to displace false memories he created.
- **Tertiary**: Distinguish Warhol's "commentary on celebrity" (no critical bearing on Goldsmith's photograph) from our "correction of specific historical inaccuracies in Riven's song."

## V. CONCLUSION

**The Post-Warhol Landscape:**

Warhol significantly constrained fair use by refocusing from "what the work is" to "what the use does." The decision prioritizes market analysis over artistic merit, creating challenges for secondary uses even when highly transformative.

**EchoCraft's Strategic Position:**

We face an uphill battle on Factor 1 under Warhol's framework, but we're not without weapons:

1. **Strong Defensive Position:**
   - Factor 4: No established educational market, no market harm from criticism
   - Factor 2: Riven's creative work nature matters less given our transformative purpose
   - Factor 3: Melodic copying justified as "heart" necessary to conjure up original
   - Holistic balancing: Even weak Factor 1 can be overcome by strong Factors 2-4

2. **Aggressive Offensive Position:**
   - "Critical bearing" argument: We criticize Riven's work specifically, not history generally
   - "Distinct purpose" argument: Critical pedagogy ≠ entertainment education
   - "Nonprofit educational" statutory language: We fit Factor 1's explicit carve-out
   - "Pedagogical necessity" argument: Must use his melody to displace false memories

3. **Key Battlegrounds (Bendy Parts):**
   - How narrowly to define "purpose" (broad "education" vs. narrow "critical correction")
   - Whether "critical bearing" extends to pedagogical criticism (factual accuracy) or only aesthetic parody
   - How much copying is "necessary" in musical educational context
