II. **Why Punish?**

    A.  **Retribution**

        a)  Essentially "Backward-looking"

        b)  Kant: Punish because and only because the person deserves it
            (moral blameworthiness)

            (i) Crimes are moral wrongs because they violate categorical
                imperatives (the penal law)

            (ii) Kant has both a negative and positive retribution
                 theory

            (iii) Punishment is necessary to correct or balance out a
                  wrong in society

            (iv) Even is society disbanded, every last convicted
                 murderer should be killed

            (v) Utilitarian theories are unacceptable unless they have a
                retributivist limit (see pg 94.2)

        c)  Moore: All retributivism requires is that there be desert
            (that punishment is justified by the moral culpability of
            those who receive it.)

        d)  Morris: Retribution is about maintaining an equilibrium of
            benefits and burdens on society

            (i) Based on a theory of a social contract of consent

            (ii) Those who break the law violate the social contract, so
                 it is reasonable and just to punish them.

        e)  Though retribution implies lex talionis few would argue for
            this outside of homicide

        f)  There is also an element of restitution/vengeance to the
            victim in some retributivist arguments

    B.  **Utilitarianism**

        a)  Essentially "forward-looking"

        b)  Bentham: Punishment should be employed if it serves a
            purpose to the world

            (i) Only punish if it will cause a benefit, to "exclude some
                greater evil"

            (ii) Not necessarily tied to what the person "deserves"

            (iii) The benefit of the punishment must be weighed against
                  its cost to society

        c)  Becker: Economic analysis applies to crime and punishment

            (i) Assume rational actors will always commit crimes at the
                right price, so some policing and punishment is required

            (ii) Must minimize the cost of both crime and
                 policing/punishment

            (iii) To eliminate crime completely would be too costly

            (iv) There is some optimal level of both crimes and
                 policing/punishment

        d)  Deterrence: Punish to deter people from committing crimes in
            the future (metrics are not reliable)

            (i) Punishment must exceed the benefit of committing a crime

            (ii) Concept of marginal deterrence: don't over punish a
                 crime because then people wont care about doing a
                 severe crime over a lesser crime

            (iii) Deterrence relates to both the probability of
                  detection and to the sentence

                  (1) Expected benefit \< probability of being
                      punished\*expected punishment/length of sentence

            (iv) General deterrence: D's punishment is meant to deter
                 the general community

            (v) Specific deterrence: D's punishment is meant to deter
                specifically D's future misconduct

        e)  Incapacitation: Punish so perpetrator will not be able to
            hurt anyone else

            (i) Works by definition (social physics)

            (ii) Involves weighting costs of certain types of crimes,
                 perpetrators, and violations

                 (1) Both in a deontological sense and a
                     monetary/societal sense

                 (2) Many jurisdictions have risk score tests based on
                     age, education, criminal history and more

            (iii) General Incapacitation: imprison everyone at risk of
                  committing another crime

            (iv) Selective Incapacitation: Only punish people most
                 likely to commit serious crimes at high rates

                 (1) 6% of people commit 50% of crime

        f)  Rehabilitation: D's punishment prevents crime by altering
            his behavior

            (i) Vitiello: "\[M\]oral imperative to offer the offender a
                chance at moral transformation."

    C.  **Model Penal Code 1.02(2): Purposes**

        a)  to prevent the commission of offenses; (Deterrence)

        b)  to promote the correction and rehabilitation of offenders;
            (Rehabilitation)

        c)  to safeguard offenders against excessive, disproportionate

    D.  **Criticisms**

        a)  While some like to show how these theories can work

            (i) If they were all commensurate, we would not need/have

        b)  What drives punishment in our criminal justice system is

III. **How Do We Punish?**

     A.  **Truth and Punishment**

         a)  Foucault: The truth we believe is produced by judicial
             forms

             (i) Trial by combat had strict rules. Necessary to allow
                 god to guide the hand of the righteous

             (ii) Ordeals like one's where the accused were thrown into
                  the water and then sunk or floated also showed if they
                  were accepted or rejected by god

             (iii) Social tests where one won by having more respected
                   people swear for them showed who was the better man

         b)  Langbein: In Pre-Enlightenment Continental Europe, torture
             was a method used to procure truth from the accused and use
             a basis for their ultimate punishment

             (i) Torture enabled direct, not circumstantial evidence
                 (history of two eye witness and confession)

             (ii) Torture was meant to be safeguard used just on those
                  who were very likely guilty

                  (1) Torture was highly regimented

                  (2) Only employed in the face of half-truths which
                      already implicated somebody's guilt

                  (3) This didn't work out all that well

             (iii) We have Same problem today "it is not possible to
                   make coercion compatible with truth"

             (iv) In England there was no torture because their justice
                  system was predicated less on absolute truth, more on
                  the jury, the "wisdom of the countryside"

         c)  Bernard Harcourt, "Post-Modern Meditations on Punishment"

             (i) Punishment is not "rational," but is culturally
                 determined

                 (1) E.g., deterrence, racial profiling, "order
                     maintenance" policing

             (ii) Each is justified as being "rational" or "efficient,"
                  but are based on assumptions and predictive tools that
                  are not reliable and/or biased.

             (iii) Solution: Randomization

             (iv) How to Use:

                  (1) Critique policies/laws that purport to rely on
                      empirical data

                  (2) Make point that law is culturally determined &
                      contains ideology beneath it

IV. **Capital Punishment**

    A.  **Death Penalty Jurisprudence**

        a)  3 historical stages to death penalty in US

            (i) Antebellum period: automatic DP if convicted of certain
                crimes

            (ii) 1860s-1960s: increased (approaching absolute)
                 discretion of judge/jury to impose DP

            (iii) Post [Furman/Gregg]{.underline}: guided jury (and
                  judge) discretion

        b)  [McGauthu v. CA]{.underline} (1971): SCOTUS rejects DP
            challenge to DP

        c)  [Furman v. GA]{.underline} (1972): SCOTUS strikes down DP on
            8^th^ Amd grounds

            (i) No controlling rationale. Certainly did not say the
                death penalty was *per se* unconstitutional

        d)  States respond by reenacting death penalty statutes: 4 main
            types

            (i) Pyramid (ex. GA, specifically approved of in
                [Gregg]{.underline} (1976))

                (1) MurderðŸ¡ªaggravated murder (specifically
                    defined)ðŸ¡ªaggravating circumstanceðŸ¡ªdeath

                (2) All these elements have statutory definitions

                (3) The death sentence is only imposed upon the
                    recommendation of the jury (or judge)

            (ii) Weighing (ex. AL)

                 (1) First have a trial for a (specifically defined)
                     capital crime

                 (2) Second jury weighs aggravating and mitigating
                     circumstances and makes recommendation

            (iii) Special Questions (ex. TX, specifically approved of in
                  [Jurek]{.underline} (1976))

                  (1) Jury asked specific questions at the end of the
                      sentencing phase, if they answer yes to each one
                      then death is necessarily imposed. A new one
                      basically asks for a jury recommendation

            (iv) Mandatory (ex. NC, Ruled unconstitutional in
                 [Woodson]{.underline})

        e)  Note inherent conflict in DP jurisprudence between
            individualized sentencing and consistency

        f)  SCOTUS has carved out thing from the death penalty ex.
            mentally retarded, juveniles, jud override

        g)  Death penalty in practice today

            (i) Bifurcated proceedings

            (ii) Most have jury sentencing

            (iii) Few states (ex. AZ) have judge sentencing

            (iv) Consideration of aggravators/mitagators (or special
                 questions)

            (v) Individualized sentencing determination

            (vi) appellate review

            (vii) stages of collateral review (the sheet from the
                  beginning of the semester

        h)  Stephen Bright: Whether or not D gets DP depends too much on
            if he can afford a good lawyer

            (i) This is reprehensible and a breakdown of the adversarial
                systtem

        i)  Steiker & Steiker: DP jurisprudence is an ineffective
            administrative tool but an effective legitimating tool. This
            legitimization had effects on 3 levels

            (i) Makes those involved in administering the DP in specific
                cases more comfortable with their role

            (ii) Makes the American public more comfortable with the
                 capital punishment system

            (iii) Makes the American public more comfortable with entire
                  legal system and social order
