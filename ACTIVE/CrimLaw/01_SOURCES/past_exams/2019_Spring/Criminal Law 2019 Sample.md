This case is all about justifications of the use of force. The prosecution will probably charge Thomas with
manslaughter in the second degree for killing Miles, with the mens rea of recklessness, as the prosecution will
try to apply MPC 3.09 to negate Thomas' self-defense claim. It will be hard for the prosecution to establish a
higher mens rea, such as intent to kill (moving this to manslaughter in the first degree) given the subjective self-

defense available.
Defense

Thomas' defense will first argue that he should not be culpable at all. Thomas may first try disclaim the actus
reus and argue this result crime was not voluntary. According to MPC 2.02, a person is not guilty of an offense
unless the conduct includes a voluntary act. The defense can argue that a narrow time frame should be used
(Kelman) and that in the moment, as in Zimmerman's case, Thomas just feared for his life and pulled the trigger
involuntarily or under the influence of extreme emotional disturbance. MPC 3.04 does not even require the
defendant to retreat, so he had a duty to stand his ground in fear for his life. Assuming arguendo this defense
fails (given the multiple bullets fired), the defense will try to argue for a lesser culpability like criminally negligent
homicide. They will say Thomas should have been aware of the risk of death but that this was not in his

conscious mental state at the moment because he was merely fending for his life.

Their strongest defense is MPC 3.04.012(2), which says one is justified in using deadly force if he BELIEVES
that using such force is necessary to prevent imminent death or great bodily harm to himself. This is a subjective
standard and thus, is very favorable to the defense. The defense will point to the sheer number of teens (7),
who carried themselves as gang, as evidence of Thomas' fear. The police reports show that the teens
threatened Thomas both on Facebook and in person (even calling the teens' behavior "foolish"). The teens
possessed illegal fireworks, a padlock, and a Taser--all of which potentially could have enhanced their threats.
Thomas could have believed the Taser was a gun--especially given the late hour of the night and darkness--in
fact, the police first reported the teens had a gun and Thomas said he feared his life was in danger. The defense
will also argue the teens stopped Thomas and tried to buy marijuana from him, which shows that the teens
were trying to engage in unlawful activity. There is no evidence that Thomas had pot on him. 3.04.012 allows
one to use deadly force if he believes he is trying to prevent the imminent commission of a forcible felony, too,
and given the subjective standard, the defense can argue that even if buying marijuana is not a forcible felony as
defined in 3.04.08, Thomas may not have known that and could've believed they were trying to commit other
felonies. Given the insults, the defense may also claim provocation as the police said Thomas had been
"minding his own business before the encounter." This defense will likely fail given that it is only usually used in

the common law as a heat-of-passion defense (ie witnessing adultery). Finally, the defense may try to argue

The prosecution will first argue the culpability of Thomas was recklessness. Under MPC 2.04, recklessness
requires consciously disregarding a substantial and unjustifiable risk--a disregard which involves a gross

next turn to the self-defense provisions. Under 3.04.012(2), a person who uses deadly force does not have a
duty to retreat UNLESS he is is engaged in criminal activity. If the prosecution can prove that the defendant
did indeed have pot on him, he may have had a duty to retreat and the self-defense provision would not be

In addition, this self-defense provision requires that the person believes the force was necessary to prevent

wider time frame to show that at the moment Thomas fired one bullet--let alone multiple bullets--he could not
have believed his life was imminently in danger because the teens were already in the SUV fleeing. Under MPC

3.04.012(2) is very subjective, the prosecution would do well to argue that Thomas was reckless in having this
belief in the imminency, just as was found in the case of State v. Norman (even though there was a "reasonable
belief" standard there). The teens were fleeing and did not even have a gun on them; the parents say the teens

force was great. In response, the prosecution will say that Thomas did not exhaust "every reasonable means to
escape" the danger as he could have started fleeing after the teens drove away. He also does not fall under (b)

promoted was flawed because it was based on equality, which assumes that every individual starts life on the
