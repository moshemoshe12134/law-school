## What We Criminalize: Criminal Law & Morality {#slide-6}

Hart-Devlin Debate

John Stuart Mill & H.L.A. Hart  -  The Harm Principle : The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others.

- Mill: It is indispensable to not injure "the interests of one another, or rather certain interests which, either by express legal provision or by tacit understanding, out to be considered as rights."

Lord James Stephen & Lord Patrick Devlin  -  Legal Moralism : The criminal law is always based on moral principles and serves to uphold a moral order.

- Devlin: "the law must base itself on Christian morals and . . . enforce them, not simply because they are the morals of most of us, . . . but for the compelling reason that without the help of Christian teaching the law will fail."

## Justifications & Excuses {#slide-11}

Justifications & excuses don't seek to refute any element of the prosecution's case. They suggest considerations that negate culpability even when all the elements of the offense are present.  

- Justification: Act-focused. The act was justified because it is not wrong/blameworthy or if it has positive effects. Justifies entire classes of behavior.

<!-- -->

- D accepts responsibility, but denies that it was bad.     

<!-- -->

- Excuse: Defendant-focused. The act is excused because the defendant did not act entirely voluntarily. Excuses only the behavior of the specific defendant. 
- D admits that the behavior was bad, but does not accept responsibility. 

Is self-defense an excuse or justification?

## Justifications & Excuses:  Battered Women Syndrome {#slide-17}

State v. Norman  (N.C., 1989)

- Facts: D's husband was badly abusive throughout their 25-year marriage. After multiple futile attempts to leave, husband beat her, refused to let her eat, and made her sleep on the floor. D brought grandchild to mother's home, returned with pistol, and shot husband while he was sleeping. 

<!-- -->

- At trial, presented evidence that she suffered from battered wife syndrome as proof of perfect self-defense. 
- Jury convicted her of voluntary manslaughter; she was sentenced to six years in prison.

<!-- -->

- N.C. Law: "Only where a D has killed due to a  reasonable belief  that death or great bodily harm was imminent can the justification for homicide remain clearly and firmly rooted in necessity."

## Justifications & Excuses:  Battered Women Syndrome {#slide-18}

State v. Norman  (N.C., 1989)

- Question: Should the court have instructed the jury on the self-defense justification where there was evidence of BWS? 
- Holding: No, D was not entitled to self-defense instruction based on BWS because the threat was not  imminent  enough since husband was asleep. Testimony about indefinite fears concerning what her sleeping husband might do at some time in the future did not tend to establish a fear of  imminent death or bodily harm  at the time of the killing.

## Justifications & Excuses:  Duty to Retreat {#slide-20}

- Traditional view

<!-- -->

- English common law imposed strict duty to retreat. A person could use deadly force in self-defense only after exhausting every chance to flee, when his back was against the wall.

<!-- -->

- Late 1800s view

<!-- -->

- The law will not permit the taking of a human life to repel mere trespass, but a true man who is without fault is not obliged to fly from an assailant.  See Erwin v. State  (Ohio, 1876).

<!-- -->

- Modern View

<!-- -->

- The "true man" doctrine (originating in  Erwin ) is rejected in some jurisdictions (e.g., Ohio) and accepted in others (e.g., Tennessee)
- True man/no retreat/stand your ground rule considered the majority approach.
- MPC adopts a version of this rule with some exceptions.

<!-- -->

- Castle Exception

<!-- -->

- In all jurisdictions requiring retreat before using deadly force, an exception is made when the defendant is attacked in their own home by an intruder.  See People v. Tomlin  (N.Y., 1914).
- Different states require different things when involving guests or co-occupants.

## Grand Juries {#slide-24}

Constitutionally guaranteed protection from the Federal government.

- Most states also use grand juries.
- For felonies, not misdemeanors and violations.
- Generally 24 jurors who sit and hear a bunch of cases in quick succession.
- More or less controlled by the prosecutor.

<!-- -->

- No defense (defense counsel or defendant) or judge. 
- Closed to public and media.
- The prosecutor instructs the grand jury on the law.

<!-- -->

- Grand juries will issue indictments in most cases.

<!-- -->

- Standard for indictment is probable cause: Requires only the kind of fair probability on which reasonable and prudent people, not legal technicians, act.

<!-- -->

- In controversial cases, state law will often allow the appointment of a special or independent prosecutor.
- A grand Jury decision  is  not a verdict.
