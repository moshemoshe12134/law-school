## Winter 2013 Question #2 {#slide-10}

You  are  a  legislative  aide  to  a  state  senator  who  is  drafting  a  new  sexual  assault  statute  for your state and trying to compare existing statutes and case law. The senator has asked you  to  read  through  the  facts  presented  in  the  New  Jersey  Supreme  Court  decision  in  State  in  the  Interest  of  M.T.S.  (see  Criminal  Law  and  Its  Processes,  9th  edition  at  363-367; 8th edition at 318-322).  The senator would like the behavior that occurred in M.T.S. to  give  rise  to  some  kind  of  criminal  responsibility.   The  senator  asks  you  a  series  of  questions. Please answer to the best of your ability. 

1. If we enacted the approach taken in Maryland reflected in the court's decision in State v. Rusk  (9th  edition  p.  343;  8th  edition  p.  302),  would  the  behavior  in  question (i.e. the behavior that occurred in M.T.S.) be subject to criminal liability? 

2. If  we  enacted  the  approach  taken  in  the  MPC  Sec.  213.1(2)  for  "gross  sexual  imposition"  (discussed,  for  instance,  at  9th  edition  p.  358;  8th  edition  p.  315),  would the behavior in question be subject to criminal liability? 

3. Is there any state statute or court decision that you are aware of, other than those from  New  Jersey,  where  the  behavior  in  question  would  subject  the  accused  to  criminal  liability?  If  so,  what  is  the  language  exactly  that  would  criminalize  the  behavior in question? 

4. What  in  your  opinion  would  be  the  best  way  to  write  statutory  language  that  would criminalize the behavior in question without too great a risk of criminalizing innocent behavior?

## Slide 11

MPC 213.1(2)

2.  Gross Sexual Imposition .  A male who has sexual intercourse with a female not his wife commits a felony of the third degree if:

(a) he compels her to submit by any threat that would prevent resistance by a woman of ordinary resolution; or

(b) he knows that she suffers from a mental disease or defect which renders her incapable of appraising the nature of her conduct; or

(c) he knows that she is unaware that a sexual act is being committed upon her or that she submits because she mistakenly supposes that he is her husband.

## Answer  {#slide-12}

Because no evidence of force / threat of force beyond penetration, statute in Rusk wouldn't cover acts in MTS

How does this differ from holding in MTS?

Does it differ based on which witness's account we believe?

If the court believed she was sleeping when MTS had sex with her,  (c) might be met -- no evidence that (b) or (a) were met; no evidence she mistakenly thought he was her husband

court in MTS concluded the victim was not sleeping at the time of the penetration, meaning that MPC 2.13(2) would not be applicable.

What statutes have we read? What other cases include statutes that could cover this behavior?

Consider whether different states allow for mistake of fact defense

\"Penetration without consent is an offense. Consent to penetration cannot be inferred from consent to kissing or non-pentrative sexual acts alone.\" Criminalize \"Sexual contact or penetration without the affirmative consent of the victim, either express or implied\" with no mention of force.
