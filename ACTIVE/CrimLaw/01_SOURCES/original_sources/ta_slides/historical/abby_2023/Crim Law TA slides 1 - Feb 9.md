## Actus Reus {#slide-7}

- Policy: a  just punishment necessitates that it be for a voluntary, wrongful, or potentially harmful conduct that is specified in advanced by a statute
- Common law  requirements :

<!-- -->

- For criminal liability to attach, there must be a voluntary act or an omission (Martin v. State)
- Criminal conduct must be voluntary

<!-- -->

- Note from class slides: not voluntary if reflex, sleep, unconscious, hypnosis

<!-- -->

- MPC requirements:

<!-- -->

- Section 2.01 

<!-- -->

- (2) Involuntary acts:

<!-- -->

- (a) a reflex or convulsion; 
- (b) a bodily movement during unconsciousness or sleep; 
- (c) conduct during hypnosis or resulting from hypnotic suggestion; 
- (d) a bodily movement that otherwise is not a product of the effort or determination of the actor, either conscious or habitual.

<!-- -->

- (3) Omissions

<!-- -->

- (1) A person is not guilty of an offense unless his liability is based on conduct which includes a voluntary act or the omission to perform an act of which he is physically capable.
- (3) Liability for the commission of an offense may not be based on an omission unaccompanied by action unless:

<!-- -->

- (a) the omission is expressly made sufficient by the law defining the offense; or 
- (b) a duty to perform the omitted act is otherwise imposed by law

## Actus Reus: Voluntariness {#slide-8}

Martin v. State

- Takeaway: Criminal liability may only be imposed when the unlawful conduct is committed  voluntarily.
- Facts: Martin was convicted of being drunk on a public highway, but was arrested at home and taken out onto highway by officer.
- Statute: "Any person who, while intoxicated or drunk, appears in any public place where one  or more persons are present...and manifests a drunken condition by boisterous or indecent  conduct, or loud and profane discourse, shall, on conviction, be fined"
- Holding: reversed Martin's conviction b/c "appear\[ing\] in . . . public" needs to be voluntary to support conviction
- Policy: See Kelman,  Interpretive Construction  Article -- thinks this court took a too narrow time frame and Martin should've been convicted when considering his voluntary acts at T1  (intoxication and wife beating inside his home)

## Actus Reus: Voluntariness {#slide-9}

People v. Newton

- Takeaway: If someone is unconscious (either mentally or physically), their acts are not  voluntary to satisfy actus reus requirement.
- Facts/Posture: D was convicted of voluntary manslaughter for shooting a police officer. He testified that he  had no gun during the altercation, but that he had been shot and was "unconscious or  semiconscious" until he arrived at the hospital. Medical expert confirmed that he could have  had a profound reflex shock reaction. 
- Rule: Where not self-induced, as by voluntary intoxication or the equivalent, unconsciousness is a complete defense to a charge of criminal homicide (Pen. Code Â§26(5)).

## Actus Reus: Voluntariness {#slide-10}

People v. Decina

- Takeaway:  Not all acts have to be voluntary for D to be liable. Here,  although action that immediately led to the accident was a result of involuntary activity (seizure), D voluntarily chose to  drive knowing his condition so he is liable.
- Facts: D drove car knowing he had had epileptic attacks that caused him to lose consciousness; suffered an attack while driving and killed 4  people. 
- Statute: "A person who operates or drives any vehicle of any kind in a reckless or culpably negligent manner, whereby a human being is killed is guilty of criminal negligence in the operation of a vehicle resulting in death."
- Rule: A criminal defendant who disregards the consequences that can result from driving a  vehicle with knowledge of a health condition that can produce involuntary actions may be  found guilty of vehicular homicide. 
- Reasoning: The earlier voluntary act of getting into the car and driving it satisfies the "voluntary act" element of the crime.
- Holding: Ds awareness of his condition and decision to still drive render him liable for culpable  negligence. 

<!-- -->

- Court draws distinction b/w foreseeability from known condition and unexpected  heart attack (no liability).

## Actus Reus: Omissions {#slide-11}

Jones v. United States

- Takeaway: In order for a D to be convicted of an omission, the prosecution must prove that the D  was under a legal duty to act.
- Facts: Jones, family friend, found guilty of involuntary manslaughter through failure to provide for  10-month old baby of Shirley Green. Conflicting evidence as to how long Green lived w/D, whether she had agreed to care for the baby,  and whether D was paid for taking care of baby. Uncontested that D had financial means to care for the child.
- Rule: "The duty neglected must be a legal duty, and not a mere moral obligation. It must be a duty imposed by law or by contract, and the omission to perform the duty must be the immediate and direct cause of death." (Beardsley)
- Holding: reversed and remanded conviction b/c trial court failed to provide jury instruction on  necessity for finding legal duty of care despite factual inconsistencies

<!-- -->

- Where is a failure to act a breach of a legal duty?

<!-- -->

- (1) statute -- imposes duty to care for another 
- (2) status -- one stands in certain status relationship to another (parent to child) 
- (3) contractual duty to care for another 
- (4) seclusion -- voluntarily assumed care and prevent others from rendering aid

<!-- -->

- Notes cases: Commonwealth v. Pestinikas - conviction upheld after agreeing to feed someone but then letting him starve

## Mens Rea {#slide-16}

Regina v. Cunningham

- Takeaway: mens rea of malice/maliciousness requires at least recklessness
- Facts: Cunningham tore a gas meter from the gas pipes and stole it. He did not turn off the gas, which caused a great volume of gas to escape and partially asphyxiate Mrs. Wade (his prospective mother-in-law) who lived next door.
- Charged with "unlawfully and maliciously caus\[ing\] to be taken"
- "Maliciously" is either (1) an intention  to do particular harm or (2) recklessness of doing an action when harm is  foreseeable. 

<!-- -->

- Defendant is liable because he was reckless, though he didn't intend harm
- Negligence is not enough to satisfy mens rea of malice

<!-- -->

- Holding: trial court erred in jury instructions by saying malice meant wickedness

## Mens Rea {#slide-17}

Regina v. Faulkner

- Takeaway: maliciousness requires being intentional and  wilful ; can't transfer intent from lesser primary  felony  to the collateral felony
- Issue: If, while a person is engaging in a felony, they accidentally do some collateral act, that if done intentionally would be unlawful, should they be found guilty?
- If another felony occurs accidentally (burning down ship) during the course of your primary felony (stealing bottles of rum on a ship), court must engage in mens rea (malice) inquiry for  each felony separately. Cannot convict D of collateral felony just for having sufficient mens rea for the first. No transferred intent or proximate cause even if one felony was a consequence of the other.
- Holding: defendant can't be convicted for an act that is the unintended and unforeseeable consequence of a lesser offense. In order to maliciously set fire to the ship, defendant must have acted intentionally and willfully.

## Mens Rea {#slide-19}

Elonis v. United States (2015)

- Takeaway: When there is no clear mens rea, use the statutory text to interpret one into the statute. If one cannot be read into the statute, apply the default rules of the jurisdiction.

<!-- -->

- MPC says that default in those jurisdictions is recklessness

<!-- -->

- Facts / Posture: Defendant was convicted of communicating online threats to ex-wife & Kindergarten class through Facebook posts, which contained violent rap lyrics
- Statute: 18 U.S.C. Â§ 875(c) makes it a crime to transmit in interstate commerce "any communication containing any threat to . . . injure the person of another."
- Reasoning: There is no mens rea requirement in the statute. But the Court generally reads into criminal statutes a mens rea requirement, even if a statute does not contain one. Criminality requires a minimum of negligence. A guilty mind is a necessary element in the indictment and proof of every crime.
- Holding: mens rea is required; in this case, a finding of at least negligence, if not recklessness, was required to convict

## Legality {#slide-21}

There must be fair warning / notice of the nature of the conduct that constitutes an offense - Due Process (14th Amendment)

Keeler v. Superior Court

- Takeaway: due process requires fair warning, which limits unforeseeable judicial expansion of statutory interpretation
- Facts: man assaulted pregnant wife â†’ premature delivery of child; unintentionally caused death of child 
- Statute at issue: California penal code section 187: murder is unlawful killing of human being with malice aforethought

<!-- -->

- Mens rea: malice aforethought (no one knows what that means but sounds like intentional)
- Actus reus: unlawful killing of a human being

<!-- -->

- Jurisdictional problem: all crimes in CA must be defined in the statute -- is a fetus a human?
- Constitutional problem: can't be punished unless there's fair warning, which needs to be specific to this act / crime
- Holding: nothing in this statute to suggest feticide could constitute homicide so that's something for legislature to decide and amend later; violates 14th Amendment due process
- Notes cases: People v. Davis, Bei Bei Shuai v. State

## Legality {#slide-22}

Rogers v. Tennessee

- Takeaway: Judiciary can change common law rules and apply to case at bar if change was not (1) unexpected or (2) unjustified/indefensible.  No fair warning issue as long as prior 2 requirements  are satisfied.

<!-- -->

- Change from  Keeler  - this is a SCOTUS case / came after  Keeler  so it rules â†’ consider scientific advancements

<!-- -->

- Facts: Tennessee decided to abolish the "1 year & a  day" rule (only murder if V dies w/in that time) b/c original justifications for rule were no longer applicable due to medical advancements
- Holding: no due process violation
- Scalia dissent: no fair warning; retroactive change was not predictable
- Notes cases: State v. Picotte, Ex parte Key, People v. LaRosa, Miller v. State

## Legality {#slide-23}

Tomlin v. Hetzel brief

- Takeaway: unforeseeable and indefensible retroactive change between commission of crime and trial  violates due process right to fair notice
- Facts: Tomlin sentenced to life imprisonment w/out possibility of parole.
- Issue: does LWOP violate due process right to fair notice ( Bouie v. City of Columbia )?
- Bouie / Rogers rule: when  a  "judicial  construction  of  a  criminal statute  is  unexpected  and  indefensible  by  reference  to  the  law  which  had been  expressed  prior  to  the  conduct  in  issue,  \[the  construction\]  must  not  be given  retroactive  effect."
- Reasoning : 1975 Alabama Death Penalty Act requires a finding of one or more aggravating circumstances from Sec. 6 to have death penalty sentence (Sec. 6 does not include double intentional murder). No aggravating circumstances in Tomlin's case, thus court "depart\[ing\] downward" from death penalty sentence is an improper retroactive application of Beck, Kyzer, Hays, or Stephens (which were decided between commission of crime and final trial), which  were unforeseeable and indefensible in 1977-78 (when the crime was committed)
- Holding: yes, violates due process right to fair notice

## Vagueness {#slide-24}

Problem with vagueness: too much discretion and too little notice (Due Process - 14th Amendment) â†’ "void for vagueness"

Morales v. Chicago

- Takeaway: An ordinance violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment if it is so vague  that it fails to provide sufficient notice to citizens regarding the type of conduct prohibited and  fails to provide adequate guidelines for law enforcement officers.
- Statute:  Gang Congregation Ordinance prohibits "criminal street gang members" from "loitering" with  one another in public places. Four predicates: (1) police officer must reasonably believe at least  one person in "public place" is a "criminal street gang member;" (2) persons must be "loitering"  ("remaining in any one place with no apparent purpose"); (3) officer must order all persons to  disperse from the area; (4) person must disobey, and whether gang member or not, violates  ordinance if disobeys

<!-- -->

- What are the vague portions? "to remain in any one place with no apparent purpose" + "shall order all  such persons to disperse and remove themselves from the area"

<!-- -->

- Holding: ordinance violates 14th Am DP Clause for vagueness b/c affords absolute  discretion to police  and too little notice to citizens.

<!-- -->

- if ordinance had second specific element in addition to loitering, would probably  be constitutional

<!-- -->

- Dissent:(Thomas, CJ, Scalia): Order to disperse is not vague and what constitutes loitering is  common sense.

## Status Crimes {#slide-26}

Problems with status crimes: no actus reus; creates continual criminal liability; punishment based on unchangeable characteristic

Robinson v. California

- Takeaway: criminalizing addiction alone violates 8th and 14th Amendment
- Facts: Robinson convicted of being addicted to narcotics
- Reasoning: addiction is an illness; defendant didn't use narcotics in the state (no actus reus)
- Holding: violates 8th and 14th Amendment 
- Harlan concurrence: does not agree that it's cruel and unusual but disagrees with jury instruction that "bare desire" to use narcotics was enough to convict (no actus reus)

## Status Crimes {#slide-27}

P owell v. Texas

- Takeaway: punishment for an act - even as a result of an addiction - is not per se unconstitutional 
- Facts: expert testified that defendant knew difference b/w right and wrong when sober and that first drink was voluntary w/qualification that voluntary exercise was influenced by "compulsion" of alcohol.
- Statute: Texas Penal Code Art. 477, "Whoever shall get drunk or be found in a state of  intoxication in any public place, or at any private house except his own, shall be fined not  exceeding one hundred dollars."
- Reasoning: The punishment of conduct that is symptomatic of chronic alcoholism is not in violation of  the Eighth and Fourteenth Amendments' proscription against cruel and unusual punishment.
- Holding: does not violate cruel and unusual punishment - not enough facts to find alcoholism a disease + took act of drinking in public
- Dissent: defendant is being punished for a state he is not in control over + could not prevent himself from appearing in public places
