1. **Three core problems** - Information asymmetry, agency costs, uncertainty
2. **Transaction design** - Structuring deals to address problems
3. **Legal mechanisms** - Contract provisions, representations, warranties
4. **Economic reasoning** - Incentives, risk allocation, efficiency

**Secondary emphasis:**

- Policy implications
- Real-world examples

- Transaction design: ~70%
- Economic analysis: ~20%

### Policy Weight

**Policy in class:** Moderate - Economic policy underlies all transaction design

**Policy on exam:** ~10% - When explaining why certain mechanisms work

**Expected depth:**

- Understand economic principles (efficiency, incentives)
- Apply policy to justify transaction design
- Focus on practical solutions first

**Key policy themes:**

- Information efficiency
- Incentive alignment
- Risk allocation (who should bear which risks)
- Loss minimization

---

### Case Depth

**Case study approach:** Deals uses case studies, not traditional case briefing

**What to focus on:**

- Core economic problem in the case
- Transaction design solution
- Legal mechanisms used
- What worked and what didn't

**What to skip:**

- Extensive factual background (focus on key deal terms)
- Irrelevant details
- Procedural history

**Length:** Varies by assignment (typically 3-5 pages)

---

### Voice and Tone

**Conversational but professional:**

- Write as if explaining to a smart classmate
- 12th grade reading level (clear, not overly academic)
- First-person plural ("we") or second-person ("you")
- Analytical, thoughtful, engaged with material

**Vocabulary level:**

- Target: 12th grade / high school graduate
- Avoid: Overly academic jargon without explanation
- Define: Key terms when first introduced
- Explain: Complex concepts in simple language

---

### Answer Style

**For YES/NO Questions:**

1. **Start with a clear YES or NO** (bolded)
2. **Provide immediate reasoning** (1-2 sentences)
3. **Elaborate with bullet points** for additional nuance

**For Definition Questions:**

1. **Bold the term being defined**
2. **Provide a clear, concise definition**
3. **Give a concrete example or application**
4. **Explain why it matters**

**For Analytical/Calculation Questions:**

1. **Show your work** (calculations, reasoning steps)
2. **Use bullet points** for clarity
3. **Highlight the answer** at the end (bolded)
4. **Explain the implications**

---

### Formatting Rules

**Headers:**

- Use `#` for main title
- Use `##` for question numbers
- Use `###` for sub-questions (A, B, C)

**Emphasis:**

- **Bold** key terms, definitions, YES/NO answers, final answers
- Use *italics* for subtle emphasis only
- **DO NOT use ALL CAPS** (except for acronyms)

**Bullet points:**

- Use for lists of 3+ items
- Keep concise (1-2 lines each)
- Use parallel structure

**Tables:**
Use for:

- Comparing options
- Tool/problem pairs
- Side-by-side analysis

## Course-Specific Conventions

### Economic Reasoning Patterns

**Incentives matter:**

- People respond to how they're rewarded/punished
- Structure deal terms to align incentives

**Risk allocation:**

- Assign risk to whoever can best bear it
- Consider: Risk tolerance, control, information, insurance, diversification

**Information is costly:**

- Due diligence and verification have costs
- Design mechanisms to address information asymmetry

**Contracts as mechanisms:**

- Deal structures solve information and incentive problems
- Trade-offs exist (every solution creates new problems)

---

### Three Core Problems Framework

**Use this framework to analyze every deal:**

1. **Information Asymmetry**

   - One party knows more than the other
   - Solutions: Due diligence, contingent price, representations & warranties, third-party vouching
   - Risk: Solution provides insurance ‚Üí moral hazard
2. **Agency Costs**

   - Decision-maker's interests conflict with principal
   - Identify: Principal and agent
   - Where interests diverge
   - How agent can benefit at principal's expense
   - Solutions: Equity comp, oversight, incentive alignment
3. **Uncertainty**

   - No one knows what will happen
   - Solutions: Contingent payments, insurance, risk allocation
   - Who best bears risk (6 factors)

---

### "Who Best Bears Risk" Analysis

When allocating risk, consider:

1. **Risk tolerance** - Who can best absorb losses?
2. **Control** - Who can influence the outcome?
3. **Information** - Who knows the risk best?
4. **Insurance** - Who can most cheaply insure against the risk?
5. **Diversification** - Who can spread this risk across many events?
6. **Predictive ability** - Who can use historical data/law of large numbers?

---

## Content Requirements

### Must Include for Each Question:

1. **Direct Answer** - Clear response to what's being asked
2. **Reasoning** - Economic or legal logic behind the answer
3. **Examples** - Concrete illustrations of the concept
4. **Connections** - Links to other questions/concepts when relevant
5. **Nuance** - Acknowledgment of complexity or trade-offs

### Special Sections:

**For "Define the Term" Questions:**

- Formal definition
- Real-world example
- Why the concept matters
- How it applies to the scenario

**For Scenario-Based Questions:**

- Facts of the scenario
- Key issue or problem
- Analysis from multiple perspectives
- Practical implications

**For Calculation Questions:**

- Step-by-step math
- Final answer (bolded)
- What the answer means
- Why it matters

## Exam Answer Style

### Structure for Transaction Design Problems

**1. Identify the Problem**

- What core problem(s) does this deal face?
- Information asymmetry, agency costs, uncertainty?

**2. Analyze the Parties**

- Who are the parties?
- What are their interests?
- Where do interests diverge?

**3. Design the Solution**

- What legal mechanisms address the problem?
- How do they work?
- What trade-offs exist?

**4. Justify Your Design**

- Economic reasoning (efficiency, incentives)
- Practical considerations
- Policy justification (if asked)

---

### Writing Style

**DO:**

- Use economic reasoning
- Consider all three core problems
- Design practical solutions
- Justify your choices
- Acknowledge trade-offs

**DON'T:**

- Propose solutions without economic justification
- Ignore trade-offs
- Use solutions that create moral hazard without addressing it
- Over-complicate when simple solution works

---

## Common Patterns to Address

### "Due Diligence vs. Insurance" Insight

Distinguish between:

- **Addressing information asymmetry** (allowing buyer to inspect/verify)
- **Providing insurance** (protecting against buyer's own actions)

**Key framing:** Solutions that go beyond the information asymmetry problem (by effectively providing insurance) create moral hazard problems.

### "Who Best Bears Risk" Analysis

Use the 6-factor framework:

1. Risk tolerance
2. Control
3. Information
4. Insurance
5. Diversification
6. Predictive ability

### "Agency Cost" Framework

When discussing conflicts of interest:

1. Identify the principal and agent
2. Explain where interests diverge
3. Show how the agent can benefit at the principal's expense
4. Propose mechanisms to align incentives

### Why Deals Reviews Are Different

**Standard review format (other courses):** Extract exam-ready material from transcript - focus on what professor emphasized.

**Deals review format:** Compare prep to transcript and note differences.

**Rationale:** Deals prep documents are already rigorous, exam-focused Q&A formats that track literal class steps. The prep already contains the analysis framework and exam-ready material. The review validates and adjusts based on what actually happened in class.

---

### Deals Review Purpose

**Primary function:** Fidelity check between prep and class discussion

**Key questions:**

1. What was covered in class vs. what was in prep?
2. Did professor follow the prep's analytical structure?
3. What additional emphasis/examples/framing did professor add?
4. What was NOT covered (deferred to next class)?

### Review Writing Process (Deals)

**Step 1: Read prep document (5 min)**

- Understand the Q&A structure
- Note the analytical framework
- Identify key concepts and tools

**Step 2: Read transcript (10 min)**

- Mark what was covered
- Note additional content
- Flag differences in emphasis or framing

**Step 3: Create coverage map (3 min)**

- Check off what was covered
- Note what was skipped
- Identify partial coverage

**Step 4: Document differences (15 min)**

- For each covered question:
  - Did class follow prep's analysis?
  - What examples/emphasis added?
  - Any different framing?
- Document additional content not in prep

**Step 5: Extract exam signals (5 min)**

- Professor's explicit signals
- Emphasis patterns (time allocation, repetition)
- Magic phrases and recurring frameworks

**Step 6: Synthesis sections (7 min)**

- Doctrinal insights
- Integration notes
- Self-assessment

**Total time:** 35-45 min

### Key Differences from Standard Review

| Standard Review             | Deals Review                        |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Extract exam-ready material | Validate prep's exam-ready material |
| Create prewrite fragments   | Prep already has analysis framework |
| Focus on professor emphasis | Focus on prep-to-class fidelity     |
| Build from transcript up    | Start with prep, note class deltas  |
| 7-section extraction format | Q&A comparison format               |

---

### When to Use Deals Format

**Use Deals review format when:**

- Prep document is rigorous Q&A format
- Prep tracks literal class discussion structure
- Prep already contains exam-ready analysis
- Goal is to validate/refine prep rather than extract anew

**Use standard review format when:**

- Prep is outline/case brief format
- Class discussion diverges significantly from prep
- Need to extract professor's unique framing
- No pre-existing exam-ready analysis
