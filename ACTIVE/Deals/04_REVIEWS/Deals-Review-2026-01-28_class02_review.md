## 6. Outline Inserts

**Tax Planning Overview**
Tax planning involves regulatory arbitrage: exploiting differences in legal treatment of economically similar arrangements. The five main levers are:

1. **Exclusion**: Making value fall outside gross income (e.g., gifts vs. salary)
2. **Valuation**: Arguing taxed items are worth less (e.g., depreciated car values)
3. **Conversion**: Reclassifying from high-tax to low-tax categories (e.g., ordinary income to capital gain via 83(b))
4. **Deferral**: Paying taxes later rather than sooner (e.g., invoice timing for cash method taxpayers)
5. **Shifting**: Moving income to lower-tax taxpayers or jurisdictions (e.g., multinational structures)

Always analyze tax consequences for all parties and consider frictions (non-tax costs) that may outweigh savings.

**Three Core Problems Application**
While not explicitly framed this way, tax planning addresses commitment problems: parties commit to structures that enforce favorable tax treatment, but must account for information asymmetries (tax authorities scrutinizing substance) and agency problems (different incentives between taxpayers).

## 3. Key Rules/Doctrines

**Tax Treatment of Gifts vs. Salary:**
- Salary: Fully taxable as ordinary income to recipient; deductible as business expense to payer.
- Gifts: Generally not taxable to recipient (with exceptions for employee gifts); not deductible to giver.
- Special rule: Gifts to employees from employers are treated as taxable compensation.

**Valuation Understatement:**
- Taxpayers attempt to exclude portions of value by arguing received property is worth less than market value.
- Example: Employer buys new car ($40K), depreciates it artificially, transfers to employee → employee claims $30K value.
- Employer may claim business loss deduction on "depreciation," but IRS substance-over-form doctrine applies.

**Conversion Strategies:**
- Ordinary income (37% max rate) vs. Capital gains (23.8% max rate).
- 83(b) Election: For restricted stock, elect to pay tax on current value (ordinary income) rather than vesting value.
- Benefit: Future appreciation taxed as capital gains; risk if stock declines.

**Deferral:**
- Delaying tax payment increases present value of money.
- Achieved through installment sales, retirement accounts, etc.
- Credit risk and liquidity risk make deferral costly to others.

## 4. Arguments Professor Pushed

**Pro-Arbitrage Arguments:**
- Economic efficiency: Allows value-maximizing transactions that might otherwise fail due to regulatory imperfections.
- Client service: Lawyers' duty to find optimal structures within legal bounds.
- Cross-border benefits: Different jurisdictions create legitimate arbitrage opportunities.

**Anti-Arbitrage Arguments:**
- Substance over form: Courts/IRS recharacterize transactions designed primarily for tax avoidance.
- Equity concerns: Wealthy taxpayers benefit disproportionately from complex planning.
- Administrative burden: Increases compliance costs and enforcement challenges.

**Balanced View:**
- Arbitrage is legitimate when based on genuine business purposes, not solely tax avoidance.
- Requires careful structuring to withstand scrutiny.
- Benefits all parties when properly implemented (e.g., sharing tax savings).

## 5. Prewrite Fragments

**Issue: When can a lawyer ethically recommend regulatory arbitrage?**

**Rule:** Lawyers may recommend structures that minimize tax burden as long as they have substantial non-tax business purpose and comply with substance-over-form doctrines.

**Analysis:** 
- Business purpose: Transaction must make economic sense independent of tax benefits.
- Substance over form: Courts look beyond labels to economic reality.
- Professional responsibility: Cannot assist in fraudulent tax evasion.

**Conclusion:** Ethical when arbitrage is incidental to legitimate business goals.

---

**Issue: How should tax planners evaluate arbitrage opportunities?**

**Rule:** Consider tax implications for all parties and ensure net benefits outweigh costs.

**Analysis:**
- Quantify savings for each party.
- Account for compliance risks and administrative costs.
- Consider alternative structures.

**Conclusion:** Implement only when benefits exceed risks for all involved.
