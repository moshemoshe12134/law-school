**Intro**

Property is the body of law that governs relations between people over valued resources.

A property right is an entitlement and good against the world, while contract is only between the people within the contract.

* Bundle of entitlements which ownership confers various rights. But not every property has the whole bundle.

Four major rights of ownership:

1. Right to exclude
2. Right to share
   1. At the same time ‚Äì ex: marital partner‚Äôs
   2. In the future ‚Äì transfer ownership rights in the future ü°™ leaving property to a descendant
3. Right to use
4. Right to compensation from loss

Source of complexity is four-fold:

1. Every right of ownership is regulated by an intricate set of rules
2. These rules derive from multiple sources
   1. Common law: some of which is multiple centuries old
   2. Statutory law: Local ordinances, state and federal
   3. State and federal constitutional law
3. Many of the rules are ambiguous, in flux, and are actively contested
   1. This is why Restatement is not as important as it is in other areas of law
4. None of the rules are neutral in origins and consequences

Justified by multiple policy goals:

1. Fairness - Equal opportunity, distributive fairness
2. Efficiency - What is a social utility?
3. Legitimacy - Consent theory
4. Administrability - If it‚Äôs easy to administer then it should be easy to enforce

4. Adjudicate a contested question in a way that considers the various consequences of the decision

RULES FOR ESTABLISHING POSSESSION

1. **Corporal Possession/Mortal Wounding** (*Pierson v. Post*); (*Popov v. Hayashi*)‚Äîto take a wild animal out of the commons, you must deprive them of their liberty through mortally wounding them or through actual corporal possession.
   1. Not always **fair**
   2. **Administrable**‚Äîif everyone gained possession on first-sight, would lead to more litigation;
   3. **Socially useful** activity‚Äîrewards good hunters
2. **Box Theory** (*Keeble v. Hickeringill-* duck case)‚Äîif an object/animal is located within boundaries of a property, it is in possession of the property owner
   1. **Fair**‚Äîwhat is on your property is yours;
   2. **Administrable**‚Äîclear rule that‚Äôs obvious to everyone;
   3. **Socially useful**‚Äîincentivizes people to box in what they own and disincentivizes ‚Äúluring.‚Äù
3. **Law of Capture**‚Äîthe first person to capture a resource owns it, even if the resource is drained or migrates from another‚Äôs land. Your ownership interest is in the opportunity to capture, an expectation constituted by custom and social relationships.
   1. **Limits to the Law of Capture:**
      1. **Correlative Rights**‚Äîyou cannot infringe on another‚Äôs reasonable opportunity to capture a common resource (*Popov v. Hayashi*), AND property interest is infringed upon the moment someone begins to exploit the common resource (*Elliff v. Texon Drilling Co*.);
   2. **Fair**‚ÄîGiven limits are followed, everyone has an equal opportunity to capture, which rewards labor;
   3. **Administrable**‚Äîit‚Äôs clear who has drilled something up and who‚Äôs responsible, and it‚Äôs too difficult to discern the original origin of individual molecules
   4. **Socially useful**‚Äîit‚Äôs efficient, because it incentivizes people to quickly claim and invest in their property.

***Pierson v. Post* (NY 1805):** **FAIRNESS, EFFICIENCY, SOCIAL UTILITY**

* Facts: Post was hunting with his dogs and chasing a fox in uninhabited Hamptons. Pierson saw that the fox was being hunted, killed it, and carried it off. Post brought a trespass suit against Pierson claiming he had legal possession of the fox based on a **pre-possessory interest**.
* Rule: Mere pursuit does not confer property right. Property in wild animals (ferae naturae, of a wild nature) is acquired by occupancy, meaning by taking the animal out of the commons with manifest intention by depriving them of their liberty and bringing within certain control through:
  + at least mortally wounding or capturing from a distance so as to deny it any chance of escape
  + at most physical possession through corporeal possession / bodily seizure.
* Holding: Pursuer, Post, has NO legal right to pursued fox. Pierson is the rightful owner. 3 Elements:
  1. Manifest intention
  2. Deprive animal of its liberty
  3. Bring within certain control
* Dissent, Livingston: Hot pursuit is enough.
  1. Closer to **Locke**. Property in wild animals acquired when: the pursuer is within reach OR has a reasonable prospect of taking physical possession.
     + **Locke**: If you invest the labor in something, your property. The act of investing effort into property qualifies it as one‚Äôs own and removes it from nature bc you own your own labor (since you own your own body), and so the fruits of your labor are yours. ‚ÄúThe hare that any one is hunting, is thought his who pursues her during the chase.‚Äù
  2. Policy Argument/Socially useful‚ÄîIt is a public benefit to kill a fox, so we do not want to dissuade hunters from putting in the labor of pursuing, if someone could just come and pick it off;
  3. Fairness‚ÄîPierson was a free rider, and he did not share in the ‚Äúhonors or labors of the chase.‚Äù
* Importance: The rule of first possession, wild animals can be possessed through occupancy, court moves towards Lockian understanding of property and away from bright line because of intuitive sense of fairness.

**CORRELARIES to *Post*‚Äî*Keeble v. Hickeringhill***

1. Keeble possessed ducks on decoy pond within his property; Hickeringhill fired a gun to scare the ducks off to kill them, disturbing Keeble‚Äôs exercise and enjoyment of the property;
2. Unlike in *Post*, this was on private property, rather than in the commons. And, private land can acquire things that are in/on it.

**AUTHORITY**

***Popov v. Hayashi* (CA 2002): FAIRNESS, PERSONHOOD**

* Facts: Toward the end of 2001 season, baseball player was expected to break his own home run record, and fans began to congregate in the areas where he was most likely to hit a home run. The ball (estimated worth $1 million) was property of MLB, then became abandoned property. Popov attempted to catch ball, glove made contact with ball but he never actually held it, and unclear if he would have had caught it. Popov was tackled to the ground and did not catch. Hayashi was standing nearby and was also tackled. Ended up seeing the ball and put it in his pocket.
* Holding: Both Popov and Hayashi had a qualified right to the ball, and therefore must sell it and split the profits.
  + Holding is narrow but it is still a good case to see how a modern court incorporates these principles
* Rule: When a person takes a ‚Äúsignificant but incomplete‚Äù step to achieve possession of a piece of property, and the effort is impeded by the **‚Äúunlawful acts of others,‚Äù** he has a pre-possessory interest in the property. This pre-possessory interest constitutes a qualified right to possession that can support a cause of action.
  + If another does come into complete possession of that object, resulting from the unlawful acts of others, he has an equal and qualified right to possession
* Importance: Rule of equitable division, continuation of acquisition by first possession/capture

Notes:

* Public policy argument: In this case, Popov had exerted some control over the ball, but had not yet acquired possession. It is unknown if he would have acquired possession because he was attacked by the fans seated around him. It would be inappropriate to create an incentive for such attacks to take place. Thus, in this situation, Popov is deemed to have acquired a pre-possessory interest in the ball. BUT, because Hayashi was not involved with the mob that attacked Popov, both Popov and Hayashi have an equal, undivided interest in the ball.
* Fairness argument:
  + Baseball fans are accustomed to dominion and control establishing possession. Popov cannot prove he had dominion and control‚Äîhe might have still lost the ball, even if he weren‚Äôt tackled. He established a pre-possessory interest that was impinged by the illegal actions of the mob. But Hayashi did nothing wrong, was also a victim of the mob. He just happened to find the ball and take possession of it.
  + Awarding the ball to either person would be unfair, so we will split it.
* Court uses language of equity, fundamental fairness, even though this fairness concern is a violation from custom.

**Radin**: This case allows us to see theory of personhood, and also to see how courts wrestle with idea of possession, a deliberately ambiguous idea which is molded to particular circumstances.

**JUSTIFICATIONS FOR PROPERY RIGHTS (often competing)**

* + - 1. **Sovereign allocation of rights** (Bentham)‚Äîthe sovereign gives you your bundle of rights, and without that sovereign to enforce those rights, there will not be property;
         1. That sovereign is essentially delegating to owners its rights over that property.
      2. **Efficiency** or social welfare maximization‚Äîpreferences individuals who will maximize overall social welfare by engaging in the most productive use of the land;
      3. **Distributive justice or equity**‚Äîthe desire to prevent gross inequality and ensure distribution to those in need;
      4. **Settled expectations**‚Äîcustoms dictate fair property rights;
      5. **Labor and investment** (Locke)‚Äîit‚Äôs fair, and encourages produce use of the property;
      6. **Possession or occupancy of the property**‚Äîpossession can occasionally, after time, ripen into property rights.
         1. **Rule of first possessor**‚Äîprevents disputes about title and encourages people to quickly find and claim property.

Sometimes these justifications point in different directions: whoever first finds property may not make the most efficient use of it.

* But, often, they can also support each other: awarding rights to the current possessor might encourage investment in the property.

POSITIVISM (i.e. Chief Justice Marshall in *Johnson v. M‚ÄôIntosh*)

1. **Positivists** identify law with the ‚Äúcommands of the sovereign‚Äù‚Äîthey believe that judges should exercise extreme discretion when there are gaps, conflicts, or ambiguities in the law.
   1. Separate law and morality. Emphasize that, although moral judgments may underlie rules of law, they are NOT fully or consistently enforced by legal sanctions, and thus we should NOT interpret law that way either
   2. **Justice Holmes‚Äôs ‚Äúbad man‚Äù test**‚Äîwe should look at the law as bad men would. They would not be interested in the moral content, but rather what sanctions would be put upon them if they broke the law;
   3. **Llewellyn**‚Äîlaw is what officials will do to resolve disputes;
   4. **Bentham**: The ‚Äúidea of property consists in an established expectation‚Ä¶of being able to draw‚Ä¶and advantage from the thing possessed.‚Äù
      1. It is the sovereign that allows you to do so. Without the sovereignty there would be no property rights.
   5. **Cheryl Harris:** discusses the role of being white and property.

**Critiques by Rights theorists**‚Äîwe should interpret gaps or ambiguities in the law in a manner that protects rights, promotes fairness, and ensures justice, rather than just what we think the actual letter of the law means.

***Johnson v. M‚ÄôIntosh* (1823):**

* Facts: Two chains of competing title:
  1. Johnson‚Äôs claim: Piankeshaw ‚Üí Viviat, Murray (1775) ‚Üí Johnson, Graham
  2. M‚ÄôIntosh‚Äôs claim: King ‚Üí Virginia Company (1609) ‚Üí Virginia Colony (aka the King) (1624) ‚Üí State of Virginia (1776) ‚Üí US (1803 and 1809) ‚Üí M‚ÄôIntosh (1818)
* Johnson sued M‚ÄôIntosh for ejectment from his lands, alleging:
  1. The Piankeshaws were owners of the land when they sold it to Viviat, and that the Indians‚Äô title and power to sell should be respected;
  2. 1763 proclamation by the King to not buy land did not apply to the Indian tribes because they were not British subjects;
  3. 1763 proclamation by the King also void concerning the purchasers, because it violated the spirit of government as a system of laws;
  4. 1779 Act of Virginia cannot keep them from claiming these lands, because the ‚Äúlegislature was not competent to take away‚Äù private property
* Holding:
  1. The titles granted to Viviat and Murray by the Illinois and Piankeshaw CANNOT be recognized in the courts. An absolute title held by Indians is incompatible with the Crown‚Äôs and US‚Äô absolute title, subject only to the Indian right of occupancy - a right which Crown/US has absolute title to extinguish.
     1. Title thus cannot be recognized by US courts.
  2. **Scope of power of Indians to give** - The law which regulates relations between conqueror and conquered was incapable of application to a people under such circumstances (fierce savages, threat of massacre by Europeans, frequent and bloody wars).
     1. Indians‚Äô were incapable of transferring the absolute title to others, but they ceded this property to the US after American Revolutionary War, so title to Johnson amounted to nothing.
  3. **Scope of power of individuals to receive -** If an individual subject of Crown/US extinguished Indian title and used for own benefit, could still only acquire through title of gov. Grant of title derives efficacy from gov‚Äôs will.
     1. The treaty ending the American Revolutionary War transferred sovereignty and power of the lands under such transfers from the British to the United States
* Rule:
  1. Land title transfers are ONLY valid when made under the rule of the currently prevailing government.
  2. **Under doctrine of discovery**, the first European to see the land possesses the exclusive right of acquiring title to it over any other European (through purchase or conquest), subject only to the Indian right to occupancy.
  3. **Under doctrine of conquest**, right of occupancy gives a party exclusive title (right to conquest & purchase), but does not give that discoverer full property rights until conquest & purchase. Marshall: Right of occupancy includes right to sell.
* Importance: Discovery of land brings with it the right to obtain title either by purchase or conquest.

**SOURCES OF AUTHORITY**

1. Common law‚Äîthe **law of conquest and discovery**
   1. When John Cabot received a commission by the monarch in 1496 to discover lands‚Äîby sailing along the coast, it counted as discovery;
      1. They also had to settle it‚Äîwhich happened with Jamestown in ~1600;
2. **Custom**‚ÄîEuropean nations who were settling the Americas at the time followed this custom, which eventually ripened into the **‚Äúlaw of nations;‚Äù**

**JUSTIFICATION**

1. **Socially useful** to give the land to the U.S. Also, **Locke**‚Äîthe Indians have NOT put in the **labor** or investment that counts as possessory acts;
   1. It is **efficient**‚Äîto give the Indians the land would be to keep it a ‚Äúwilderness‚Äù and undeveloped‚Äîthis is the best way to maximize welfare
2. **Custom of first possessor** among European nations‚ÄîDiscovery by subjects gives title to their government
   1. **Socially useful:** Rule was supposed to minimized conflict between European Nations
   2. **Fair**: what the Europeans did may have been wrong, but they couldn‚Äôt just incorporate the Indians into their society because they were ‚Äúfierce savages‚Äù who couldn‚Äôt be governed as a ‚Äúdistinct people;‚Äù
      1. Normally it would be fair to incorporate them, but that is not possible here‚Äîso there is an ‚Äúexcuse.‚Äù
   3. There is an **expectation** around this custom that should be respected: **Theft ripening over time into property rights through the growth of expectations.**
3. **Consequences and the role of the courts**‚ÄîMarshall cannot acknowledge that the Indians had title without angering the American public and unraveling the entire system of title/property
   1. In particular, this would create questions about the legitimacy of the Louisiana Purchase and the purchase of Florida
      1. So, by justifying this injustice, he is perhaps preventing another
   2. Not the **role** of the courts to determine this‚Äî‚Äúthe **discovery doctrine** is the law because the government in charge said so, and the government has the power to say who can acquire property rights and how;‚Äù
      1. **Positivism**‚Äîit is not Marshall‚Äôs role to address rightness or wrongness of a rule even if it is contrary to the ‚Äúlaw of the creator.‚Äù‚Äî‚ÄúConquest gives a title to which the courts of conqueror cannot deny‚Ä¶not for the Courts of this country to question the validity of this title;‚Äù

**THEME**: **Property rights created by and in favor of those in power**‚Äîthe discovery doctrine is the law because the government in charge said so.

## Contemporary Debates and Relativity of Title

### FOUND PROPERTY

**CASES**: *Armory v. Delamarie (1722), Charrier v. Bell (1986), Wilcox v. Stroup (2006)*

Property rights are sometimes relative‚Äîthey are good against some in certain circumstances, but not against others.

1. When the strength of your title can vary based upon the parties claiming the property.
2. Ex. Your right to exclude doesn‚Äôt hold in the face of an emergency‚Äîit‚Äôs not trespass to crash your plane onto another‚Äôs land.

Finding lost property or possessing land, even without official title to the property, will create rights against subsequent possessors, but NOT against the title owner.

* Common illustration of relativity of title: Who truly owns the property depends on who the conflict is with (original owner/possessor of the property, subsequent possessor, or owner of the land on which the property is found) and whether the property is considered lost, mislaid, abandoned.

LAW OF FINDERS:

1. Finder vs. Subsequent Possessor (*Armory*)
2. Finder vs. ‚ÄúRightful‚Äù Owners (*Wilcox* & *Charrier*)
3. Finder vs. Owner of the Premises Where Property Was Found: Discussed Below
   1. Place Open to the Public, or on Private with Owner‚Äôs Permission? ‚Äì Courts are divided:
      1. Lost? ‚Äì Finder Often Prevails
      2. Mislaid? ‚Äì Owner of Premises Often Prevails

**TWO GENERAL RULES**

1. **Law of Finders**‚ÄîFinding lost property or possessing land, even without official title to the property, will create rights against subsequent possessors, but NOT against the title owner
   1. **JUSTIFICATION for Law of Finders:**
      1. **Efficient**‚Äîreduces incentive to steal
      2. **Fair**‚Äîlets people who were there ‚Äúfirst‚Äù establish a claim against those whom would kick them off merely because they can
      3. **Administrable**‚Äîthe rule allows decisions to be made even if the true owner is not identifiable.
   2. For **real property** *(Christy v. Scott*)‚Äîmostly, in order to claim better title relative to someone else, you MUST show a history of possession: Examples:
      1. Use and building on the land/improving it through labor (Locke)
      2. Excluding others from it.
   3. **THEME**: theft can, over time, ripen into property rights.
2. Who truly owns the property depends on (1) WHO the conflict of title is with and whether the property is determined to be lost, mislaid, or abandoned:
   1. **Lost**‚Äîtrue owner accidentally misplaced it
   2. **Mislaid**‚Äîtrue owner intentionally left it and then forgot where they put it
   3. **Abandoned**‚Äîtrue owner formed intent to relinquish all rights to the property.
      1. The older the artifact, the more the true owner *intended* to abandon it, because she did not make an attempt to recover it (*Armory*)
      2. There are also considerations of **custom** (*Charrier)*‚Äîyou put things in the garbage that you intend to abandon, you put things by a gravesite that you intend to remain there, so you have not abandoned them.
3. **Finders** v. **true owner:** if **lost or mislaid** then goes to true owner, but finders will generally win against the true owner if the property is abandoned.
4. Finder v. Property Owner (but NOT original object owner:
   * 1. Lost, mislaid, or abandoned object‚Äîthe **landowner will win if the finder was trespassing** at the time they found the object.
     2. If the finder was NOT trespassing then courts are divided:
        1. If it‚Äôs in a **private home**, the homeowner will usually win;
        2. If it was lost, and the landowner was not the original owner, the finder will win;
     3. If it‚Äôs in a **public space** or if on **property with the owner‚Äôs permission**:
        1. **Lost** property typically awarded to the **finder**;
        2. **Mislaid** property typically awarded to the **owner of property**;
           1. *Benjamin v. Lindner Aviation*‚ÄîPerson found $18,000 rolled up on a plane and turned it in; the court awarded it to the bank that owned the plane, finding that it was **mislaid**.
     4. If it‚Äôs **embedded in the soil**, the property is usually awarded to the landowner rather than the finder‚Äîeven if lost, mislaid, or abandoned.
        1. Exception is the **doctrine of the treasure trove**‚Äîgold/silver buried in ‚Äúantiquity‚Äù/more than a century usually goes to the finder, as long as they weren‚Äôt trespassing at the time.
   1. Criticism: depends too much on determinations of the unknown owner‚Äôs **intent** and perverse **incentives** for finders.

***Wilcox v. Stroup* (4th Cir. 2006): Finder vs. ‚ÄúRightful‚Äù Owners**

* Facts: Wilcox found Civil War documents that were in his family for generations. The docs concerned Confederate communication and were appraised at $2.4M. Wilcox allowed South Carolina Dept of Archives and History to microfilm the docs, then he tried to sell to third party. Dept brought temporary restraining order preventing Wilcox from selling. Wilcox brought this suit for a declaratory judgment that he owned the docs.
* Holding: Where neither party can establish ownership of an object, the person in possession is presumed to be the owner. In other words, possession is prima facie evidence of ownership. ‚ÄúAbsent proof to the contrary, settled expectations will continue undistributed.‚Äù
  1. This presumption: avoids confusion, maintains the status quo, and encourages those in possession to make improvements to that which they possess. Such a presumption is used when both parties unable to establish title and when: doing so promotes stability, protects public peace against violence/disorder, and does not frustrate public interest. Prevents court from ‚Äúpresiding over a historical goose chase.‚Äù
  2. Supported by administrability and social utility
* Rule: When there isn‚Äôt enough evidence to establish title to an object (aka object is of unknown provenance), presumption that whoever is in possession of the object is rightful owner, not the finder.
* Importance: Rule of Finders vs. ‚ÄúRightful‚Äù Owners ‚Äì Owners. Presumption in favor of possession.

Notes:

* SC‚Äôs argument: these documents are public records that are part of state archives. Evidence: Based on when family moved out, there is argument to be made that had it not been for the Union‚Äôs invasion, docs would have remained in state archives. Also, evidence of a system of archiving the docs that was in the works.
* The court determined that without the benefit of clear chain of title, evidence of original ownership, eyewitness testimony, or other useful aids in determining ownership, the court presumes ownership in favor of possession.
* In this case, neither Wilcox nor Stroup are able to present sufficient records to establish ownership of the Civil War docs by a preponderance of the evidence. As a result, the court rests on the presumption that because the documents have been in possession of Willcox‚Äôs family for 140 years, he is the rightful owner. Further, because Stroup does not have any evidence sufficient to rebut this presumption, this court concludes that Willcox is the owner of the docs.

***Charrier v. Bell* (La. 1986): Finder vs. Landowner**

* Facts: An amateur archaeologist obtained consent of the caretaker of some land to survey it for possibility of burial locations of the Tunica-Biloxi Indians. The archaeologist excavated over 150 burial sites, realized that the caretaker was not the true owner, but continued to work in the site.
  1. Archaeologist tried to sell the artifacts to Harvard, but he could not because he couldn‚Äôt prove ownership.
  2. Archaeologist sued the landowners for declaratory relief confirming that he was the true owner and requested compensation under the theory of unjust enrichment for his time and expenses
  3. Argument: Archeologist claimed ownership b/c insufficient evidence to show tribe is legal descendant of burial, tribe abandoned artifacts. Even if not owner wanted compensation for unjust enrichment.
* Holding: Tribe is true owners of artifacts and archaeologist NOT entitled to them.
  1. When individuals bury objects with the deceased, their intentions are that those objects remain there forever, not that they become free for the taking. (Based on context ‚Üí NOT abandonment). In such cases, intent to relinquish possession is not equivalent to intent to relinquish ownership.
  2. Justifications:
     1. **Custom** of the area: when you put artifacts on gravesites, you are intending them to remain there perpetually;
     2. **Efficient**: disincentivizes grave-robbing;
     3. **Radin**: Buried property is bound up in personhood, it is NOT alienable
* Rule: In the absence of a perfect chain of title, a federally recognized tribe can establish ownership over excavated burial groups when (1) The tribe claiming ownership is an accumulation of the descendants of the original owners, and (2) The original owners‚Äô intention in interring the objects was not to abandon the good, but rather, for the burial goods to remain there perpetually.
* Importance: Rule of Trespassing Finder vs. Landowner ‚Äì Landowner wins.

Note:

* Consideration of custom ‚Äì you put things in the garbage that you intend to abandon, you put things by a gravesite that you intend to remain there, so you have not abandoned them
* Court also rejected Charrier‚Äôs claim of unjust enrichment. Could not show a resulting impoverishment because the majority of the excavation occurred while he had knowledge that he did not have consent to be on the land, aka was trespassing.
  1. If Charrier was trespasser, then artifacts belong to property owner.
  2. A thief has a superior claim under relativity of title to subsequent thieves, but a thief cannot pass on true title, and only has possessory interest

Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act of 1990 (NAGPRA): corrects gaps in law and morality that left Native American graves unprotected

***Armory v. Delamirie* (KB 1722): Finder vs. Subsequent Possessor**

* Facts: Chimney sweep found a jewel, brings to goldsmith for appraisal but apprentice appropriated jewel through deceit and refused to return. Cause of action is trover (wrongful interference with personal property). Seeking return of jewel, or payment of its value.
* Holding: Chimney sweep, though he does not have absolute property or ownership, has superior claim against all but the rightful owner. ***The Originality Requirement ‚Äì Feist v. Rural Telephone (1991)***

* Facts: Telephone Service published phonebook alphabetically listing the names of its subscribers in its white pages. But Feist used Rural listings anyway, so Rural sued Feist for copyright infringement.
  1. Feist insisted that facts CANNOT be copyrighted. Organization of facts can be though but MUST be an original style of organization.
     1. Emphasizes the progress of science and only establishes copyright if it is truly original.
  2. Rural‚Äôs proposed rule is that putting in work give copyright ‚Äì sweat of the brow.
* Holding: An alphabetical phonebook, although a specific selection and arrangement of facts, is NOT creative enough to be considered an original work and thus does not warrant copyright protection.
  1. Facts are not copyrightable, and although compilations of facts may be if consist of some degree of creativity, they are not copyrightable per se.
  2. Rural selected which facts to include, they are arranged alphabetically, which is NOT a creative arrangement. Rural‚Äôs phonebook is not copyrightable and Feist cannot be held liable for copyright infringement.
* Importance: To be granted copyright protection, works must be original, meaning that they entail some minimal degree of creativity. Facts alone are not copyrightable, but a creative/original compilation of facts may be copyrightable.

***SunTrust Bank v. Houghton Mifflin (11th Cir., 2001):* The Fair Use Defense**

* Facts: Houghton Mifflin published The Wind Done Gone (TWDG) based on the famous book, Gone with the Wind (GWTW). TWDG served as a critique of the depiction of slavery in the south in GWTW by using its characters, plot, and major scenes. SunTrust is copyright holder of GWTW and brought copyright infringement suit against Houghton Mifflin.
* Holding: Not enjoined from publication based on copyright infringement bc Fair Use Doctrine. In determining whether a work is entitled to the Fair Use defense, court looks at following factors. Court looks at all factors, but weighs some more heavily than others.
  1. Purpose and character of the work
  2. Nature of the copyrighted work
  3. Amount and sustainability of the portion used
  4. Effect on the market value of original
* **Copyright Act** ‚Äúpromotes public access to knowledge b/c it provides an economic incentive for authors to publish books and disseminate ideas to the public.‚Äù And ‚Äúwithout the limited monopoly authors would have little economic incentive to create and publish their work. Therefore, by providing this incentive the copyright law promotes the public access to new ideas.‚Äù
  + Very utilitarian type argument

Use of a copyright is permitted to fulfill one of the important purposes listed in Copyright Clause:

1. Promotion of learning

* Provides public access to knowledge bc it provides an economic incentive for authors to publish books, thus increasing public access to new ideas and concepts

1. Protection of the public domain

* Grant of a copyright encourages public to create new works, and the limitation ensures that that the works will eventually enter the public domain, which protects public‚Äôs right of access and use

1. Exclusive rights of the author

* To encourage creation of original works

HOW YOU FRAME ISSUE IS VERY IMPORTANT

* Court concludes that the book ‚Äúexploits‚Ä¶.‚Äù . Then analyzes the Fair Use defense.

PROCESS FOR ANALYZING:

1. Threshold question: Would a reasonable person see the work as being a parody?
   1. Rule: Parody = having an aim ‚Äúto criticize a prior work by appropriating elements of the original in creating a new artistic, as opposed to scholarly or journalistic, work.
2. Does the work fall within the Fair Use Doctrine in light of the four factors listed in the statute?

* **Purpose and character of the work** **(commercial nature OR for nonprofit education purposes)**
  + TWDG is a commercial product, which weighs against fair use, but because TWDG adds a new meaning to GWTW by transforming its copyrighted elements into a critique, this factor as a whole weighs **in favor**.
* **Nature of the copyrighted work**
  + B/c GWTW is an original work of fiction, it is entitled to a high degree of protection and so this factor weights **against**.
* **Amount and sustainability of the portion used**
  + TWDG does take a substantial portion of GWTW; however, a certain portion is necessary in order to ‚Äúconjure up‚Äù an image of GWTW, which is necessary for the criticism. Copyright protection affords greater protection to original works of fiction, but this prong is irrelevant here bc TWDG is parody, drawing on publicly known work. As a result, this factor remains **neutral**.
* **Effect on the market value of original**
  + Unlikely that TWDG would supplant GWTW in the marketplace because they are such different works in different genres, so this factor weighs **in favor.**

Accordingly, based on an analysis of the factors as a whole, TWDG is entitled to a fair use defense / copyright use bc only 1/4 factors weighs against defense, but 2/4 weigh in favor.

### Trespass

**CASES**: *State v. Shack* (public policy), *Commonwealth v. Magadini* (necessity), and *Jaques v. Steenberg* (deciding remedies).

A trespass is an **unprivileged intentional intrusion on property possessed by another**.

1. The intrusion occurs the moment you enter the property
   1. Can be physical entry or from an object
2. ‚ÄúIntentional‚Äù means that the trespass was voluntary.
   1. You can still trespass if you did not intend to violate the possessor‚Äôs property rights.
3. A **‚Äúprivileged‚Äù** intrusion that is NOT wrongful can include:
   1. Entry that is done with the **consent** of the owner.
   2. Entry that is justified to prevent a more serious harm/injury (**necessity**).
   3. Entry that is otherwise encouraged by **public policy**‚Äîto stop a crime, to rescue, etc.
4. The possessor does not have to be the owner;
5. Trespass can include *ad coelom et ad inferos* (‚Äúup to the heavens and down to the depths‚Äù)‚Äîexceptions are now included for acts like fracking, flying planes, etc.

**THEME**: *sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas* (‚Äúone should use his property as not to injure the rights of others‚Äù);

###

**THE RIGHT TO EXCLUDE**

Property owners have an **absolute right to exclude** others from their commercial property for any reason not specifically prohibited by statute, **unless they are a common carrier**

* Justifications for why the **right to exclude is central property** right:
  1. **Socially useful**: it protects owners and empowers them to put property to its most productive use;
  2. **Efficient**: economizes information costs by sending a clear message to people other than the owner
  3. ‚Äúone of the most essential sticks in the bundle of rights that are commonly characterized as property.‚Äù Dolan v. City of Tigard (1994). A right is unreal if it is unenforceable.

1. Justifications for the **common carrier exception**:
   1. **Socially useful**: because common carriers are monopolies, denial of service is like a denial to the right to travel or the right to seek shelter;
   2. **Necessity**: common carriers provide necessities whose denial would place individuals at risk;
   3. **Fair** (reliance/expectations): the public relies on the expectation that common carriers are available to serve public needs

Other property rights include the right to (1) possess; (2) alienate; (3) enjoy the fruits of profits of the property; and (5) the right to destroy.

***State v. Shack (public policy as privileged entry) (N.J. 1971)***

**FACTS**

1. Defendants entered Plaintiff‚Äôs property to try to aid migrant farmworkers employed and housed there;
   1. Defendants (members of an organization funded by the Office of Economic Opportunity) were there to administer medical care and give out literature about the farmworker‚Äôs legal rights.
2. Plaintiff insisted that he be present for the meeting, and the defendants declined, asserting their right to meet the workers in private;
3. Plaintiff demanded that they leave, the defendants refused, and the plaintiff ejected them and sued for trespass according to a local statute;
   1. Defendants allege that, under NJ state law, they cannot be barred from administering government services, which they were there to do.
4. **ISSUE**‚ÄîCan P exclude Defendants who were providing government-protected services?

**HOLDING**‚ÄîNo, the conduct was **‚Äúprivileged‚Äù** and thus beyond the reach of the trespass statute

1. The conduct was encouraged by **public policy**‚Äîthe Economic Opportunity Act of 1964 targets assistance for seasonal migrant workers. It frustrates the purpose of policy to exclude the Defendants.
2. The conduct **necessary** to prevent harm to the farmworkers‚Äîone of them needed medical care.
3. This is not a normal landlord/tenant relationship‚Äîthey have a quasi-property interests, like the right to privacy in their boardinghouses.

**Rule:** Your absolute property rights are qualified by the **necessity of others** residing or occupying your property who also have property interests, and/or by those who are **privileged** to enter for certain protected reasons.

**JUSTIFICATIONS**

1. **Expectations/reliance**‚Äîthe farmworkers have come to rely on the idea that they can receive visitors, and a reasonable person would assume that the plaintiff should know about this expectation/reliance. To the point where it would become unreasonable to disallow them to have visitors.
   1. It is different if the plaintiff bars the defendants on day 1, rather than more than a year out‚Äîthe longer the workers are there, they more they *rely* on the good will of the plaintiff.
2. **Radin**/***Moore v. Regents***‚ÄîYou cannot alienate personal, fundamental rights for money
   1. ‚Äú*Rights are too fundamental* to be denied on the bases of an interest in real property and too fragile to be left to the unequal bargaining strength of parties.‚Äù (**Radin**)
   2. Same worry as *Moore*, about creating a ‚Äúblack market‚Äù that makes a human body a commodity;
   3. Risk of **unequal bargaining power** and **exploitation**: ‚Äú[T]he needs of the occupants may be so imperative and their strength so weak, that the law will deny the occupants the opportunity to contract away what is deemed essential to their health, welfare, and dignity.‚Äù
3. Risk of **slippery slope**‚Äîif one group of farmworkers contracted their rights away, there would be a race to the bottom of owners demanding farmers contract their rights away.
4. Your rights are relative, you cannot do everything you want on your land if others have interests that affect and are affected by your land.

**Three** main ways to think about why the court came out the way it did:

* 1. The court was working with a **common law definition of ‚Äúprivilege‚Äù** to determine that the Defendants were not trespassers under the **public policy exception.**
  2. The court was concerned about the worker‚Äôs **expectations** and **reliance interests** on the plaintiff‚Äôs good will.
  3. \*The court was depriving the workers the ability to alienate/contract away certain rights because they were too tied up in personhood, and it was worried about setting a baseline expectation that the rights will be contracted away.

**IMPLICATIONS**‚ÄîMaybe you have lost the right to exclude because of the workers‚Äô consent and reliance issues, or may you never had it to begin with, because certain types of people are privileged to enter.

***Commonwealth v. Magadini (M.A. 2016):* Necessity as privileged entry**

* Facts**:** Defendant was homeless and entered buildings he was barred from by no-trespass orders. Six different charges against the defendant are challenged in this case. In each circumstance he trespassed the property after being barred from them.
  1. Requested a jury instruction on the defense of **necessity**, asserting that his conduct was justified as the only lawful alternative for a homeless person facing the ‚Äúclear and imminent danger‚Äù of exposure to the elements during periods of extreme outdoor temperatures.
  2. ISSUE‚ÄîDid Magadini meet the requirements for defense of necessity to the jury (just jury instruction, not whether the jury would rule on it)?
* Holding: Trial court committed error in refusing the defendant‚Äôs request for a jury instruction on the necessity defense when, as here, the defendant satisfied the foundational elements entitling him to the defense by showing that there was ‚Äúreasonable doubt‚Äù about the availability of legal alternatives to abate a clear and imminent danger. There was ‚Äúlittle question‚Äù that the weather conditions presented a ‚Äúclear and imminent danger‚Äù for a homeless person.

**Four-part test** to meet the requirements:

* 1. There must be clear and imminent danger that is not speculative.
  2. There must be a reasonable expectation that the action will be effective.
  3. There must be no legal alternative that will abate the danger.
     + Magadini‚Äôs evidence: He stayed in an outdoor gazebo year-round, he was told to leave the only homeless shelter and had been denied previously during a blizzard. Unable to rent an apartment and could only stay at public parks.
     + Court: the alternatives the plaintiff presented‚Äîlike going to a shelter in another town‚Äîwere NOT effective alternatives. The standard is NOT that the defendant must consider *all* possible alternatives when he is in clear and present danger.
  4. The legislature has not acted to preclude the defense.

The four-part test is a balancing of harm: you can claim the necessity defense if the harm to yourself of *not* alleviating the danger is greater than the harm inflicted by alleviating the danger.

* **Justification: Efficient**‚Äîcreating incentives to minimize harm as much as possible by, for example, sleeping in a public area rather than knocking on people‚Äôs homes

***Jacque c. Steenberg Homes (Wi. 1997)***

* Facts: Steenberg Homes sold a mobile home to a neighbor of the Jacques. Because of snow on the nearest town road, the easiest way for Steenberg to deliver the mobile home was over the Jacques‚Äô property. However, the Jacques refused to grant Steenberg permission to do so. Steenberg proceeded to deliver the mobile home across the Jacques‚Äô property anyway. The Jacques notified the sheriff, and Steenberg was issued a $30 citation. Jury awarded the Jacques $1 nominal in damages and $100,000 in punitive damages.
  + Arguments:
    - Steenberg: a jury cannot award punitive damages in this case because there were no actual damages.
    - Jacques: individuals and society want to deter trespass regardless of measurable harm that results.
* Holding: the Wi. SC said that there is a harm in trespass in the intrusion in the property owner‚Äôs right. If you infringe upon the right then you lose the right.
* Reasoning:
  + Enforcing Rights: The actual harm here is not in any physical damage to the land, but in the denial of the owner's legal right to exclude all others. Private landowner‚Äôs right to exclude is **‚Äúone of the most essential sticks in the bundle of rights that are commonly characterized as property.‚Äù *Dolan v. City of Tigard* (1994). A right is unreal if it is unenforceable.**
  + Individual Interests: There‚Äôs a strong individual interest in right to exclude. That individual right requires protection from state. Every trespass causes actual harm, regardless of whether compensatory damages
  + Societal Interests: Promote integrity of and reliance on legal system. Punitive damages are necessary to prevent further wrongdoing. Interest in preserving integrity of legal system, and if not self-help will happen. (Bentham)

Note:

* The general rule in Wisconsin is that punitive damages are unavailable if unsupported by compensatory damages. *Barnard v. Cohen* (1917). The rationale for this is simply that society‚Äôs interest in deterring unlawful but unharmful conduct is not great enough to warrant punitive damages.
* Nevertheless, the question of whether punitive damages are available when supported by a nominal damage award for intentional trespass has not been answered.
* An exception to *Barnard* is warranted in cases of intentional trespass. Repeated trespasses could actually threaten the individual‚Äôs ownership by giving the trespasser rights by prescription or adverse possession.

### ADVERSE POSSESSION: FROM TRESPASS TO TITLE

**CASES**: *Brown v. Gobble*; *Nome 2000 v. Fagerstrom*

Unprivileged entry onto land possessed by another is a trespass, but title may pass from the true owner to that trespasser if the possession of land is: CHOSE

1. **C**ontinuous
2. **H**ostile
3. **O**pen and Notorious
4. For the **S**tatutory period
5. **E**xclusive

**THREE WAYS ADVERSE POSSESSION CLAIMS CAN BE BROUGHT**

1. As a claim to **quiet title** against the record owner, where the claimant seeks a declaratory judgment that the adverse possessor has become the owner of the disputed property
2. As a defense to trespass
3. As a defense to ejectment (*Nome 2000*)

JUSTIFICATIONS FOR ADVERSE POSSESSION

* **Stale claims/Promotes alienability** ‚Äì Uncertainty inhibits transactions or raises their costs, thereby lowering their profitability.
  1. People can sell only what they own, but if unclear what they own, inhibited from entering transactions because of the risk that they will not in fact be buying/selling what they are purporting to
* **Quieting title / Administrability** ‚Äì giving root of title you extinguish potential litigation baggage that could drag down market for real estate. Don't have to re-litigate every time try to transfer right of possession to next person
* **Penalizing the sleeping owner** ‚Äì Prevents valuable resources from being left idle, efficiency concern
* **Efficiency** ‚Äì Lowers the cost of identifying who the owner is
* **Reliance** ‚Äì Protecting the possessor‚Äôs interests and expectations
* **Locke** ‚Äì Your labor.
* **Cooter & Ulen**: Two economic justifications for adverse possession:

1. By quieting title & closing off stale claims, AP ‚Äúlowers the cost of establishing rightful ownership claims by removing the risk that ownership will be disputed on the basis of the distant past.‚Äù
2. Prevents valuable resources from being left idle for long periods of time by specifying procedures for a productive user to take title from an unproductive user.

* **Sterk**: Willingness to pay is a poor metric for measuring degree to which adverse possessor values the property.
* **Penaver & Kaytal**: AP‚Äôs long-term use/improvement of the property, combined with the risk of civil/criminal sanctions, will in many cases constitute strong prima facie evidence that adverse possessor places high value on the property.
* **Radin**: Translates Holmes‚Äô explanation into language of justice/fairness. Fungible v. personal property. AP‚Äôs interest is initially fungible but becomes increasingly personal with time, while titleholder‚Äôs interest moves oppositely, and finally to nothingness. Timing where attachment/detachment demands a switch requires a moral judgment.
* **Merrill**: Moral basis for adverse possession lies in ‚Äúthe reliance interests that the possessor may have developed through longstanding possession of the property.‚Äù Adverse possession is justified by moral principle of protecting reliance on relationships.

**Analysis Against Adverse possession:**

* Not **efficient**: rewards wrongdoing;
* But perhaps **socially useful** (Locke)‚Äîgood for underutilized property to go towards a more productive use;
* And there is an **efficiency** argument in that it would encourage landowners to defend their land.
* **Good Faith** (like the Gobbles): In some jurisdictions, only those who mistakenly occupy property owned by someone else can acquire ownership

Adverse possession is very jurisdictional and have to look to states common law and statutory law to determine parameters of it. Property law is an accumulation of laws over time.

SIX ELEMENTS OF ADVERSE POSSESSION: CHOSE

1. Possession must be **Continuous** through the statutory period;
   1. The possessor must exercise control over a property in ways **customarily pursued by owners of that type of property.**
      1. Extended absences or seasonal uses cannot be used to defeat the claim if that is normal use of the land.
   2. **Tacking** can be used to establish continuous use. This uses successive periods of occupation to establish required statute of limitations.
      1. Passed down through Privity (voluntary relinquishment of possession by the earlier AP in favor of the later one)
      2. ‚ÄúTo tack different adverse possessions to make up the period of bar the persons holding such possessions must be connected by privity of title or claim.‚Äù (*Brown v. Gobble*)
      3. It doesn‚Äôt count if the successor dispossessed the original owner forcibly.
   3. Loses continuous is True owner asserts possession before adverse possession ripens.
   4. If AP for 5 years and then another person tries to AP for a few years and then you return and AP, you only need 5 years
2. Possession must be **adverse or Hostile**
   1. The possessor‚Äôs use must be **Non-permissive**‚Äîthere‚Äôs a presumption that possession of another‚Äôs property is usually non-permissive.
      1. This means that if the owner does nothing it means that it is adverse.
   2. Majority rule uses an **objective test**: the possessor‚Äôs state of mind is irrelevant, you only need a lack of permission and consistent usage that an average owner would do.
      1. **BUT** to acquire AP against a co-owner, you need to make an explicit statement of intent to take possession‚Äîan **ouster**.
      2. **AND** possession that was originally permissive stays that way until the owner makes an explicit statement otherwise.
   3. Minority rule has three possible subjective tests:
      1. **Claim of right**: Adverse possessor must allege a ‚Äúclaim of right‚Äù like an intention to appropriate land‚Äîsome states will defeat an adverse possession claim where the possessor knew where the boundary was but never *intended to claim* property outside her borders as her own.
         1. **Intentional dispossession**: the adverse possessor must be aware she is occupying property owned by someone else and must intend to oust or dispossess.
3. Possession must be **Open and notorious**
   1. Possessory acts must be sufficiently visible and obvious to put a **reasonable owner on notice** that her property is being occupied by a non-owner, like:
      1. Enclosing land in a fence or a wall (*Brown v. Gobble*)
      2. Building a structure or laying down a driveway
      3. Mowing grass, clearing trees, clearing land, etc.
      4. Isolated clearing of trees, but not enclosing, is **not enough**.
4. The **Statute of Limitations must pass**
   1. **Tacking** can be used to establish the passage of the statute of limitations
   2. If the owner of the property has disability (minor etc.), then the statute of limitations will be **tolled**
      1. But if clock has already started on statue of limitations, then the onset of disability will NOT stop it.
   3. Some states lower the statute of limitations when the possessor has **color of title**
   4. State dependent. Usually 10-20 years, but TX has 40.
5. Possession must be **Exclusive**
   1. The use ‚Äúis of a type that would be expected of a true owner of the land in question‚Äù and that ‚Äúthe adverse claimant‚Äôs possession cannot be shared with the true owner.‚Äù (*Nome 2000)*.
   2. Occasioned entry by the true owner is NOT enough to defeat the claim.
   3. Two adverse possessors can become co-owners‚Äîyou just have to exclude the true owner.
6. **Occupancy:** Possession must be **actual possession** ‚Äì physical occupation;
   1. The adverse possessor must physically occupy the land and put it to ‚Äúordinary use to which the land is capable and such as an owner would make of it.‚Äù (*Smith v. Hayden*)
      1. Can be shown by enclosing it in a fence‚Äîbut not a ‚Äúfence of convenience,‚Äù such as one to keep out animals, that does not count.
      2. Can also be shown by building on it, improving on it, **acting as an** **average owner** would‚Äîthat‚Äôs the most key part.
   2. May be established with **color of title** if the deed is defective.
7. Some states also require **color of title** or **payment of property taxes** (*Romero*)

**COLOR OF TITLE**

AP assumes possession in reliance on a document that seems to convey title, but for some reason is legally insufficient to convey title. (Ex: Deed that is void, or deed from fake-owner)

1. **Justifications**/critiques:
   1. It‚Äôs **fair**‚Äîit protects expectations of passage of title from one person to another, and all the bundle of rights that are transferred;
   2. It‚Äôs **inefficient**‚Äîit incentivizes sloppiness.
2. **Constructive possession**‚ÄîAdverse possession of *any* portion of land described in a document providing color of title is sufficient to establish adverse possession over *all* of the land described in the document.
3. A defective deed/intended conveyance does not usually constitute permission and defeat the adverse and hostile element of adverse possession.

**HOW TO DEFEAT ADVERSE POSSESSION CLAIMS**

1. Give **permission**‚Äîdefeats the adverse or hostile element
2. File suit before the end of the statutory period

**OTHER ELEMENTS OF ADVERSE POSSESSION DOCTRINE**

1. It is generally held that adverse possession claims will NOT prevail against **government property**‚Äîalthough some states have abolished government immunity
   1. 43 U.S.C. ¬ß1068‚Äîyou can have adverse possession of federal land if it has been occupied (1) for over 20 years; (2) in good faith on a claim of color of title; and (3) cultivated/constructive/improved upon.
2. Adverse possessors usually get non-possessory interests as well, like mineral interests.
3. There are still questions about what is necessary for an adverse possessor to use rural land as an ‚Äúaverage owner‚Äù would, and whether the acts to be open and notorious must be greater to properly give notice to a reasonable owner.

#### **Brown v. Gobble (tacking and adverse possession) (W. Va. 1996)**

* Facts**:** The Gobbles (defendants) and Browns (plaintiffs) were owners of neighboring properties in West Virginia. The properties were divided by a 2 ft wide tract of land that appeared to be part of Gobble property, b/c was enclosed on the Gobbles‚Äô side of the property line by a fence. Browns decided to build a road along the tract, Gobbles asserted it was theirs, Browns sued to enjoin the Gobbles from using tract. At the time of the suit, the Gobbles had been in possession for only 9.5 years, when the statute of limitations required 10 years.
* Holding‚ÄîThe Gobbles had satisfied all elements of adverse possession
* Rule: Tacking can be used to establish continuous possession of the disputed land‚Äîat the time of the suit, the Gobbles had been in possession for only 9.5 years, when the statute of limitations required 10 years. But the previous owners had owned it for longer and that got over the required 10 years.

**JUSTIFICATION**

1. **Actual possession**;
   1. Gobbles had kept a garden, fenced it in, cared for and developed the land.
2. **Exclusive**;
   1. Original owners of the Browns‚Äô land testified that they never objected to the Gobbles‚Äô or their predecessor‚Äôs claim;
   2. The Gobbles had exclusive control and dominion.
3. **Open** and **Notorious**;
   1. Witnesses agreed that the reputation of the land was that it belonged to the Gobbles‚Äô prior owners;
   2. Enough to put the Browns on **notice**.
4. **Continuous**;
   1. Without the tacking doctrine, the Gobbles‚Äô could only claim to be in possession for 9.5 years, which would fall short of the 10-year statutory period;
   2. BUT with the tacking doctrine, which the court accepted, the Gobbles could trace possession as far back as 1937 through two prior owners.
5. **Adverse** or **hostile**;
   1. The tract had been fenced in since 1937, and prior owners claimed that the tract was theirs.
6. For a prescribed **statutory period** (usually 10 years)‚Äîsee element above.

**GENERAL RULE**: When a property owner opens his or her property to the general public, they have the right to exclude others for any reason not specifically prohibited by federal laws, provided: the property is not an inn or common carrier, which have a duty to serve the public (*Madden v. Queens*, NY 1947).

**COMMON CARRIERS & INNKEEPER EXCEPTION:** Property owners have an **absolute right to exclude** others from their commercial property for any reason not specifically prohibited by statute, **unless they are a common carrier.** Justifications for rule:

* **Socially useful**: since common carriers are monopolies, denial of service is like a denial to the right to travel or seek shelter
* **Necessity**: common carriers provide necessities whose denial would place individuals at risk
* **Fair** (reliance/expectations): the public relies on the expectation that common carriers are available to serve public needs

**USTON OUTLIER**: When a property owner opens his or her property to the general public in the pursuit of their own property interest, they have a duty NOT to act in an arbitrary or discriminatory manner toward persons who come on their premises.

* *Uston* reflects an older view of property rights from the mid-19th century that CHANGED when courts began to ALLOWED commercial entities to exclude African Americans
* Effectively: **you need a good reason to exclude someone**, otherwise you have a duty to serve. Cannot exclude unreasonably.

***Uston v. Resorts* (NJ 1982): THE NARROWING OF THE DUTY TO SERVE**

* Facts: Resorts refused to allow Uston near the blackjack tables at its casino because Uston‚Äôs strategy of card counting increased his odds of winning. Uston claimed no right to exclude him b/c of playing strategy. Resorts argued it had right to exclude arbitrarily.
* Holding: Resorts does NOT have the right to exclude arbitrarily. An individual has a right of reasonable access to property open to the public.
  + The **reasonableness** of a decision to exclude an individual must be determined on a case-by-case basis.
  + Here, Resorts‚Äô casino is open to the public and Uston‚Äôs desire to enter the casino and play blackjack is not unreasonable (not acting unreasonably either; not disrupting operations nor threatening any occupants‚Äô security).
* Importance: This is the minority rule.
  + **Majority rule:** you CAN exclude arbitrarily unless you are an innkeeper or a common carrier (in those cases, minority rule governs).
* Rule: When property owners open their premises to the general public in the pursuit of their own property interests, they have NO right to exclude people arbitrarily.
  + Duty not to act in an arbitrary or discriminatory manner b/c an individual has a right of reasonable access to property open to the public.
  + The only time it is ‚Äúreasonable‚Äù to exclude is in cases of disruptive conduct or when it threatens security.

Notes:

* Unlike *State v. Shack*: no unequal bargaining power, no fundamental right to state services implicated.
* **Outlier**: In most jurisdictions, the majority common law rule is that when a property owner opens his or her property to the general public, they have the right to exclude others for any reason not specifically prohibited by federal laws provided they are not an inn or common carrier, which have a duty to serve the public. (*Madden v. Queens County Jockey Club,* NY 1947)

***Arguello v. Conoco* (5th Cir., 2003):**

* Facts: Father and daughter Arguello stopped at Conoco store to buy gasoline and other goods. Subjected to racially discriminatory treatment by clerk.
* Holding: No valid claim for racial discrimination under Civil Rights Act of 1866 ¬ß1981. No agency relationship.
* Reasoning: Cannot establish that their attempt to contract was thwarted. Father voluntarily left good on the counter. When he later tried to gain access to the store and was locked out, he wasn‚Äôt denied an opportunity to purchase because his intention was to determine the clerk‚Äôs identity, not to buy something. The daughter also does not have a valid claim because she was able to successfully complete the transaction.
* Rule: To state a claim under the **Civil Rights Act of 1866** **¬ß1981 in** retail context, MUST demonstrate loss of an actual (not speculative or prospective) contract interest (an attempt to contract that was thwarted by merchant).
  + Court will NOT recognize claims with respect to conduct occurring after the consummation of the sales contract.
* Other Rule: In order for an employer to be held liable for discriminatory acts of its employees under federal statutes, an **agency relationship must be established**.
* An agency relationship exists between: an employer and an employee who performs acts unacceptable to the employer in the course of employment. Factors:
  + Time, place, purpose of act
  + Acts similar to those authorized to do
  + Extent to which departed from normal methods
  + Whether employer would reasonably expect employee to act in racist manner
* An agency relationship does NOT exist between a company and stores displaying the company‚Äôs brand if the company is NOT involved in the store‚Äôs day-to-day operations.

### The First Amendment Right of Access

**CASES**: *Marsh v. Alabama* (1946), *Lloyd Corporation v. Tanner* (1972), *Prager v. Google* (2018)

STATE ACTION DOCTRINE

* Bill of Rights constrains the actions of the state, NOT of private actors**.** State Action Doctrine is solution to a problem that emerged after the 14th Amendment: What is the reach of equal opportunity & privileges and immunities?
* **OLD RULE**: Focus should be on the states, and prohibiting them from denying freed slaves constitutional rights. Private actors can do what they want.
  1. *Hudgens v. NLRB*: ‚ÄúIt is, of course, a commonplace that the constitutional guarantees of free speech is a guarantee only against abridgment by government, federal or state
* **NEW RULE**: Equal opportunity duties can limit private property if the private property is acting as a state does.
  1. When this does happen‚Äî*Marsh v. Alabama* (company town); *State v. Shack*
  2. When this doesn‚Äôt‚Äî*Lloyd Corp.* (malls).

HOW TO APPLY THE NEW RULE

1. **Figure out which source of law you‚Äôre operating under**
2. Figure out what the requirements are
   1. If you‚Äôre **under federal Constitution**‚Äîthere must be a clear public square where you would not be able to get word out if that public square denied access or speech
   2. If you‚Äôre **under state law**‚Äîcan be more expansive than the Constitution, so a place like a mall might apply (CA and NJ have granted free speech in shopping malls)

**Policy Reasons Why Public Actors Should Have Greater Duty Than Private Actors:**

* Monopoly over force
* Monopoly over facilities/services ü°™ Congress has monopoly over this
* Democracy-enhancing effects: State is legislatively elected body, so presumably confident in representativeness.
* Legitimacy: Protect settled public expectations of 1st Amendment values upheld in public square, which don‚Äôt apply in private settings

***Marsh* Rule:** Formulating Two Possible Approaches To Deciding State Action

1. Public Use Standard ‚Äì ‚ÄúThe more an owner, for his advantage, opens up his property for use by the public in general, the more do his rights become circumscribed by the statutory and constitutional rights of those who use it.‚Äù
2. Public Function Test ‚Äì ‚ÄúA private entity that owns all the property and controls all the municipal functions of an entire town is a state actor that must run the town in compliance with the Constitution.‚Äù (*Prager v. Google*‚Äôs description of Marsh‚Äôs holding).
   1. A facility that is built and operated primarily to benefit the public and whose operation is essentially a public function is a state actor for purposes of 1st/14th Amendment analysis.

***Marsh v. Alabama* (1946): PRIVATELY OWNED COMPANY TOWN - LOOSENED DOCTRINE**

* Facts: Town of Chickasaw, Alabama was owned by a private corporation, Gulf Shipbuilding Company. With the exception of its private ownership, the town operated as any other and was bordered by and indistinguishable from surrounding municipalities. Marsh was a Jehovah‚Äôs Witness who stood on sidewalk distributing religious literature. Was told she could not distribute without a permit and that no permit would be issued to her. Protested, arrested, charged with violating Alabama state law. Argued that her activities were protected under 1st and 14th Amendments. District and circuit courts rejected because she was on private property (old rule of the state action doctrine).
* Holding: A privately-owned town cannot deny traditional 1st and 14th Amendment protections of speech and religion to individuals because it is acting like a state. Alabama state statute that enforces the denial of these protections is in violation. Alabama cannot suspend people‚Äôs 1st and 14th Amendment rights while they are in the town because the Company has opened up the town to the public, and many residents from other surrounding areas frequently use the town for commercial purposes.
* Importance: Right to free speech/access in company towns. The more publicly you open your land, the more subject to regulation. In balancing the interests of the Company as property owners, with the interests of the people in freedom of press and religion, the latter are more important.
* Rule: The 1st and 14th Amendment protections of speech and religion still apply to individuals when operating in a privately-owned town if the town is open to the public and used for public purposes. Regardless of whether a corporation or municipality owns the town, the public it allows in has an identical interest in the town‚Äôs functionality and the freedom of communication within its borders.

After *Marsh v. Alabama*, TWO BIG THINGS HAPPENED:

1. The sit-in movement‚Äîbusinesses started excluding patrons based on race, so civil rights protestors would take a seat at a diner counter and refuse to leave.
   1. Argument: even though it looked like only a private actor was excluding you, it was the state‚Äôs arbitrary exclusion laws that were allowing them to do it.
2. Civil Rights Act passed.

*Lloyd Corp. v. Tanner* (1972): **MALL - TIGHTENED STATE ACTION DOCTRINE**

* Facts: Lloyd Corp. owned a large shopping mall. Area completely enclosed with landscaped ‚Äúmalls‚Äù running throughout. Tanner was a member of a group that quietly/orderly passed out handbills on one of the malls. Handbills invited members of general public to a meeting of the ‚ÄúResistance Community‚Äù to protest the draft and the Vietnam War. A customer complained, and a guard told plaintiffs they would be arrested for trespassing if did not leave. Tanner filed suit arguing that shopping mall‚Äôs policies violated their First Amendment rights to distribute anti-war literature.
* Holding: Malls are not company towns for purposes of free speech/access rights. Would stretch *Marsh* logic too far, since mall does not assume full spectrum of state/municipal powers/functions. *Marsh* involved the assumption that a private enterprise does have attributes of a state-created municipality and exercises semi-official municipal functions as a delegate of the state.
* Dissent, Marshall: The mall is clearly equivalent to a public business district. Civic entities have access: there are doctors, dentists, lawyers, bankers, travel agents. Constitutional concern: moreover, for people who don‚Äôt have an easily accessible platform, hand-billing may be their only way to get the word out. Slippery slope worry ‚Äì unless we adhere to *Marsh*, ‚Äúonly the wealthy may find effective communication possible‚Äù
* Importance: Malls are not company towns for purposes of free speech/access rights. Property does not lose its private character merely bc the public is invited to use it for designated purposes; size is not dispositive characteristic. Narrows *Marsh* to only apply to company town (unique/anachronistic historical phenomenon).
* Rule: A private business may constitutionally exclude the distribution of handbills on its property when those handbills are completely unrelated to the business‚Äô functions, and there are alternative means for distributors to relay the message.

Note: *Marsh* Public Function Test narrowly defined here under *Lloyd*, but can be read more broadly

* \*In *State v. Shack* the NJ SC decided to rule based on NJ common law as opposed to 1st Amendment b/c the NJ ruling/interpretation of the 1st A would be subject to the US SC interpretation of the A.

***Prager v. Google* (N. Dist. Cal., 2018): INTERNET**

* Facts: Prager University sued Google/YouTube for censorship of its some of its politically conservative YouTube videos. YouTube placed some of the videos into ‚Äúrestricted mode,‚Äù which blocks the videos‚Äô availability to YouTube users who voluntarily operate in that mode. Prager claimed the censorship was anti-conservative discrimination + that YouTube is like a company town in *Marsh*: performs traditional government functions.
* Holding: *Marsh* cannot be extended to support Prager‚Äôs contention that YouTube should be treated as a state actor subject to 1st Amendment scrutiny, just bc it extends and operates its private property as a forum for expression of diverse viewpoints. Prager did not show that YouTube/Google engaged in any one of the ‚Äúvery few‚Äù public functions that have been traditionally ‚Äúexclusively reserved to the State.‚Äù
* Importance: *Lloyd*, post-*Marsh* demonstrates that *Marsh* reach is narrow.
* Rule: Not a state actor obligated to uphold 1st Amendment protections under Marsh state action doctrine if not engaged in one of the ‚Äúvery few‚Äù public functions that have been traditionally ‚Äúexclusively reserved to the State.‚Äù

***Dale v. Boy Scouts of America*** (U.S. 2000) (p. 53)

* Reversed a NJ court‚Äôs decision requiring the boy scouts to allow gay members.
* Rule: The First Amendment right to expressive association may in some cases be an exception to public accommodations laws.

The First A: The general rule ü°™ Constitutional guarantee is only a guarantee against government abridgement, federal or state. Things get complicated when we try and step past that.

**Why we create 1st A rights of access for government owned property:**

1. *Monopoly rationale*. Government-owned property may be the only public forum that exists, i.e. there is only one U.S. Congress; only one City Council; only one State House.
2. *Democracy rationale*. Government-owned property is often a formal venue for making and enforcing laws, a process in which all citizens have a right to participate.
3. *Necessity rationale*. Government-owned property is often the site of lawmaking that, to be effective and legitimate, requires the free-flow exchange of information.
4. *Coercion rationale*. Government-owned property is often more immediately protected by the state, such that without a right of access, government could exercise its power to exclude through coercion.
5. *Reliance/Expectations rationale*. People expect access to government-owned property; in addition, government-property is effectively ‚Äúowned‚Äù by the people who in turn, have a settled expectation that they can access the property.

In reaching this holding, the Court offered at least two different formulations of the test, prompting considerable debate among subsequent Justices and litigants as to what rule of law *Marsh* stands for.

1. **‚ÄúPublic Forum Test.‚Äù** On the one hand, *Marsh* could be read as standing for a broad right of access, i.e. ‚Äúthe more an owner, for his advantage, opens up his property for use by the public in general, the more do his rights became circumscribed by the statutory and constitutional rights of those who use it.‚Äù
2. **‚ÄúPublic Function Test.‚Äù** On the other hand, *Marsh* could‚Äìand has‚Äîbeen read as creating a narrow right of access, i.e. as establishing a bright-line rule that ‚Äúa private entity that owns all the property and controls all the municipal functions of an entire town is a state actor that must run the town in compliance with the Constitution.‚Äù

The ‚Äúpublic function‚Äù test has won out of these two views.

Examples of the ‚Äúvery few‚Äù functions that have been deemed to be **‚Äútraditionally the exclusive prerogative of the state‚Äù** include:

1. Holding public elections;
2. Governing a town;
3. Serving as an international peacekeeping force;
4. Owning and controlling all the municipal functions of a town, i.e.
   1. Residential buildings,
   2. Streets,
   3. System of sewers,
   4. Sewage disposal plant;
   5. Business block. (*Marsh v. Alabama*).

**Delegation rationale:** The state has delegated its governance role to a private entity, thereby triggering all of the same reasons why we confer a First Amendment right of access to government-owned property (i.e. concerns about monopoly, democracy, necessity, coercion, reliance/expectations)

**The Public Forum Test (DO NOT FOLLOW): Justifications**

1. Precedents:
   1. Broad language in *Marsh*, i.e. ‚Äúthe more an owner, for his advantage, opens up his property for use by the public in general, the more do his rights become circumscribed by the statutory and constitutional rights of those who use it‚Äù;
   2. Justice Marshall‚Äôs dissent in *Lloyd Corp*., citing the same broad language in *Marsh* as the holding of the case.
2. Policy Rationales:
   1. The judiciary has a duty to update the common law (see *State v. Shack*); this role should extend to updating its prior precedents interpreting the Constitution.
   2. Although the Supreme Court has read *Marsh* narrowly (see *Lloyd Corp*. and the overturning of *Logan Valley),* the dramatic rise of social media platforms in the decades since *Logan Valley* calls for revisiting these interpretations;
   3. The same factors that counsel in favor of a First Amendment right of access to government town halls call for First Amendment right of access to social media platforms.

### The Public Trust Doctrine

**CASES**: *Matthews v. Bay Head* (NJ 1984); *Juliana v. U.S.* (D. Or. 2016)

PUBLIC TRUST DOCTRINE: The ownership dominion and sovereignty over land flowed by tidal waters, which extend to the mean high watermark, is vested in the State in trust for the people. The public‚Äôs right to use the tidal lands and water encompasses navigation, fishing and recreational uses (boating, bathing, swimming, and other shore activities added later).

* Most jurisdictions include water that are not tidal waters as well (lakes and such)
* "[T]he power of governing is a trust committed by the people to the government, no part of which can be granted away." The public trust doctrine rests on the fundamental principle that "[e]very succeeding legislature possesses the same jurisdiction and power with respect to [the public interest] as its predecessors." (*Stone v. Mississippi*)

Three types of restriction on governmental authority:

* + - 1. Must be available for use by the general public
      2. Property may not be sold
      3. Property must be maintained for particular use

Three common law doctrines besides public trust to grant access rights to beaches by the general public:

* + - 1. **Dedication**: gift of real property from private owner to public at large. Requires an offer by owner and acceptance by public. Must be made by some unequivocal act by the property owner giving clear evidence of an intent to dedicate the land to public use*.*
         1. But a longstanding acquiescence in use of beachfront property may constitute *implied* dedication.
      2. **Prescription**: if public has used property possessed by another for a particular purpose for a long time, the public can acquire such rights permanently through prescriptive easement. (*Zuni Tribe v. Platt).*
         1. 3 reasons why this concept was not used in past ‚Äì p. 80
      3. **Custom**: longstanding, uninterrupted, peaceable, reasonable, uniform use of beachfront by public for recreational uses confers continuing rights of access.
         1. Different from prescriptive easement b/c of scale; looking at general habit as opposed to individuals or particular groups crossing.
         2. Oregon SC limited to public use of beaches for which actual proof of past public use exists
         3. Hawaii has a system where public use is allowed from water until stable vegetation line

Application of the public trust doctrine to natural resources predates USA:

* Justinian‚Äôs Institutes (Constantinople, 533) ‚Äì ‚ÄúWild animals, birds, and fish‚Ä¶as soon as they are caught by anyone become at once the property of their captor by the law of nations; for natural reason admits the title of the first occupant to that which previously had no owner.‚Äù *Arnold v. Mundy,* NJ: Recognizes this doctrine carrying over.
  + Oysters can be taken by anyone.

HISTORY OF PUBLIC TRUST DOCTRINE

* 19th century: Public trust lands were important to commerce, and the state wanted to keep the use open out of a concern about monopolies/fairness/equal access.
* ‚ÄúPublic trust asset‚Äù within the public domain: Lands covered by tidal waters, wet-sand of the beach
* Theories of access working behind the scenes of public trust doctrine:
  + Navigable waters serve **socially useful** purpose of promoting commerce
  + **Custom** and **common law**: we‚Äôve been doing it this way for a while, developed **expectations** that this will continue. (Expectations of access. Connected to doctrines of adverse possession and prescription/prescriptive easement)
  + Doctrine of **public accommodation**: you have been invited in, and thus your rights in some ways will constrain the rights of the property owner
    - Like *Marsh v. Alabama*; *Logan Valley*; *State v. Shack*

**The Public Trust Doctrine: Rationales?**

1. *Commercial necessity.* Waterways used to be the primary means of transport, therefore, assuring equal access was vital to ensure a functioning economy.
2. *Monopoly rationales*. Waterways and water over tidal lands may be a unique resource, i.e. there may be no alternative site for transporting goods, fishing, or beach recreation.
3. *Equality rationales.* In light of their importance and rarity, ensuring equal access may be important for ensuring equality of citizens in a democratic polity.
4. *Settled Expectations/Reliance Interests*. Over time, the public may develop a reliance interest on a right of access to waterways; the law should protect settled expectations and reliance interests.
5. *Environmental rationales*. There are some resources that are so valuable they should not be subject to private monopoly and ownership, but instead, preserved in trust.

**Over time, the public trust doctrine was expanded in geographic scope:**

* Pre-*Avon,* *Illinois Central v. Illinois*, 1892: Land covered by tidal/navigable fresh waters (up to the mean high tide line) belonged to the people for fishing and navigation
* *Avon*: Public trust applied to municipally owned, dry sand beach immediately landward of high-water mark, and extended public‚Äôs right to use the tidal lands to recreational uses. Three changes in finding for public rights:
  + Includes the dry sand *AND* foreshore
  + Limitedly applies to **municipally owned** beaches
  + Extended public‚Äôs right to use for the purposes of recreation, in addition to fishing and navigation.
* *Matthews*: Dry sand of beaches owned by a non-profit charitable association. What about quasi-public organizations that are not municipalities?
  1. In cases where the public is denied reasonable access to the dry sand area and foreshore, quasi-public organizations must take on the responsibility to provide it.
  2. Ambient air, wild flora, fauna (*Robinson v. Penn. Pa.* 2013, footnote 10 in Juliana, 2016)
* States apply the doctrine differently: Some allow public access rights to be extinguished upon conveyance to private entity, others say the rights are inalienable.

**Different approaches of courts in deciding whether to extend the trust:**

* Does the resource share the same general features/functions as an existing public trust asset? (*Illinois Central,* 1892)
* Is the resource necessary for the enjoyment of an existing public trust asset and, when combined, creates a unique, irreplaceable resource that is in limited supply and yet currently operated by a non-profit charitable association that acts as a public entity but excludes the public? (*Matthews,* NJ 1972)
* Is resource something that has been enjoyed for ages without dispute and furthers general welfare? (*White v. Hughes*, 1939)

**To decide if a court can enforce public trust duties:**

1. Is the property within the public domain and thus subject to public trust? Different approaches by courts:
2. Has the entity violated one of the fiduciary duties of the trust?
3. Is the title owner an entity for which a court can enforce trust duties (to grant public right of access)?
   1. State government (*Illinois Central*)
   2. Municipal government (*Avon*)
   3. A quasi-public entity (*Matthews*)

   4. Examples: timeshares, fractional shares in vacation property

   6. Tenancy in common allows you to transfer your right to another owner
   7. Issue with tenacity in common is that it allows a property to have many fractional owners which would allow ANY owner who wanted to sell it to force partition proceedings (**law of partition**).

***Olivas v. Olivas* (N.M. 1989): DUTY TO PAY RENT**

* Facts: Sam (P) and Carolina (D) Olivas divorced. Had held their property as TIC, but about two months before the divorce, Sam chose to move out of their house and Carolina remained in possession. Sam brought an action for rent, claiming that he was **constructively ousted** from the house.
  + He must show that he was constructively ousted b/c ordinarily a co-tenant has no obligation to pay rent to another tenant ü°™ this is b/c you have right to occupy so should not get paid for rent unless you were ousted.
* Holding: There is not only evidence that Sam chose to move out and that his move was one of the reasons Carolina filed for divorce, but also that upon moving out, Sam moved in with a girlfriend. Therefore, Sam has not carried his burden in proving that there has been a constructive ouster. Rather, he chose on his own to move out and possibly even created the impetus for divorce. Sam is not entitled to rent.
* Rule: Although a divorced spouse forced to move out of the marital residence MAY be entitled to rent from the remaining spouse under the doctrine of constructive ouster, this will not be the case where the departing spouse chose on his own to move out and thus contributed to the divorce. On the contrary, the remaining spouse will be liable for rent **only if** the realities of the situation, without there being any fault by either co-tenant, prevent the co-tenants from sharing occupancy
  + Ex: the parties cannot be expected to live together reasonably on the property, and they come to a mutual agreement that one of them must move out.

***Ark Land v. Harper* (W. Va. 2004): RIGHT TO JUDICIAL PARTITION**

* Facts: Caudill family-owned property exclusively for 100 years and sold a portion to Ark Land Co ü°™ now TIC. Since Ark wanted to extract the coal from entire property, they wanted to purchase the remainder of the property, but Caudill‚Äôs declined. Ark filed suit seeking to partition and sell the property.
  + **Caudill‚Äôs** sought a partition in kind and presented expert testimony that the property was capable of being partitioned in kind.
  + **Ark** presented expert‚Äôs testimony suggesting that the partition in kind would cause an increase in several million dollars in mining costs.
  + Partition in kind: physically split up the property
  + Partition in sale: sell property and everyone gets cut of the sale
* Holding: Partition in kind ordered. While this court recognizes the resulting economic burden on Ark Land, it does NOT justify the sale of the Caudills‚Äô ancestral family home. Caudill family owned the property for over 100 years; uninterested in land‚Äôs monetary value; sought only to maintain family ownership of property.
  + Non-economic interest would be prejudiced if land is partitioned by sale.
  + Economic hardship is NOT the sole factor to consider. Extremely relevant but not decisive factor.
    - Sentimental value should play a role ü°™ *Radin* (cell line case)
    - Court justifies their decision b/c **Ark should have known** that their was a possible economic loss when they invested in the property.
  + Concurrence: Right test, but improperly applied. Partition by sale b/c Caudill‚Äôs interest in the property does not outweigh the great economic burden Ark will have to endure.
* Rule: Previously, this court has ordered partition by sale only where: (1) partition in kind is not convenient; (2) the sale will result in the promotion of at least one of the parties‚Äô interests; and (3) the sale will not prejudice the interests of the other parties. The economic value of property is not the decisive factor in determining whether to partition in kind or by sale. Other relevant factors include longstanding family ownership & sentimental/emotional interests.

*O‚ÄôBrien v. O‚ÄôBrien* (NY 1985): **DEFINING ‚ÄúMARITAL PROPERTY‚Äù**

* Facts: Mr. (P) and Mrs. (D) O‚ÄôBrien got married and P enrolled in medical school. * Holding: A license acquired during marriage is marital property subject to equitable distribution under NY statute Domestic Relations Law 236(b)(5) bc it is a thing of value (interest in a professional career potential) arising out of marital relationship (direct or indirect contributions of non-title holding spouse). Here, most of the marriage was devoted to P getting his license and D played a major role in that effort. Reasoning:

* **Textual**: NY statute Domestic Relations Law 236(b)(5) directly says that contributions of one spouse to other‚Äôs career represents investments in economic partnership.
  + Not clear though if equitable distribution means that the courts should be allowed to invent new forms of property
* **History/purpose** behind statute: Statute was an attempt to replace existing system with equitable distribution of marital property, based on the premise that marriage is an economic partnership to which both parties contribute.
  + In past, have said that pension benefits are shared property (shared)
* **Locke**: Your labor is your property. Working spouses make great sacrifices and forgo the acquisition of marital assets that could have been accumulated if the professional spouse had been employed, rather than occupied with the study and training necessary to acquire a professional license.

Concurrence: A professional in training who is not finally committed to a career choice when the distributive award is made may be locked into a particular kind of practice simply because the monetary obligations imposed by the distributive award made by the court. DOCTRINES OF ESTATE SYSTEMS

1. **Presumption Against Forfeiture** (*Edwards v. Bradley*) - reluctance to recognize future interests
2. **Doctrine Against Waste** (*McIntyre*) - seeks to mediate conflicts between the interests of life tenants and interests of future interest holders by preventing present possessory owners from unreasonably damaging the estate
3. **Rule Against Creation of New Estates** (*Johnson*) - Prohibits owners from creating ownership packages that do not fit within one of the established estates. People cannot create new kinds of estates. Courts will re-write wills, ignore the grantor‚Äôs intent in order to preserve the status quo.
4. **Rule Against Racial Restrictions** (*Shelley, Evans v. Abney*) - Covenants restraining the ownership, use, or enjoyment of land by racial and ethnic minorities are prohibited. *Evans* suggests that this should be applied only in circumstances where there is clear disparate treatment
5. **Rule Against Restraints on Alienation (*MORE BELOW*)** (*Serio, Woodside*) - Restraints on Marriage: If a condition is designed to support a spouse until he or she remarries, then it is valid. If the condition is not so designed, it is invalid under the rule.

* Absolute restraint? Void. An absolute restraint on alienation is ‚Äúrepugnant‚Äù to the nature of a ‚Äúfee simple‚Äù interest in real property. (*Serio*)
* Partial restraint? Reasonableness test. Reasonableness is determined by ‚Äúweighing the utility of the restraint against the injurious consequences of enforcing the restraint.‚Äù (*Woodside*)

1. **Rule Against Perpetuities** - ‚ÄúNo interest is good unless it must vest, if at all, no later than 21 years after the death of some life in being at the creation of the interest.‚Äù

TERMS

**Future Interest:** The right to take possession in the future under specified circumstances.

1. Future interests exist at the moment they are created, though the holder of a future interest as no right to **possess** the property until the triggering event occurs;
2. Transfer of possession may be certain or contingent on the occurrence of certain events.

**Devisees:** those entitled to real property under a will

**Heirs:** those entitled by law to inherit the property if the owner dies **intestate** (without a will)

1. If no relatives qualify as heirs, the property will **escheat** to the state.

**Trust:** a legal arrangement in which one person or entity holds title to property for the benefit of another.

CONTEMPORARY ESTATES SYSTEM

**Fee simple estate**: present estate, could potentially last forever‚Äîthere are 4 fee simple estates:

1. *Fee simple absolute*: a fee simple without an associated future interest.

* Language: (1) O to A, (2) O to A and her heirs (3) O to A in fee simple;
* Form of **absolute ownership** ‚Äì full bundle of rights of property ownership. If A dies intestate (with no will), property goes to heirs (or gov‚Äôt if no heirs).
* **One cannot create a condition totally prohibiting/restricting the alienability of a fee simple estate, or requiring a forfeiture upon alienation *(Edwards)*.**

1. *Fee simple determinable*: A type of defeasible fee where the future interest **reverts** **automatically** to the grantor on the happening of a stated event.

* Describes the interest with *words of duration*: O to A ‚Äúas long as used for‚Äù/ ‚Äúduring‚Äù used for specific purpose
* The future interest is called a ‚Äú**possibility of reverter**.‚Äù
* If condition violated, ownership *automatically* shifts to O/heirs/devisees. The language need not explicitly state the possibility of reverter.

1. *Fee simple subject to condition subsequent*: A type of defeasible fee in which the grantor may choose to retain for herself or her heirs the right to decide, at the time a specified condition is violated, whether to retake the property.

* Language (uses words of *condition):* ‚Äúon condition that‚Äù; ‚Äúprovided that‚Äù; ‚Äúbut if used for [another] purpose‚Ä¶shall have right to terminate‚Äù.
* The future interest is called **right of entry**
* Interest does not automatically shift back to grantor; grantor must assert the right
* If grantor fails to assert right of entry after condition is satisfied, **doctrine of** **laches** may prevent the holder of the right of entry from waiting too long to assert her right of entry; laches prevents recovery when an unreasonable delay in asserting legal rights unfairly prejudices another.

1. *Fee simple subject to executory interest*: When the future interest in a defeasible fee belongs to **someone other than the grantor**, the present interest is called a fee simple subject to executory limitation.

* Language: O to A ‚Äúso long as used for residential purposes, then to B‚Äù
* Future interest is called an **executory interest**
* O to A so long as used for residential purposes, then to B (but this violates the rule against perpetuities because B‚Äôs executory interest could vest too far into future).

**Life estate**: A conveyance designating a present ownership to a grantee for the duration of his lifetime.

* ‚ÄúTo Sarah for life, and then to her heirs‚Äù
* Future interest: If FI goes to grantor - *reversion*. If FI goes to 3rd party ‚Äì *remainder*.

**Term of years**: Any estate limited by specific period of time (not lifetime/event). A lease lasting for more than 1 year is term of years.

* *[Fee tail* ‚Äì ‚Äúto A and the heirs of his body‚Äù ‚Äì i.e. son to son to son - most states no longer recognize (was used to keep the estate in the family): either fee simple or life estate with remainder in A‚Äôs heirs]

**HIERARCHY OF PREFERENCES IN ESTATES** (if there is language ambiguity re: type of estate. Hierarchy tracks the presumption against forfeitures.)

1. **Fee Simple Absolute**
2. **Covenant** (Must have a benefitted/burdened estate, unless in gross to government, charity, HOA)
3. **Fee Simple Subject to a Condition Subsequent** (If it is ambiguous as to whether a conveyance creates a fee simple determinable or fee simple subject to a condition subsequent, the presumption against forfeitures leads courts to prefer the latter. 4. **Fee Simple Determinable**

**INTERPRETIVE RULES**

1. **\*\*One cannot grant more than one has**‚Äîif you have a Life Estate with reversion to grantor, you cannot grant a fee simple absolute
2. **Honor Grantor‚Äôs Intent**‚ÄîReadthe plain language of the deed, considered as a whole, to determine the intent of the parties. The language must ‚Äúclearly state the grantor‚Äôs intent to create a discretionary power to terminate‚Äù *(Wood)*
   1. Assume that if draftsmen wanted to create a certain present estate with a future interest, would have used the required terminology (*Edwards*)
   2. Precatory language (below)
3. **Presumption against forfeitures**‚ÄîIf it is possible to interpret the language in a conveyance to avoid loss of the property by the current owner, courts will generally adopt this interpretation
   1. ‚ÄúIt is a well-settled rule that conditions tending to destroy estates, such as conditions subsequent, are not favored in law. They are strictly construed.‚Äù (*Wood)*

**TREATMENT OF PRECATORY LANGUAGE (**not intended to have any legal significance**)**

1. When conveyances include language explaining the purpose of the transfer, the vast majority of courts will hold the language to be precatory ‚Äì not intended to have any legal significance ‚Äì and will interpret the conveyance to have transferred all the interests the grantor owned.
2. Follows from the presumption against forfeitures.

**JUSTIFICATION OF INTERPRETIVE RULES**

1. **Efficiency:** Property should be able to shift to its most valuable, productive, or desired use over time;

* Enforcing the interests of a grantor over a current owner facilitates **dead hand control** of an estate, which operates to the detriment of alienability and economic efficiency
* It also risks **concentrating ownership** in the hands of certain groups to the exclusion of others, perpetuating distributive injustice. The above interpretive rules grant current owners greater freedom to alter land uses as economic and social conditions change
* **Counterargument:** one could argue that future interests may promote alienability and efficiency in some circumstances, as they make grantors more willing to part with their property if they can control who owns it or how it is sued in the future.

1. **Administrability:** Strict formal requirements for the creation of future interests are easier to administer by courts;
2. **Fairness:** Granting ability to create future interests a reward for labor. But is it fair to restrict the right of the current owner, who‚Äôs more recent labor has engendered an expectation interest in being able to use the property for whatever purposes the current owner sees as most beneficial?

* While most courts are reluctant to find a future interest in the absence of clear language creating one, *some courts are eager to find a future interest when the property is donated for charitable purposes*.

STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS ON ADVERSE POSSESSION CLAIMS OVER FUTURE INTERESTS

1. The **statute of limitations on a fee simple subject to a condition subsequent** only starts to run from the moment the grantor exercises his or her right to re-entry;
2. The **statute of limitations starts to run on a fee simple determinable** from the moment the condition is violated.

CONTINGENT AND VESTED REMAINDERS

1. **Contingent:** Remainders are contingent if (1) the remainder will take effect only upon the happening of an event that is not certain to happen, or (2) if the remainder will go to a person who cannot be ascertained at the time of the initial conveyance.
   1. Example of a **class gift**: ‚ÄúO to A for life, then to the children of B.‚Äù Contingent remainder in the children of B if B has no children upon the conveyance from O to A, because the children cannot be individually identified.
   2. May be destroyed if contingency does not occur
2. **Vested:** Remainders to persons who are identifiable at the time of the initial conveyance and for whom no conditions must occur before the future interest becomes possessory, other than the death of the life estate tenant.
   1. **Absolutely Vested:** O to A for life, then to B
   2. **Vested Remainders Subject to Open:** Remainder that is vested in some individuals but may be divided with others who join the class in the future ‚Äì e.g. in a class gift when some children are born but others might be born in the future;
   3. **Vested Remainders Subject to Divestment:** A vested remainder that may be lost due to an event that occurs after the original conveyance ‚Äìe.g. O to A for life, then to B, but if B marries a lawyer, the property shall then revert to O. ### Restrictions on Estates & Charitable Trusts

**CY PRES**‚ÄîThedoctrine of cy pres, or ***equitable reformation***, permits a court to modify a grant to validate it on the presumption that this is **consistent with the intent of the grantor.**

1. **Burden** on the party seeking reformation to prove that the donor had a **general charitable intent.**
2. Modern trend is to **presume** general charitable intent.
3. Many courts (contrary to *Evans*) reform trusts that illegally restricted benefits on the basis of race or sex.

**\*\*RESTRAINTS ON ALIENATION\*\***

1. There is a strong ***presumption that property owners should be able to transfer their interests***. Promoting alienability may allow property to shift easily to a more valued use, enhance freedom and flexibility for current owners, encourage dispersal of property to new owners, and prevent discriminatory exclusion
2. **Rules constraining restrictions/future interests to promote alienability**: (1) rule against creation of new estates (2) rule against unreasonable restraints on marriage (3) rule against unreasonable restraints on alienation (4) rule against perpetuities (5) prohibition on unreasonable racial restrictions (below).
3. **Test for reasonableness:** To determine reasonableness, court must **‚Äúweigh‚Ä¶utility of the restraint against the injurious consequences of enforcing the restraint.‚Äù** *(Restatement (Third) of Property (Servitudes)).*
   1. Impact on Fair Market Value?
   2. Number of Buyers Excluded?
   3. What proportion of a class of people are affected? Does it affect one particular class disproportionately?
   4. Because alienability is a core property right necessary to ensure efficient allocation of property interests to their most valuable use, the greater the restraint, the stronger the justification that must be made to support it.
4. Absolute restraints are almost always struck down. (*Serio*)
   1. Private controls on land use may make property less alienable by eliminating buyers who do not want the land with the restrictions. Such restrictions may be challenged as **indirect restraints on alienation** (hard to win).
5. **RULE**: Covenants that require owners to obtain consent of the grantor/developer to sell, lease, or otherwise convey their property are usually struck down by courts as unreasonable restraints on alienation (*Serio*).
   1. However, covenants that grant such powers to HOAs/condominium associations are presumed to be valid, unless it can be shown the restrictions are ‚Äúarbitrary, against public policy, or in violation of some fundamental constitutional right‚Äù (*Woodside*).
6. **Charities:** Restraints on alienation are generally allowed when the holder of the property interest is a charity.
7. **Low income Housing:** Restraints on alienation that prohibit leasing of low- income housing property or sale to family that is not low-income have been held to be *reasonable* restraints on alienation because ‚Äúthe provision of housing for low and moderate income persons is in keeping with the public policy of this state.‚Äù (*City of Oceanside*).

***Evans v. Abney* (1970): CY PRES AND CHARITABLE TRUSTS; RULE AGAINST RACIAL RESTRICTIONS**

* Facts: Senator Bacon willed a park to the city of Macon, GA with the restriction that the park was for whites only. However, Bacon‚Äôs intentions could not be fulfilled b/c of *Evans v. Newton* (U.S. 1966), which held that the continued operation of the park must be without racial discrimination ‚Äìpublic character of the park dictates that it should be viewed as a public institution subject to command of 14th A.
  + On remand, petitioners argued that the trust should be saved by applying the *cy pres* doctrine to amend the terms of the will and strike the racial restrictions
  + The trial court refused to apply *cy pres*, stating that the ‚Äúsole purpose‚Äù of the trust was to make the park open only for white people, which was in irreconcilable conflict with the holding of *Evans v. Newton*;
  + *Evans*: three different interpretations ‚Äì (1) state action when passed law allowing for segregated parks, (2) public reliance,
* Rule: The interpretation of trusts and wills, and application of the cy pres doctrine, is governed by state law.
* Holding: Since Bacon‚Äôs sole intent was for the park to be for white people, there is NO way that the language of the will can be changed while still effectuating its purpose. The **trust is voided** and the land will revert to Bacon‚Äôs heirs.
  + Saying that the person who is responsible for injecting the discrimination was a private individual. The only person acting with racial discrimination is the senator, NOT the state.
  + Exercising Cy Pres doctrine is a neutral law with no racial animus.
* Dissent, Brennan: Shelley holds that when parties of different races want to deal with one another, a state court CANNOT prevent it by enforcing a private scheme of racial discrimination. As far as the record shows, this is a case of a state court enforcing a racial restriction to prevent willing parties from dealing with one another, and thus denying equal protection.

***Johnson v. Whiton* (Mass. 1893):** **RULE AGAINST CREATION OF NEW ESTATES**

* Facts: Royal Whiton willed 1/3 of his estate to his granddaughter Sarah Whiton ‚Äúand her heirs on her father‚Äôs side,‚Äù and the rest to his other grandchildren.
  + Whiton tried to convey the land willed to her but it was refused because she could not convey a fee simple absolute under the interest willed to her.
* Holding: A conveyor of land may NOT create new types of inheritance. When a conveyance attempts to do this, the **court will construe the conveyance as in the most similar established type of estate.** In Royal Whiton‚Äôs will, he devised one third of his estate to Sarah Whiton and her heirs on her father‚Äôs side. This is an unrecognized kind of inheritance. The words ‚Äúon her father‚Äôs side‚Äù are rejected, leaving Sarah Whiton with a fee simple estate that she is entitled to convey.
  + Court rewrites the will; fee simple absolute.
* Rule: If a land conveyance that does NOT directly qualify as one of the existing types of inheritance it will be interpreted to create the most similar estate.

Notes: Words of limit (‚Äúfor life‚Äù) vs. words of purchase (‚Äúto A‚Äù)

Some scholars have said this should be to case representing property law ü°™ that the court will only respect and enforce property rights which are already recognized

Case represents the (unspoken) doctrine and goals of **Numerous Clausus**

* The doctrine of numerus clausus is a general rule against the creation of new estates. A conveyance that does not fit within any of the established categories must be interpreted to fit an established estate.
* This means that grantors MUST put their conveyances in a recognizable form if they want courts to recognize the package of rights they intend to create.
* It also means that certain packages of rights will NOT be recognized.

### Leasehold Estates

**CASES**: *Vasquez v. Glassboro* (N.J. 1980), *Sommer v. Kridel* (N.J. 1977)

Landlord agrees to transfer possession of the property for a specified period to the tenant. In return for the tenant‚Äôs promise to make a periodic rental payment.

1. Possession reverts to landlord at the end of the tenancy
2. A lease is both a **conveyance** of a property interest as well as a **contract** between landlord and tenant

**FOUR TYPES OF TENANCIES**

1. **Term of Years:** Lasts for specified period of time determined by the parties. May terminate prior to the end of the fixed period upon happening of some event or condition.
   1. Landlord‚Äôs future interest is a **reversion**, but lease may also grant a **remainder** to third party.
   2. Landlord may NOT evict prior to end of term unless tenant is breaching material term, such as the covenant to pay rent.
   3. Death of either party does NOT terminate leasehold
2. **Periodic Tenancy:** Renew automatically at specified periods unless either landlord or tenant chooses to end the relationship.
   1. Notice is required prior to termination
   2. **Month-to-month** tenancies are a form of periodic tenancy. Uniform Residential Landlord Tenant Act (ULTRA) requires 60-day notice.
   3. Death of either party does NOT terminate.
3. **Tenancy at will:** Similar to periodic tenancy except it can be ended with NO notice to either party..
   1. Most jurisdictions now require notice by statute
   2. Still be distinguishable from periodic tenancy in some instances where death of either landlord or tenant may terminate tenancy at will.
4. **Tenancy at sufferance (holdover tenant):** Where a tenant who was rightfully in possession of a leasehold interest but wrongfully stays after the leasehold terminates.
   1. Intended to distinguish between tenant who wrongfully overstays and a trespasser. Different legal procedure for ejecting ‚Äì eviction proceeding followed by court judgment is required.
   2. Some states say if landlord continues to accept rent from holdover tenant, implies new lease term has begun.
5. Same rules generally apply to commercial or residential leases, but courts more inclined to adopt common law rules regulating residential leases on the assumption that they have less bargaining power or expertise to shape contractual arrangements in their best interests than commercial tenants.

**CONFLICTS ABOUT RENT**

There are several things a tenant/landlord can do in these situations:

1. **Stop Paying:** If tenant stops paying rent and stays on premises, owner may sue for back-rent and possession (eviction).
   1. Tenant may assert affirmative defenses
2. **Holdover Tenant Keeps Paying:** If holdover tenant continues to pay, landlord may choose to accept new tenancy.
   1. Most states say it is periodic tenancy.
   2. Minority say new term created if original tenancy was term of years. Landlord may sue for possession, but may have to stop accepting proffered checks to avoid creation of new tenancy. May also have to give notice as if a month-to-month tenancy if checks accepted.
3. In most states landlords barred from using self-help to evict ‚Äì e.g. change locks (*Berg v. Wiley*).
   1. Old rule said self-help OK if peaceable rather than forcible.
4. **Summary Proceedings/Forcible Entry and Detainer/Unlawful Detainer/Summary Ejectment**: Statutes limit the issues that can be addressed in lawsuit.
   1. **Traditional:** 2 claims (Rent passed due; tenant in possession post lease term expiry) and 2 defenses (Rent was paid; term hasn‚Äôt expired).
   2. **Today:**More defenses permitted, including implied warranty of habitability, retaliatory eviction, etc.
   3. Policy concern: landlords more often represented by counsel than tenants; impacts outcomes

**DUTY TO MITIGATE**

The Landlord has 3 options if the tenant stops paying and moves out before the end of the term:

1. **Accept tenant‚Äôs surrender:** Tenant‚Äôs departure an implied offer to end the term of years. Landlord can accept, and still sue for expectation damages (back rent owed but not paid for the time prior to tenant‚Äôs abandonment), but NOT for future rent.
   1. Agreed price ‚Äì (fair market price + cost of search), if >0.
2. **Re-let on Tenant‚Äôs Account:** Refuse to accept surrender by giving notice. Look for new tenant and re-let. When new tenant is found, landlord may sue for damages = new rent ‚Äì old rent. New rent must be reasonable.
   1. Implications of accepting surrender or not: Assume 1-year tenant moves out after 6 months, and new tenant is month-to-month and moves out after 4 months.
3. **Wait and Sue for rent at end of term vs. mitigate damages:** Almost all states reject this option. Have held landlords have duty to mitigate damages.
   1. Leaseholds increasingly considered under contract law rather than property law, which considered them conveyances of a property interest.
   2. ULTRA stipulates that if landlord fails to undertake reasonable efforts to mitigate, rental agreement is deemed to be terminated.
   3. If reasonable effort undertaken, and fail to let, get market rent-(contract rent+cost).
   4. If new rent is lower than contract rent, landlord may seek to recover damages from original tenant only if the landlord did not accept original tenant‚Äôs surrender.

**Arguments for and against duty to mitigate:**

1. For: More efficient for landlords to rent premises than leave them vacant.
   1. Ability to still get expectation damages if new rent low leaves landlord in same position otherwise would have been, and the tenant is better off as well.
   2. Unfair (and inefficient as it causes loss in social wealth) to require tenants to remain locked-in to an apartment and forgo moving elsewhere.
2. Against: Landlord bargained for the right not to have to look for another tenant.
   1. If tenant is unwilling to compensate landlord for right to terminate lease early, however, cost to landlord MUST outweigh the benefit to the tenant.

**Burden of proof**: majority rule like *Sommer* (landlord‚Äôs burden to show reasonable efforts), at least for residential leases. Commercial leases different. Minority of states place burden on tenant or have burden shifting framework.

**Tenants are entitled to basic rights:**

1. Legal process prior to eviction
2. Continued occupancy
3. Habitable conditions

**Leases (tenants) v. Licenses (p. 824-5)**

1. Main test is whether owner has transferred exclusive ‚Äúpossession‚Äù of a defined space ü°™ if **yes**, then **lease**. If **no**, then probably not then likely a **license**.
   1. URLTA 1.202 exempts students living in dorms or apartment managed by school from protection (they are considered licenses)
   2. *\*\*Vasquez* shows that even if NOT a tenant, the students may have other legal protections
2. Matters if leases v. license for whether landlord can use self-help or not (*Vasquez*)
3. **Licenses**
   1. Limited rights to enter or use land that do not with the land and are usually **revocable at will** by the grantor.
   2. No writing is required‚Äîmany licenses are implied by the circumstances (i.e. inviting a friend into your home);
   3. Non-transferrable, cannot be inherited, no property interests protected by the U.S. Constitution or Statute of Frauds
4. **Leases**
   1. Possessory rights to use a defined space for all uses not explicitly/implicitly prohibited in the lease
   2. As opposed to Easements‚Äînon-possessory, for a limited purpose, not necessarily in a defined space

***Vasquez v. Glassboro* (N.J. 1980):** **DISTINGUISHING TENANCIES FROM OTHER RELATIONSHIPS**

* Facts: Farmers contracted with Glassboro (nonprofit) for services of migrant farm laborers from Puerto Rico. Operated a labor camp in NJ with barracks, common facilities, no real privacy. Contracts did NOT require the workers to pay to live in the camps and provided that if a worker was fired, Glassboro would NOT provide for their transportation back to Puerto Rico. Vasquez was fired. Though there was still an open bed, Glassboro did not allow Vasquez to remain overnight.
  + Farmworkers Rights Project filed a complaint on Vasquez‚Äôs behalf, seeking an order permitting Vasquez to reenter the barracks and an injunction prohibiting Glassboro from denying Vasquez use of the barracks except through judicial process.
  + \*\*Court considered recurring question of whether migrant farmworkers should be evicted through a judicial proceeding rather than through a landlord's **exercise of self-help.**
  + At **common law**, one who occupied premises as an employee of the owner and received the use of the premises as part of compensation they are NOT considered a tenant. And therefore, an employer could use self-help.
  + **NJ Anti-eviction Act**: is an employee carve-out which allows eviction in employer/employee relations.
* Holding: Migrant workers staying at Glassboro's barracks are NOT tenants of Glassboro: have no privacy, their occupancy is sporadic, no contract requiring rent. As a result, they are NOT afforded statutory protection against self-help evictions b/c traditionally, only tenants have been entitled to a guaranteed summary action. However, the employment contract between Glassboro and Vasquez was unconscionable, as it did not provide Vasquez with any time to find alternative housing.
  + On **public-policy grounds**, a resolution not involving the contract is necessary.
  + Uses logic of *State v. Shack* that common law is changing with the changing times.
* Rule: A farm-labor service MUST use judicial proceedings to evict a farmworker who remains in possession of his living quarters after the termination of his employment. A landlord may NOT use self-help to evict someone who is legally in possession of his property, even if they are not a tenant protected with guaranteed summary action.
  + Judicial summary process provides an equitable, quick resolution to the dispute that is suitable to both parties.

***Sommer v. Kridel* (NJ 1977): DUTY TO MITIGATE UNPAID RENT**

* Facts: Kridel entered into a two-year lease with Sommer from May 1, 1972 ‚Äì April 30, 1974. About two weeks after the lease began, Kridel wrote to Sommer stating that his position had substantially changed, that he can no longer continue the lease, and that Sommer can keep the two months‚Äô rent already paid ‚Äì Sommer never replied. A third party approached Sommer about renting the apartment but Sommer told her that it was occupied, even though he knew it wasn‚Äôt. Eventually, in 1973 Sommer re-let the apartment but sued Kridel first. The trial court ruled in favor of Kridel, even though the prevailing rule at the time stated that there was NOT a duty on the part of Sommer to mitigate damages.
* Holding: A landlord has a duty to mitigate damages by making a reasonable effort to re-let the premises when seeking to recover rent from a defaulting tenant.
  + Such a duty is consistent with basic justice and fairness.
  + Trend to treat leases not as conveyances of land, but as contracts. As a result, application of the contract doctrine of mitigation of damages is appropriate. If a landlord fails to mitigate damages when he has the opportunity, the defaulting tenant may be relieved of his duty to pay rent.
  + In this case, a third party was ready, willing, and able to rent Kridel‚Äôs apartment and asked specifically about it. This actually made Sommer‚Äôs duty to mitigate damages very easy as it would have taken little to no effort to re-let the apartment.
* Rule: A landlord has a duty to mitigate damages when he seeks to recover rents due from a defaulting tenant

### Regulating Living Standards: Warranty of Habitability & Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

**CASES:** *Javins v. First National Realty Corp* (D.C. 1970), *3000 B.C. v. Bowman* (Penn. 2008)

**THEME**: Evolution of property law from agrarian roots to modern times has led to incorporation of elements of contract law.

**Moral Hazard Problem**: Tenant has no long-term interest in property, no incentive to care for it. Conversely, landlord has no incentive to make property better today.

**REMEDY #1: IMPLIED COVENANT OF QUIET ENJOYMENT**--Landlord impliedly promises not to disturb the tenant‚Äôs quiet enjoyment of the property.

1. Old rule was that tenant‚Äôs obligation to pay rent ceased when landlord barred tenant from premises. Rule expanded (new rule) says that if landlord does *anything* to induce tenant to move out, obligation to pay rent ceases ‚Äì becomes constructive eviction.
   1. \*You need to move out and then sue which isn‚Äôt really possible when asking people to leave their homes

**REMEDY #2: CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION‚Äî**Where tenants are deprived of ‚Äú**substantial interference with quiet enjoyment of the premises**‚Äù, may claim they were constructively evicted (*East Haven*).

1. **Actual eviction** ‚Äì landlord breaches the lease by physically barring tenant from the property ‚Äì e.g. putting new locks on doors.
   1. Tenant may sue landlord for damages for trespass and may seek injunction ordering landlord to re-convey possession.
2. **Constructive eviction** may be asserted as affirmative defense, or provide a cause of action against landlords.
   1. Traditional rule is that tenant can raise defense of constructive eviction ONLY IF he moves out within a reasonable period of time. Must claim interference with **quiet enjoyment** if interference is sufficiently substantial.
   2. Today, tenants only need to show the premises (or portion thereof) is uninhabitable in that ‚Äúnobody in his right mind would stay.‚Äù
   3. If stop paying and don‚Äôt move out? Staying may be evidence that interference is not sufficiently serious to allow tenant to stop paying rent without moving out.
      1. Landlord may be liable for **damages** to tenant under constructive eviction doctrine
         1. Rent abatement, proportional to portion of property no longer usable (*Minjack*)
         2. Punitive damages if wanton disregard for health and safety of tenants (*Minjack*)
         3. \*‚Äúall losses which [tenant] can prove he has actually sustained, or which he will necessarily sustain, under the circumstances, as a result of the unlawful eviction.‚Äù (*3000 B.C*.)
3. If only portion of property effected, **partial constructive eviction** allows tenant to stay on part of premises from which the tenant does not claim to have been constructively evicted.
   1. Requiring tenants to move out is unfair b/c there is ‚Äúmajor shortage‚Äô in residential housing which drastically undermines the bargaining power of tenants in relation to landlords.

**REMEDY #3: IMPLIED WARRANTY OF HABITABILITY‚Äî**Obligates landlords to provide premises that are safe and suitable for habitation. Breach of the warranty entitles tenants to move out before the end of the lease term, or to stay and either stop paying rent or pay a reduced rent until the improper conditions of the premises are fixed.

1. **Old rule** was NO implied representations that apartment was habitable, and no duty to repair.
   1. Only duty was to fix latent defects known to them and not easily discoverable. (Caveat Lessee)
2. Litigation about constructive eviction now tends to focus less on physical condition of premises and more on acts of landlord.
3. Leases may include clauses giving landlord right to evict tenants distributing quiet use and enjoyment of other tenants.
4. **New rule:** Gives rise to implied duties on landlords:
   1. If furnished apartment, covenant requires premises to be habitable
   2. Duty to disclose latent defects (not discoverable in a reasonable inspection)
   3. Duty to repair/maintain common areas
   4. If landlord assumes obligation to repair, must repair non-negligently
   5. Duty not to fraudulently misstate state of premises
   6. (Maybe) an obligation to abate nuisances
5. **8 Remedies if breach the warranty:**
   1. **Rescission** or right to move out before end of term: Tenant may repudiate contract if landlord is in breach of covenant, even when change in conditions would NOT amount to constructive eviction.
      1. If tenant moves out early, and landlord sues, tenant can assert LL violated implied warranty of habitability as **affirmative defense**.
   2. **Rent withholding**: Right to stop paying rent if LL is in breach. Breach may be raised as defense in eviction proceeding or claim for back rent.
      1. Distinguishes implied warranty of habitability from constructive eviction ‚Äì **latter ordinarily requires tenant to move out to become entitled to stop paying rent.**
      2. **Consult statute:** some states require tenant to give notice of defect prior to rent withholding. Some require complaint to be verified by inspector. Some require rent to be paid into escrow.
      3. Always a good idea to pay into escrow if withholding, in case you lose.
   3. **Rent abatement**: If breach, right to reduce rent.
      1. May elect not to withhold but sue for declaratory judgment and ask landlord to reimburse for all or portion of rent paid during period of breach.
      2. More common to withhold and wait to be sued. If defect serious, will get to keep all unpaid rent. If less serious, may have to pay portion.
      3. Amount to be withheld usually based on FMV test ‚Äì FMV without defect ‚Äì FMV with. Check statutes.
   4. **Repair and deduct**: Tenant may repair and deduct cost of repair from rent. Some statutes limit by how much.
   5. **Injunctive relief/specific performance**: Some states allow tenant to sue for injunction to get landlord to comply with code.
   6. **Administrative Remedies**: In some states, may call upon inspector to inspect premises, assess repairs to be made, and order landlord to pay.
      1. Inspectors may initiate suits for civil damages.
   7. **Criminal Penalties**: Some landlords face criminal liability of fail to fix dangerous and unlawful conditions.
      1. Judges have ordered landlords to live in buildings as punishment
   8. **Compensatory Damages**: Most jurisdictions limit recovery to rent aid, but some allow for damages > rent (cost of staying in hotel, loss of property etc.)
6. Breach for events beyond control of LL?
   1. *Park West Management Corp v. Mitchell* (1979): when owner‚Äôs staff went on strike and garbage piled up, tenants withheld rent and won in suit for back-rent claiming breach of warranty. Owner found responsible for conditions.
   2. Traditional rule was that landlord was NOT responsible for **acts of other tenants** unless lease specifically included an obligation to control other tenants.
      1. Today, landlord may be liable not only for actions he takes, but also for the acts of third parties ‚Äúperformed on property in which the landlord has an interest, which conduct could be legally controlled by him.‚Äù (See Restatement (Second) of Property).
   3. **Not waivable, in most jurisdictions, in residential leases** ‚Äì paternalistic, but warranted because otherwise tenants would always waive (no real choice given bargaining position.)
      1. **Similar reasons given for why other property rights non-waivable:**
         1. ***State v. Shack*** ‚Äì migrant workers would always waive access to essential services given weak bargaining position.
         2. If one party waives, precedent would be set for the rest of the market to follow ‚Äì imposes externalities on neighbors
         3. **Radin:** Habitable dwelling an inalienable right ‚Äì right to livable dwelling tied up in notion of personhood. Wouldn‚Äôt waive it if on equal footing with LL.
   4. Incorporated by statute in most jurisdictions post ***Javins.***
   5. **Most jurisdictions do not hold *commercial* leases contain implied warranties.** Jurisdictions that hold implied warranties do exist in commercial leases also make them **disclaimable**.
   6. **Impact:** Formal limits restrict application of doctrine ‚Äì requirements to show rent withheld for repairs, practice of landlords obtaining protective orders requiring tenants to deposit rent as condition of raising warranty defense, etc.

***Javins v. First Nat‚Äôl Realty* (D.C. 1970): RIGHT TO A HABITABLE PREMISE**

* In Javin the judge upended 500 years of property law.
* Facts: Two ppl rented apartments in the same building from First National Realty. They each withheld rent from FNR due to numerous violations of the housing code, which not arise until after leases began. FNR sought ejectment b/c of failure to pay rent.
* Holding: A warranty of habitability (measured by the standards set out in the Housing Regulations for DC) is implied in residential leases and it requires landlords to maintain residential premises in livable condition. If the landlord does not maintain the premises, the tenant is deprived of all value for which he contracted, and breach of this warranty gives rise to the usual remedies for breach of contract.
  + A landlord‚Äôs requirement to maintain the premises in livable condition is consistent with contract principles that are increasingly being applied to leases.
  + Although courts have historically not held landlords liable for repairs, the nature of leases has changed and those **precedents are outdated**.
* Rule: An implied warranty of habitability is read into residential leases and breach of that warranty frees tenants from the obligation to pay rent.

***3000 B.C. v. Bowman* (Pa. 2008): COVENANT OF QUIET ENJOYMENT**

* Facts: 3000 B.C. operated a spa and required a peaceful and quiet environment for its successful operation. Rented spa space from Bowman Properties for 12 years, after which Bowman began renting the apt above to a barber shop. Construction and resulting noise from the barber shop harmed spa‚Äôs business, which suffered financial loss and eventually moved to another location mid-lease.
  + Sued for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.
* Holding: This is a **constructive eviction**. Bowman breached the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment in its lease with 3000 B.C. It knew that the utility of the leased space was based on quiet tranquility. Its lease of the apt above substantially and fundamentally impaired the utility of the premises and constituted a breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.
  + In seeking to put 3000 B.C. in the position it would have been in absent breach, court awarded damages for period beginning when construction started and ending at the termination of the lease: lost profits, moving expenses, spa credits paid out to customers for ruined sessions, and loss of business goodwill.
  + Court declined to award damages for speculative repeated renewals of the lease.
* Rule: A **covenant of quiet enjoyment is implied in every lease.**
  + A landlord violates tenant‚Äôs implied right of quiet enjoyment of premises if landlord‚Äôs conduct substantially and fundamentally impairs the utility of the premises for the tenant, amounting to a constructive eviction, for which damages may be awarded.
  + In awarding damages, the court treats this like a contract action: seeks to put the non-breaching party in the financial position it would have been in had the contract been fulfilled and not breached (can include lost profits of a business, attorney‚Äôs fees, additional rent, reputation cost).

## Regulating the Right to Use through Nuisance Law

### Law of Nuisance

**CASES**: *Dobbs v. Wiggins* (Ill., 2010), *Johnson v. Paynesville Farmers* (Minn. 2012)

In 1992, WeAct sued NYC for putting in sewage treatment plant. Possible causes of actions:

Nuisance, trespass, public trust doctrine, zoning, cy pres, and equal protection.

The three major laws relating to the right to use are:

1. LAW OF NUISANCE
   1. Nuisance: Substantial and unreasonable interference with the use or enjoyment of land.
   2. Protects the right to quiet enjoyment of the land.
   3. LAW OF TRESPASS
      1. Protects the right of exclusive possession.
      2. Requires NO showing of harm b/c protects right of exclusive possession.
2. LAW OF SERVITUDES
3. LAW OF ZONING

**Distinguish Nuisance from Trespass Claims:**

* *Paynesville*: ‚ÄúThe law of nuisance deals with indirect or intangible interference with an owner‚Äôs use and enjoyment of land, while trespass deals with direct and tangible interferences with the right to exclusive possession of land‚Äù
* Nuisance is about harm, and trespass is the right to exclude
* INTANGIBLE TRESPASS PROBLEM: ‚ÄúThe expansion of the tort of trespass to include invasions by intangible matter potentially subjects countless persons and entities to automatic liability absent any demonstrated injury.‚Äù (*Paynseville*)
  + SOLUTION: Add a harm requirement (Washington & Alabama).
  + ALTERNATIVE: Refuse to recognize intangible trespass. Preserve distinction between laws of trespass/nuisance.
  + Washington + Alabama has a foreseeability + harm requirement with trespass of particulate particles
  + Paynesville ‚Äì trespass at its core is protecting a right to exclude and adding a harm requirement would be diluting the bundle of rights.
    - Preserved distinction between law of trespass and nuisanc

WHEN TO BRING A NUISANCE CLAIM:

1. Ex ante: Not too early under the **doctrine of anticipatory nuisance**, which bars a nuisance claim before the use is in place.
   1. Could enter world of contracts (servitudes - contracts that run with the land forever and bind future people there; zoning ordinances - pass law)
   2. You have to prove two things:
      1. ‚ÄúNear certainty‚Äù that the nuisance would take place
      2. That once nuisance-causing activity begins, cost of dismantling may weigh against finding a nuisance
2. Ex post: Not too late. You must sue before the **statute of limitations** runs out.
   1. Many courts distinguish between a **temporary** and **permanent** nuisance:
      1. **Permanent** - statute of limitations begins at the time nuisance begins
      2. **Temporary** - your claim ‚Äúaccrues anew upon each injury‚Äù

**MAJORITY RULE: REASONABLE USE TEST:** Restatement (Second) of Torts, 3-4

1. Is defendant‚Äôs act a proximate cause?
2. Is the plaintiff‚Äôs interest within the right to the ‚Äúuse and enjoyment of land‚Äù?
   1. Examples: (1) freedom from pollution, (2) noise, (3) odor, and (4) smoke.
3. Is the interference substantial?
   1. A court will consider the **effect on a normal person of ordinary habits and sensibilities**, focusing on factors that include the:
   2. What is the **extent** of the harm?
   3. What is the **character** of the harm?
4. [Is the invasion **intentional or negligent**? ]
5. Is the harm **unreasonable**?
   1. What are the economic and social values of the activity?
   2. Is the activity suitable for the location?
   3. Who came first, plaintiff or activity?
   4. What is the practicability and fairness of making either party avoid the conflict?
   5. A court will weigh the gravity of the harm done to the plaintiffs against the benefits caused by the defendant‚Äôs use of the land and the suitability of the use (Coasean Influence). A court may consider the:
      1. Economic and social value of the conflicting activities
      2. Suitability of the activities for the location
      3. Ability of either party to avoid the conflict and practicability of and fairness of making the party do so.
   6. **NOTE:** In determining whether to classify the defendant‚Äôs conduct as a nuisance, courts weigh the utility of the defendant‚Äôs conduct in the defendant‚Äôs favor ü°™ A defendant whose behavior is socially beneficial gets more leeway to impose costs on its neighbors than a defendant whose behavior is not socially beneficial.
   7. However, the focus remains on the **impact of the defendant‚Äôs conduct on the plaintiff** (Classical Influence). The fact that a defendant‚Äôs conduct is socially valuable is not decisive.
6. Any **additional fairness and/or welfare concerns**?
7. Fairness:

* Character of the harm: aesthetic harms will be viewed as not as serious as health and safety
  + - Courts have historically been reluctant to find visual annoyances (without smell/threat to safety) to constitute a nuisance. But becoming more allowed to sue based on this (*Rattigan v. Wile:* p. 353).
* Distributive considerations: is it fair to make owner bear costs of defendant‚Äôs socially beneficial activity, or should the costs be spread to the owner causing damage?
* Fault: is owner engaged in disfavored activity? Inappropriate for area? Did plaintiff come to the nuisance?

1. Welfare:

* Costs and benefits of allowing the harmful conduct v. cost of prohibiting it
* Incentives: to engage in the respective activities. What effects will liability/immunity have?
* Lower cost avoider: which party can more cheaply avoid the cost?

1. What is the **appropriate remedy**? (discussed below)

**Policy Critiques**

* Standard, not rule. No hard and fast test, no hierarchy of how factors weigh, so gives a lot of power to trial judge
* Rule would be more administrable/efficient. Clear, so people would sue each other less
* Doesn‚Äôt give adequate warning to person who might create the nuisance. What‚Äôs too loud for your neighbors?

**CIVIL LAW/NATURAL FLOW RULE (‚Äúsic utere‚Äù)**

Property owners must use their property in a way that does not injure their neighbors. You have a strict liability/veto rights remedy‚Äîthe injured plaintiff has legal rights to injunction and damages

1. **Justifications**:

* **Fair**‚Äîeveryone gets protection from damages to their investment‚Äîyou get security.
* **Efficient**‚Äîit would give incentives against property damage and to make your investment safe to use

**What is the remedy?**

  2. In deciding which remedy to apply, courts will consider variety of factors, focusing in particular on the **costs and benefits of the defendant‚Äôs conduc**t.
  3. This focus on the costs and benefits of the defendant‚Äôs conduct reflects the influence of law and economics analysis of nuisance.
     + \*\*Instead of simply granting an injunction, a court will consider a variety of factors to decide how to protect the entitlement in an efficient way. These factors include transaction costs, i.e. the likelihood that the parties will bargain. [Coasean Influence; Melamed & Calabresi Influence]

The question of whether there is a nuisance and what is the remedy is 2 prongs and should NOT be conflated with one another. In assessing whether invasion of interest was unreasonable court should weigh utility of D conduct. But the social utility of the D‚Äôs actions is NOT definitive.

* First step on P, second step is on the D‚Äôs conduct,
  + Cost/benefits focused on the second part of the analysis

***Johnson v. Paynesville* (Minn. 2012): ESTABLISHING CAUSATION**

* Facts: Johnsons owned organic fields next to conventional non-organic fields. Paynesville sprayed pesticides onto adjacent fields and wind allegedly caused some pesticides to drift onto Johnsons‚Äô fields, so Johnsons stopped selling their soybeans and alfalfa as organic for 3 years.
  + Brought suit for trespass AND nuisance .
* Holding: The sprayed pesticides are intangible, particulate matter. Thus, even if they did drift onto the Johnsons‚Äô crops, such drift CANNOT constitute trespass under Minnesota law. The Johnsons do not assert that the Cooperative‚Äôs spraying prevented them from possessing their land exclusively. Rather, the Johnsons‚Äô claim is that the spraying prevented them from selling their crops organically. This is a claim of interference with the Johnsons‚Äô use of their land, which is a claim for nuisance.
* Importance: Court declines to get rid of the distinctions between trespass and nuisance.
* Rule: When the invasion of a plaintiff‚Äôs land is committed by an intangible agency, such as particulate matter, such invasion does NOT constitute trespass. But, this type of invasion may constitute nuisance if the intangible agency interferes with the landowner‚Äôs use or enjoyment of his land.

Notes

* Court cites *Bradley* (Alabama law) which says that particulate matter CAN be trespass.
  + Concern would be that this would open up floodgates of litigation. To mitigate against this risk *Bradley* requires **reasonable foresee-ableness that it will cause damage.**
  + Common law should change b/c of how we live.
* Court rules against this logic, saying that *Bradley* mixed up trespass and nuisance. So, if you only say its trespass when there is damage (b/c you are mixing it up with nuisance) then you are taking away one of the main aspects of trespass law (*Jack v. Steinberg* case of pulling mobile home across the land ü°™ court applied trespass even though there was no damage).
  + \*Nuisance is about harm, and trespass is the right to exclude

**Coase Theorem**: Asks the court to neutralize the assumption that the person who is causing the damage is the only person creating costs. Courts should consider the action which the person must refrain from as a cost.

* Reciprocal nature of costs ‚Äì Costs are imposed on both sides.

***Dobbs v. Wiggins* (Ill. 2016): ESTABLISHING SUBSTANTIAL AND UNREASONABLE HARM; DECIDING REMEDIES**

* Facts: Wiggins moved in next door 15 years after Dobbses and began breeding, selling, kenneling dogs. Had up to 100 on his property at one point. As he increased number of dogs on his property, neighbors began complaining and he attempted unsuccessfully to mitigate the noise. Dobbses sought an injunction on grounds that Wiggins‚Äôs barking dogs constituted a nuisance. Testified that noise prevented them from enjoying their land as they had before he moved in: kept windows open, yard work, sat/hosted outside.
* Holding: The noise was **substantial** and **unreasonable**. The circuit court was within its discretion to outweigh the harm to the Dobbses v. the utility of Wiggins‚Äôs business & the suitability of his land for such a business, and determine that the use was a nuisance.
  + Dobbses were on the land first, and given Wiggins‚Äôs prior failed attempts, mitigating the noise without limiting the number of dogs is not possible.
  + Affirmed but remanded to determine the correct number of bird dogs allowed(erred in limiting to only 6, must be some number between 6 and 69 that won‚Äôt tank the business and won‚Äôt be a substantial invasion for plaintiffs.)
* Rule: Reasonable Use Test (majority rule) ‚Äì To maintain a claim for nuisance, a plaintiff must prove that the alleged invasion of his right to use/enjoy his property is (1) **substantial**, (2) **intentional or negligent,** (3) **unreasonable**.
  + In determination, courts examine the effects of the invasive conduct on a reasonable person. The allegedly invasive conduct must be substantially physically offensive to the senses.

### Nuisance Part 2: Choice of Remedy

**CASES**: *Boomer v. Atlantic Cement Co.* (NY 1969), *Fontainebleau v. Forty Five* (Fla. 1959)

Early America: Nuisance ‚Üí Automatic Injunction. Industrialized America: Retreat from Automatic Injunction.

**Law and Economics Theories (380-92)**

According to economic analysis we try to have the most efficient outcome and rule of law. This would try and minimize externalities.

1. **Classical Economics** ‚Äì Externalities: costs imposed on third parties that are NOT taken into account in the actor‚Äôs own cost-benefit determinations.
   1. Internalize: classic economic analysis of nuisance sought to make the factory to pay for and therefore internalize the external harms that its operation causes.
   2. Internalize externality harm to 3rd party and would just have to pay
2. **Coase Style Argument (1960):** think about reciprocal costs ‚Äì costs on BOTH parties (externalities are reciprocal). Therefore, the judge must think about JOINT costs.
   1. In Coase‚Äôs view, the classical economics approach doesn‚Äôt help courts decide who, as between two conflicting land uses, should be internalizing the externalities.
      1. In a world where every land use generates costs, why not instead ask homeowners to internalize the costs of their land use (homeowning next to a factory) by asking these homeowners to pay $100,000 to a factory to install pollution control devices‚Ä¶or leave?
   2. Care about who values the land/entitlement the most. When not able to determine who values the cost the most the court will look to who is able to avoid the costs best.
   3. Case-by-Case ‚Äì Both parties will buy back their entitlements in a second transaction.
      1. If transaction costs are zero and property rights over the relevant resources are well-defined, parties involved in an externality situation will bargain to an efficient resolution, regardless of to whom the property rights are initially assigned.

Other theories:

1. Pigou Style Argument: Bright-Line Rule ‚Äì Nuisance-feasors should have to internalize its costs of doing business. Assign entitlements to injured parties to be free of harm.
2. Sunstein and Thaler: Case-by-case ‚Äì Coase is wrong! Transaction costs are too high and people are not rational enough! Courts should assign the entitlement to the party most willing to pay in order to maximize total welfare.
3. Posner: Case-by-Case ‚Äì But if initial distributions of wealth are unjust, deriving entitlements from willingness to pay will undermine claims to justice.
4. Prioritarianism (a): Case-by-Case ‚Äì Weigh costs/benefits felt by worse-off party more heavily than those felt by better-off.
5. Prioritarianism (b): Bright-Line Rule ‚Äì Serve distributive justice and anti-commodification values by creating bright-line rules to protect vulnerable populations!

A court that has moved from classical model to Coase, the remedy is formulated as most efficient outcome.

1. After the court has handed out their **initial legal allocation**, the parties are able to bargain and theoretically whoever values the property the most can either keep it or buy it from opposing party.
   1. This assumes that there is NO transaction costs
2. But there are transaction costs which would make it hard for parties to properly bargain for efficiency ü°™ p. 384 lists transaction costs
3. **So the court could help efficiency by assigning entitlement to the parties who values the entitlement the most.**
   1. Issue with this is how does the court decide who values it the most?
   2. Can measure value on open market, etc.
4. If the court can‚Äôt evaluate who values the costs the most, then liability rules should be applied.
   1. Property is injunction, liability is damages ü°™ Calabresi & Melamed article
   2. Under Calebrisi the parties can bargain

***Boomer v. Atlantic Cement Co.* (NY 1969):** **INJUNCTION V. DAMAGES IN NUISANCE**

* Facts: Atlantic Cement has factory that releases dust, smoke, vibrations. Boomer and neighbors sued for injunction and damages alleging injury to property.
* Holding: Grants injunction until Atlantic Cement pays Boomer permanent damages. Injunction here would require Atlantic to completely close its operation unless a cleaner method of producing cement could be found; unfair to Atlantic (pollution problem is experienced by all cement factories). The economic benefit of keeping the factory open is greater than the harm suffered by Boomer, so most equitable solution is to award a temporary injunction.
  + **Efficient**: Liability rule is the best here, because there are potential transactions costs that come with group bargaining on the Boomer side‚Äî‚Äúholdover‚Äù and ‚Äúfree rider‚Äù problems;
    - The court just decides how valuable the entitlement is- MUCH SIMPLER
    - The court might not value Boomer‚Äôs home as much as they do, since it is so personal to them
  + **Efficient**: the factory internalizes its costs, rather than externalizing them onto Boomer
  + **Socially useful**: Atlantic Cement still gets to operate
  + **Fair**: everyone gets a little of what they want‚ÄîBoomer gets damages, Atlantic Cement gets to operate
* **Dissent**: By deviating from the rule that continuous nuisances are subject to injunctions, the majority is perpetuating the country‚Äôs pollution problem. By allowing Atlantic to continue its operations upon paying permanent damages, no further incentive to develop clean technology. Essentially, majority is allowing Atlantic to continue to commit a wrong, if they pay upfront for it.
* Rule: Permanent damages, as opposed to an injunction, are appropriate when the damages resulting from a nuisance are significantly less than the economic benefit derived from the party causing the harm.

***Fountainbleau v. Forty-Five Twenty-Five* (Fla. 1959):**

  + **Judicial role:** this is a zoning problem in a rapidly changing society, it might be better for the Miami legislature to figure out what it wants to preference here
    - A court could never predict how its decisions would change Miami‚Äîso it should stick to the existing rule (which it does)
* Rule: A landowner may use his property in a reasonable way as long as it does not injure the legal rights of another.

**LAW AND ECONOMICS PERSPECTIVE**

* The court rejects the civil law rule here and adopts the common enemy rule. ER might NOT want the Coase theory applied and would rather want FB to **internalize costs of the harms**.

## Regulating the Right to Use through Servitudes

### Law of Servitudes: Express Easements

**CASES**: *Green v. Lupo* (Wash. 1982), *Cox v. Glenbrook Co.* (Nev. 1962)

**Servitude**: right or an obligation that ‚Äúruns with the land‚Äù meaning that it automatically passes to subsequent owners or possessors of the land.

* More akin to law of contracts than of nuisance

**SOME BASIC VOCAB**

1. **Affirmative servitude (Easement)**: a right to use ANOTHER‚ÄôS land for a limited purpose
   1. Easements, right of way for ingress/egress, leases, and profits (right to gather resources from another‚Äôs land)
2. **Negative servitude (Covenant)**: a restriction with respect to what OWNERS can do with their land
   1. Negative easements, real covenants, and equitable servitudes
3. **Burdened/Servient Estate**: Land subject to the **burden** of a servitude
4. **Benefited/Dominant Estate**: Land benefited from the servitude
5. **Appurtenant servitudes**: Servitudes that ‚ÄúRUN WITH THE LAND,‚Äù and automatically passed to subsequent owners or possessors
6. **Servitudes ‚Äúin gross‚Äù**: Servitudes in which the benefit is held by a PARTICULAR INDIVIDUAL OR ENTITY rather than running with a parcel of land
   1. Ex. A utility company that moves its headquarters still has the servitudes of the power lines

**Affirmative servitudes** **(EASEMENTS)**: rights to use another‚Äôs land for a limited purpose.

* Most common type: right of way.
* Another type: profit a prendre ‚Äì allows non-owners to collect resources from the land.
* Fee simple/and of the fees ü°™ main difference is what happens when you breach.
  + Covenants viewed through breaching contract, breaching Fee Simple is usually forfeiture.

**Negative servitudes** **(modernly - ‚Äúcovenants‚Äù)**: restrictions with respect to what owners can do with their own land.

* Traditionally limited to:
  1. Right to lateral support of one‚Äôs building
  2. Rights to prevent both light and air from being blocked by construction on neighboring land
  3. Right to prevent interference with the flow of an artificial stream such as an aqueduct
* Examples of negative easements:
  1. Conservation easements ‚Äì preventing development of land for environmental purposes
  2. Historic preservation easements ‚Äì preventing destruction or alteration of buildings that have historical or architectural importance
  3. Solar easements ‚Äì to protect access to sunlight for solar energy
* **Appurtenant Easement:** runs with land
  1. Dominant Estate ‚Äì benefited by the appurtenant easement
  2. Servient Estate ‚Äì burdened by the appurtenant easement
* **Easement in Gross**: benefit is held by a particular individual/entity rather
  1. Servient Estate: burdened by the easement in gross
  2. Apportionability: question of divisibility of the easement
  3. Nonexclusive: grantor of the servient estate has reserved for himself the right to use the easement in conjunction with the grantee, easement is generally held to be nonapportionable
  4. Exclusive: grantor has NO right to use the easement in conjunction with the grantee

**PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS FOR EXPRESS EASEMENTS**

An express easement will run with the burdened or benefited estate if:

1. Writing ‚Äì the original easement is in writing and complies with the Statute of Frauds
2. Notice ‚Äì for the burden to run on subsequent owners of the servient estate, the subsequent owners must have notice
   * Actual notice ‚Äì if the subsequent owners in fact know about the existence of the easement
   * Inquiry notice ‚Äì if there are visible signs of use by non-owners, such as telephone poles, above-ground utility lines, or a path across the property, the owner may be put on inquiry notice
   * Constructive notice ‚Äì if the deed conveying the easement is recorded in the proper registry of deeds in the proper place, and if a reasonable search of the registry would lead to discovery of the deed, then subsequent owners are deemed to be on constructive notice
3. Intent ‚Äì the grantor of the easement intends for the deed to bind future owners of the servient estate (or future owners of the dominant estate).

INTERPRETING AN EXPRESS EASEMENT

1. Is the written instrument **ambiguous**?
2. Is there a **presumption** against easement in gross? (See 521-2)
3. Is the servient owner entitled to **impose reasonable restraints** on a right of way? (See 522)

***Green v. Lupo* (Wash. 1982): CATEGORY OF EASEMENT**

* Facts: Greens own a parcel of land with a house, sold northern portion to Lupo. Lupo requested a deed release for part of the property, and Greens agreed in exchange for the promise of an easement over part of Lupo's portion. The terms of the easement were contained in a written agreement, which provided, among other things, that the easement was promised to "Don Green and Florence B. Green," and that easement was "for ingress and egress for road and utilities purpose." The easement was to allow the Greens to have access to the land retained by the Greens to build and live in a cabin.
  + Greens used some of their remaining land for mobile homes, and some of the mobile-home residents used the easement as a practice runway for their motorcycles. This caused tension between Lupo and Greens, and Lupo refused to formally grant the easement. Lupo also blocked the easement to restrict access from the Greens' property.
  + The Greens brought suit to specifically enforce the easement agreement.
  + **ISSUE**‚ÄîDo the occupants of the mobile home have an easement, as well as the Plaintiffs? ü°™ Was easement in gross or appurtenant?
* Holding: Easement was granted specifically to Greens for the purpose of "ingress and egress for road and utilities." This is an ambiguous conveyance, b/c it is both to specific people AND for a purpose that benefits the dominant estate. Considering all the facts and circumstances, however, was an EASEMENT APPURTENANT. Granted to give access to the land for purposes of building/inhabiting cabin on the property. This reference to the land with no language limiting access to only the Greens, when combined with the presumption of appurtenance, weigh in favor of finding the easement to be appurtenant.
* Rule: There is a presumption that an easement is appurtenant to a parcel of land and NOT an easement in gross.
  + **Practical reasons** for the presumption: it‚Äôs easier to have one easement attached to land than for many people have get to have an in gross easement, as well as all the people they convey their property to;

Notes:

* An easement granted for purposes such as roads, utilities, ingress, and egress is an easement appurtenant because it benefits the adjacent parcel. An easement appurtenant becomes part of the land that it benefits, and it passes with possession of the dominant parcel unless it is otherwise limited.
* \*\*By contrast, an easement that **designates a specific person** as the dominant owner ü°™ more likely to be considered an easement in gross.
* If the conveyance of land is ambiguous, **parol evidence** is admissible to help determine whether the parties intended an express easement to be appurtenant or in gross.
  + The court may consider the unique circumstances of the parties and the property, as well as the parties‚Äô conduct and admissions, in making this determination.

***Cox v. Glenbrook Co.* (Nev. 1962): SCOPE OF EASEMENT**

* Facts: Glenbrook Company resort granted easement to Quill for use of Glenbrook back road to get to Quill house. The road primarily used (back road) had only enough room for one car to pass through at a time. Glenbrook granted an easement in writing ‚Äúover the roads of [Glenbrook] as now located‚Äù to Quill, ‚Äúhis heirs and assigns forever.‚Äù When Quill died, Kenneth Johnson bought property, then sold to Cox who proposed dividing property into smaller parcels to develop it into a residential area, widening back road, and possibly allowing future residents of development to use it.
  + Parties sought declaratory judgment on scope and extent of easement.
* Holding: Cox may subdivide the easement, allowing for multiple easement holders so long as their use does not cause an UNREASONABLE BURDEN to Glenbrook in the future.
  + Widening of the back road so that two cars can pass at the same time is NOT a kind of use that Glenbrook or Quill contemplated in the Quill Easement, and would impose an undue burden on servient estate and CAN‚ÄôT be done.
* Rule: When a right-of-way granted by easement appurtenant is conveyed to a new easement holder, the use of the easement must be of the kind contemplated by the grantor and must NOT constitute an unreasonable burden on the servient estate that was not contemplated by the grantor. The use of the easement must conform to the intent of the original parties, or it risks reaching beyond the scope.
* Argument FOR the ***Green v. Lupo*** SUBDIVISIBILITY RULE:
  + **Necessity**: all subdivided owners would still need it, not just the original owners;
  + **Efficient**: Makes land more alienable and promotes development, because you can build beyond just one single-family home;
  + **Fairness**: expectations are frustrated if deprived of use.
* Argument AGAINST the subdivisibility rule:
  + **Efficiency:** Glenbrook property is made *less* alienable if burdened by dominant estate owner‚Äôs use (why we have **undue burden** test). Also, may incentivize development to an excessive degree.
    - Was **intended** only for Quill, and for one person.
  + **Fairness**: Glenbrook developed first (should be awarded for labor, investment)

### Easements By Implication: By Estoppel, Prior Use, Necessity

**CASES**: *Lobato v. Taylor* (Colo. 2002), *Granite Properties v. Manns* (Ill. 1987), *Finn v. Williams* (Ill. 1941)

Four ways an easement can be created without writing (**implied easements**):

1. **Easements by estoppel** (*Lobato*)‚Äîwhen a landowner grants the claimant **permission** to use the property in such a way that (1) would foreseeably induce a reasonable user to substantially change position in reliance on the believe that the permission will not be revoked; (2) the user substantially so changes her position; and (3) revoking the permission would cause substantial injustice
   1. When courts will find an easement by estoppel:
      1. Parties intended to create an easement but failed to comply with the statute of frauds
      2. Reasonable reliance on the continuation of consent‚Äîthere is no proof of the grantor‚Äôs intent to create an easement but the grantor permitted another to use the land and the elements above are satisfied
      3. Where there has been fraud or misrepresentation about the existence of or intent to grant an easement.
   2. Some jurisdictions reject easement by estoppel completely (*Kitchen v. kitchen)*
   3. **Constructive Trust**: an equitable exception to the statute of frauds that may be granted where legal title to property for the express benefit of another has been obtained through actual FRAUD, misrepresentations, concealments, taking advantage of one‚Äôs necessities, or under circumstances otherwise rendering it unconscionable for the holder of legal title to retain beneficial interest in property (note: apply same elements as estoppel)
2. **Easements by Prescription**: created through adverse and open use of the land continuously until the expiration of the statute of limitations for trespass (*Lobato*; *Community Feed Store*)
   1. Elements the same as an adverse possession claim, **but need to only show *use*, not *possession***
   2. Negative easements **cannot** be acquired by prescription
   3. **Acquiescence**‚Äînumber of states require the claimant to prove acquiescence by the true owner; acquiescence may mean the true owner:
      1. Failed to bring a trespass action
      2. Knew about the use and allowed it to continue
      3. Should have known about the use and did nothing
   4. Some courts maintain that acquiescence merely means that the owner did NOT assert her right to exclude by bringing a trespass action, or that a reasonable owner should have known.
3. **Easements by Prior Use/Quasi-easement/Easement by Implication** (*Lobato*)
   1. Created when:
      1. The servient and dominant estates were once under common ownership;
      2. One parcel was previously used for the benefit of the other parcel in a manner that was
         1. continuous and
         2. apparent
      3. The use is ‚Äúreasonably necessary‚Äù OR ‚Äúconvenient‚Äù for the enjoyment of the dominant estate
         1. Usually for rights of way for passage of people/vehicles
      4. A contrary intention is neither expressed nor implied
4. **Easements by Necessity** (*Finn*): servient and dominant estate were once under common ownership and upon severance the part of the state becomes LANDLOCKED, requiring a right of way over the other part of the severed estate to access a public road.
   1. **Dormancy**: An easement by necessity may lay dormant through successive transfers of title, and yet pass with each transfer as appurtenant to the dominant estate and be exercised at any time by the holder of title thereto. (*Finn*)
   2. The easement by necessity disappears when, at any point a legal right of ingress/egress is accessed through alternative routes. A mere license to use an alternative route however, will NOT eliminate the easement by necessity.
   3. **Policy** justification:
      1. Promote efficient utilization of property‚Äîbuyers will not agree to purchase a landlocked property
      2. BUT some courts will say no necessity if grantor and buyer *knew* they were buying a landlocked property

***Lobato v. Taylor (Colo. 2002):* EASEMENT BY ESTOPPEL**

* Facts: After Mexican American War, Beaubien acquired a grant of about 1 million acres in future Colorado from Mexico. In 1850s, he recruited families to settle and live on land as farmers.
  1. In 1863, he signed Beaubien Document that granted the settlers rights of access to common lands for resource-gathering. In 1864 Giblin purchased the land on condition that the rights are preserved.
  2. In 1960, Taylor bought a piece of Beaubien land, deed stated that he acquired the tract subject to ‚Äúclaims of the local people by prescription or otherwise to right to pasture, wood, and lumber and so-called settlement rights in, to, and upon said land.‚Äù However, Taylor refused to allow local landowners access to the land. Farmers claimed easement rights to access the land.
  3. **ISSUE**‚ÄîDo the farmworkers have a property interest in the Taylor ranch, even though the Beaubien document does not meet the formal requirements for the express grant of rights?

1. Holding: Easement by estoppel for pasture, firewood, and timber. Requirements (LOOK UP LIST): (1) The rights were **expected** by the settlers, intended by Beaubien under the terms of the Beaubien Document, and **necessary** because utilization of the timber was needed to heat and build homes, and the ability to graze was needed to feed cattle. (2) Settlers moved their entire families from miles around to build houses and farms (**reliance- changed position**). (3) The access rights at issue have been around for over 100 years and were a condition of the land grant to Taylor. To eradicate these rights would constitute an **injustice**.
   1. **Also easement from prior use** ü°™ (1) servient and dominant estate once under common ownership (Beaubien and Gilpin). (2) Rights alleged were exercised prior to severance of the estate. (3) The use was not merely temporary. (4) Continuation of use was necessary to the enjoyment of the parcel)

* Dissent: Majority should not have found an easement by estoppel because there is NO evidence that Beaubien or his successors in interest intended to have the plaintiffs‚Äô predecessors in interest rely to their detriment on the grant.
* Rule: A court CAN imply an easement created by estoppel when: (1) the landowner permitted another party to use the land under circumstances in which it was reasonably foreseeable that the user would substantially change position believing that the permission would not be revoked, (2) the user substantially changed position in reasonable reliance on that belief, and (3) injustice can be avoided only by establishment of a servitude.

Notes

* Taylors would want an easement in gross. Other side would want easement **appurtenant** ü°™ wouldwant to look to intent of the original giver of the easement.
  1. Presumption against easement in gross is also a good argument here. This is b/c easements in gross will add uncertainty.
* Potential argument: wouldn‚Äôt apply easement by estoppel when there is a use for profit. But the court says this doesn‚Äôt apply.
* Court holds that hunting, fishing, and recreation is NOT part of easement.

***Granite Properties v. Manns* (Ill. 1987):** **EASEMENT BY PRIOR USE, IMPLIED INTENT AND NECESSITY**

* Facts: Granite sold to Manns a part of tract that was in between apartment building and shopping center. Two driveways were each situated on Manns land and Manns knew this when bought the property. Manns found no recorded easements (no notice) but did see the two driveways before buying (constructive notice). Driveways were used by Granite for years before. Even though there was another driveway that led to back of shopping center, use of this driveway was necessary for proper functioning bc there was not enough room in other driveway for large delivery trucks to exit; other driveway led to parking lot of apartment complex and was no other feasible place to put parking lot, was only access point. After Manns bought the tract, told Granite to stop using driveways. Manns want the standard to be ‚Äúabsolutely necessary,‚Äù not ‚Äúreasonably necessary‚Äù for access by Granite.
* Holding: Easements from prior use.
  1. (1) Owned entire tract of land and subsequently conveyed the land on which the driveways are situated to the Manns. (2) Manns admitted to noticing Granite‚Äôs use of both driveways when they bought the land, Granite‚Äôs use was continuous for many years before the conveyance, and Granite‚Äôs use is permanent because the driveways were covered in gravel, thus fixed as its present use. (3) Without driveway use, shopping center and apartment complex cannot be run properly.
  2. Easement which benefits the grantor ‚Äì easement by grant
* Rule: A landowner establishes an easement implied by prior use if he proves: (1) ownership over both the dominant and servient parcels and a subsequent transfer of ownership to the servient parcel, (2) that, before the transfer, the use being claimed was apparent, continuous, and permanent, and (3) the easement is necessary and beneficial to the owner‚Äôs use of the property he kept. These requirements are in place because proof of prior use is evidence that the parties intended an easement even if it was left out of the deed.

***Finn v. Williams* (Ill. 1941):** **EASEMENT BY NECESSITY**

* Facts: In 1895, Williams sold part of a parcel to Bacons, who then sold it to Finns. The only means of getting to the nearest highway and market from the Finns‚Äô land was by crossing over Williams‚Äôs land or that of a stranger. However, Williams refused to give the Finns permission to cross over her land.
* Holding: Easement by necessity was implied with the conveyance from Williams to Bacon because the ONLY available route to the highway and market for Bacon was over Williams‚Äôs land.
* Rule: An implied easement by necessity may lie **dormant** through multiple transfers of land, but it passes with each transfer and may be exercised at any time.
  + Easement by necessity is implied where the only available means of ingress and egress to a highway is over the conveyer‚Äôs land or that of a stranger.

### Introduction to Covenant

**CASES**: *Neponsit v. Emigrant Savings* (1938), *Davidson v. D. Katz & Sons* (N.K. 1990), *Nahrstedt v. Lakeside Village* (Cal. 1994), *Neuman v. Grandview* (Fla. 2003)

**Covenant**: Negative servitudes. Restrictions on use of land or obligations regarding land, held by one who does NOT own the land.

* Key difference with covenants and fee simple determinable/FSSCS is that breaking fee results in forfeiture of land. Not so in covenants.
  + Breach of covenant is remedied by damages or injunction
* Covenants created common rules for ownership development in 20th century (ex: height of houses, exclude people based on race)

**A note on terminology:** In the past a negative easement, real covenant, equitable servitudes all had their own meaning. As time went on there has been a movement away from this and to treat all three of these under the single heading of covenant on the land.

**Negative easements ü°™ Real Covenants and Equitable Servitudes**:

At early common law, negative easements were restrictions on land held by others. Over time negative easements were limited b/c of concern that keeping of the status quo would prevent useful development of land. Limited to three categories:

1. Rights of lateral support of one‚Äôs buildings
2. Rights to free flow of light and air
3. Rights to water from an artificial stream

Landowners entered into contracts to get around these limitations. But these contract rights generally did NOT bind/benefit subsequent parties. Had to be in PRIVITY OF CONTRACT, or parties to original contract, to be bound or entitled to enforce its benefits. So real covenants developed to solve the problem that negative easements didn‚Äôt run with the land.

**Real Covenants**: Agreement could bind successors to land if it was. (\*note that most jurisdictions have tacked on notice req here as well)

1. In **writing**
   * Complying with Statute of Frauds. And like all transfers of property interests lasting more than a year, the covenant must be created in writing signed by the grantor.
     + A covenant written in a deed satisfies this requirement.
2. **Intended** to bind future tenants/run with land
   * Intent may be established through language declaring the covenant is ‚Äúon behalf of owner and his successors or assigns‚Äù, or that the covenant ‚Äúruns with the land‚Äù
   * If the covenant is appurtenant, courts presume the grantor intended it to run with the land (*Sun Oil Co. v. Trent Auto Wash, Inc.*)
   * Some courts require clear evidence of intent to run with land (*Charping v. J.P. Scurry* & *Tennsco Corp. v. Attea*)s
3. **Touched and concerned** the land (affected use of the land itself) (ONLY SUBSTANTIVE REQUIREMENT of the 4/5)
   * Intended to identify the kinds of obligations that should attach to land as opposed to individuals
   * Benefits the dominant estate and burdens the servient estate. On the burden side, covenant must affect the use and enjoyment of the land. On the benefit side, an obligation touches and concerns a dominant estate if it improves enjoyment of the land OR increases its market value.
     + When NO land if benefited T&C can fall away
     + In *Neponset* court found that fees, which are usually contract law, were T&C b/c fees were used to maintain common element of community and preserving the value of the properties in the community
   * \*No clear test so rule of thumb: the covenant must affect the LEGAL RIGHTS of the parties as landowners
4. There was **privity of estate** (mutual ownership of the land/simultaneous interests in same land) between covenanting parties
   * **Instantaneous** **privity**: Finding privity if a covenant was created during the legal transfer of land. Fiction: at the moment the deed passes from seller to buyer, parties have a fleeting simultaneous interest in property.
     + Prevents restriction of real covenants to landlord-tenants, expands to seller-buyer.
   * **Horizontal** privity: between original covenanting parties
     + Common Examples of no privity: (1) common agreement between owners without a transaction; (2) after the fact privity.
     + If all the owners of properties get together and decide to do something, then there is NOT horizontal privity b/c there is NO sale or friction of a sale to bind the parties.
       - **No transaction, No privity**
   * **Vertical** privity: between original covenanting parties and succeeding owners
     + **Strict**: transfer of ownership. Grantor does NOT retain any future interests in the land. Outright sale.
     + **Relaxed**: lease. Allows covenants to run to all those assigned possession, tenants. Adopted by many courts. (*Whitinsville Plaza v. Kotseas*)
   * Modern trend is to do away with privity requirement but lots of states still have
5. Almost all court today now require notice as well (below in Equitable Servitudes)
   * **REMEMBER: Equitable Servitudes require the same thing, except that (1) it swaps privity for notice AND (2) the relief is injunctive, not damages.**

**Real Covenants v. Equitable Servitudes**:

* Real covenants require privity, equitable servitudes do NOT. ES require notice instead.
* Remedy for real Covenants is damages; remedy for ES is injunction.
* After WW2 many courts relaxed restrictions on covenants, and covenants and equitable servitudes law merged. Modified /abolished privity requirement, touch and concern requirement
* \*\*\*Some courts still adhere.

**Equitable Servitudes**: Same things as Covenants, except that (1) it swaps out privity for NOTICE of restriction AND (2) the relief is injunctive, not damages. Three types of notivce.

1. **Actual Notice**: Actually told about it, or otherwise made aware of it.
   1. *Neponsit*: Unclear if Emigrant was on actual notice when purchased property at foreclosure sale.
2. **Implied/Inquiry Notice**: if condition of the premises would make a reasonable purchaser inquire about the condition of the covenant
   1. Less likely to be found than inquiry notice for easement (right of way) b/c in easements its easier for buyer to observe (b/c they are positive things) while in ES/covenants they are negative non-actions which are harder to observe.
3. **Constructive**: if restriction was recorded within the registry of deeds and could be found via a reasonable search of the records prior to sale.
   1. Reasonable purchaser is expected to search the title to find out whether the property is burdened by land use restrictions
   2. *Neponsit*: Every deed in Emigrant‚Äôs chain of title provided that the conveyances were ‚Äúsubject to covenants and restrictions of former deeds of record‚Äù

**Third Restatement:** Tries to restructure covenants to make them enforceable unless against public policy. Drafters of restatement tries to clean up everything up and goes a bit too far. Most states are somewhere between traditional view and restatement.

1. Abolishes the distinction between negative easements, real covenants, and equitable servitudes
2. Abolish the horizontal privity requirement entirely, on the basis that **intent and notice requirements serve the interests of intent and notice more directly**
   1. Still required in *Cunningham v. Greensboro (N.C. 2011)*
3. \*Abolish the T&C requirement, and instead place burden on party seeking non-enforcement to show that the covenant VIOLATES PUBLIC POLICY for reasons that include, but are not limited to‚Ä¶
   1. Arbitrary, spiteful, or capricious,
   2. Unreasonably burden a fundamental constitutional right
   3. Impose an unreasonable restraint on alienation
   4. Imposes an unreasonable restraint on trade or competition.
   5. Unconscionable.
4. \*\*But‚Ä¶elements of the old T&C remain:
   1. Covenants that have NOTHING to do with the land may be arbitrary
   2. Requires that **beneficiary of the covenant be identifiable + legitimate interest.**
5. Provides that covenants should be interpreted to further the intent of the parties and carry out purposes.

**Implied Reciprocal Negative Servitudes**

Problem: If a developer wants to restrict development for residential purposes, she may place covenants in each deed binding purchasers to restrict the property.

1. While purchasers get benefit of covenants earlier buyers are subjected to, those earlier buyers cannot enforce covenants on later buyers ‚Äì lack of privity. The **Doctrine of Implied Reciprocal Negative Servitudes** (IRNS) solves that problem
2. When an owner sells a number of parcels with **evidence of intent** to create a common plan or scheme of development, then 1) **covenants made to the seller benefit all parcels** within the plan, and 2) **all parcels within the plan are bound** by the covenants.
3. How to show **intent**:
   1. Statutes may require developers to file a **declaration** prior to selling individual lots, describing area to be covered.
      1. Declaration constitutes promise to constrict remaining lots when they are sold (**grantor covenant**)
      2. Buyers are on **constructive notice** of the restrictions in the declaration **if it is recorded prior to their purchase**. (*Riley v. Bear Creek*)
   2. **Observance by owners** of similar development (*Sanborn*)
   3. Intent to create a common plan may be inferred (*Sanborn*) based on number of lots in a subdivision that have a covenant.
      1. Owners are on **inquiry notice** to determine if there were restrictive covenants on neighboring lots that might be interpreted to create a plan to restrict an entire neighborhood (*Sanborn*)
   4. The presence of restrictions in all or most deeds in the area
   5. A recorded plat (map) showing the restrictions
   6. Presence of restrictions in the **last deed** (since common owner no remaining interest);
   7. **Language of deed itself** that says it runs with the land.

***Neponsit v. Emigrant Savings* (1938): TRADITIONAL TEST FOR REAL COVENANTS**

* Facts: In 1911, Neponsit Realty Company developed land as a residential community and sold the property to Deyers. The deeds contained a covenant requiring the purchaser and all successors to pay an annual fee to the Neponsit Property Owners‚Äô Association (POA) for maintaining common areas like roads. Covenant gave Neponsit/successors the right to foreclose a lien for failure to make payment, expressly stated that it was a real covenant running with the land.Emigrant bought the property at a foreclosure sale. Emigrant‚Äôs deed contained the covenant. Emigrant failed to pay the fee, POA sued to foreclose on the lien.
* Holding: POA may enforce the covenant. Generally, there must be privity of estate for a claimant to enforce a real covenant. There is technically no privity of estate between a property owner and a homeowners‚Äô association, but based on equitable principles, restrictive covenants have been enforced without privity of estate if the covenant was entered into to benefit the claimant. This case does not involve a restrictive covenant, but the court may look behind the corporate form of the POA. The covenant provided that the POA would be the assignee of the benefit of the covenant. The POA is merely acting as a ‚Äúmedium through which enjoyment of [the owners'] common right may be preserved.‚Äù There is privity of estate between the property owners and the POA ‚Äúin substance, if not in form.‚Äù
* Importance: Also gives opportunity to examine what equitable servitudes would look like. Case occurs when ppl are starting to find defeasible fees unappealing.
* Rule: (1) A covenant contained in a deed requiring the payment of money ‚Äútouches and concerns‚Äù the land if it substantially affects the rights of the parties as landowners. (2) Privity of estate will exist in substance if not in form between property owners and an owners‚Äô association when the **association is acting as a medium through which enjoyment of a common right is preserved**.

Notes:

* \*\*Once Neponset sold all the land, then they do not have right to enforce the rights b/c they are not involved in the land anymore ‚Äì no interest in the land.
  + There are 2 exceptions: (1) government and (2) charities
* **Applying Privity Requirement**
  + **Horizontal privity** between Neponset and Dyers ‚Äì instantaneous privity (new and adapted form of privity).
  + **Vertical Privity:** The original covenanting parties transferred their interest to the subsequent possessors of the parcel.
    - In this case, Dyers transferring land to other party is vertical privity
    - No real vertical privity b/c Neponset realty never transferred any land to Neponset owner‚Äôs association. Therefore, under the old regime, there would NOT be vertical privity to the owner‚Äôs association.
    - This court gets around this by saying that we will treat Neponset Owner Association as representatives of the property owners.
  + Vertical privity is being applied in a relaxed modern form. This leads to modern American covenants and organizational systems for communities.
  + \*\*Won‚Äôt do ‚Äúblind adherence to ancient formula‚Äù
* **Touch and Concern: ‚ÄúRealism‚Äù** In this case, the Court says that payment of fee, while not directly effecting the land, has ‚Äúa burden upon an interest in land, which on the other hand increases the value of a different interest in the same or related land.‚Äù
  + Enjoyment is improved as well as the money is being used for beach and street upkeep which would also raise market value.

8. Whether changed circumstances since the covenant‚Äôs execution have made the covenant unreasonable.

***Northwest v. Serio* (Md. 1929): RULE AGAINST RESTRAINTS ON ALIENATION**

* Facts: Northwest conveyed land to the grantee in fee simple, with a provision that grantees could not sell the property for a certain period of time without Northwest‚Äôs consent. Subsequently, grantees contracted to sell the lot to Serios. When Northwest did not consent to the sale, Serios brought suit for specific performance of their sale agreement on the grounds that the grantor consent clause in the original deed was invalid.
  + Serio would argue that fee simple should grant them full ownership and determining who they can sell to is invasion of their rights
* Holding: Grantor consent clauses in deeds are generally void as invalid restraints against alienation.
  + The clause in the deed from Northwest that prohibited the grantees from selling the property for a certain period of time without Northwest‚Äôs consent is INVALID because it improperly restricts the transferability of the property.
* **Dissent**: The purpose of the grantor consent clause in the deed is to give Northwest the power to control the character of its development for a certain period of time. This is reasonable in light of developer‚Äôs interest in securing a return on its investment and the public‚Äôs interest in allowing venture capitalist developers such as Northwest to commonly develop a residential area.
* Rule: A clause in a fee simple deed requiring grantor consent to transfer the property is generally invalid as repugnant to the estate.

***Woodside Village v. Jahren* (Fla. 2002): RULE AGAINST RESTRAINTS ON ALIENATION**

* Facts: Woodside Village is a condo development governed by the Declaration of Condominium of Woodside Village. Declaration contained restrictions on leases, as well as a provision allowing amendment of Declaration, and was amended to further inhibit leases, limiting them to no more than 9 months. Condo owners in Woodside all purchased their condos subject to the Declaration and as a result, notice that the Declaration was subject to amendment. Year after the amendment passed, Jahren was engaged in a lease for more than nine months, but did NOT terminate it when notified that he was required to comply with amendment.
* Holding: The amendment is VALID and ENFORCEABLE, in line with historical treatment of condo restrictions. Jahren was on **notice that Declaration could be amended at any time** and that he would be subject to such amendments; the amendment is not arbitrary, furthers Woodside‚Äôs desire for stability in the community and continuity of residence.
* Concurrence: The majority was correct in its findings that the amendment is valid. However, the amendment has deprived Jahren of a strong benefit in his property that existed when he purchased it. The Legislature should consider placing restrictions on condominiums‚Äô ability to change the rights of existing unit owners by amending their bylaws.
* Rule: Amendments to a declaration of condominium are valid if they are reasonable and do not violate public policy or the condo owners‚Äô constitutional rights.
* Importance: Condos are essentially different from other forms of property (live in close proximity, common facilities, have form of governance that is essentially a ‚Äúdemocratic sub-society.‚Äù), and are thus generally entitled to a greater degree of control over the condo owners‚Äô property uses.

Notes: Legislature later overruled *Woodside* to say that amendments to a declaration do not restrain buyers who purchase condominium before the amendment passes

* Differentiating this from *Serio* ‚Äì In Serio there was a restriction on complete alienation, while here it is a limit on leasing.

***Shelley v. Kramer* (1948): RULE AGAINST RACIAL RESTRICTIONS**

* Facts: In 1911, 30 property owners on a Missouri street signed/recorded a restrictive covenant, providing that no races other than Caucasians allowed as tenants on property for next 50 yrs. In 1945, Shelleys (black) bought a house on one of the restricted parcels without knowledge of restrictive covenant. Kraemers + other white property owners brought suit to enforce covenant, seeking to enjoin Shelleys from taking possession and divest them of title.
  + Case was consolidated with similar Michigan case before Supreme Court, here determining whether Equal Protection Clause prohibits a state's courts from enforcing racially restrictive covenants.
* Holding: Agreements alone cannot be regarded as violation of 14th A rights because no state action. However, court enforcement of the covenant constituted a state action to deprive petitioners of 14th A rights. [The issue here is whether state court enforcement of racially restrictive covenants entered into by private actors constitutes state action. 14th A does not apply to purely private discrimination, so people are free to enter into/voluntarily abide by racially restrictive covenants. Nevertheless, the actions of state courts are state actions within meaning of 14th A. States may NOT make available the full coercive power of government to allow private individuals to deny the Shelleys‚Äô rights on the basis of race. Judicial enforcement of the racially restrictive covenants violated the Equal Protection Clause, so the rulings of the state courts are reversed.]
* Rule: State court enforcement of racially restrictive covenants constitutes state action, which violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment (which guarantees equal treatment of all people under the law, including in their exercise of various property rights). Restrictive covenants that discriminate solely on the basis of race would be invalid on equal protection grounds if created by a state or local law.

Notes:

* Similar to *Marsh v. Alabama* in that non-state actors are found to be violating state action in a sense.

***Village of Euclid v. Ambler* (1926): ESTABLISHES EUCLIDEAN ZONING AS CONSTITUTIONAL**

* Facts: Ambler Realty owned land in Village of Euclid, a largely residential suburb of Cleveland. In 1922, Euclid enacted comprehensive zoning ordinances (concerned that urban environment would keep expanding into suburb; suburb wants to preserve residential character) and created a board of zoning appeals charged with enforcement. The regulations created different districts based on the class of use, including purely residential, mixed use, commercial, and industrial and partitioned Amber‚Äôs land in terms of uses permissible on it (notably, portions zoned prohibiting development of industry.)
  + Ambler alleged the zoning ordinance violated the 14th Amendment and Ohio Constitution by **depriving Ambler of liberty and property without due process.**
  + Specifically, Ambler argued that the ordinance reduced the land‚Äôs value by 75% and deterred potential buyers.
* Holding: The zoning restriction scheme here may well prove to be unconstitutional as applied, but it is **constitutional on its face**.
  + J. Southerland‚Äôs looks to common law of nuisance as means to solve urban life. Can think of zoning as replacing the law of nuisance.
  + Court says that this is a VALID exercise of police power.
  + Court explains that important to have apt and houses separate for kids sake, but it is unclear why apt living is bad for kids.
* Rule: Municipal zoning regulations are constitutional (even when substantially diminishes property values and separates people with discriminatory effect), unless arbitrary and unreasonable with no substantial relation to public health, safety, morals or general welfare.
  + (Even thin rationale is sufficient; just claim the measure somehow benefits community policy goal.)
* Euclid is the landmark case in zoning law which determines that zoning ordinances will be given deference by the court.

***Village of Belle Terre v. Borass* (1974): SINGLE-FAMILY ZONING CONSTITUTIONAL; MARSHALL DISSENT**

* Facts: Dickmans owned a house in Belle Terre, leased to 6 students of nearby university. Belle Terre had zoning ordinance which restricted land use to one-family dwellings.
  + Defines ‚Äúfamily‚Äù being defined as persons related by blood, adoption, or marriage, or two unmarried cohabitating people.
  + Belle Terre cited violation of this ordinance b/c the students were not a ‚Äúfamily‚Äù (none related by blood, adoption, marriage). Dickmans and tenants brought suit alleging that the ordinance violated their equal protection rights.
  + Whole slew of complaints: interfering with right to travel, right to privacy, making unnecessary distinction between married v. unmarried, and there is no rational basis.
* Holding, Douglas: Ordinance is constitutionally permissible.
* \*\*No constitutionally-protected interest (fundamental right) was implicated by the regulation and rational basis test applies.
  + The ordinance does NOT prohibit racial minorities from living within the city. The ordinance also does not burden fundamental rights such as the right to vote, the right of association, the right of access to the courts, or any right of privacy.
  + The definition of ‚Äúfamily‚Äù is an exercise of **legislative discretion** and is outside the realm of judicial second-guessing.
* *Euclid*: Violates 14th Amendment only if unreasonable + arbitrary. Ordinance here not arbitrary b/c was designed to reduce noises/traffic, provide quiet/open spaces for children to play.
* Dissent, Marshall: **Minority Rule:** ‚ÄúIt can withstand constitutional scrutiny only upon a clear showing that the burden imposed is necessary to protect a compelling and substantial government interest.‚Äù
  + If town could achieve same goal via ordinance that does NOT discriminate constitutionally protected lifestyle choices then unconstitutional.
  + Village‚Äôs ordinance violates freedom of association (right to establish a home under 14th Amendment) + right to privacy (discriminates on the basis of personal lifestyle) ü°™ both fundamental rights
  + Ordinance is NOT narrowly tailored. The burden imposed by the ordinance is both overinclusive and underinclusive with respect to stated goals (controlling population density; preventing noise, traffic, and parking problems; and maintaining reasonable rental controls): Permits a ‚Äúfamily‚Äù of twenty to live in one house so long as they were all related by blood, marriage, or adoption, but would not permit a much smaller group of persons to share a house simply because they are not so related.
* Importance: Fundamental right test for zoning constitutionality in Marshall Dissent. Majority maintains deferential *Euclid* standard.
  + Some state courts have held that their State constitutions prevents barring definitions for families (*Dinolfo*)

***Moore v. City of East Cleveland* (1977): FUNDAMENTAL RIGHT TEST FOR CONSTITUTIONALITY**

* Facts: City of East Cleveland enacted a housing ordinance that limited the occupancy of a dwelling unit to members of a single family. The ordinance narrowly defined the term ‚Äúfamily‚Äù as encompassing only a few categories of related individuals. Moore lived in a home with her son and two grandsons, outside housing ordinance‚Äôs legal definition of ‚Äúfamily.‚Äù Moore refused to modify her living arrangement and was charged with criminal penalties, convicted, and sentenced to a fine and five days in jail.
* Holding, Powell (plurality): ***Belle Terre* Test:** Ordinance violates fundamental right. Legitimate government interests here, but there are less restrictive means that gov could use, so ordinance NOT necessary.
* CEC argued that its housing ordinance should be sustained based on *Belle Terre.* However, this case is distinguishable from *Belle Terre* b/c the CEC ordinance limits blood relatives from living together (breaking up the sanctity of a family).
  + Intrusive regulation of family that **examined under strict scrutiny**.
* Govt objectives (preventing overcrowding, minimizing traffic, not financially burdening school system) only marginally served by ordinance, which is NOT necessary to accomplishment
* Brennan/Marshall: Talks about how this ordinance implicates racial injustices b/c ordinance is imputing a model for suburb living which are not as common amongst black families.
* **Dissent,** Stewart/Rehnquist: The ordinance should be upheld as a constitutional restriction on a NON-fundamental right.
* **Dissen**t, White: More deferential, like *Euclid.* Ordinance did not violate fundamental liberty interests, b/c does not totally foreclose the possibility of this family living arrangement.
  + Still permits her to live with grandchildren elsewhere in Cleveland metropolitan area.
* Rule: The right of related family members to live together is protected by the Due Process Clause of the 14th Amendment, and necessarily encompasses a broader definition of ‚Äúfamily‚Äù than just members of the nuclear family.

### Pre-Existing Rights & Particularized Zoning

**CASES**: *Town of Belleville v. Parrillo‚Äôs Inc.* (N.J. 1980), *Stone v. City of Wilton* (Iowa, 1983), *Durand v. IDC Bellingham* (Mass. 2003)

***Town of Belleville v. Parrillo‚Äôs* (NJ 1980): PRIOR NONCONFORMING USE**

* Facts: Parrillo‚Äôs operated as a restaurant in Belleville, which subsequently enacted zoning ordinances itemizing permitted uses for each zone. Operating a restaurant was NOT permitted under the new zoning laws. But b/c operated as restaurant prior to enactment, permitted to continue because of its preexisting nonconforming use. However, some years later, Parrillo‚Äôs ceased its primary operation as a restaurant and began operating as a discotheque, which was also NOT permitted under the new zoning regulations. Fined for $250.
  + Belleville claims that disco is NOT substantially like restaurant
  + Parillo‚Äôs argument would be that the NJ statue does not say anything about the new use being ‚Äúsubstantially similar‚Äù to the old one.
* Holding: A substantial change CANNOT be upheld as a preexisting nonconforming use; needs to be substantially same kind of use. Presumption against allowing an existing nonconforming use to continue if there is doubt.
  + Here: several significant changes after switch to discotheque, including: a reduction in tables, a change in lighting, an addition of loud music, and dancing replacing eating as the primary activity.
  + Public Policy/general welfare of community wins out over autonomy of the owners ü°™ also, a nuisance here.
* Rule: An existing nonconforming use will be permitted to continue, ONLY IF it is a continuance of substantially the same kind devoted at the time of the passage of the zoning ordinance.

Notes:

* Many states require nonconforming use to be phased out over time through **amortization**
* Under many zoning ordinances, if non-conforming use is abandoned or discontinued status is lost

***Stone v. City of Wilton* (Iowa, 1983): VESTED RIGHTS CLAIMS**

* Facts: Stones bought an undeveloped parcel of land with plans to build a multi-family housing project. They paid architecture and engineering fees and secured a loan for construction. Before the start of construction, City of Wilton rezoned the land to only permit single-family residences b/c of insufficient sewage, water, and electric services. Seek invalidation of rezoning their property.
* Holding: For a property owner to have a vested right in a zoning classification, the owner must have made substantial efforts under the classification toward accomplishing the proposed project. Stones‚Äô efforts too preliminary, not so substantial as to create vested rights.
  + Landowner paying for architectural and engineering services and devising a construction plan do NOT constitute a substantial enough effort.
  + Weaknesses: maybe too much deference here? Timing suspicious?

**Rezoning**:

* Many states require rezoning to accord with comprehensive plan and for decisions to be made with long-range view
* Some states don‚Äôt require consistency

***Durand v. IDC Bellingham* (Mass. 2003): CONTRACT ZONING CLAIMS**

* Facts: Town wanted to increase its property tax base by industrializing an area. Picked area zoned for agricultural use that was next to an area zoned for industrial use as the place they should change. IDC Bellingham wanted to build power plant there and needed town to rezone, so agreed to make an **$8 million gift** to town if it voted to rezone. Town voted in favor of rezoning. Residents who owned nearby land sued claimed rezoning was void b/c it constituted **illegal contract zoning**.
* Holding: When someone offers money in exchange for the promise to vote for rezoning, it‚Äôs considered contract zoning which is invalid, but here NO promise was made contingent upon the money where the board promised to vote one way or the other. Thus, b/c the agreement does NOT invalidate the vote & the rezoning was valid (not arbitrary or unreasonable), does not violate any law.
  + Possible issue with majority is that it opens up the possibility for corruption.
  + Strong presumption of validity
* Dissent: The agreement between IDC and Bellingham constitutes illegal contract zoning. The town agreed to exercise its police power to rezone the parcel of land in exchange for a promise of $8 million. This payment was unrelated to any aspect of the proposed development and therefore was not a lawful basis for the town‚Äôs vote.
  + **Doctrine of extraneous conditions:** IF the offer is NOT related to (1) ANY aspect of the proposed development OR (2) any public need arising out of the zoning change, then NOT a legitimate basis for the vote.
* Rule: An agreement including a voluntary offer of public benefits in exchange for a vote in favor of rezoning a parcel of land does NOT, standing alone, invalidate the legislative decision by the town to rezone if the agreement was consummated after the vote took place. A zoning by law will NOT be held invalid unless there is a demonstration by preponderance of evidence that the zoning regulation is arbitrary or unreasonable. The motives for granting a zoning bylaw does not affect the analysis of whether a zoning bylaw is valid.

***Kelo v. New London* (2005): EMINENT DOMAIN**

* Facts: New London, Ct. approved a new development project that involved using its eminent domain authority to seize private property to sell to private developers.   + Kelo and nine other private owners claimed violated the ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù requirement of Fifth Amendment b/c economic development is NOT a ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù
  + Precedent for P claim: bright line rule that you can‚Äôt transfer property from one private owner to another private owner.
    - Other policy arguments for bright line rule:
* Holding: The city MAY validly exercise its eminent domain authority to take private property and distribute it to private developers without violating the Fifth Amendment's public use requirement. The area was sufficiently distressed to justify program of economic rejuvenation. State legislative judgments about the prudence of programs providing for the public good in these precedent cases are entitled to great deference from the judicial branch.
  + Emphasize the downstream benefits as well as the fact that it is hard to draw a clear line between public and private
  + Court cites three precedent for the fact that literal public use is NOT required and that PUBLIC PURPUSE counts as well:

    - *Midkiff* (1984 Hawaii): make ownership broader and reduce land oligopoly)

  + Although courts should be deferential to legislative judgments, they should still engage in a sufficient factual inquiry to determine that eminent domain programs are not being used to unfairly advantage one private group over another in violation of Equal Protection Clause.

* Dissent (Thomas): The protection of private property outweighed public necessity in the minds of the Framers, as they drafted significant legal protections of private property directly into the Constitution.
* Dissent (O‚ÄôConnor): Economic development takings are unconstitutional.

  + Reads these precedent cases as being limited to remedying a harm
* Rule: A state‚Äôs use of eminent domain to condemn property from private individuals and redistribute it to other private individuals constitutes a ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù under the Fifth Amendment if it is rationally related to a conceivable public purpose, including an exclusively economic one.

***Penn Coal v. Mahon* (1922): HISTORICAL ORIGINS of REGULATION TAKING**

* Facts: In 1878, Penn Coal Co. conveyed the surface of a plot of land it owned to Mahon. Retained the right to mine underneath the property, and an explicit provision in the deed stated that Mahon was taking the land subject to any risks associated with mining beneath it. In 1921, Pennsylvania enacted Kohler Act preventing coal mining that could possibly affect the integrity of any surface land. Mahon then sued Penn Coal, arguing that the new state law barred Pennsylvania Coal from mining under the property
* Holding: Permitted to continue its mining operation, b/c Mahon took the land subject to the risks associated with mining. Overregulation would be a taking.
  + Differentiate *Plymouth Coal*, b/c in that case there was a benefit to the coal companies, but here there is none ü°™ **reciprocity factor** ü°™ govt. doesn‚Äôt need to pay you if you are getting value from the new regulation.
  + Note on reciprocity ‚Äì don‚Äôt need to have a reciprocity to justify taking when there is a real harm
* Dissent (Brandeis): The right of an owner to use his land as he sees fit is NOT absolute. The use of the police power will always interfere with one‚Äôs right to use his land however they see fit. However, a statute intended to protect the public is a valid use of the police power. Even though in this case, only one member of the public is involved, the statute still aims to protect the public at large. Therefore, the statute should not be considered a taking, but a valid use of the police power.
* Importance: Mahon Factors For Determining if Taking has Gone ‚ÄúToo Far‚Äù:
  + (a) Extent of the diminution of the value of the property,
  + (b) Nature of the public interest served by the legislation
* Rule: At some point regulation of ones land by the government is considered a taking.

***Penn Central v. NYC* (1978): AD HOC TEST**

* Facts: NYC passes preservation law designating Grand Central Terminal a landmark. Owners want to build a 50-story office building above the station, and enter into long-term lease with occupant. But law imposes duty on owner to maintain exterior features of a landmark, unless Landmarks Preservation Commission allows.
  + NYC would argue that there is a reciprocity here (like *Plymouth Coal*) and therefore should NOT need to pay
  + Penn Central argument: The regulations are somewhat arbitrary and the amount of harm is quite great ü°™ complete destruction of rights above ground.
* Holding: The law is NOT a taking. When determining whether a state regulation constitutes a taking of private property for public use with the requirement of just compensation under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, courts should consider:

1. Economic impact of the regulation on the owner
   * 1. The economic impact of the law on Penn Central does NOT constitute a total diminution of the value of its property, as it can still generate revenue from renting out portions of Grand Central Terminal. It is merely prohibited from gaining additional revenue from leasing the airspace rights above the building.
2. Interference with the owner‚Äôs reasonable investment-backed expectations
   * 1. Revenue from developed airspace was not an option when Penn Central first invested in the property.
3. The character of the government action
   * 1. Whether govt. is signaling out someone AND if they‚Äôre physically doing something

* Dissent (Rehnquist): The law‚Äôs landmarks designation is problematic b/c it imposes significant costs/restrictions on landowners, without affording many benefits beyond honor of designation. Cost of preserving landmarks should be borne by all NYC taxpayers ‚Äì should be taking.
* Importance: Precedent decisions have been essentially ad hoc, factual inquiries based entirely on the facts of an individual case, with no precise standard articulated for when principles of fairness and justice require the payment of just compensation for a taking. In response to its frustration over this lack of standards, a new multi-factor test is articulated for determining when a taking requires the payment of just compensation to a property owner.
* Rule: In determining whether a state regulation constitutes a taking under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, courts should consider (1) the economic impact of the regulation on the owner, (2) the extent to which the regulation has interfered with the owner‚Äôs reasonable investment-backed expectations, and (3) the character of the government action involved in the regulation.

Penn Central‚Äôs overall assertion that any economic restriction imposed on the use of their property under the Landmarks Preservation law constitutes a taking which requires just compensation is rejected.

* The economic impact of the law on Penn Central does not constitute a total diminution of the value of its property, as it can still generate revenue from renting out portions of Grand Central Terminal.
* It is merely prohibited from gaining additional revenue from leasing the airspace rights above the building.
* Penn Central‚Äôs investment-backed expectations are not significantly impaired by the regulation, as the revenue from developed airspace was not an option when Penn Central first invested in the property.
* Finally the governmental invasion caused by the regulation is not physical (an invasion which is almost always upheld as a taking.) Rather, the government ‚Äúinvasion‚Äù in the present case is merely a prohibition on further development of Penn Central‚Äôs property.
* Additionally, a significant public interest in light space was furthered by regulation which prohibits the development of airspace above the terminal.

***Loretto v. Teleprompter* (1982): PERMANENT PHYSICAL INVASIONS**
