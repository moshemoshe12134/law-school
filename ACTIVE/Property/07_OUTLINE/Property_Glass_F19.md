**A Starting Point: A Fox on a Beach**

***State v. Shack* (NJ 1971)*‚Äì* p.4 ‚Äì** this case illustrates what the defining characteristics of property law are. ‚ÄúProperty rights serve human values. They are recognized to that end, and are limited by it.‚Äù

* **Facts:** Defendants entered private property to talk to farm workers about their rights.
* **Holding:** Court declined to charge for trespassing. Balance of rights of owner and rights of individual. Some rights are too fundamental to be denied on the basis of an interest in real property and too fragile to be left to the unequal bargaining strength of the parties.
* **Reasoning**: Not going to reach constitutional arguments (1st Amendment, Preemption). Property must serve human values. Migrant workers are rootless and isolated and don‚Äôt know their own rights. Government set out to provide them assistance. Communication is key to that assistance. Property rights are limited. Over time, we recognize a shift from pure individualism to increasing public needs (industrialization, complex societies) Not about Tenants rights. Property rights cannot allow a landowner to deny the migrant worker practical access to those things he needs or that are fundamental to being a citizen.
* **Takeaways:**
  + ‚ÄúProperty rights serve human values. They are recognized to that end, and are limited by it.‚Äù
  + Right to exclude is not absolute.

***Pierson v. Post* (NY 1805) ‚Äì p.136 ‚Äì** rule of first possession

* **Facts:** Post, the plaintiff in this case, was hunting a fox using hounds on public property, jointly owned by the members of the town. Pierson, knowing that the fox was being chased by Post, killed it first, and took the fox home
  + The trial court found for Post; Pierson appealed.
* **Issue:** Whether Post‚Äôs pursuit entitled him to the right to kill the fox?
  + Better stated: what acts amount to possession?
* **Holding:** Because ancient legal texts entitle the killer to the spoils of the hunt, and because no contrary principles are at play, Pierson is entitled to the animal.
  + Mere pursuit is not enough.
  + **Rule of first possession**
* **Reasoning:**
  + Historical legal texts say that only physical possession of a wild animal constitutes possession.
    - Because Post did not possess the animal, he is not entitled to its ownership.
  + Other principles may entitle him to ownership: (if he wounded the animal, then continued his pursuit OR if the fox were on his land), but this isn‚Äôt the case here.
  + Certainty/administrability
  + **Counterarguments:**
    - **Locke** ‚Äì If you invest labor into acquiring something, it should be yours. Because you own your own body and your own labor ‚Äì so you should own the fruits of your labor.
* **Dissent:**
  + This should never have been decided by a court ‚Äì rather it should have been adjudicated by arbitration.
  + **Efficiency:** The decision discourages people from investing in the pursuit if anyone could pick off the spoils: if the hunter expended resources, they should be entitled to spoils.
  + **Hot** pursuit should establish property rights over the animal.
* **Notes:**
  + Given the common law requirement to have control over one's possessions, merely giving chase was not sufficient. Something more was needed, otherwise law would create a slippery slope. Among the authorities cited by the court in its opinion were the works of William Blackstone, Fleta, Jean Barbeyrac, Samuel von Pufendorf, Hugo Grotius, and Justinian I.

***Popov v. Hayashi* (CA 2002) ‚Äì** p.142 ‚Äì personhood, illustration of what property can mean to individuals, establishing a pre-possessory interest

* **Facts**: Barry Bonds hit his 73rd home-run of the season on October 7, 2001. The ball was the property of MLB, then became abandoned property. The Ball landed in Popov‚Äôs mitt. Popov was tackled by a mob, assaulted by them. Hayashi was also pushed down by the mob, found the ball and picked it up.
* **Legal question**: Who has ownership of an item when one acquired it legally, but lost it due to the criminal act of another third party, allowing the other person to acquire the item legally?
* **Holding**: ‚ÄúBoth men have a superior claim to the ball as against all the world. Each man has a claim of equal dignity as to the other.‚Äù
* **Reasoning**: **Fundamental fairness** to both men. Baseball fans are accustomed to dominion and control establishing possession. Popov. established a pre-possessory interest that was impinged by the illegal actions of the mob. But Hayashi did nothing wrong, was also a victim of the mob. He just happened to find the ball and take possession of it.
  + **Personhood** as another factor when thinking about property - **Radin**

**Theories of Property**

Locke (Labor Theory of Property)

* Two key premises
  + Self-ownership (you own yourself and your labor)
  + Original common endowment of resources (everything in the world belongs to everyone)
* In order to own something, you need to mix your labor with it.
* Note: the theory is derived from the larger idea of natural rights

Bentham & Marshall (Positivist Theory of Property)

* Bentham
  + ‚ÄúProperty and law are born together, and die together. Before laws were made there was no property; take away laws, and property ceases.‚Äù
  + Property rights are what the law says are property rights
* Marshall
  + The Antelope: according to natural rights, slavery is wrong, but we see all these laws saying that slavery is okay ü°™ positive law supersedes natural law

Harris: *Whiteness as Property*

* ‚ÄúNot all first possession or labor gave rise to property rights; rather the rules of first possession and labor as a basis for **property rights were qualified by race**. This fact infused whiteness with significance and value because it was solely through being white that property could be acquired and secured under law.‚Äù
* American system of slavery
  + All African people are slaves = being black makes you a slave
  + No white people are slaves = being white makes you free
    - Whiteness functions as a form of property that protects you yourself from the worst form of dehumanization, that is, being ‚Äúproperitized‚Äù
* Taking of Native American land
  + Whiteness is a form of property because the only kind of first possession that matters for staking claim to the land is the kind of possession that is culturally white‚Äîthat is, modifying the land rather than leaving it in its natural state

Demsetz, Ostrom, Rose (Utilitarian Theory of Property)

* Social Utility: we should value the property rights that do the most good for the most people
* Forward looking; based on incentives
* Demsetz (Tragedy of the Commons)
  + The primary function of property rights is to internalize externalities
    - Why? Tragedy of the Free Rider: but for property protections there won‚Äôt be incentives for people to engage in work
  + Solution: either give everyone a private property right, or make the public good subject to government regulation
* Ostrom (Myth of the Tragedy of the Commons)
  + You don‚Äôt always have a free rider
  + You don‚Äôt always need a robust private property right
    - Private actors faced with the challenges of under-consumption or over-production will come together to find a co-operative solution
* Rose (Comedy of the Commons)
  + ‚ÄúUsed in commerce, some property had qualities akin to infinite ‚Äúreturns to scale.‚Äù Thus here, the commons was not tragic, but comedic, in the classical sense of a story with a happy outcome. And customary doctrines suggest that commerce might be thought a ‚Äúcomedy of the commons‚Äù not only because it may infinitely expand our wealth, but also, at least in part, because it has been thought to enhance the sociability of the members of an otherwise atomized society.‚Äù

Radin (Personhood Theory of Property)

* There is intrinsic value in certain objects; these objects cannot be commodified
* **Radin**
  + Types of goods: fungible v. personal
    - Fungible: ‚ÄúProperty that is held instrumentally‚Äù; replaceable, not unique, indifference to existence if net worth unchanged; *alienable*
    - Personal: ‚ÄúProperty that is bound up with a person‚Äù; unique, irreplaceable, not held for exchange, *inalienable*
  + Three key questions:
    - Why are you holding on to a good? (for exchange or for sentimental value)
    - Can the good be replaced? What would happen if you couldn‚Äôt replace it?
    - Is the good an external or internal good?

**Claiming Land**

***Johnson v. M‚ÄôIntosh* (1823)** ‚Äì p.88 ‚Äì acquiring property rights in land, doctrine of discovery

* **Facts**: Johnson claimed the land by purchase from the Piankeshaw Indians. M‚ÄôIntosh claimed the land because he had been granted a title to it by the U.S. government, tracing chain of title back to the King of England deeding the land to Virginia.
* **Issue:** Whether plaintiffs' title to land, conveyed by a Native American tribe, was superior to defendants' title, conveyed by the United States government.
* **Holding**: Johnson does not have title to the land that can be sustained by the court of the U.S.
* **Reasoning**: Under the **doctrine of discovery**, the first European to see the land possesses the exclusive right of acquiring title to it vis-a-vis any other European, subject only to the Indian right of occupancy.
  + *Positivism*: property rights are what the law says are property rights: ‚ÄúConquest gives a title which the courts of conqueror cannot deny‚Ä¶not for the Courts of this county to question the validity of this title" ‚Äì Marshall.
  + *Sovereignty:* Property is the protection of expectations recognized by the sovereign government (**Bentham**: you have to look to what the law says)

**Claiming People**

***Dred Scott v. Sanford (U.S. 1857)* ‚Äì p.248**

* **Facts:** Dred Scott was an enslaved person in Missouri. From 1833 to 1843, he resided in Illinois (a free state) and in the Louisiana Territory, where slavery was forbidden by the Missouri Compromise of 1820. After returning to Missouri, Scott filed suit in Missouri court for his freedom, claiming that his residence in free territory made him a free man. After losing, Scott brought a new suit in federal court.
* **Issues:** What is the status of Scott? Is he a ‚Äúcitizen‚Äù and therefore entitled to sue in a U.S. court?
* **Holding**: No. African-Americans ‚Äúwhose ancestors were imported into [the U.S.], and sold as slaves,‚Äù whether enslaved or free could not be an American citizen and therefore did not have standing to sue in federal court. The Missouri Compromise was unconstitutional. Congress cannot free enslaved people who flee into the territories. Enslaved people are property under the 5th Amendment, so a law depriving slave owners of their ‚Äúproperty‚Äù is unconstitutional.
* **Reasoning:** Justice Taney uses history and the textual provisions of key documents to argue that Scott should not be recognized as a citizen. Concept of whiteness=property rights and blackness=status as property was so deeply entrenched in society at the time (Harris). Positive law in place that reinforces system of slavery. Sovereignty (Bentham) ‚Äì the law is what the government who is in charge says it is.
  + **Counterarguments**: Locke ‚Äì you own your body and you own the fruits of your labor.

***Wilcox v. Stroup (4th Cir. 2006)* ‚Äì p.151, finder v. claimed true owner**

* **Facts:** W (finder) tries to sell the papers; S attains a temporary restraining order from the state
* **Issue:** In the absence of clear chain of title, does W have title to the papers?

***Charrier v. Bell (La. 1986)* ‚Äì p.158, finder v. landowner [Charrier v. Tunica-Biloxi, finder v. claimed true owner]**

* **Facts:** amateur archeologist (Charrier) uncovered tribal burial ground on private land and removed 2.5 tons of artifacts from it without permission of the landowner. He attempted to sell artifacts, but buyers were concerned that it he wasn‚Äôt the actual owner. He filed a suit against the Trudeau Plantation to get a declaratory judgment that he was the owner of the artifacts so he could proceed with sales.
* **Issue:** Does the burial of artifacts or other objects with the deceased result in abandonment of those artifacts?
* **Holding:** The finder (archaeologist) does not have a property interest in the artifacts. Burying objects with deceased is not equivalent to abandonment or relinquishment of ownership because they were placed there for customary, religious or spiritual belief. (In the circumstances and context ‚Üí not abandonment)
  + Rejected his claim of unjust enrichment (could not show a resulting impoverishment because the majority of the excavation occurred while he had knowledge that he did not have consent to be on the land.)
* **Reasoning:** The fact that items were buried with the deceased due to some customary, religious, or spiritual belief does not result in a conclusion that the goods were abandoned because it is not intended as a means of relinquishing ownership to a stranger. Unjust enrichment does not apply.
* **Rule:** In the absence of a perfect chain of title, a federally recognized tribe can establish ownership over excavated burial goods when, as here,
  + the tribe claiming ownership is an accumulation of the descendants of the original owners (p.160); and
  + the original owners‚Äô intention in interring the objects was not to abandon the good, but rather, for the burial goods to remain there perpetually (p.161)

***Armory v. Delamirie (K.B, 1722)* ‚Äì p.156** ‚Äì **finder v. subsequent possessor**

* **Facts:** Chimney sweeper‚Äôs boy found a jewel and carried it to defendant‚Äôs shop, who was goldsmith. Gave it to goldsmith, master offered boy money, who asked for jewel back instead. Apprentice gave him back socket without stones. Boy files suit in **trover** (damages for wrongful interference with personal property)
* **Issue:** Who owned the jewel or had a right to it?
* **Holding:** Boy, though he does not have absolute property or ownership, has superior claim against all but the rightful owner.
  + Relativity of title = finder can have title that is good against everyone except the true owner
* **Justifications:**
  + ***Administrability:***Easy to determine who found property.
  + ***Efficient:***Holding that jeweler could obtain property would create incentive to steal. Holding that finder may own incentivizes people to look for lost items and to take greater care of these items.
* Note: Rule would not hold if chimney sweeper‚Äôs boy was a trespasser.

***INS v. AP (U.S. 1918)* ‚Äì p.112 ‚Äì** creating property rights in information

* **Facts:** AP is upset about the news‚Äîthe historical fact‚Äîthat they‚Äôre claiming INS is taking from them. Harvesting this news requires significant effort & expense (analogy to Livingston dissent in *Pierson*).
  + World of tangible property rules doesn‚Äôt fit as neatly here.
  + Analogy to *Johnson v. M‚ÄôIntosh*? Doctrine of discovery ‚Äì designed to resolve competition between parties for some kind of resource.
    - BUT incentives are tricky and different here: we don‚Äôt want to build a monopoly in the area of information. Also, you can have many ‚Äúdiscoverers‚Äù of news‚Äîmakes it hard to administer this rule.
  + News cannot be copyrighted! Common law copyright doesn‚Äôt help us either, because the news has already been published.
* **Arguments**:
  + AP relies on Demsetz theory of ‚Äúthe tragedy of the commons‚Äù ‚Äì creating individual property rights will promote more efficient use of that property. You need property rights to incentivize journalists
    - Counteragument: Ostrom: Collaboration/collective action is good; you don‚Äôt need an individual property right, just a kind of quasi-property right. Let people create their own self-governing principles
      * Clearly defined boundaries should be in place.
      * Rules in use are well matched to local needs and conditions.
      * Individuals affected by these rules can usually participate in modifying the rules.
      * The right of community members to devise their own rules is respected by external authorities.
      * A system for self-monitoring members' behavior has been established.
      * A graduated system of sanctions is available.
      * Community members have access to low-cost conflict-resolution mechanisms.
      * Nested enterprises-that is, appropriation, provision, monitoring and sanctioning, conflict resolution, and other governance activities-are organized in a nested structure with multiple layers of activities.
  + INS arguments: AP has published the news‚Äîno common-law copyright
  + AP is only seeking a right to exclude their competitors ‚Äì a type of quasi-property
* **Holding:** No property right in the news, but AP has a quasi-property right.
  + Doctrine of misappropriation: There has to be some level of **labor and investment**. The property needs to have some **value** ‚Äì someone wants to buy it.
  + This quasi-property right disappears as soon as the information ceases to be valuable.
* **Holmes dissent**: This is more a question about fraud ‚Äì potential solution is for INS to attribute AP as source of news. Also, property does not arise from value ü°™ it arises from the law (Bentham). Labor is also not enough to get you a property right.
* **Brandeis dissent:** The general rule of law is that once something is published, it‚Äôs free for common use. The courts shouldn‚Äôt just invent a new property right ‚Äì there should be a separation of powers between judiciary and legislature. It‚Äôs not the court‚Äôs role to do this.

**JUSTIFICATIONS FOR COPYRIGHT PROTECTIONS**

* US Constitution: to promote the progress of science and arts
* Utilitarian/economic theories ‚Äì without protection, people would not have the incentive to create and innovate.
  + Public goods ‚Äì there are some goods where it‚Äôs impossible to exclude others from using it, and one‚Äôs consumption of it does not deplete the resources for other people
* Locke ‚Äì people should enjoy the fruits of their own labor ‚Äì ‚Äúsweat of the brow‚Äù
* Hegel/Radin ‚Äì personhood ‚Äì the creator‚Äôs identity is wrapped up with their creation, and we should protect these creators and their investments. These are non-fungible goods, highly creative/original works, and are more about the creative energy of the artist.

**SUMMARY: REQUIREMENTS FOR COPYRIGHT PROTECTIONS**

* **Originality (*Feist):*** the work must be an independent creation of the author and must demonstrate at least some minimal degree of creativity; cannot copyright facts‚Äîconsumers need facts and can produce them without copyright; proxy for added value for Congress‚Äînot always line up, such as shitty art v. phonebook; has *fair use exception*. ‚Äì originality, not labor is the basis of copyright.
* **Work of authorship:** Federal statute identifies eight of such works: literary works (including computer programs); musical works; dramatic works; pantomimes and choreographic works; pictorial, graphic, and sculptural works; motion pictures and other audiovisual works; sound recordings; and architectural works.
  + Not included: any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery.
  + Law protects expressions, not ideas.
* **Fixation:** the work must be fixed in some kind of tangible medium.

***Feist v. Rural Telephone Services (U.S. 1991)* ‚Äì p.189 ‚Äì** Copyright Act Section 102(a) and relationship to (b); originality requirement

* **Facts:** Rural Telephone is seeking copyright protection for its telephone book
* **Issue:** What constitutes an original work of authorship?
* **Holding:** Rural Telephone‚Äôs telephone book is not copyrighted because there was nothing original or creative about placing its subscribers‚Äô basic information in alphabetical order in the white pages.
* **Reasoning:**
  + Facts cannot be protected (see: *INS v. AP*); work of authorship is compilation of facts
  + There are 2 potential theories re: whether the compilation is original:
    - Sweat of the brow (Locke)
      * Good proxy for level of investment/time ‚Äì we should create the right incentives and protect endeavors where the upfront costs would be very high (utilitarian)
    - Creative selection ‚Äì there needs to be an arrangement, selection
      * There needs to be some modicum of creativity in the arrangement and selection of the compilation. Originality is the essential factor here.
* **Rule:** Originality Requirement: there are two requirements for something to be original to the owner
  + It must be independently created
  + It must possess some minimal degree of creativity

***Suntrust Bank v. Houghton Mifflin ‚Äì* p.195 ‚Äì** question of who gets to be an author? Fair use doctrine

* **Facts:** Suntrust has copyright in the classic *Gone with the Wind* (GWTW). Alice Randall writes a book titled T*he Wind Done Gone* (TWDG), which uses several characters, plots and scenes from GWTW. Randall claims that her book is a critique of GWTW‚Äôs depiction of slavery and the Civil-War era American South. Suntrust brings suit to enjoin Randall‚Äôs publisher from publishing her work.
* **Issue:** Whether a book that is intended to parody a copyrighted work by admittedly appropriating aspects (like characters, plot and scenes) can be enjoined from publication based on copyright violations.
* **Holding:** No, the book is entitled to a fair-use defense under the copyright statute.
* **Reasoning:**
  + **Threshold question:** Is this a work of parodic character?
    - **Rule:** a work will be treated as a parody if its aim is to comment upon or criticize a prior work by appropriating elements of the original in creating a new artistic, as opposed to scholarly or journalistic, work.
  + **Next question:** is the use a fair use of the copyright in light of the four factors listed in the statute?
    - Whether the purpose and character of the use is of a commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes;
      * Randall‚Äôs work is of commercial nature, but this prong weighs in favor of her because her work is ‚Äútransformative‚Äù (creating a new work told from a different perspective)
    - the nature of the copyrighted work;
      * Copyright protection affords greater protection to original works; but this prong is irrelevant here because Randall‚Äôs work is a parody, which invariably draws on publicly known work
    - the amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole;
    - the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work.
      * Randall‚Äôs work will not act as a market substitute for GWTW ‚Üí this prong is in favor of her

**Claiming Inventions: Patents and Biotechnology**

**PATENT LAW: IP STATUTES**

There are two forms of IP protected by law:

1. Copyright: ‚Äúoriginal works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression‚Äù. Exclusive right to use or authorize the use of work for a limited time ‚Äì dates back to Statute of Anne.    * Note that power to create and provide patents is provided to Congress by Article 1, Section 8 of the Constitution.

***A.M.P. v. Myriad Genetics* ‚Äì p.209** ‚Äì patenting human genetic material, interpreting & debating sec. 101

* **Facts**: Myriad discovered the breast cancer gene and got a patent giving them the exclusive right to isolate it (thereby giving them the exclusive right to test women for it and to conduct research on it) as well as a patent protecting cDNA made based off the DNA strand.
* **Rule:** Naturally occurring phenomenon are not patentable.
* **Holding**: BRCA1 and BRCA2 are naturally occurring molecules and are not patentable. cDNA is patentable because it is not naturally occurring but instead is produced by the lab technician based on the DNA.
* **Reasoning:**
  + **BRCA1 and BRCA2:** Naturally occurring, not created by Myriad ‚Äì it‚Äôs common property; Groundbreaking, innovative, or even brilliant discovery does not by itself satisfy the ¬ß101 inquiry; extensive effort alone is insufficient to satisfy the demands of ¬ß101. There‚Äôs a difference between man-made DNA and naturally-occurring matter
  + **cDNA**: a lab technician creates something when cDNA is made
  + **Counterarguments:**
    - Demsetz ‚Äì tragedy of the commons: people will stop inventing; without clear property rights, it would be hugely complicated to get permission to use those ideas to continue inventing
    - ‚ÄúSweat of the brow‚Äù

***Moore v. Regents ‚Äì* p.268** ‚Äì what are the rights of the donor to their body parts?

* **Facts:** Moore undergoes treatment for hairy-cell leukemia at UCLA Medical Center. Has spleen removed, blood drawn. Doctors manufacture cell line based on blood cells (lymphokines) for profitable research, without obtaining Moore‚Äôs consent.
  + Moore files two claims against doctors, UCLA Regents, Researchers, Genetis Institute, Pharma company Sandoz for: 1) Property Conversion, and 2) lack of informed consent/breach of fiduciary duty. Defendants file motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim.
  + Moore claims his cell line is his property. Cell lines are unique, like a human likeness, which is protected under privacy rights ‚Äì celebrities have proprietary interest in own likeness, and unauthorized business use of likeness is redressable as a tort.
  + Regents claim cell line not unique, value created through their labor (**Locke**), labor also created an expectation to gain advantage from ownership (**Bentham**). Cell lines being marketedare new creations in which they can have a patent right.
* **Rule**: There is no property interest/ownership in these discarded cells. He neither possesses them nor has title to them.
* **Holding (Panelli):** Moore‚Äôs complaint states a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty and lack of informed consent, but not for conversion
* **Reasoning:**
  + ***Fairness:*** Lymphokines, unlike name or face, have same molecular structure in every human being. **Not unique**.
  + ***Consistency with Existing Law:*** Statues already regulate tissue disposal limit control over excised cells for reasons of public health and safety. Such statutes already eliminate rights ordinarily attached to property.
  + ***Fairness/Labor:*** Patented cell line is factually and legally distinct from cells in Moore‚Äôs body. Federal law permits patenting organisms that are products of ‚Äúhuman ingenuity,‚Äù not discovery of naturally occurring organisms (the original cells in Moore‚Äôs body)
  + ***Social Utility*:** Risk of disincentivizing research by exposing researchers engaged in socially useful activity to liability ‚Äì thousands of lines already exist in tissue repositories; routinely copied and distributed.
  + ***Role of Courts/Institutional Competence:*** ‚ÄúIf scientific uses of human cells are to be held liable for failing to investigate the consensual pedigree of their raw materials, we believe the legislature should make that decision.‚Äù
* **Dissent (Mosk):**
  + ***bundle of rights:*** Property, a creation of law, is a ***bundle of rights*** that may be exercised with respect to an object ‚Äì **1) right to possess, 2) right to use, 3) right to exclude, 4) right to transfer.** Majority says because discarded tissue is regulated, there is no property interest. But it is possible to recognize some property interests and not others in a given object (contracts can limit transfer of real property, etc.). It is possible for Moore to still have a property interest of some sort in the cells.
  + ***Fairness:***  Cell line is primarily the work product of Regents, but Moore‚Äôs contribution is critical. Without his cell, the product wouldn‚Äôt exist. Regents were unjustly enriched, and equity demands compensation to Moore.

**Trespass and Defenses**

**Trespass Definition**

* An (**1) unprivileged, (2) intentional (3) intrusion on property** possessed by another.
  + *Intent*: doesn‚Äôt mean that you have to intend to trespass, just that you intended to be somewhere.
    - You are not trespassing if you are kidnapped; it is trespass if you unknowingly enter.
  + *Intrusion*: occurs the moment the non-owner enters the property
* Trespass is *Privileged* (not wrongful) if
  + (1) entry is done with the owner‚Äôs consent,
  + (2) entry is justified by necessity, or
  + (3) entry is encouraged by public policy.
    - The court defines public policy privilege on a case-by-case basis.
* NOTE: Right to exclude is among the core rights in the ‚Äúbundle of sticks.‚Äù Protecting expectations with respect to this right is an essential function of property law ‚Äì *Jacque v. Steenberg Homes*

### ***Commonwealth v. Magadini* (Mass. 2016) ‚Äì p.9** ‚Äì necessity defense, exception to trespass

* **Facts:** Defendant has been homeless for over a decade. He was charged with trespassing on three properties in Great Barrington, where he was sleeping at night near a heater to avoid the winter‚Äôs cold.
  + The defendant did not contest that he had violated the no-trespass order; rather he claimed the necessity defense. Because he had been kicked out of a nearby shelter, and had no home, he claimed that there was no other alternative to escape from the cold.
* **Question:** Whether Magadini could claim the necessity defense to defeat his trespass charge.
* **Rule of Law:** A trial court committed error in refusing the defendant‚Äôs request for a jury instruction on the necessity defense when, as here.
  + The defendant satisfied the foundational elements entitling him to the defense by showing that there was ‚Äúreasonable doubt‚Äù about the availability of legal alternatives to abate a clear and imminent danger, and there was ‚Äúlittle question‚Äù that the weather conditions presented a ‚Äúclear and imminent danger‚Äù for a homeless person.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Elements to the necessity defense: (1) clear and imminent danger; (2) reasonable expectation that actions will be effective as the cause of abating the danger; (3) there is no legal alternative; (4) the legislature has not precluded the defense by clear and deliberate choice.
    - There was a **clear and imminent danger**: the February/March weather conditions in Massachusetts
    - **Availability of legal alternatives**: there is evidence that the defendant lacked legal alternatives to trespass during the cold days.
      * Could show that some of his options were foreclosed: no home, no shelter in town
      * He doesn‚Äôt have to show that there he conceptualized all possible alternatives, just that there is reasonable doubt about the availability of legal alternatives.
* **Notes:** Court potentially leveraging criminal procedure to indirectly influence policy re: homelessness
  + *Ploof v. Putnam*: the court held that necessity justifies a trespass when needed to save lives or property and the defendant committed a tort against the plaintiffs by unmooring the boat
  + *Vincent v. Lake Erie Transportation*: a steamship moored to a private wharf was required to pay damages when the wind thrust the ship onto the wharf

***Desnick v. American Broadcasting Co*. (7th Cir. 1995) ‚Äì p.19** ‚Äì consent as limitation on right to exclude

* **Facts:** Seven employees of ABC posed as potential patients for Desnick in order to report an undercover story about the eye doctor‚Äôs practice. Separately, ABC requested to film inside the practice with a promise to provide ‚Äúfair and balanced‚Äù reporting.

* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + This isn‚Äôt unprivileged fraud because there was no interference with the types of interests that trespass generally tries to protect.

* **Notes:** Consent obtained through fraud can be legitimate in some instances ‚Äì especially where no harm resulted. This was also in a place of business open to the public

***Jacque v. Steenberg Homes, Inc.* (Wis. 1997) ‚Äì p.38** ‚Äì right without a remedy is not a right

* **Facts:** Steenberg Homes transported a mobile home across the Jacques property, despite repeated pleas from the Jacques not to do so. The Jacques repeatedly refused Steenberg the right to pass, and pointed on a map to their property line
  + The trial court found for the Jacques, and awarded $1 in nominal damages, and $100,000 in putative damages. The appeals court set the putative damages aside.

* **Rule of Law:** Punitive damages are appropriate in a private trespass action.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The right of exclusion from property is fundamental to any real property rights regime.
    - Courts recognize a harm, even if it isn‚Äôt reflected in terms of nominal damages.
    - The right loses its meaning if there is no punishment for its infringement.
      * Society writ large has an interest in deterring trespass.
  + Punitive damages were sufficient in this case to ensure that Steenberg does not find it more profitable to trammel on rights than to respect them.
* **Takeaway:** Right to exclude is among the core rights in the ‚Äúbundle of sticks.‚Äù Protecting expectations with respect to this right is an essential function of property law.

  + Preserves confidence in the legal system (**meet people‚Äôs expectations**)

**Adverse Possession: From Trespass to Full Title**

**ADVERSE POSSESSION**

* Definition: Adverse possession allows title to transfer from the ‚Äútrue owner‚Äù to the adverse possessor when one possesses another‚Äôs land in a manner that is:
  + **Open and notorious**: possession must be visible to others (like enclosing with a fence).
  + **Actual**: the possessor must physically occupy the property in some manner that demonstrates engagement ‚Äì land buildings, or paying property taxes suffices
  + **Exclusive**: Use is of the type expected by an exclusive owner
  + **Continuous for a period beyond the state statute of limitations**
    - **Tacking** doctrine: The adverse possession of predecessors in title counts towards the adverse possessor‚Äôs ownership.
  + **Adverse and hostile**: must be occupying the land without the true owner‚Äôs permission.
    - **Question re**: **permission**: does this jurisdiction have a **presumption of permissiveness** (presume that the owner permits third-party use) or a **presumption of non-permissiveness** (presumption that third-party use is adverse)?
    - Plus, there are a number of potential mental states required of the adverse possessor:
      * *Objective Test*: The court will not inquire into the state of mind of the adverse possessor.
        + Still must show that the defendant lacked permission to be on the land. [Moot if in jurisdiction with presumption of non-permissiveness] This is the law in the majority of states
      * *Subjective Tests:*
        + *Good Faith Test*:The adverse possessor can only make out a claim if she or he thought she or he was ***not on*** someone else‚Äôs land. Minority of states: Colorado, GA, NM, Iowa
        + *The Intentional Test*: The adverse possessor can only make out a claim if she or he was intentionally interfering with someone else‚Äôs possession; i.e. she or he knew it was someone else‚Äôs land and did it anyway. Minority of states: SC

**Policy Justifications for Adverse Possession**

* Economic justifications
  + Lower costs: in the long-run, making it less expensive to provide title.
    - Inability to prove ownership will increase transaction costs, and have a chilling effect on acquisition and conveyance of property.
    - Means that prospective owners will not need to conduct extensive inquiries into the status of title.
  + Efficient Use: the property should be used by those who value it most, and are willing to put it to work.
    - It protects the investment made by the adverse possessor, and increases their willingness to maintain value of their property.
* Moral justifications
  + The longer one holds a piece of property, the more it becomes a part of your persona (Radin)
  + Reliance: one comes to rely upon ownership of a particular piece of land

**Claim of Title** ‚Äì when the person is acting with the intent to own the property

**Color of Title** actions

* An adverse possessor acquires color of title when a conveyance appears to meet the requirements of a valid transfer but in fact does not. Statutory period can be shorter in some jurisdictions because the possessor basically owns the land, but just there‚Äôs a defect in the deed

***Brown v. Gobble* (W. Va. 1996) ‚Äì p.287** ‚Äì elements of adverse possession; tacking doctrine

* **Facts:** The Gobble family buys their property in 1985, and are told that their property goes to the fence. The Browns purchased their adjacent property in 1989. Before purchase, the Browns were told that there was a two-foot tract between belonging to them fenced in by the Gobble family.
  + The plaintiffs then sued to reclaim the land.
* **Question:** Whether the Gobble family had taken adverse possession of the adjacent land fenced in.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the Gobbles meet each of the prongs of the adverse possession test, they adversely possessed the adjacent two feet of land originally owned by the Browns.
* Legal Reasoning:
  + **Actual Possession:** The Gobbles had actual possession of the land‚Äîit was fenced in and maintained by them.
  + **Hostile or adverse:** The land was fenced in 1937 by a predecessor to the Gobbles in ownership.
    - Applied the ‚Äúobjective test‚Äù in deciding that the court will look at the possessor‚Äôs actions and not inquire about their state of mind when taking possession.
  + **Open and notorious:** Called witnesses to testify that the possession was well-known in town when it was originally fenced in in 1937.
  + **Exclusive:** The fencing was never objected to by any previous owners, and it was used by the Gobbles
  + **Continuous:** Has been held continuously by the Gobbles‚Äô predecessors in title since 1937. Therefore, adverse possession was taken by the previous ownership years before the Gobbles ever owned it.
    - **Tacking doctrine**: Even though the Gobbles only held adverse possession for 9.5 of the statutorily-required 10 years, the adverse possession of predecessors in title counts towards their adverse possession.

***Nome 2000 v. Fagerstrom* (Alaska 1990) ‚Äì p.303** ‚Äì whether seasonal use is sufficient to establish adverse possession

* **Facts:** Beginning in the 1970, the Fagerstroms, Alaska natives, began using the property as their own from June through September each year. This included recreational use, and the construction of cabins, picnic benches, and other small-scale items. An anthropologist, and many people present in the town testified at trial that their possession was consistent with ownership.
  + Nome 2000, the true owner sued to eject the Fagerstroms; the Fagerstroms countered that they had taken adverse possession of the property.
* **Question:** Whether the Fagerstroms had taken adverse possession of the land owned by Nome 2000 by their annual summer use.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the Fagerstroms meet all the elements of adverse possession by the objective test, they have taken adverse possession.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Nome 2000 alleges that the Fagerstroms did not do enough to improve the property in order to take adverse possession in a continuous, exclusive, and notorious fashion.
    - Not based upon how significant or substantial an improvement is; rather the question is whether, under the circumstances, it is reasonable to understand improvements as possession.
  + The Fagerstroms met each prong of the adverse possession test.
    - **Exclusive:** Annual use throughout the warmer seasons, including the improvements made, are more significant than mere casual use.
    - **Continuous:** Used with consistency for the statutory period in question
    - **Open and notorious:** any serious effort to investigate the premises for signs of use would have revealed the Fagerstrom‚Äôs use
    - **Hostile or adverse:** Applies the ‚Äúobjective test‚Äù to show that the Fagerstroms used the land as if they actually owned it.
    - **Actual Possession:** There isn‚Äôt sufficient evidence on the record to show what the Fagerstroms actually possessed, and what they did not.
      * Because they met each of the other elements of adverse possession, this is remanded for the trial court to decide.

**Prescriptive Easements: From Trespass to Use Rights**

**Prescriptive Easements**

* Definition: if the scope of a non-owner‚Äôs possession is limited to a narrower use of the owner‚Äôs property, she may be granted a prescriptive easement.
  + Affirmative Easement: The right to engage in a particular use of another‚Äôs land.
    - Right of Way: the right to cross another‚Äôs land.
  + Negative Easement: The right to limit or block a particular use of another‚Äôs land.
* Factors for determining Prescriptive Easement
  + **Actual use or possession**;
  + **Open and notorious;**
  + **Adverse and Hostile;**
  + **Exclusivity is not required by most courts**
  + **Continuous For a period beyond the state statute of limitations**
  + **Acquiescence by the true owner required by some states.**
    - Whether this applies depends on the jurisdiction.

***Community Feed Store, Inc. v. Northeastern Culvert Corp.* (Vt. 1989) ‚Äì p.315**

* **Facts:** Plaintiff operates a retail store selling animal feed. Behind the store is a gravel lot used by customers to pull in and out of the store. Vehicles would use the lot to come into the store and leave.
  + It was conclusively established that the defendant owned the bulk of the area used by the plaintiff om the basis of a new survey
  + Defendant sought to evict the plaintiff from the land. The Plaintiff sued for a declaration of prescriptive easement, and lost at trial.
* **Question:** Whether the plaintiff is entitled to prescriptive easement.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the plaintiff can establish all elements of adverse possession and because the use was consistent with a grant of easement, the plaintiff has met their burden in establishing prescriptive easement
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The elements of prescriptive easement are coextensive with those of adverse possession; only difference is that adverse possession culminates in the acquisition of non-fee interest.
    - Where it is claimed, it must be shown through the **extent of actual occupation** and **use.** Burden of proof to show actual use
      * Extent of use doesn‚Äôt have to be proven with absolute precision, but **general outlines** must be consistent with the pattern of use throughout the prescriptive period.
      * Here the case was clear that the plaintiff had used the area in a manner **generally consistent with granting an easement.**
  + Elements of prescriptive easement
    - Open and notorious: if this is established, then it will be considered adverse possession.
      * Established here: has been used by vehicles of all kinds since 1920.
    - Hostile and continuous: Under doctrine of ‚Äútacking,‚Äù the use and chain of title dating back to 1920 is sufficient to establish prescriptive easement.
* Notes:
  + Some states require the claimant of prescriptive easement to claim **acquiescence**.
    - Definition differs: in some states it means that the landowner must have known and passively allowed it. In others it must mean the owner never objected.
  + Presumption of permissiveness rather than adverse in some states (making it harder to claim adverse possession)

***United States on Behalf of the Zuni Tribe of N.M. v. Platt* (D. Ariz. 1990) ‚Äì CW ‚Äì** using property law after dispossession; plaintiffs are asking for limited and periodic use every four years

* **Facts:** Plaintiff Tribe has been making a religious pilgrimage across Arizona for at least a century, and by some accounts since the 1500s.
  + The defendant now seeks to exclude the plaintiffs from his land. The United States intervened as plaintiff to seek a prescriptive easement for the tribe.
  + It is well known that the plaintiffs have been making this pilgrimage for decades on a regular basis.
* **Question:** Whether the plaintiff is entitled to prescriptive easement in order to complete their religious ritual.
* **Rule of Law:** Because each element of adverse possession is fulfilled here, the Zuni Tribe is entitled to prescriptive easement.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Prescriptive Easement requires that possession be (1) actual; (2) open and notorious; (3) hostile; (4) under a claim of right; (5) continuous for the statutory period of at least 10 years.
    - Must prove all of these things, but their burden is lower because they are asking for limited and periodic use every four years.
    - **Actual:** Have possession of the land every four years when they conduct the pilgrimage because they recognize no other right to the land
    - **Continuous for Statutory Period**: They have used the land every four years for a long time (at least since the 1920s)
    - **Open and Notorious:** Well-known within Mr. Platt‚Äôs community. Court infers that he would have been aware of it.
    - **Hostile:** during the period, claims exclusive rights, and denies the true owner title.
      * Fulfilled here: coerced the owners to create gates in their land such that they were bound to respect the pilgrimage.
    - **Claim of right:** intent to appropriate the land and use as one‚Äôs own.
    - **Exclusive:** So long as they are using it for pilgrimage, nobody else can.

* **Facts:** Resorts International Hotel excluded the respondent, Uston, from its hotel and casino because he engaged in card counting. Uston contested the exclusion on the grounds that there was no common law or statutory right to exclude him for card counting.

* **Rule of Law:** ‚ÄúWhen a property owner opens his or her property to the general public in the pursuit of their own property interest, they have a duty not to act in an arbitrary or discriminatory manner toward persons who come on their premises.‚Äù
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The current majority rule is an absolute right to eject or exclude someone for any reason consistent with state and federal civil rights law.
  + When a private owner opens itself up to public use, it must accommodate the rights of the people who use that property.
    - Duty to be reasonable is applicable to all property owners that open themselves up to the public.
    - Uston did not threaten security, nor was he being disruptive; he should have full access.
* **Notes:**
  + **THIS IS NOT THE MAJORITY RULE**
    - When a property owner opens his or her property to the general public‚Ä¶they have the right to exclude others for any reason not specifically prohibited by federal laws, *PROVIDED:* the property is **not an inn or common carrier, which have a duty to serve the public.** (*Madden v. Queens*, N.Y. 1947)
      * The underlying rationale is that businesses like common carriers and inns serve monopolistic functions; therefore exclusion from one is total exclusion, which should be avoided.
    - The market should be allowed to determine who will enter/be excluded

**The First Amendment**

**Standard: A facility that is built and operated primarily to benefit the public and whose operation is essentially a public function is a state actor for purposes of First/Fourteenth Amendment analysis.**

**BIG QUESTION:** What happens when someone is trying to access property to express their religious beliefs/exert their free speech rights?

**Overview: First Amendment**

* Has a state actor requirement
* Fourteenth Amendment extends this to state governments
* At what point does a private actor become a state actor for the purposes of First Amendment analysis?
  + *Marsh v. Alabama* (company-owned town) - A company town is functionally equivalent to a state actor, so allowing company managers to infringe on the rights of free expression in the town is a violation of the First Amendment.
    - Articulates two potential tests for determining whether an entity is a state actor:
      * Public Use: the more you open your doors to the public, the more circumscribed your right to exclude becomes (like *Uston*)
      * Public Function: the property becomes a state actor to the extent that it is taking on an essential public function usually reserved for the state
    - Why does a state actor have more duties than a private actor, generally?
      * Monopoly over force and facilities
      * Composite of a democratically-convened group of people
      * Settled expectations/reliance interests
  + *Lloyd v. Tanner* ‚Äì shopping mall did not engage in state action. Invitation to the public to transact business at a shopping center did not dedicate private property to public use that entitled exercise of First Amendment rights unrelated to the shopping center‚Äôs operation. Narrowing of *Marsh*
    - Unlike *Marsh*, the mall did not take on all the attributes of a company town
    - *Lloyd* essentially overrules *Logan Valley* ‚Äì rule is now a very strict public function test
  + *Praeger University v. Google* ‚Äì YouTube does not take on a ‚Äúpublic function‚Äù as a platform for media, so it is not a state actor.
    - Narrow reading of *Marsh* ‚Äì there‚Äôs a very high bar for an action to be considered a ‚Äúpublic function‚Äù; Praeger unsuccessfully attempting to put spin on *Marsh* by calling YouTube a ‚Äúpublic forum‚Äù

***Marsh v. Alabama* (1946) ‚Äì CW ‚Äì** privately-owned town

* **Underlying Facts:** The town of Chicksaw, AL was owned entirely by Gulf Shipbuilding. Aside from the fact of ownership of all the land, and the employment of all residents, it functioned as a normal town. Marsh, a Jehovah‚Äôs Witness was distributing religious literature in the town. The company asked her to leave, but she refused, and was arrested for trespassing.
* **Question:** Whether a company that owns and runs a company town is a ‚Äústate actor‚Äù
* **Holding:** A company town is functionally equivalent to a state actor. Thus allowing company managers to infringe on the rights of free expression in the town is a violation of the First Amendment.
* **Reasoning:**
  + Neither a state nor a municipality can bar distribution of literature containing religious or political ideas on the streets, sidewalks or other public places.
  + The fact that the town is owned entirely by a private actor makes no difference ‚Äì it works on a sliding scale: ‚Äúthe more an owner, for his advantage, opens up his property for use by the public in general, the more do his rights become circumscribed by the statutory and constitutional rights of those who use it.‚Äù
    - State actors have monopolies on coercion in important public spaces ‚Äì people cannot opt out and chose an alternative.
  + Balancing rights of property owners with the rights enjoyed by the people living in a company town, the rights of the people to free expression and religion occupy the preferred position.
* **Notes:**
  + There is an open question as to whether this articulates a *Public Use* or a *Public Function* test in determining whether an entity is a state actor:
    - Public Use: the more you open your doors to the public, the more circumscribed your right to exclude becomes (like Uston)
    - Public Function: the property becomes a state actor to the extent that it is taking on an essential public function usually reserved for the state.
  + Why does a state actor have more duties than a private actor (generally)?
    - Monopoly over force and facilities
    - Composite of a democratically-convened group of people
    - Settled expectations/reliance interests

**Lloyd Corp., Ltd. v. Tanner (1972) ‚Äì p.65 ‚Äì shopping mall**

* **Facts**: A private citizen was distributing handbills in a public shopping mall in protest of the Vietnam War, when a security guard informed them that they were prohibited from distributing handbills in the center. The plaintiff refused to leave, and was subsequently removed
  + They subsequently sued the mall, alleging that the mall served a ‚ÄúPublic Function,‚Äù and was consequently unable to restrict patrons‚Äô First Amendment Rights.
* **Question:** Whether a shopping mall engaged in State Action under the ‚ÄúPublic Function‚Äù test.
* **Rule of Law:** Invitation to the public to transact business at a shopping center did not dedicate private property to public use that entitled exercise of First Amendment rights unrelated to the shopping center‚Äôs operation.
  + *Lloyd* essentially overrules *Logan Valley* [Court extended rationale of *Marsh* to peaceful picketing by a union of a store located in a large shopping center] ‚Äì rule is now a very strict public function test
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Uninvited guests have no right to exercise free speech on private property, and the mall is essentially private.
    - Unlike *Marsh*, the mall did not take on all the attributes of a company town.
    - Property does not lose its private character just because the public is invited inside.
* **Other Opinions:** *Marshall Dissenting* ‚Äì when the mall opens itself to the public, it must subject itself to further regulation.
  + Lloyd‚Äôs served a public function, akin to a public square: it satisfied all of its residents‚Äô needs.
  + In relative terms, Lloyd‚Äôs has a much smaller interest in policing the speech of its residents.

***Praeger University v. Google LLC* (N.D. Cal. Mar. 26, 2018) ‚Äì CW -** Youtube

* Facts: Plaintiff, a conservative media outlet, alleges that the Defendant, YouTube, discriminated against Praeger on the basis of its conservative viewpoint. Specifically, plaintiff alleges that it placed age restrictions on the plaintiff‚Äôs videos in a discriminatory fashion.
  + Plaintiff sues, alleging that the defendant is a state actor under the ‚Äúpublic function‚Äù test, and therefore cannot curtail its First Amendment Rights.
* Question: Does YouTube have the right to exclude speakers on its platform?
* Rule of Law: Yes, because YouTube does not take on a ‚Äúpublic function‚Äù as a platform for television, it is not a state actor.
* Legal Reasoning:
  + The court reads *Marsh* holding narrowly ‚Äì state actor is one that runs a town
  + There is a very high bar for an action to be considered a ‚Äúpublic function.‚Äù : akin to holding public elections or ‚Äúgoverning a town.‚Äù
    - YouTube is a *private* company providing a platform for videos and must be allowed to self-regulate
    - In light of the *Lloyd Corp.* holding, there is no cause of action.
* Praeger argument: Putting spin on *Marsh* ‚Äì calling YouTube a ‚Äúpublic forum‚Äù (court does not buy this argument)

**The Public Trust Doctrine**

**Principles of Public Trust Doctrine**

* Duties of a Public Trust
  + Must be held available for use by the general public;
  + Must not be sold;
  + Must be maintained for particular types of uses.
* Deciding if a property is a Public Trust:
  + **Is the property within the public domain?**
    - Lands covered by tidal waters (Early1800s)
    - Wet-sand of the beach (Early 1800s)
    - Lands covered by fresh waters (*Illinois Central Railroad v. Illinois, 1892* ‚Äì Supreme Court held that ownership of/dominion over lands covered by tide waters belong to the states - subject to the paramount right of Congress to control their navigation so far as may be necessary for the regulation of commerce)
    - Dry-sand of beaches owned by a municipality (*Neptune City v. Avon*, N.J. 1972. Contrast with Massachusetts, which has limited public access to tidal lands, not the dry sand area)
    - Dry-sand of beaches owned by a non-profit charitable association/quasi-public body (*Matthews v. Bay Head Improvement*, N.J. 1984)
    - Ambient air, wild flora, fauna (*Robinson v. Penn.*, Pa. 2013, see FN 10 in *Juliana*, 2016)
  + Deciding on whether to apply public trust doctrine:
    - **Type of resource:**
      * Scarce/important: Is the resource necessary for the enjoyment of an existing public trust asset and, when combined, create a unique, irreplaceable resource that is in limited supply? (*Matthews v. Bay Head Improvement Ass‚Äôn*, N.J. 1972)
      * Fairness
      * State actor
  + **Type of entity:**
    - State government
    - Municipal government (See *Avon*)
    - A quasi-public entity? (See *Matthews*)
    - The Federal government? (See *Juliana*)
* Notes: Public Trust doctrine is dynamic, not static
  + *Matthews v. Bay Head Improvement Association* - Because the dry sand beach is in the public trust, and the Bay Head Improvement Association is a quasi-public entity, it can‚Äôt restrict access.
    - The court has already ruled that the public has a right to access and use wet sand beaches; extending the public domain to dry sand beaches allows them to access the wet sand within the public trust.
    - If a resource is within the public trust (e.g. wet beaches), the public must be able to access it (by having access to dry beaches). ‚ÄúReasonable access‚Äù
    - Oregon relies on custom to hold that longstanding reasonable use of a beachfront by the public for recreational purposes conferred continuing rights of access

Building out a Public Trust claim:

1. Is the asset/resource subject to public trust? Is the property in the public domain?
2. Has the entity violated one of the fiduciary duties of the trust?
3. Is the title owner an entity against which a court can enforce trust duties?

***Matthews v. Bay Head Improvement Ass‚Äôn* (N.J. 1984) ‚Äì p.72** ‚Äì public trust doctrine is dynamic, NOT fixed or static

* **Facts:** The sand beach in Bay Head is owned by the public, but the Bay Head Improvement Association owns all the plots surrounding the public beach. It charges an annual fee for membership, and pays lifeguards and for the beach‚Äôs maintenance. It also restricts daytime access to the beach to those paying annual membership. Membership is only open to residents of the town of Bay Head.
  + The plaintiff in this case is a non-resident denied access to the beach and admittance to the Association.
* **Question:** Whether the public has a right to access and use the beach through the quasi-public body.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the dry sand beach is in the public trust, and the Bay Head Improvement Association is essentially a public body, it cannot restrict access.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The court has already ruled that the public has a right to access and use wet sand beaches; extending the public domain to dry sand beaches allows them to access the wet sand within the public trust.
  + If a property is within the public trust, the public must be able to access it. ‚ÄúReasonable access‚Äù
  + The Improvement Association is essentially a public entity performing a public function.
    - Because of its public nature, it cannot restrict access to only Bay Head residents
* **Notes:**
  + New test formulation:
    - Is this essential to domain access?
    - Is it ‚ÄúUnique, irreplaceable, limited‚Äù?
    - Is the private owner acting essentially as a public one?

***Olivas v. Olivas* (N.M. Ct. App. 1989) ‚Äì p.673** ‚Äì each joint tenant has the right to possess entire property; constructive ouster (vs. traditional ouster ‚Äì affirmative physical act)

* **Facts:** Husband moved out of house after separation. The party eventually divorced. He sought to recover rent from ex-wife on the theory that he is a tenant in common and is entitled to his share of the property value because he was constructively ousted.
* **Issue:** whether the lower court erred in finding that the husband was not constructively ousted and thus not entitled to payments from ex-wife
* **Holding:** no, upheld the lower court‚Äôs holding
* **Rule:** the obligation of the occupying co-tenant to pay rent may arise in the absence of ‚Äúactual‚Äù ouster when the realities of the situation, without there being any fault by either co-tenant, prevent the co-tenants from sharing occupancy. In the marital context, when the emotions of a divorce make it impossible for the parties to occupy the same residence together, there may be an ouster in effect.
* **Court‚Äôs analysis:** The husband chose to leave and live with a girlfriend and was thus not forced out, which did not compel a finding of constructive ouster.
  + **Justification:** Fairness (who needs it more? Who values it more?) and efficiency

***Ark Land Co. v. Harper* (W. Va. 2004) ‚Äì p.691** ‚Äì presumption against partition for sale (presumption of partition in kind). Economic value not the exclusive test; sentimental/emotional interests should also be considered

* **Facts:** Dispute involving tract of land exclusively owned by family for 100 years. In 2001 Ark acquired 67.5% interest in the land from several family members to use for mining. **Spouses & Unmarried Partners**

Marital Property

* Historical systemic evolution of property system
  + **Coverture:** historical property scheme.
    - Husband and wife are treated as a single person in the eyes of the law.
      * Husband can convey land without his wife‚Äôs consent but not other way around.
      * Husband‚Äôs debts can be settled using his wife‚Äôs property.
    - Same rules as tenancy by entirety, except in the modern world women have more rights.
  + **Married Women‚Äôs Property Acts**: abolished the coverture system
    - Tenancy by entirety is still allowed in the modern form, however it is a reciprocal form rather than unilateral.
      * Wives‚Äô property cannot be alienated by husband without their consent.
  + **Same-Sex Marriage:** *United States v. Windsor* (2013) and *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015) used the right to pass on property free of unfair encumbrance as a principal justification for same-sex marriage.
* Separate vs. Communal property systems
  + **Separate Property:** **Majority Rule**
    - Spouses own their respective property separately, except to the extent they choose to share it or mingle it with their spouse‚Äôs property.
      * *Marriage*: all property is earned and kept separately.
      * *Divorce*: equitable distribution of property owned by each party on divorce based on a variety of factors (economic need, status, contributions of the respective parties)
        + Provisions exist to provide alimony too.
      * *Death:* may dispose of property by will, but widow can override the will and get a statutorily provided portion of the property (1/3 to ¬Ω)
  + **Community Property:**
    - All property is owned in equal part by the spouses.
      * *Marriage:* all earnings are communal.
      * *Divorce:* Allocated mechanically by giving the spouses their discrete halves.
      * *Death:* Spouse disposes of property by will; one half mechanically goes to spouse.
* Premarital Agreements: divide property ownership between spouses *ex ante*.
  + Enforced unless unconscionable, or otherwise against public policy.

***O‚ÄôBrien v. O‚ÄôBrien* (N.Y. 1985) ‚Äì p.706 ‚Äì** what constitutes marital property?

* **Facts:** The plaintiff and defendant are husband and wife. The wife moved to Mexico, and supported her husband financially while he attended medical school. In doing so, she gave up the possibility of earning a teaching certification and obtaining professional advancement. Shortly after he obtained his medical license, and began his residency, he moved out and asked for a divorce.
  + She sued, claiming that her husband‚Äôs medical license is marital property.
* **Question:** Whether a medical license is marital property.
* **Rule of Law:** Because a medical license represents the outcome of joint investment in the marriage, and fits well within the definition provided by statute, it is property.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + An interest in a profession or professional career can be marital property.
    - The state statute defining property can easily be read to encompass a medical license.
      * Even though it is not alienable, it has significant value, and contributes to husband‚Äôs future earning capacity.
  + Husband claims that it represents a personal achievement, and is not property.
    - When both spouses contribute to the marriage, the outcome is generally recognized as their joint property.
      * Wife contributed 76% of their income.

  + Damages were assessed assuming that he would become a general surgeon, when in fact, it is unclear that he wants to be one at all.

  + This case is a singular outlier ‚Äì no other state has held that licenses are marital property.

    - Fairness: this will lock husband into a career

***Watts v. Watts* (2015) ‚Äì p.717 ‚Äì** what about unmarried partners?

* **Facts:** The plaintiff and defendant cohabitated, had kids, and presented themselves to the world as a married couple for 14 years, even though they never actually got married. The wife took the husband‚Äôs last name, quit her job, and contributed to homemaking.

Basic Vocabulary

* Temporal Vocabulary
  + Present/Possessory Estate: the right of present possession.
    - Not preceded by any other interest; and
    - Not subject to any conditions precedent.
  + Future Interest: a legal interest that presently exists, but does not entitle the owner to possession until a time in the future, and cannot cut short or divest the preceding possessory or vested estate.
    - Types of future interest:
      * Reversion: when the grantor of the estate holds the future interest.
      * Remainder: when the future interest is held by a third party (not the grantor).
  + Contingent Remainder exists if
    - It is created in an unascertained or unborn person; or
    - It is subject to a condition precedent other than natural termination of the preceding possessory estate.
  + Vested Remainder: it is created in a known person, and has no conditions precedent other than termination of the preceding possessory estate.
    - Vested Remainder Subject to Divestment: created in a known person, but could be prevented from ever becoming possessory by a different future interest.
    - Vested Remainder Subject to Open: a vested remainder that could be diluted by the addition of new members of a class of persons entitled to it.
* Quantum of Estate
  + Fee Simple Absolute: A present possessory interest that continues forward in perpetuity.
    - Conveyance language: ‚Äúto A‚Äù or ‚Äúto A and her heirs‚Äù
  + Life Estate: an estate that confers a right to possession for the life of its owner.
    - Conveyance language: ‚Äúto A for life.‚Äù
    - Life estates are alienable *inter vivos* (during the life of the estate holder).
      * The recipient of a life estate is called a *life estate pur autre vie*.
      * CANNOT convey a fee simple interest, but they can sell the life estate.
  + Defeasible Fee: an estate that can be divested or cut short by a future interest.
    - Fee Simple Determinable: a future interest that cuts short the prior estate is created in the Grantor using words of duration (there is a *possibility of reverter*)

      * Words of condition: provided that, on condition, but if
    - Fee Simple Subject to Executory Limitations: a future interest that cuts short or divests a prior interest is created in a Grantee.
      * Springing Executory Interest: an executory interest that divests an estate held by the grantor.
      * Shifting Executory Interest: an executory interest that divests an estate held by another grantee.
        + ‚ÄúO gives this land to O, but if B ever marries, to B.‚Äù

Especially relevant when it comes to charitable

  + Precatory: Non-legally-binding language

***Edwards v. Bradley* (Va. 1984)** ‚Äì presumption against forfeiture; grantor‚Äôs intent

* **Facts:** Viva Parker Lilliston died and included in her will a provision leaving the estate to Jones, but forbidding sale of the estate in order to pay creditors. Edwards attempted to have her husband and children sign a waiver allowing her to sell the farm, but Beverly Bradley refused.
  + When Jones died, she left each child an equal share in the family farm, except Bradley, who she left $1. Bradley sued alleging that she had a future interest, and that Edwards was initially given a life estate.
* **Question:** Whether the testatrix devised a fee simple estate or a life estate in real property.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the conditional limitation indicated that the children were to take over the farm after their mother‚Äôs death this should be construed as a life estate.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + In Virginia, there is no presumption against alienability; rather the rule is that the will should be construed to most faithfully execute the *grantor‚Äôs intent*.
  + Experienced attorneys drafted the agreement., using ‚Äúfee simple‚Äù throughout the will, but not in that section (absence is demonstrative of intent).
  + Future interest vested in children ‚Äì showed that she intended that they would inherit the property eventually.
* **Note:** This demonstrates the minority rule ‚Äì most states use the presumption against forfeiture.
  + Separate note: Courts are eager to find a future interest when the property is donated for charitable purposes.

***McIntyre v. Scarbrough* (Ga. 1996) ‚Äì p.764** ‚Äì doctrine of waste

* **Facts:** McIntyre sold her land to Scarbrough, but kept 1.2 acres of the land for herself as a part of a life estate, and lived there in a mobile home. The Scarbroughs brought a claim of waste to end McIntyre‚Äôs life tenancy on the grounds that she had left her mobile home in a state of severe disrepair.
* **Question:** Whether McIntyre forfeited her life estate in the property due to waste.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the evidence conclusively established that the life tenant failed to exercise ordinary care for the preservation of the property, she forfeited her interest in the estate.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Theory of occupancy ‚Äì McIntyre is no longer living there per the conditions set forth
  + Doctrine of waste ‚Äì McIntyre didn‚Äôt keep up the property per the conditions, so her life estate is extinguished.
  + A life tenant is entitled to their estate, provided they take reasonable and ordinary care, and does not do anything to unreasonably harm the remainder interest.
    - In this case, the defendant failed to pay her taxes on the property, which was enough to forfeit her interest in the estate.
* **Other Opinions:** *Dissent ‚Äì* the damage was unintentional; must be intentional in order to constitute waste.

***Evans v. Abney* (1970) ‚Äì p.770** ‚Äì rule against racial restrictions

* **Facts:** Georgia Senator Bacon left his estate to his home city of Macon for the creation of a public park, on the grounds that the park remain for the exclusive use of its white citizens. The Supreme Court determined that the park is not allowed to be segregated on that basis. The Court remanded it to the State Courts to determine Senator Bacon‚Äôs intention in bequeathing his estate.
* **Question:** Whether Senator Bacon‚Äôs principle intention in bequeathing his estate can be fulfilled, given that the racial restrictions are unconstitutional.
* **Holding (J. Black):** Not a violation of the 14th Amendment to allow reversion to Bacon‚Äôs descendants because reversion is automatic. No court needed to enforce/intervene, so it is not really state action.
  + No state action in reluctance to do equitable reformulation under cy pres because no constitutionally protected rights directly violated by the refusal to reform the trust and allow automatic reversion.
  + Court not motivated by racial discrimination ‚Äì sole intention was to ‚Äúeffectuate as nearly as possible the explicit terms of Senator Bacon‚Äôs will.
  + Unlike *Shelley*, eliminating the park was a loss ‚Äúshared equally by the white and Negro citizens.‚Äù
  + Georgia courts treating park as fee simple determinable with automatic reversion to honor grantor‚Äôs intent. Charitable purpose so not going to read language/magic words as precatory.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Georgia Courts are entrusted with carrying out the intentions of a will.
    - The *cy pres* doctrine is used to allow the court to effectuate the testator‚Äôs intentions, and cannot be used if their intentions are thwarted.
      * Georgia Courts concluded that the Senator‚Äôs intention wasn‚Äôt to create a park, but rather to create a *segregated* park ‚Äì thus the court has no alternative, but to return the park to his heirs.
  + Because there is a private party on the other side, this does not constitute ‚Äústate action‚Äù; there is no indication that any judges or people within the state are motivated by racial animus.
* **Other Opinions:** *Brennan Dissent ‚Äì* This is discriminatory state action that should be prohibited under the 14th Amendment
  + But for the state‚Äôs intervention, this would have been allowed.
  + State intervened continuously to beautify and maintain the park.
* **Notes:** This adopts the narrower Disparate treatment view of the 14th Amendment.

***Johnson v. Whiton* (Mass. 1893) ‚Äì p.781** ‚Äì rule against creation of new estates

* **Facts:** Royal Whiton conveys her property to ‚Äúmy granddaughter Sarah A. Whiton and her heirs on her father‚Äôs side . . .‚Äù
* **Question:** Whether the conveyance established that Sarah Whiton has a fee simple absolute interest in Royal Whiton‚Äôs conveyance.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the words of limitation divert from an existing estate type, the conveyance is impermissible under Mass. Law; it reverts to a fee simple absolute interest.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + This does not convey an established estate type, and therefore is impermissible.
  + Courts will refuse to recognize a new interest ‚Äì it will interpret conveyances to try to fit them into existing forms
    - **Policy**: facilitates efficiency, protects settled expectations. Courts are also concerned about limits on alienability

**Holding:**

**Reasoning:**

  + *State v. Shack*
  + ‚ÄúContract is inconsistent with the enlightened attitude of the legislature and the courts towards migrant workers.‚Äù

***Sommer v. Kridel* (N.J. 1977) ‚Äì p.856** ‚Äì remedies for landlords; landlord duty to mitigate

* **Facts:** There are two companion cases where the tenant originally entered into the lease, but left the tenancy early, Sommer never even entered the building. In both cases the landlord left the apartment vacant, and chose not to show it to prospective tenants.
  + Landlord later brought suit against Sommer for 9 months of back-rent. Sommer counter-sued demanding return of his deposit.
* **Question:** Whether a landlord has a duty to mitigate damages as a result of a defaulting tenancy.
* **Rule of Law:** A landlord does have a duty to mitigate where there is a claim for damages under a residential lease.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The difference between a lease and an ordinary contract in the modern area has shrunken away to nothing. Potential argument that property law is merging with contract law
  + Failing to apply the mitigation rule is unjust under the circumstances.
    - Landlord needs to make reasonable effort to re-let apartment
    - *Fairness*: You would expect the landlord to mitigate
    - *Efficiency*: There‚Äôs no reason to leave a room open if someone wants it
  + There is no standard for assessing whether the landlord has diligently pursued remediation, but the rule should approximate best efforts.

* **Notes:** Duty to mitigate isn‚Äôt universal: California has a duty, but New York doesn‚Äôt.
  + Duty to mitigate has become the majority rule

**Regulating Living Standards**

Implied Warranty of Habitability: Originates in *Javins v. First Realty Corp.* (D.C. Cir. 1970). Refers to implied terms that the place you are renting will actually be habitable. Courts will read this warrant into *all* leases regardless of whether it is explicitly included. What the implied warranty of habitability requires is measured by the standards set out in local housing codes/regulations.

If the warranty is broken, tenants can break lease and leave or stay. If they stay, they do not need to pay rent for as long as repairs are needed.

***Javins v. First National Realty Corp.* (D.C. Cir. 1970) ‚Äì p.874** ‚Äì warranty of habitability

* **Facts:** The landlord filed suit, alleging that the tenant had failed to pay rent. The tenant alleged that they had stopped paying rent due to the over 1,500 hosing code violations, and were seeking an equitable set-off for as long as it took to repair the building.
* **Question:** Whether housing code violations which arise during the term of a lease have an effect on the tenant‚Äôs obligation to rent.
* **Rule of Law:** A warranty of habitability, measured by the Housing Regulations for the District of Columbia is implied by operation of law into the leases of urban dwelling units covered by such regulations.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Traditionally, the landlord‚Äôs only common law duty is to provide access to the land.
    - This common law duty should be modified for today‚Äôs world: people aren‚Äôt seeking an interest in land, but rather are looking for a place to stay of reasonable quality.
      * Tenants aren‚Äôt ‚Äújacks of all trades,‚Äù and are more transient than middle ages.
    - Courts have modified the old rules to include an implied warranty on all manners of other consumer goods ‚Äì there should also be one on homes.
    - Due to urban housing shortages, there is a severe imbalance between tenants and landlords.
  + Judged as a contract, the lease must be made in compliance with existing law ‚Äì including the housing code.
  + Finder of fact must decide the extent of the violations
  + Counterargument:
    - Eviction proceedings ‚Äúmore drawn out‚Äù now that tenants can assert violation of housing code or analogous failures as defense for nonpayment, even where premises is habitable.
* **Note:** The implied warranty of habitability has become the majority rule, and is non-waivable and non-disclaimable.

***3000 B.C. v. Bowman Properties Ltd.* (Pa. Com. Pl. 2008) ‚Äì p.868** ‚Äì Covenant of quiet enjoyment: more expansive set of harms than covered by warranty of habitability, e.g. privacy, peace and quiet, right of use, safety and security

* **Facts:** 3000 B.C. is a spa that was forced to move because the landlord began construction, and allowed a hair cuttery to move in upstairs.
* **Question:**
  + Whether the landlord‚Äôs actions breached the covenant of quiet enjoyment.
  + Whether the plaintiff is entitled to damages beyond the end of the lease.
* **Rule of Law:** The plaintiff‚Äôs implied covenant of quiet enjoyment was breached. The plaintiff is entitled to damages approximating their financial position, assuming there was no initial breach.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + There is an implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, the breach of which constitutes a constructive eviction.
  + Damages should approximate the respective positions of the parties, assuming that there was no constructive eviction [i.e. the tenant vacated the premises in a unreasonable time]
    - Cannot be awarded beyond the end of the lease, or assume that the lease would automatically be renewed.

***Johnson v. Paynesville Farmers Union Cooperative Oil Co.* (Minn. 2012)** ‚Äì p.363 ‚Äì trespass v. nuisance; causation/proximate cause required for nuisance claim

* **Facts:** Paynesville Farmers Union Cooperative Oil Co. sprayed pesticide that drifted towards and contaminated the crops on Johnson‚Äôs Organic farm. As a consequence of the drift, the Johnsons could not sell their crops as a premium ‚Äúorganic‚Äù price for three years, according to the National Organic Program.
  + The Johnson brought an action against Paynesville for trespass and nuisance.
* **Question:** Whether chemical drift onto one‚Äôs property constitutes either trespass or nuisance.
* **Rule of Law:** There is no cause of action for trespass because there was no physical entry on the land, and there is no cause of action for nuisance because the plaintiff alleged no substantial harm.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + No trespass
    - Trespass requires intentional or tangible entry onto another‚Äôs land.
      * There was no physical entry here
      * Court doesn‚Äôt want to require a showing of harm for trespass, as this would erode the right to exclude
        + Opens door for a whole host of liability for invasion of property by intangible property if you don‚Äôt have a requirement of harm
    - Washington and Alabama have allowed a trespass cause of action for chemical drift, but this conflicts with existing Minnesota law, and conflates trespass and nuisance law.
  + **No nuisance cause of action**
    - The Johnsons seek a nuisance finding because 7 C.F.R. ¬ß205.202(b) forbid them from selling their crops as organic crops.
      * Ct. believes that the Johnsons are misreading the statute: the proper interpretation is that they are forbidden from *intentionally* applying pesticides.
      * For this reason, they did not properly allege a **substantial harm** resulting from the application of pesticides.
      * Court also does not find *causation* between the conduct and the harm

***Dobbs v. Wiggins* (Ill. App. 2010) ‚Äì p.340** ‚Äì establishing substantial and unreasonable harm

* **Facts:** The Wiggins family has lived on their property for 15 years, and has been raising dogs there. Currently there are over 70 dogs, which bark all day and all night at a volume audible over two miles away.
  + The Dobbs, who have lived on their property for over two years now bring suit for nuisance, alleging that the noise is too great.
* **Question:** Whether the Wiggins dogs‚Äô barking constitutes a nuisance.
* **Rule of Law:** Because this was a substantial and unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the Dobbs‚Äô land, this constitutes a nuisance.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The three elements are all satisfied to show that this is a nuisance.
    - The harm was **substantial**: the plaintiffs weren‚Äôt especially sensitive to noise, and the trial court found as a matter of fact that it was enough to constitute a substantial harm.
    - The noise made by the dogs was **unreasonable**: plaintiffs were there first, the noise can‚Äôt be reduced or stopped, and the defendant has made efforts to lower the volume unsuccessfully.
    - The noise was sufficient to interfere with the **use and enjoyment of the land**.
  + Case is remanded to determine the appropriate equitable remedy. Unclear whether forcing Wiggins to reduce number of dogs to 6 makes sense ‚Äì is there a less restrictive solution?

**Nuisance: Choice of Remedy**

**Reciprocal costs:** core to theory of law and economics. What happens when two similarly useful uses of land come into contact with one another?

**Economic Analysis of Nuisance**

* Entitlements: rights that are enforced by the states, e.g. right to be secure from harm, right to engage in factory use
* How should a court decide who gest the entitlement?
  + Economic analysis: identify the most efficient rule of law
    - Kaldor-Hicks efficiency: benefits > costs, even if someone is worse off
* Classical Approach: Force Internalization of Externalities
  + *Externalities:* costs imposed on third parties that are not accounted for in the actor‚Äôs cost-benefit decision.
  + Classical approach forces factories to pay for the costs they impose on others so that it integrates into their own cost-benefit analysis.
  + Clears up the accounting ‚Äì maintains benefits of factory operation while forcing factory to axcount for harms to society (e.g. pay damages)
* Coase Theorem: Efficiency Analysis / critique of classical economics
  + *Joint Costs*
    - When one party is causing harm to the other, any shifting in responsibilities/ equilibrium would cause harm to the other
    - The problem isn‚Äôt about causing harm, but **how to distribute shared costs**.
    - Which land use maximizes social welfare? (NOT at internalizing externalities)
  + *Allocation of Legal Entitlements*
    - The Court‚Äôs goal is to distribute entitlements to those who value them most under the circumstances.
      * Regardless of how the court rules, the parties will always engage in *ex post* bargaining for the most efficient allocation.
      * Assuming that there are no transaction costs, it doesn‚Äôt matter how entitlements are distributed: **the outcome will always be the same, with the parties reaching equilibrium**.
      * Court is giving parties a starting point for negotiation: whoever values the entitlement more will keep it or buy it from the other party ‚Äì IF the transaction costs are zero
  + *Accounting for Transaction Costs*
    - Because there are transaction costs, the courts can reach the most efficient outcome by **awarding an entitlement to the party that would purchase the entitlement absent any transaction costs**.
      * Transaction costs: free rider problem; holdout problem; strategic bargaining costs; bilateral monopoly (when two parties negotiate, the costs can be especially high).
* Contra Coase: Behavioral Model
  + *Posner:* Relying on a willingness to pay disadvantages the poor ‚Äì we should weigh their interests more highly when we model this.
  + *Endowment Effect:* Humans are inherently risk-averse, and value freedom from loss more than prospective gain.
    - Once a court initially distributes entitlements, they calcify, and become sticky such that parties are unwilling to surrender the initial distribution.
  + *Offer-Ask Gap:* there is often a huge gulf between what homeowners can offer a factory, and what the factory requires to cease operation.
  + *Commodification*: physical property (especially homes) have significant value beyond the financial value to homeowners, which may increase the offer-ask spread, exacerbating the holdout problem, and making transaction costs untenable.

***Fontainebleau Hotel Corp. v. Forty-Five Twenty-Five, Inc.* (Fla. Dist. Ct. App. 1959) ‚Äì p.370** ‚Äì rights of access to light and air

* **Facts:** Fontainebleau and Eden Roc Hotels are adjacent to one another on the Miami beachfront. Fontainebleau is building a 14-floor addition to its hotel that would block the sunlight from the Eden Roc‚Äôs cabana, swimming pool and sunbathing area.
* **Question:** Whether a landowner has a legal right to light or air from their neighbor‚Äôs adjoining property.
* **Rule of Law:** Because there is no legal right to light or air flowing from an adjoining property, there is no legally-recognized harm.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + A property-owner need not use their land in a way that does not infringe on the rights of adjoining landowners
    - There is no right to light or air that comes through an adjoining property.
* **Notes:**
  + Most states do not recognize a right to light or air.
  + Exceptions to this rule:
    - *Spite Fences:* structures erected for the sole purpose of interfering with their neighbor‚Äôs light
    - *New Hampshire rule:* light and air should be recognized as permissible sources of damages so that nuisance law can be flexible enough to respond to future demands should they arise.

***Boomer v. Atlantic Cement Co.* (1970) ‚Äì p.358** ‚Äì permanent injunction for a nuisance

* **Facts:** Atlantic operates a cement factory, which damages the neighboring homes due to dirt, smoke and vibrations. The court has already found that it constitutes a nuisance.
* **Question:** Whether the court should award damages, or an equitable remedy to the plaintiffs.
* **Rule of Law:** In order to compensate the plaintiffs, while still dealing fairly with the defendant, the court grants damages to the plaintiff. They will grant an injunction on the defendant until the defendant pays.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The traditional rule is to provide an injunction, but that would permanently shutter a cement factory that employs a large number of people.
    - Delayed injunction would have the same effect: it would force the defendant to either move or else find a less disruptive alternative, which doesn‚Äôt presently exist.
  + Risk of damages is enough to spur research into prevention of the original harm, and act as a deterrent.
* **Notes:**
  + Pivots from the traditional property rule to a liability rule.
  + Indicative of the Progressive-era approach towards forcing nuisance
  + Court may order permanent injunction even when losses suffered by plaintiffs are much smaller than the ‚Äúbenefits‚Äù that the defendant generates

***Green v. Lupo* (Wash. Ct. App. 1982) ‚Äì p.520** ‚Äì interpretation of ambiguous easements ü°™ grantor intent

* **Facts:** The Greens sold land to the Lupos using an installment contract (deed does not transfer until the last payment). Lupos request a deed release on the condition that they grant an easement between their two respective properties for a driveway.
  + The Greens then construct a mobile home park on their half of the property. Residents use the easement as a runway for loud motorcycles. The Lupos block the easement, refusing to allow them to pass
* **Question:** Whether the easement between their property was appurtenant or an in gross easement granted specifically to the Greens.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the language is ambiguous, and there is a strong presumption of appurtenant easement, this easement is appurtenant.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The easement itself is ambiguous: while it specifically names the Greens, usually an easement for ingress is appurtenant.
    - **When the language is ambiguous, there is a strong presumption for finding an appurtenant** **easement.**
      * *Efficiency:* would force each subsequent owner to bargain for use of the easement.
      * *Fairness:* allows owners to negotiate among themselves.
  + While there is an opportunity to reach an equitable remedy, a total ban on motorcycles is unreasonable and an abuse of discretion.

***Cox v. Glenbrook Co.* (Nev. 1962) ‚Äì p.524** ‚Äì scope of an easement

* **Facts:** Quill negotiated an easement with the Glenbrook Company in order to access his property from the highway through Glenbrook‚Äôs estate.
  + Subsequently, Cox & Detrick acquired the Quill property and began to develop it. They then sought to pave and expand the narrow, dirt road constituting the easement over the Glenbrook estate.
* **Question:** Whether Cox can pave and expand the ‚ÄúQuill Easement‚Äù over Glenbrook‚Äôs land ‚Äì what‚Äôs the scope of the easement? To what extent does the grantor‚Äôs original intent inform how we interpret the rights granted by the easement?
* **Rule of Law:** The right of way constitutes an appurtenant easement, which can be improved so long as it hews closely to the original intent of the grantor. You can‚Äôt extend the road past its current width.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The easement is appurtenant because it is in writing, provided fair notice, and was intended to benefit future owners of the Quill estate.
    - It doesn‚Äôt matter that the Quill estate is being subdivided ‚Äì it will benefit all future owners anyway.
  + The right of way can be repaired, so long as it ‚Äúis reasonably calculated to promote the purposes for which the easement was created‚Äù
    - It cannot be expanded: Quill did not contemplate use by many people ‚Äì just himself.
  + There is insufficient evidence to support the finding that the development constituted an undue burden

**Easements by Implication**

* 4 different types of Easements by Implication:
  + **Easement by Estoppel:**
    - a landowner grants the claimant **permission** to use the property,
    - and the claimant **so changes their position**
    - **in reasonable reliance** on the continuation of the permission,
      * Reliance must be reasonable, and foreseeable.
    - that it would constitute a **substantial injustice to revoke** it
    - NOTE: Easements by estoppel may, alternatively, be found in 3 different kinds of circumstances (depends on jurisdiction):
      * Noncompliance with the Statute of Frauds
      * Reasonable reliance on continuation of consent
      * Fraud or misrepresentation
    - Some courts (e.g. Michigan) reject this doctrine entirely
  + **Prescriptive Easements (see dedicated section)**
  + **Easement by Prior Use**
    - Servient and dominant estates were **once under common ownership**;
    - One parcel was **previously used for the benefit** of the other parcel in a manner that was both:
      * Apparent and
      * Continuous
    - The use is either ‚Äú**reasonably necessary**‚Äù or ‚Äú**convenient**‚Äù for enjoyment of the dominant estate
  + **Easement by Necessity**
    - Servient and dominant estates were **once under common ownership**;
    - At the time of severance, the dominant estate became **landlocked**.

Negative easements‚Äì limited categories:

1. Rights to lateral support of one‚Äôs buildings
2. Rights to free flow of light and air

***Lobato v. Taylor* (Colo. 2002) ‚Äì p.537** ‚Äì easement by estoppel

* **Facts:** In the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, Carlos Beaubien purchased land in Colorado, and recruited families to live on the land, with the promise that he would ‚Äúgrant rights of access to the common lands to settlers. Taylor purchased the land in 1960; his deed indicated that ‚Äúclaims of the local people by prescription or otherwise right to pasture, wood, and lumber and so-called settlement rights on the land.‚Äù Then he refused to give locals right to the land, plaintiffs sued.
* **Question:** Whether there is an easement by estoppel granted to the local people, based upon their use.
* **Rule of Law:** A court can imply an easement created by estoppel when the landowner permitted another party to use the land under circumstances in which it was reasonably foreseeable that the user would substantially change position believing that the permission would not be revoked, the user substantially changed position in reasonable reliance on that belief, and injustice can be avoided only be establishment of servitude.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + There is easement by estoppel in this case
    - **Permission:** the prior landowners (preceding Taylor) granted permission to the settlers.
    - **Changes their Position:** the Beaubein promise attracted settlers to the area, by promising them the use of the land.
    - **Reasonable Reliance:** Beaubein knew that there would be reliance ‚Äì survival in the absence of the promised material would have been substantially more difficult.
    - **Substantial Injustice of revocation:** Taylor received the land, knowing about the conditional use by surrounding peoples, and disregarded it.
* **Other Opinions:**
  + *Martinez Dissenting in part ‚Äì* Agrees with substantially all of the court‚Äôs findings; would extend the remedy to fishing, hunting, and recreation.
  + *Kourlis Dissenting ‚Äì* Would decline to apply the principles of easement by estoppel under the circumstances because there was no misstatement or materially misleading information provided by prior ownership, which has always been the case when this doctrine has been applied.

***Granite Properties L.P. v. Manns* (Ill. 1987) ‚Äì p.547 ‚Äì** easement by prior use

* **Facts:** Granite owned the property originally, but sold a piece of it to Manns. The plaintiff inadvertently sold two pieces of driveway essential to access to their respective properties on either side of the defendant‚Äôs. They then sued seeking declaratory judgement that both were covered by an easement
* **Question:** Whether, in conveying a portion of the property now owned by the defendants, did the plaintiff retain an easement by implication to the land.
* **Rule of Law:** Yes, A landowner can establish an easement by implication over land he conveyed if they still own the adjacent tract and the use of the conveyed land is apparent, continuous, and permanent.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + **Common Ownership**: Not in dispute here. The plaintiff owned both original properties.
  + **Previously used for the benefit of the other parcel that was apparent and continuous:** Previously used to show to be continuous and apparent ‚Äì there were constantly trucks coming to deliver goods to the supermarket
  + ‚Äú**Reasonably Necessary**‚Äù: The necessity prong is lessened by the continuous and open use; nevertheless, there is a strong argument that it was reasonably necessary to use the properties so that they can actually function.

***Finn v. Williams* (Ill. 1941) ‚Äì p.554 ‚Äì** easement by necessity

* **Facts:** Williams conveyed land to Finn, however the only way to access the property was through Williams‚Äô property. While there were two private roads that used to be accessible, both have now been closed.
* **Question:** Whether Finn is entitled to easement by necessity under the circumstances.
* **Rule of Law:** Yes, an easement by necessity implied with a transfer of land may go unused through subsequent transfers, but still exercised at any time by the title holder.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + **Once under common ownership**: the defendant used to own both tracts of land.
  + **Landlocked**: because all other roads into and out of the plaintiff‚Äôs property have been blocked, there is no other way to access the property except by way of the defendant‚Äôs property.
* **Notes:** Necessity must arise at the moment of severance ü°™ the fact that there was permission of ingress and egress does not negate the fact that there was necessity

***Neponsit Prop. Owners‚Äô Ass‚Äôn v. Emigrant Indus. Savings Bank* (1938) ‚Äì p.563** ‚Äì allowing the creation of homeowners‚Äô associations

  + The owner association doesn‚Äôt own the land - will they meet the vertical equity requirement?

* **Notes:** Neponsit decision:

### ***Nahrstedt v. Lakeside Vill. Condo. Ass‚Äôn, Inc.* (Cal. 1994) ‚Äì p.607** ‚Äì reasonableness review of covenants; interpretation of state statute; presumption of validity

* **Facts:** The conditions and restrictions in developers‚Äô declaration for condo prohibited animals. Plaintiff lived with 3 cats, but argued that restriction shouldn‚Äôt be enforced because it was ‚Äúunreasonable as it applied to her since cats were kept indoors and were ‚Äúnoiseless‚Äù and ‚Äúcreated no nuisance.‚Äù
* **Issue:** Is the restriction at issue enforceable/ reasonable?
* **Holding:** Pet restriction is enforceable. Conditions and restrictions will be enforced unless unreasonable (‚Äúwholly arbitrary, violate fundamental public policy, or impose burdens on the use of land that outweighs any benefit‚Äù)
* **Reasoning:** Presumption of validity that restriction is reasonable unless arbitrary or against public policy.
  + Pet restriction in covenant is not arbitrary, but rationally relates to health, sanitation and noise concerns that are legit for residents of high-density condo.
  + Under the reasonable standard, enforcement of a restriction does not depend on conduct of particular condo owner, but must be uniformly enforced in development unless the owner can show that burden it imposes on affected properties so substantially outweighs benefits of restriction that should not be enforced against any owner.
  + Condos have a unique hybrid nature of property rights that account for new and innovative forms of ownership.
* **Dissent:** Substantial benefit of pet ownership and undue burden on use of property. Lauded the virtues of cats. Acknowledges that some restriction in condo development but owning pets shouldn‚Äôt be part of that compromise.
  + Argues that the restriction is unreasonable because the burden of restrictions outweighs the benefits; also is wholly arbitrary because your home is your private space
* **Notes:** Why generally a presumption of validity?
  + Discourages lawsuits by individual owners seeking exceptions
  + Best way to *protect settled expectations* ‚Äì ‚Äúsocial fabric‚Äù depends on expectations
    - Shouldn‚Äôt treat written instruments as ‚Äúworthless paper‚Äù

***Neuman v. Grandview at Emerald Hills, Inc.* (Fla. Dist. Ct. App. 2003) ‚Äì p. 620** ‚Äì bylaw; similar presumption of validity as if it were a deed, First Amendment review of covenants

* **Facts:** Neuman and other members of his condo association held regular religious services in their auditorium. When it found out, the Condo Association voted to ban use as a religious space by a 70% margin. Neuman sued alleging that this was a violation of his constitutional rights, and an unreasonable restriction.
* **Question:** Whether a condominium association rule banning the holding of religious services in the auditorium of the condo constitutes a violation of Florida‚Äôs condo law.
* **Rule of Law:** Rules in a declaration of condominium are valid if reasonable and don‚Äôt violate public policy or owners‚Äô constitutional rights.
* **Reasoning:**
  + Condo association agreements are akin to the ‚Äúconstitution‚Äù of the association.
    - Provided the rules within this are ‚Äúreasonable,‚Äù and do not violate the resident‚Äôs legal rights or the underlying public policy, the rule is valid.
    - Unique character of condominium living which, for the good of the majority, restricts rights residents would otherwise have were they living in a private separate residence
  + In this case, the board has not unreasonably restricted the right to assembly ‚Äì this is not a categorical ban on people gathering; it has just provided that there is one particular type of peaceable assembly that is not allowed.
    - Avoiding the potential divisiveness between various factions is a valid reason for restricting this right.
* **Notes:** Here, the court again shows broad deference to the statute and to the condo association‚Äôs rules and regulations.

***Davidson Brothers, Inc. v. D. Katz & Sons, Inc.*** (N.J. Super. Ct. App. Div. 1994) ‚Äì p.602 ‚Äì reasonableness review test with 8 factors; public policy review of covenants

* **Facts**: Davidson Brothers, a local supermarket chain in New Brunswick, NJ, sold their inner-city location to D. Katz, a rug manufacturer, and moved out to the suburbs. Before doing so, they placed a restrictive covenant on their property, forbidding it to operate as a supermarket for 40 years.
  + The city later acquired the space from D. Katz, and leased it to C-Town, a discount grocery store.
* **Question:** Whether Davidson Brothers can enforce their restrictive covenant against use as a grocery store.
* **Rule of Law:** No, the covenant was contrary to public policy, and did not meet the reasonableness test, so it should not now be enforceable. [Still in the world of real covenants, but court determines that ‚Äúrigid adherence‚Äù to the requirements is no longer warranted.]
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + New Jersey replaced the touch and concern test with an 8-factor analysis, of which this violates three:
    - Unreasonably long: restraint for 40 years is unreasonable.
    - Restraint of trade: this is simply rent-seeking behavior, meant to handicap competitors at the public‚Äôs expense.
    - Contrary to public policy: the inner-city area of New Brunswick is subject to serious and ongoing redevelopment efforts; this handicaps that.
      * There are no grocery stores within walking distance for a poor and immobile population segment.
      * Adverse impact on the city‚Äôs health
      * Sources: congressional committee report; expert testimony; demographic report (in the absence of a statute, this is evidence of state intervention)
    - Legislature will not enforce such a covenant that runs contrary to the established public policy.

**Limitations on Covenants: 14th Amendment & Restraints on Alienation**

   2. *Woodside Village Condo Association v. Jahren* ‚Äì prohibitions on leases of 9 months or more in a condo building are not unreasonable restraints on alienation; such partial restraints on alienation are ok, especially in a condo association, where you balance community goals with the individual freedom of alienation
      1. Presumption of validity also applies to restriction in a condo association‚Äôs declaration
5. Constitution / Federal statutes and law
   1. *Shelley v. Kraemer* ‚Äì state court enforcement of a racially restrictive covenant constitutes state action that violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment

State action revisited: from *Shelley* to *Evans* (Fourteenth Amendment) ‚Äì 3 theories:

1. State prevented ‚Äúwilling parties‚Äù from engaging in a transaction; ‚Äúbut for‚Äù state action
2. State offered its ‚Äúfull enforcement power‚Äù to support private discrimination ‚Äì accumulation of support
3. State provided a judicial forum for parties to enforce a state law; any action is state action

***Northwest Real Estate Co. v. Serio* (Md. 1929) ‚Äì p.638** ‚Äì rule against unreasonable restraints on alienation

* **Facts:** Northwest Real Estate developed a residential neighborhood in Baltimore. In each deed they placed a covenant requiring that they receive the consent of Northwest Real Estate before they sell to any third party.
  + Serio sought to purchase a home, but Northwest withheld its consent.
* **Question:** Whether the restraint on alienation sought to be imposed is void as repugnant to the granted estate
* **Rule of Law:** This policy is void as against the normal alienability of land.
  + Allowing the grantor to have control over whether a property is sold or not is incompatible with the freedom of alienation.
* **Other Opinions:** *Bond Dissenting ‚Äì* This rule thwarts the property owner‚Äôs ability to protect expectation interest in value of the land. Grantor was racist: ‚Äúprotect from invasion into the new neighborhood.‚Äù

***Shelley v. Kraemer* (1948) ‚Äì p.626** ‚Äì constitutional limitations on covenants

* **Facts:** Majority of property owners on both sides of a street signed a racially restrictive covenant. Shelley (a black man) bought one of the parcels in question. Respondents, who are owners of other property subject to the terms of the restrictive covenant, brought suit to restrain Shelley from taking possession of the property.
* **Rule:** State Action Doctrine - State court enforcement of a racially restrictive covenant constitutes state action that violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment
* **Holding:** By enforcing such restrictive covenants, states have acted to deny petitioners the equal protection of the laws guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment.
* **Reasoning:** Court enforcement would have the government, through its judicial branch, facilitating discrimination.

***Woodside Village Condo Ass‚Äôn, Inc. v. Jahren* (Fla. 2002) ‚Äì p.640** ‚Äì partial restraints on alienation

* **Facts:** Noticing that many of the condo units were being leased to non-owners, the Condo Association Board amended its constitution to prohibit leases to third parties lasting more than nine months, and forbidding leasing within the first twelve months of ownership.
  + Jahren sued, on the grounds that this was an unreasonable restrain on alienation.
* **Question:** Whether the Condo Association‚Äôs amendment prohibiting leases of 9 months or more is an unreasonable restriction on alienation.
* **Rule of Law:** Because Jahren opted into this democratic form of property ownership, he is bound to the amendments. Provided amendments are reasonable, they can be accepted.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Here, we‚Äôre weighing community goals with the individual freedom of alienation
  + Restriction contained in a Condo Association‚Äôs Declaration is clothed with a ‚Äúvery strong presumption of validity.‚Äù
    - Condo Association is democratic ‚Äì the owners opt into the ownership form, and choose willingly to abide by its rules.
    - Provided for completely by statute.
  + Provided that the amendment is reasonable, it is allowed.

**ZONING: Regulating the Power to Use through Zoning Law**

**Origins, Authority, Process, & Constitutional Limits**

***Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co.* (1926) ‚Äì p.424** ‚Äì zoning as valid use of state police power; Euclid deference

* **Facts:** The Village of Euclid imposed a zoning map, restricting use of certain pieces of property. Plaintiff‚Äôs property use was restricted in party to only single and two-family dwellings; in other parts it was restricted to also include certain types of commercial uses.
  + Plaintiff sued, alleging that this was a breach of his 14th Amendment Rights.
* **Question:** Whether imposing zoning laws constitute a violation of due process rights.
* **Rule of Law:** Zoning laws are sustainable as a valid use of the state‚Äôs police power, provided there is a rational basis (health, safety, morals or general welfare) for such regulation. If validity of the legislative classification for zoning purposes is fairly debatable, the legislative judgment must be allowed to control. Provisions must be clearly arbitrary and unreasonable to be unconstitutional, with no substantial relationship to public welfare
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Euclid deference ‚Äì if validity of the legislative classification for zoning purposes is fairly debatable, the legislative judgment must be allowed to control
  + The police power can be used to stop nuisance ‚Äì no serious debate that is an invalid use of the police power.
    - Zoning is arguably necessary to improve the health, safety, morals or general welfare.
      * Apartments can block the sun, be an eyesore, and depress value of surrounding properties.
* **Notes**: This deference opens the door for covenants and ‚Äúneutral‚Äù zoning policies that are concerned with racial segregation, even though using zoning to directly regulate is illegal (*Buchanan v. Warley* ‚Äì struck down a Louisville Kentucky statute that prohibited both white and black people from moving into and occupying a residence on a street that included a majority of owners of the opposite race).

***Belle Terre v. Boraas* (1974) ‚Äì p.1132 ‚Äì** ‚Äúrational relationship‚Äù between ordinance and the state‚Äôs objective; ordinance does not violate the Fourteenth Amendment; rational basis review is appropriate

* **Facts**: An ordinance in Belle Terre (small town of 700 people) restricts zoning to single-family dwellings and further limits the definition of family (two people, related by blood, adoption or marriage‚Ä¶).
* Owners leased home to SUNY students, and village served owners with Order to Remedy Violations. Students challenge constitutionality of ordinance.
* **Issue**: Is the zoning ordinance that prohibits groups of unrelated persons from sharing a home an appropriate use of the government‚Äôs police power/ constitutional?
* **Holding**: Ordinance is constitutionally permissible
* **Reasoning**: Under Euclid deference, ordinance is not unreasonable and arbitrary. The city had a rational basis for its prohibition on housing large numbers of unrelated individuals because creating a quiet neighborhood is a permissible state goal. The legislature is the correct body to define ‚Äúfamily,‚Äù rather than the judiciary. No fundamental right being burdened here ü°™ rational review should be applied. The ordinance did not restrict the freedom of association, as homeowners may entertain at their discretion.
* **Dissent**: Ordinance infringes a fundamental right of the First Amendment freedom of association and privacy and should be evaluated under strict scrutiny, not rational basis review.
  + Zoning officials may restrict the use of land, but may not properly restrict who the persons living on land may be, as that is protected by the due process clause (Fourteenth Amendment). Ordinance is both underinclusive and overinclusive. The court is overreaching.

***Moore v. City of East Cleveland* (1977) ‚Äì p.1135** ‚Äì Fourteenth Amendment rights violated by ordinance; must survive strict scrutiny

* **Facts:** a city‚Äôs zoning ordinance limited the number of unrelated people who could live together in one household and defined ‚Äúunrelated‚Äù to keep Mrs. Inez Moore (the appellant) from living with her two grandsons who were first cousins.
* **Holding:** The zoning ordinance was declared unconstitutional for infringing on the rights of the extended family (namely the fundamental right to keep the family together that includes an extended family) ‚Äì Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment
* **Reasoning:**
  + ‚Äú[When] the government intrudes on choices concerning family living arrangements, this Court must examine carefully the importance of the governmental interests advanced and the extent to which they are served by the challenged regulation . . . When thus examined, this ordinance cannot survive.‚Äù
* **Other Opinions**
  + *Brennan Concurring ‚Äì* Argues that the meaning of family is different in different racial and ethnic communities. Meaning is to be determined by reference to a broader social text that consists of history and tradition and our lived experience.
  + *White Dissent* ‚Äì this is a valid exercise of the police power because there is a rational basis.
  + *Stewart Dissent* ‚Äì *Belle Terre* should govern here, and restrict use because there is no valid use. Argument here would be that there is a ‚Äúrational relationship‚Äù between the ordinance and the city‚Äôs objectives to prevent overcrowding; Euclid deference should apply
* **Commentary:**
  + Ideology of the family, ideology of the judicial role and what it means for the court to be developing and enforcing ideas about rights that have no clear location in the text, language, or design of the constitution
  + It‚Äôs different to regulate the family itself, rather than just the household (as in *Belle Terre*)

**Challenging Zoning & Rezoning**

**Analyzing a zoning code/ordinance - FRAMEWORK**

1. Is there a rational relationship between the ordinance and the policy objectives?
   1. i.e. is this an appropriate use of state police power? (*Euclid / Belle Terre*)
2. Does the ordinance infringe upon a classification under the Fourteenth Amendment? Or a fundamental right (like First Amendment right of freedom of association)?
   1. If so, need to apply strict scrutiny of ordinance

**Notes on Prior Non-Conforming Use**

* Prior Non-Conforming Use: if a non-conforming use precedes the implementation of zoning rules, it is entitled to continue its current use, provided that there is **no substantial change** in the type of use or non-conformity.
  + *Amortization*: the city may allow prior non-conforming use, but make it time-bound and phase them out.
    - Test for proper amortization: whether the owner can obtain a reasonable return on their investment, weighing public gain against private loss.
  + Prior non-conforming uses can be transferred, but the city is also entitled to restrict such transfers by statute.

**Notes on Contract/Conditional Zoning**

* Definition: When municipalities rezone property in return for certain types of concessions.
* Source of challenges:
  + Unauthorized by a zoning enabling act
  + Inconsistent with the comprehensive plan
  + Illegal, preferential ‚Äúspot zoning‚Äù
* Today, some states allow contract zoning, and others do not. [see *Durand*]

***Belleville v. Parillo‚Äôs Inc.* (N.J. 1980) ‚Äì p.437 ‚Äì** prior nonconforming use

* **Facts:** Parillo‚Äôs operated as a restaurant in an area zoned for residential use; this was allowed because it was a preexisting non-conforming use. The owners made renovations and attempted to reopen as a disco; the city denied the permit, but Parillo‚Äôs went forward anyway.
  + The city gave Parillo‚Äôs a volation ticket, which it disputed as a permitted use under the prior non-conforming use exception.
* **Question:** Whether the prior non-conforming use exception to zoning laws applies in the case of a change from ‚Äúrestaurant‚Äù to ‚Äúdisco.‚Äù
* **Rule of Law:** An existing nonconforming use will be permitted to continue only if it is a continuance of substantially the same kind devoted at the time of the passage of the zoning ordinance. Because this was a discontinuity in use-type, it is prohibited.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Although non-conformity preceding the zoning code is permitted, future non-conformity must be of the same type.
    - Crowds, lines, loud music, and fewer tables elide the fact that the character of the use has substantially changed.

***Stone v. Wilton* (Iowa 1983) ‚Äì p.441** ‚Äì vested rights

* **Facts:** The Stones purchased a property in Wilton with the purpose of building federally-subsidized low-income housing. In selecting the site, they relied on an existing zoning map.
  + The Stones apply for a building permit, which the town of Wilton denies, and rezoned the property to a single-family use. The Stones sued on the grounds that they had a vested right to the property, and that the rezoning constituted a taking.
* **Question:** Whether the re-zoning was a taking under the **vested rights** doctrine.
* **Rule of Law:** No: in order to have vested rights in a zoning classification, the owner has to have made substantial efforts under the classification toward accomplishing the proposed project.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + There is no vested right here because there was not enough substantial action in conformity with the plan to build multifamily housing.
    - The Stones spent money securing financing, and obtaining permits, but it had not yet secured contractors, started construction or signed up tenants.
  + There is a rational basis for the taking in this case: worries about traffic and support of the electrical grid.
* **Notes:** States will have different rules re: vested rights. E.g. in WA, you get vested rights as soon as you apply for a permit! ü°™ ‚Äúearly vesting approach‚Äù promotes certainty because it identifies a moment in the development processes where people know the rules will remain. *Abbey Road Group v. City of Bonney Lake*
  + Some states allow vesting if a developer obtains site-specific approval for development even before a permit is issued (NJ)
  + Some states even grant developers vested rights to use their land in accordance with the zoning law in place at the time that they apply for site-specific approval (CO, MA, TX)

***Durand v. IDC Bellingham, L.L.C.* (Mass. 2003)** ‚Äì p.447 ‚Äì ‚Äúsale‚Äù of police power

* **Facts:** IDC Bellingham owns and operates a power plant in the town. It asked for the town to rezone a part of the surrounding area, but the town rejected their request. It then offered $8 million to help the town‚Äôs budget shortfall, and to assist in the construction of a nearby school.
  + Citizens of the town brought a civil action against IDC Bellingham, alleging that the zoning was void because it was illegal ‚Äúcontract zoning.‚Äù
* **Question:** Whether the zoning ordinance was void because it constituted ‚Äúcontract zoning.‚Äù
* **Rule of Law:** An agreement including a voluntary offer of public benefits in exchange for a vote in favor of rezoning a parcel of land does not invalidate the legislative decision to rezone.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The test is whether rezoning was arbitrary, unreasonable, and not substantially related to the public health, safety, or general welfare of the town (*Euclid*)
  + ‚ÄúContract zoning‚Äù is illegal if the municipality promises to rezone a property (before a vote) in return for negotiated consideration.
    - Here, IDC made a voluntary offer to the city, which the city did not need to accept. There was no bargaining involved
    - All appropriate processes were followed
    - The court is deferential to legislative decision making ü°™ ‚Äúfairly debatable‚Äù, so apply Euclid deference
  + There was a valid public purpose, so this clears the hurdle for the police power doctrine.
* **Other Opinions:** *Spina Dissenting* ‚Äì the parties struck a deal: money for a zoning change. Because there were extraneous considerations, the validity of the agreement can be doubted.
  + This was a ‚Äúsale of police power‚Äù

**HUD: Building a Disparate Treatment claim under the FHA: [based on 2013 rule]**

1. Plaintiffs establish prima facie case of disparate treatment.
   * Impact of zoning on minorities in community
   * Sequence of events leading to zoning decision
2. Burden shifts to defendant to provide a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for its actions
3. Burden shifts back to plaintiffs to show intent to discriminate
   * Whether defendant would have taken same action solely based on alleged legitimate reasons
   * Whether racial animus was a motivating factor

**HUD: Building a Disparate Impact claim under the FHA: [based on 2013 rule]**

1. Plaintiffs establish prima facie case of disparate impact
   1. Adverse impact on protected group, or
   2. Zoning ordinance perpetuates segregation
2. Burden shifts to defendant to prove that actions furthered legitimate bona fide government interest and that no alternative would serve that interest with less discriminatory effect
3. Plaintiffs need to prove that there‚Äôs an available alternative practice that has less disparate impact

***NAACP v. Mount Laurel* (N.J. 1975) ‚Äì p.463 ‚Äì** Mount Laurel doctrine; NJ state law ONLY

* (Minority Rule ‚Äì ‚Äúinclusionary‚Äù zoning/town must provide lower income housing options ‚Äì responsibility to surrounding communities)
* **Facts:** Town‚Äôs land use regulation creates large industrial zone (mostly unused), for large residential zones, and one small retail zone. Residential zones include restrictions to protect home values, making property unaffordable for any but relatively wealthy residents.
  + Town‚Äôs rationale for regulation is taxes ‚Äì low density housing with fewer children means good ratables ‚Äì use that generates taxes with less demand for services.
  + Lower property density leads to higher property values ‚Äì same reasoning as Sutherland in *Euclid*.
* **Issue:** Whether community may, by a system of land use regulation, make it physically and economically impossible to provide low and moderate income housing in the municipality for the various categories of persons who need and want it and thereby exclude such people from living within its confines.
* **Holding:** **Under NJ Constitution -** General welfare not promoted by excluding class of people ‚Äì not permissible use of police power. Developing municipalities have both an affirmative and negative obligation to provide the reasonable opportunity for housing for all categories of people who may want to live there.
  + Obligations must be met unless municipality can meet burden of establishing valid superseding reasons
  + **‚ÄúLand use regulation is encompassed within the state‚Äôs police power. It is required that, affirmatively, a zoning regulation, like any police power enactment, must promote public health, safety, morals, or the general welfare.‚Äù**
  + **Prima facie** case established. Township‚Äôs justification ‚Äì fiscal concerns/good ratables ‚Äì not sufficient. If this justification were deemed permissible, no town would provide housing essential for lower income people.
  + Shelter is among most basic human needs. Adequate housing for all is essential in promotion of general welfare. Town can‚Äôt deny housing options to lower income people primarily for local fiscal reasons.
* **Aftermath:** Created constitutional mandate for inclusionary zoning. *Mount Laurel II* extends Mount Laurel doctrine to cover all municipalities and authorizes affirmative remedies to encourage low-moderate income housing, with regional trial judges to handle cases. Courts empowered to order affirmative remedies, including government subsidies and incentives for private developers ‚Äì builders remedies also created, ordering municipalities to allow developer to construct projects with low-income housing unless environmental or other concerns. Legislature responded by creating state FHA. Also allowed municipalities to pay neighboring communities to fulfill their inclusionary zoning obligations.

***MHANY Management v. Cty. of Nassau* (2d Cir. 2016) ‚Äì p.1041** ‚Äì building a disparate treatment and disparate impact claim re: zoning

* **Facts:** Garden City is an overwhelmingly white suburb in Nassau County, and the site of the Social Services building. Nassau County originally planned to sell its former to a private developer of multifamily housing, but many residents objected, so the city council jammed a downzoning through, such that it could be developed for single-family or townhomes.
  + Even though it would yield substantially less money for the county, it had the effect of preserving the racial makeup of the community.
* **Question:** Whether Garden City‚Äôs choice to change the zoning laws violated the disparate impact prong of the Fair Housing Act.
* **Rule of Law:** Because the District Court applied the incorrect standard for determining disparate impact, the case should be remanded for further consideration.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The District Court applied the traditional burden-shifting analysis used in the Second Circuit.
  + However, since then, HUD has adopted the regulations enumerated in 24 C.F.R. ¬ß100.500, explaining a new burden-shifting regime.
    - Under *Chevron, U.S.A., Inc. v. NRDC, Inc.*, the Second Circuit should defer to HUD‚Äôs analysis.
  + Evidence is incomplete based upon the HUD standard.
    - Plaintiffs ‚Äúmore than established a prima facie case.‚Äù
    - Defendant identified legitimate government interest going the opposite way: traffic and strain on public schools.
    - The case is remanded to determine of the Plaintiffs had offered enough evidence to overcome the Defense‚Äôs legitimate justifications.
* **Notes:** New rule from HUD as of June 2018; could do away with disparate impact claims altogether

**Eminent Domain**

**Eminent domain** ‚Äì takings are rooted in the Fifth Amendment: **‚Äú**nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.‚Äù

1. Private property
2. Public use
3. Just compensation

**When may the government exercise its eminent domain power to acquire private property?**

Public use ‚Äì what is a ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù?

1. Public use = public purpose (*Kelo* majority)
2. Public use = truly public purpose (Kennedy)
3. Public use = prevention/elimination of social harm (O‚ÄôConnor)
   1. Defining harm: *Midkiff* = land oligopoly; *Berman* = ‚Äúblight‚Äù (no set definition)
4. Public use = public ownership or access (Thomas)
5. Public use = public necessity + public oversight + independent public significance (MI ‚Äì *Wayne County v. Hathcock* ‚Äì economic development was not a sufficient purpose to justify the condemnation of private lands for transfer to another private owner)

Just Compensation

* Eminent domain requires being compensated the ‚Äúfair market value‚Äù of a property in a taking.
  + Fair market value will always undercompensate the owner because they are not willing sellers at the market price.
    - This also does not compensate owners for the costs associated with moving or other types of hardship.
  + Fair market value as compensation for a failure of the political process
  + Also cheaper to determine the fair market value instead of the owner‚Äôs subjective valuation.
  + Some states have statutory compensation schemes mandating compensation at 125% or 150% of the market value.

***Kelo v. City of New London* (2005) ‚Äì p.1150** ‚Äì defining ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù

* **Facts:** The city of New London had been designated by the State as a ‚Äúdistressed municipality.‚Äù To increase taxes and revitalize, the city Council approved a development plan that would allow the New London Development Corporation to create a private development.
  + The development would contain new homes, private commercial development, a hotel, and an office park
  + The City authorized the Development Corporation to use eminent domain in support of its development efforts. Kelo and 15 other property-owners refused to sell, and alleged that eminent domain was improper under the circumstances.
* **Question:** Whether a private development undertaken by a private developer could permissibly be called a ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù within the meaning of the Takings Clause of the 5th Amendment
* **Rule of Law:** ‚ÄúBecause the plan unquestionably serves a public purpose, the takings challenged here satisfy the public use requirement of the Fifth Amendment.‚Äù
* **Stevens‚Äôs Legal Reasoning/majority [public purpose]**
  + The Court has a longstanding tradition of deferring to the legislature on questions of the ‚Äúpublic interest‚Äù
    - *Berman v. Parker*: Court upheld a taking as a part of a redevelopment plan targeting a blighted area of Washington DC. Even though the owner‚Äôs department store itself was not blighted, the legislature determined it was a public use.
    - *Hawai‚Äôi Housing Auth. v. Midkiff*: deferred to the state‚Äôs determination that concentrated ownership could be redressed by a taking.
    - *Ruckleshaus v*. *Monsanto Co.*: could allow other chemical companies to access trade secrets submitted to the Government so long as there is just compensation.
    - Legislature here shows blight ‚Äì that‚Äôs enough to uphold the decision here.
  + There is no bright line rule against development ‚Äì the legislature has decided that the public will benefit. Even if private developers get money, public still can be served. Institutional competency argument ‚Äì should defer to the legislature
  + No assurance necessary that public benefits will actually accrue ‚Äì would break from the Court‚Äôs traditional Jurisprudence and grind development to a halt.
* **Kennedy‚Äôs Concurring Opinion [Public purpose + heightened review re: favoritism]**
  + Agrees that the legislature should receive substantial deference in the form of a rational basis review.
  + BUT there should be a review of the record by the court to determine whether there was any signs of favoritism toward the private party involved.
    - Not the case here: it wasn‚Äôt even known that Pfizer was the private party involved before the takings commenced.
* **O‚ÄôConnor‚Äôs Dissenting Opinion [elimination of a harm]**
  + Economic development takings are unconstitutional because it is not intervening to eliminate a harm.
    - *Berman* and *Midkiff* are both distinguishable in that the government was intervening to eliminate a harm of an extraordinary nature.
    - New London doesn‚Äôt allege that Kelo‚Äôs house was a blight ‚Äì nothing in the majority‚Äôs opinion could stop taking from one private landowner, and giving to the other.
      * This will inevitably favor the rich.
* **Thomas‚Äôs Dissenting Opinion [actual use by the public]**
  + The most natural reading of the Takings Clause is to construe ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù as allowing the government to take property only if the government owns, or the public has legal right to use the property.
    - This is supported by a dictionary definition from the Founding Generation.
    - Early use of eminent domain was only for public use
  + The current use will inevitably disfavor the urban poor, and people of color.
* **Notes:** States have interpreted ‚Äúpublic use‚Äù differently:
  + Justice Kennedy [public purpose + heightened review]:
    - Arizona
    - Montana
    - Pennsylvania
  + Justice O‚ÄôConnor [elimination of a harm]:
    - Colorado
    - New Jersey
    - Ohio
    - Michigan (Public necessity + Public oversight + Independent public significance) ‚Äì see *Hathcock*
  + Justice Thomas [actual use by the public]:
    - Washington

**Regulatory Takings**

**Regulatory takings** typically involve efforts to weight benefits and harms.

* Issue: has there been a taking?
* General rule: While property may be regulated to a certain extent, if regulation goes too far, it will be recognized as a taking. ‚Äì Holmes, *Penn Coal v. Mahon*
  + Determining whether the harm should be compensated is dependent on 1) the relative harms imposed/the public interest, and 2) the extent of the diminution of value caused by the harm
  + Note: Court effectively repudiated result of *Mahon* in *Keystone Bituminous Coal Association v. DeBenedictis*, but the principle *Mahon* established is still settled law
  + Relevant question is whether the action forces some people alone to bear the burden of conduct that should be borne by society as a whole. *Armstrong v. United States*
* Justifications for regulatory takings:
  + Tradition: This justification forces the government to compensate outside of where the actor is engaging in a public nuisance or a noxious use.
    - This justification reinforces the traditional definition of property, which allows owners to use their property so long as it is a permissible kind of use, and forces compensation when the government needs to take otherwise.
  + Efficiency: Compensation should be required to promote welfare-maximizing takings.
    - Forcing compensation means the public internalizes the costs of takings legislation, and will only undertake it if it is worth the cost.
  + Distributive Justice: Rests on the question of whether or not the property owner, in all fairness and justice, should bear the costs
    - If an individual owner is suffering a unique burden, they should be compensated for it.

***Penn Central* 3-prong test: ü°™** apply this unless you can make an argument for per se taking; ad-hoc test for justice and fairness; takings are circumstance-dependent

1. Economic impact
2. Nature of government action
3. Interference with investment-backed expectations

Note: In *Penn Central v. New York City* specifically, the Court found that landmark designation has not interfered with rights of the parcel. This was a limited restriction on a large plot of land ‚Äì could have built a smaller building on top of station

* Precedent for this decision:
  + *Hadacheck v. Sebastian* ‚Äì diminution in property value alone is not enough to establish a taking (87.5% diminution in value caused by zoning law)
  + *Miller v. Schoene* ‚Äì no taking when the state ordered destruction of many ornamental red cedar trees to prevent the transmission of cedar rust fatal to apple trees cultivated nearby because the apple industry was important in that states

***Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Mahon* (1921) ‚Äì p.1184** ‚Äì underlying principle that if regulation goes too far, it constitutes a taking

* **Facts:** Pennsylvania Coal Co. conveyed the rights to the surface of land to Mahon in 1878, but reserved the right to mine underneath the surface for coal. Mahon brought the action to enjoin PA Coal from continuing to mine underneath their property, on the basis that the PA legislature passed a law forbidding mining in a way that causes homes to cave in.
* **Rule of Law:** Property may be regulated to a certain extent, but if regulation goes too far it will be recognized as a taking.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Holmes balancing test: Determining whether the harm should be compensated is dependent on 1) the relative harms imposed/the public interest, and 2) extent of the diminution of value caused by the harm
    - This is the case of an individual losing their property ‚Äì there is a limited public interest in this individual‚Äôs property ‚Äì it is a private loss.
      * Also took a risk investing in the surface rights above a coal mine.
    - The coal company loses its property, which is the essence of its business interest and source of its income.
* **Other Opinions:** *Brandeis Dissenting* ‚Äì This does not constitute a taking, but rather a ‚Äúprohibition of noxious use‚Äù. Should adopt a bright line rule that if there is a public nuisance, then regulation should be allowed.
  + Police power‚Äôs job is to protect the public from danger
    - The public is a group ‚Äì not just a collection of individual property owners.
* **Notes:** Result of this case is no longer good law, but underlying principle is alive and well

***Penn. Central Transp. Co. v. New York City* (1978) ‚Äì p.1192 -** ad-hoc test for fairness and justice; *Penn Central* multi-factor test

* **Facts:** The New York Landmark Preservation Committee designated Grand Central Station a landmark. Penn Central, its owner, subsequently proposed to build a tower above Grand Central. The Preservation Committee rejected both suggestions.
  + The owners of Grand Central then moved directly to challenge the denial, claiming that the Landmark Preservation Committee had ‚Äútaken‚Äù their property without just compensation.
* **Question:** Whether the application of New York City‚Äôs Landmark Preservation Law constitutes a ‚Äútaking.‚Äù
* **Rule of Law:** Because the restrictions are substantially related to the promotion of the general welfare, and did not substantially interfere with the rights or uses of the property, this was not a taking under the 5th Amendment.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + *Penn Central* multi-factor test: Takings are circumstance-dependent: factors for determining a taking include 1) the economic impact of the regulation, and the way it interferes with the property; 2) the character of the government‚Äôs action; 3) interference with a distinct or reasonable investment-backed expectation.
  + Landmark designation has not interfered with the rights of the parcel.
    - This represents a limited restriction on a large plot of land (which included much of east midtown).
    - Regulations can significantly diminish the value of the land (see, e.g. *Euclid*)
    - Not like ‚Äúreverse spotting‚Äù ‚Äì designations are not arbitrary or discriminatory, but are based on a well-conceived public interest in preserving buildings.
      * There is reciprocity of advantage.
  + The property can still be used in the way it was intended.
    - The primary purpose of the station is to operate as a train station, which is uninterrupted by the regulation.
    - Air rights could be sold to a third party for market value.
    - Could have built a smaller building above Grand Central, which could have conceivably received the Landmark Commission‚Äôs approval.
  + There is precedent for this decision ‚Äì see *Hadacheck*
* **Other Opinions:** *Rehnquist Dissenting*
  + The question instead should be whether the costs associated with landmark preservation should be imposed exclusively on owners.
    - There is no reciprocity in advantage when 1/10th of 1% of owners forfeit huge sums of money for the public benefit.
    - This isn‚Äôt preventing nuisance or noxious use of the property; but rather is prescribing the proper use.

**Per Se Takings**

A search for bright line rules: ‚Äúper se takings‚Äù:

* Physical invasions ‚Äì temporary/limited vs. permanent (*PruneYard / Loretto*)
* Complete deprivation of economically-viable use (*Lucas*)

***PruneYard Shopping Ctr. v. Robins* (1980) ‚Äì p.1206** ‚Äì Penn Central factors; temporary, limited physical occupation

* **Facts:** A group of high school students, handed out pamphlets in protest of the United Nations resolution against ‚ÄúZionism.‚Äù A security guard asked them to vacate the premise.
  + The students sued that this was a violation of their free speech rights under the California Constitution, and won. The shopping center appealed, claiming that such a law constitutes a taking that should be compensated. (cf. *Lloyd v. Tanner*)
* **Question:** Whether California must compensate PruneYard for removing the ability to restrict speech on its premise.
* **Rule of Law:** Because this is a temporary physical intrusion that can be curtailed if it interferes with the property‚Äôs use, it does not constitute a taking.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + The relevant question is whether this forces some people alone to bear the burden of conduct that all should be borne by society as a whole (*Armstrong*). The answer here is no.
    - Application of *Penn Central* multi-factor test
    - Not prohibiting time, place, and means restrictions ‚Äì just categorical restrictions
    - Property was opened to the public ‚Äì PruneYard loses some of its ability to make categorical restrictions as a result.
  + Court distinguishes from *Kaiser Aetna* case (private marina) ‚Äì in *Kaiser*, they did a ton of work to clear out the marina and relied on their expectation that they‚Äôd be able to charge club membership fees afterwards to recoup their investment
* **Other Opinions:**
  + *Marshall Concurring* ‚Äì allowing compensation would freeze the common law, and hinder states‚Äô ability to exercise the police power.
  + *Powell Concurring* ‚Äì the state cannot force an owner to endorse or support speech that the owner finds repugnant.

***Kaiser Aetna v. United States*, 444 U.S. 164 (1979**) ‚Äì cited in PruneYard and other cases

* **Facts:** The owner of a private piece of land invested in connecting a shallow private lagoon to the ocean, and making it a private marina accessible only to dues-paying members. The Federal Government attempted to force them to make it accessible to the public afterwards.
* **Rule of Law:** Requiring public access to the water compromised the owners‚Äô ability to exclude, one of their most vital rights, and thus constituted a taking.
  + It also interfered with the owner‚Äôs reasonable investment-backed expectations.

***Loretto v. Teleprompter Manhattan CATV Corp.* (1982) ‚Äì p.1212** ‚Äì per se rule: permanent physical invasion

* **Facts:** Jean Loretto purchased a rental apartment building in New York. New York state law compels landlords to allow cable television companies to install cable boxes, with only $1 compensation. the previous owner had given Teleprompter permission to install a cable television box in the apartment.
  + Loretto did not discover the cable box until she had purchased the building. She brought a lawsuit alleging that it was a taking without just compensation.
* **Question:** Whether a minor but permanent physical occupation of an owner‚Äôs property authorized by government constitutes a ‚Äútaking‚Äù of property for which just compensation is due under the 5th and 14th Amendment.
* **Rule of Law:** A permanent physical occupation authorized by government is a taking without regard to the public interests it may serve.
* **Legal Reasoning:**
  + Physical intrusion of government is a property restriction of ‚Äúunusually serious character.‚Äù
    - When that restriction is permanent, there is a bright-line categorical rule against doing so without compensation.
    - Physical occupation threatens to destroy existing property rights by dispossessing them of the right to exclude and the right to use.
  + Teleprompter‚Äôs intrusion was physical, and meant to last in perpetuity ‚Äì that‚Äôs precisely the kind that is not supposed to happen without compensation.
  + Potential animating concern: that the government might require a landlord to devote a substantial portion of their building to, say, vending machines, with all profits to be retained by owners of those services ü°™ an example of impermissible favoritism? (see Kennedy concurrence in *Kelo*)

  + The economic impact here is *di minimus* ‚Äì there is no compromise of investment expectations.
  + If the owner does not want the physical occupation, they can change its use.
  + *PruneYard* represented a deeper and more pervasive invasion of the owner‚Äôs property.

***Lucas v. S.C. Coastal Council* (1992) ‚Äì p.1223** ‚Äì per se rule: deprivation of all economically-viable use; denominator problem

* **Facts:** Lucas purchased beach front land on Isle of the Palms with the intention of building a house on it. Then the state passed the *Beachfront Management Act*, which forbade development on certain points of erosion. The effect was to prohibit development on Lucas‚Äô land.
  + The local court found that this was a taking without just compensation, and that the effect was to render the land ‚Äúvalueless.‚Äù
* **Question:** Whether a regulation that renders a piece of land valueless, constitutes a ‚Äútaking‚Äù without just compensation.
* **Rule of Law:** Unless the use constitutes a nuisance at common law, any action that deprives the owner of all economically-viable use constitutes a taking.
* Legal Reasoning:
  + There is a bright-line rule: where a regulation renders a piece of land valueless, it constitutes a taking.
    - There is a heightened risk of the government making a false and pretextual claim of harm. Whether a legislature can justify a taking by citing a harm depends on whether they have a ‚Äústupid staff‚Äù
  + The ‚Äúharmful or noxious use‚Äù principle does not decide this case; the proper inquiry is the ‚Äútotal taking inquiry‚Äù ü°™ state‚Äôs restrictions must be consistent with existing, background principles of common law of nuisance
    - Applying state nuisance law to assess the degree of harm to public, adjacent property, posed by the claimant‚Äôs proposed activities, social value of the claimant‚Äôs activities
* Other Opinions:
  + *Kennedy Concurrence* ‚Äì while the court traditionally shows deference to district court holdings, it is hard to believe that beach front property would have no value.
    - Nuisance is too narrow a frame for when a taking should be allowed without compensation: courts should instead consider the impact on owners‚Äô reasonable expectations.
  + *Blackmun Dissent* ‚Äì The Court is inserting its own values in an attempt to establish a value-free takings jurisprudence.
    - There is no objective means of determining value of a property, and when it is truly deprived of ‚Äúall value.‚Äù
    - In determining nuisance at common law, there is always a value-driven judgement.
      * Understandings of what constitute a nuisance that must be regulated has shifted over time ‚Äì why are we grounding this analysis in a ‚Äútraditional‚Äù understanding?
  + *Stevens Dissent* ‚Äì The rule established by the majority is totally arbitrary. Conceivably, someone who loses 95% of their property value would not be able to recover, but someone who loses the entire value of their property would.
    - The rule also doesn‚Äôt solve the denominator problem: does a regulation prohibiting development of 5 acres constitute a 100% taking of 5 acres or 10% taking of the 50 acres (example: developer who seeks to develop 5 acres). Supreme Court has emphasized that the inquiry ‚Äúmust focus on the parcel as a whole‚Äù ü°™ but what does this mean?
      * *Loveladies Harbor v. US* ‚Äì Federal Circuit held that courts must use a ‚Äúflexible approach‚Äù to determine the appropriate denominator
      * Another possibility is the ‚Äúwhole property‚Äù owned by the developer
      * Or the ‚Äúdevelopable tract‚Äù
      * When air rights are at issue, a height limit does not constitute a 100% taking of air rights over that limit; the denominator is the tract as a whole
* Additional notes:
  + Only a 100% diminution in value of the property triggered the *Lucas* rule. *First English Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of LA*
  + *Palazzolo v. Rhode Island* (2001) ‚Äì p.1237 ‚Äì Acquisition of title after a regulatory law was passed did not protect the state from a takings claim. The fact that the owner took title with notice of the regulation did not, by itself, immunize the state from facing a challenge to the regulation as a taking of property ‚Äì some enactments of regulations might be unreasonable, and property owners have the right to challenge what they view as unreasonable limitations on the use and value of the land
  + Destruction of real and personal property in cases of ‚Äúactual necessity‚Äù need not be compensated, e.g. *Eggleston v. Pierce County* (Wash. 2003) ‚Äì no taking when a home was rendered uninhabitable because of damages caused by police searching the house pursuant to search warrant. Also, seizure of evidence for a criminal trial can never be a taking (*Kam-Almaz v US*)
  + Temporary taking = owners might be able to obtain damages if they were prevented from using their property for any period. Regulatory body can choose between enforcing the regulation (compensation must be paid for the permanent taking) or rescinding the regulation (in which case the owner may have a claim for damages for a temporary taking for the period when the regulation was enforced). *First English Lutheran Church*
    - *Tahoe-Sierra*: how long must a ban be to give rise to a taking? An owner cannot isolate the slice of time over which it had the right to build as a distinctive (temporal piece of property. The parcel-as-a-whole rule prohibits this kind of conceptual severance [similar to the denominator problem for takings claims]

***Babbitt v. Youpee* (1997) ‚Äì p.1251** ‚Äì ILCA and whether the remedy constitutes a taking because it abrogates individuals‚Äô right to pass on property; application of *Penn Central* multi-factor test

* **Facts:** Congress enacted the Indian Land Consolidation Act (ILCA) in 1984 to remedy the extreme fractionation of land resulting from previous land allotment policies (designed to destroy Indian culture by diving tribal land among individual owners).
  + In *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock*, the Supreme Court upheld the allotment of land against challenges based on the Fifth Amendment.
  + Congress tries to fix the problem of fractionation, but there‚Äôs a problem with ¬ß 207 of the ILCA ‚Äì we essentially have a problem of heirs‚Äô property.
  + In *Hodel v. Irving*, the Court found that this constitutes a taking (because it‚Äôs an abrogation of the right to pass on property), since the land escheats back to tribe after the owner‚Äôs death, if there is no will (instead of to the owner‚Äôs heirs)
* **Issue**: Whether the amended ¬ß 207 of the ILCA constitutes a taking
* **Holding**: Amended ¬ß 207 is still a taking.
* **Reasoning:** Court applies *Penn Central* multi-factor test (i.e. Court declines to find a categorical taking)
  + Reviews each of the amendments made in light of each factor
  + Determinative factor is the character of the government action ‚Äì it is a restriction on an important right: the right to pass on property
* **Stevens dissent:**
  + Government has a valid interest here in addressing the fractionation issue
  + Youpee had notice and opportunity to draft a will in order to pass on his property to his heirs
  + The problem is not with the constitutionality of the statute, but rather with the fact that Youpee may have lacked access to good lawyers / legal advice

**HUD‚Äôs Discriminatory Effects Rule: 24 C.F.R. ¬ß 100.500 Discriminatory Effect Prohibited.**

* Liability may be established under the Fair Housing Act based on a practice's discriminatory effect, as defined in paragraph (a) of this section, even if the practice was not motivated by a discriminatory intent. The practice may still be lawful if supported by a legally sufficient justification, as defined in paragraph (b) of this section. The burdens of proof for establishing a violation under this subpart are set forth in paragraph (c) of this section.
  + (a) Discriminatory effect. A practice has a discriminatory effect where it actually or predictably results in a disparate impact on a group of persons or creates, increases, reinforces, or perpetuates segregated housing patterns because of race, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin.
  + (b) Legally sufficient justification.
    - (1) A legally sufficient justification exists where the challenged practice:
      * (i) Is necessary to achieve one or more substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interests of the respondent, with respect to claims brought under 42 U.S.C. 3612, or defendant, with respect to claims brought under 42 U.S.C. 3613 or 3614; and
      * (ii) Those interests could not be served by another practice that has a less discriminatory effect.
    - (2) A legally sufficient justification must be supported by evidence and may not be hypothetical or speculative. The burdens of proof for establishing each of the two elements of a legally sufficient justification are set forth in paragraphs (c)(2) and (c)(3) of this section.
  + (c) Burdens of proof in discriminatory effects cases.
    - (1) The charging party, with respect to a claim brought under 42 U.S.C. 3612, or the plaintiff, with respect to a claim brought under 42 U.S.C. 3613 or 3614, has the burden of proving that a challenged practice caused or predictably will cause a discriminatory effect.
    - (2) Once the charging party or plaintiff satisfies the burden of proof set forth in paragraph (c)(1)of this section, the respondent or defendant has the burden of proving that the challenged practice is necessary to achieve one or more substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interests of the respondent or defendant.
    - (3) If the respondent or defendant satisfies the burden of proof set forth in paragraph (c)(2) of this section, the charging party or plaintiff may still prevail upon proving that the substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interests supporting the challenged practice could be served by another practice that has a less discriminatory effect.
  + (d) Relationship to discriminatory intent. A demonstration that a practice is supported by a legally sufficient justification, as defined in paragraph (b) of this section, may not be used as a defense against a claim of intentional discrimination.
