## WATER RIGHTS

- **[Diffuse water]{.underline}**: pest to be eradicated; water flowing
  everywhere ðŸ¡ª Every landowner has a right to use self-help to expel
  diffuse water from their property, **even if that means pushing the
  water onto someone else's property**

  - Note this right is reciprocal - the "someone else" will have every
    right to push the water back onto your property!

  - Some jurisdictions have a requirement that the steps taken to expel
    the water be **reasonable** (i.e. "reasonable requirement")

- **[Defined channel]{.underline}**: valuable resources

- Riparian rights: pertain to properties adjacent to a waterway that (a)
  governs the use of surface water and (b) gives all owners of land
  contiguous to streams, lakes, and ponds equal rights to the water,
  whether the right is exercised or not. Runs w/the land + can't be
  severed.

**1) Egalitarian Natural flow**: Rejected in US; [as a prop owner near a
natural water source, **can remove water [to the extent that you
preserve the natural water flow to the people downstream of you/do not
disrupt natural flow]{.underline}**]{.mark}

- **Disadvantages**: Implicit idea that everyone is entitled an equal
  amount, everyone gets the same so long as they don't disrupt the
  natural flow

- Can prevent beneficial use of water

  - If even a small amount removed impacts natural flow, could wind up
    w/ most water unused

- Can present **holdout problem**

  - You can use amount that does disturb natural flow if you get
    permission from riparian owners downstream

    - But when you get to last riparian owner, they will likely hold out
      for \$\$\$\$

- Does not apply in the US bc US legal system generally favors
  **productive property use**

- Rejected in ***Evans v. Merriweather***

**2) Riparian Owner Reasonable use ðŸ¡ª East Coast:** [each riparian owner
has a right to use the water, but the use must be **reasonable, and the
use must not more injurious than is necessary to accomplish the
reasonable use**]{.mark}

- taken in ***Evans v. Merriweather***

- **Reasonable use**: socially valuable ðŸ¡ª natural use \> artificial use;
  DEP ON CONTEXT

  - **[Artificial use]{.underline}**: for commercial use, \$\$, comfort

  - **[Natural use]{.underline}**: essential (hydration/household) ðŸ¡ª
    depends on environment

- **[Advantages]{.underline}**: Allows for more productive use, and
  specifically reasonable use

- **[Disadvantages]{.underline}**: need criterion for determining what
  is reasonable

- Evans v. Merriweather privileges natural uses over artificial uses
  (modern language: essential vs. non-essential uses)

> *If both riparian owners have artificial uses, both can use it, but
> neither has a priority*

**3) First Appropriation ðŸ¡ª Dry, Western States[: First appropriator of
water in a stream for a beneficial purpose has a right to that amount to

]{.mark}**

- ***Coffin v. Left Hand Ditch Company***

## AD COELUM

- General Idea: if you own the surface of the land, then you own up to
  the core and above the surface up to infinity

**SUBSURFACE**:

**Edwards v. Sims, 232 Ky. 791 (1929):** ad coelum / use of subterranean
land of which you do not have surface rights to requires you to **share
profits** with surface right owner

- **FACTS**: Court applied ad coelum doctrine + said that cave creator
  had to share his profits w/ neighbor who owned some property above the
  mine

- **MAJORITY**: cites ad coelum doctrine as it applies to mines; **the
  owner of realty, unless there has been a division of the estate, is
  entitled to the free and unfettered control of his own land above,
  upon, and beneath the surface** ðŸ¡ª no real policy issues

- **DISSENT'S ALTERNATIVE RULE:** Focuses on the policy issues (favors
  active, creative owners; easily administrable) + rejects ad coelum; Ad
  coelum/possession rule should be that owners of land own everything
  above and under the surface **to the extent that owner can:**

  - **(1) subject the land to his dominion/control AND**

  - **(2) use it to his profit/benefit**

- Thus, the cave should belong absolutely to him who owns the entrance,
  and this ownership extends to wherever is connected and explored after
  the entrance

- P did not have access to the entrance of cave from their own property,
  D had the access to the cave on his property; D did not dig the cave
  out, make it tourist attraction, didn't do any of the work

- Implies active users should be rewarded

- P couldn't have exercised control over the space below, had no way of
  doing so

- What D owed was something "indefinable... it was a vision translated
  to reality"

## Adverse Possession

- Adverse possessor's (AP) right is even good against the original
  owner, despite the wrongdoing.

  - **When an owner sits on her original right to exclude, the statute
    of limitation for challenging the original unlawful entry expire**s,
    **the AP becomes the new owner and can exercise the right to exclude
    against all world and the original owner**.

- **Policy:** [(1)]{.mark} The AP has come to realize that he has this
  land; [(2)]{.mark} Favoring active users over people who are sleeping
  on their rights, b/c AP doesn't happen when the true owner (TO) is
  around. TO does not actually use the property but monitor the property
  to keep out trespassers; [(3)]{.mark} Decreases the transaction cost
  of determining title

- [ELEMENTS:]{.underline}

- **(1) actual possession**

- **(2) exclusive**

- **(3) open, notorious, and visible;**

- **(4) continuous and interrupted**

- **(5) adverse (hostile) under claim of right or color of title**

- **(6) for the statutorily required time**

> **Scott v. Anderson-Tully Co.: [illustrated (1) actual, (2) excl, (3)
> open + notorious]{.mark}**

- **FACTS:** AP painted fence, cut timber, issued hunting license for
  underdeveloped land

<!-- -->

- **REASONING:** In assessing whether AP had possession of the title,
  the court added a 7th requirement: **must be peaceful**

**[(1) ACTUAL POSSESSION]{.underline}**: Possessory acts vary
w/character of land, have to use it as a landowner would

- Telling everyone that you own a land; physically occupying a part of
  the land ðŸ¡ª not enough actual possession

- May only possess the section of land on [which you are conducting
  activities allocating AP;]{.underline} recreational activities$\neq$AP

**[(2) EXCLUSIVE]{.underline}**: can't share (esp. w/TO)

- **(1) show intention to possess land to the exclusion of everyone
  else's** claims to the land, and

- **(2) conduct must give unequivocal indication** that you are
  exercising dominion as the sole owner

- **inviting** **others** to use the property does not negate ownership

**[(3) OPEN AND NOTORIOUS:]{.underline}** Possession is **open and
notorious** when it is the kind of use the usual owner would make for
the land. The adverse possessor's occupation must be **sufficiently
apparent to put true owner on actual or constructive notice** that
trespass is occurring.

- i.e. issuing license to hunt, (a month-long) harvesting woods,
  developing the land

**[(4) CONTINUOUS/UNINTERRUPTED]{.underline}**

- Continual: repeatedly

- Continuous: without interruption

**Howard v. Kunto (1970): [CONTINUOUS possession]{.mark}** [may be

  by D ðŸ¡ª P then brought suit to quiet title on the land occupied by D

- TC held that D, having occupied the property for less than a year, had
  not satisfied the requirements for AP, and thus the land belonged
  to P. ðŸ¡ª D appealed

- **RULE**: CONTINUANCY dep on **(1) ordinary use by owners of like
  property of a like nature** (dep on nature of the property); and **(2)

  to the stat req.

- **HOLDING**: Continuity satisfied ðŸ¡ª court held in favor of Kuntos

- **REASONING**: Case ended up hinging on **continuity**; issue: stat
  req is 10 yrs

  a summer home - not year round ðŸ¡ª not continuous ?

- Court said that summer occupancy constituted ordinary use by owners of
  like property of a like nature; i.e. cont. using it the way that ppl
  ordinarily use this type of property

  - Note impt of the nature of the prop in det how it applies to this
    case

- Court further says that APs are allowed to tack immediate predecessors
  to see if it adds up to the statutory requirement

  - Court notes that trad there had been a **[privity]{.underline}**
    requirement ðŸ¡ª some sort of legally relevant, reasonable connection
    sufficient to raise that claim above that of an ordinary trespasser
    ðŸ¡ª trad typical that it would be satisfied by the deed

  - In this case, if we look at the law fxnlly, we consider WHY there is
    a privity requirement ðŸ¡ª fxns to diff squatters from APs

      squatters from adverse possessors

- Continuity satisfied ðŸ¡ª court held in favor of Kuntos

**[(1) Affirmative waste]{.underline}**

- Person w/ present possessory interest DOESN'T act

- Significant change in the property

  - Typically "better" = increases market valuable

  circumstances**

  - You can do ameliorative waste sometimes, not always

**Brokaw v. Fairchild (1929):** [ameliorative waste, even though it
makes property value better, can still be form of waste / **life tenant
has right to use estate, but NOT right to act like owner** / knocking
building down is acting like owner]{.mark}

**Facts:** P, a remainderman, had inherited a mansion from his father
and wanted to knock the mansion down and build apartments, claiming he
couldn't rent the mansion for enough to cover the tax bill. The other
remaindermen who also inherited mansions located nearby objected and
argue knocking the mansion down is waste.

[**Holding:** If you have a life estate, you do not own the property,
cannot act like an owner, and must pass the property to remainderman in
similar condition.]{.mark} Thus, P cannot knock the mansion down because
to do so is wasteful.

- Demolishing property = act of ownership

- The property must pass to the remainderman "as nearly as practicable,
  unimpaired in its nature, character, or improvements," otherwise, it's
  waste

<!-- -->

- If you have a life estate, you can just use the estate, but **you
  can't impair what it is that is handed down on to the remainderman**

- Doesn't matter if the remaindermen would or would not be financially
  better off

    - Even if everyone would be better off financially, doesn't mean

    - They may have lost irreparable sentimental value

## Partition 

**PARTITION**

- Available to any cotenant at any time

- If you want out, you can

- Two ways to achieve:

## 

**Delfino v. Vealencis (1980)**: partition by sale / partition in kind;
**Court says that policy is to favor partition in kind unless (1) PIK
would be impracticable and inequitable, AND (2) the partition by sale
would better serve the owners\' interests**

**Facts:** Two families owned a plot of land as tenants in common. The
Delfinos owned 69% and Vealencis owned 31%. Vealencis lived on the
land + operated trash removal business out of property as well. Delfinos
did not actually have possession of their parcel (they lived elsewhere).
Delfinos wanted the court to order **partition by sale**, but Vealencis
wanted **partition in kind.**

- [**By sale** - sold at auction + divide \$ proportionate that matches
  ownership shares]{.mark}

- [**In kind -** physically dividing property up between parties + each
  party will own their part all by themselves]{.mark}

<!-- -->

- Vealencis want partition by kind bc she wants to remain on the
  property and continue operating her business

- Delfinos want to build residential subdivision on their property
  post-partition; betting they will win at auction to buy Vealencis part
  of the plot

**Rule:** Policy is **to favor partition in kind**, unless both of
following are true:

> *Have to have both conditions!*

- [Dividing in kind would be impractical or inequitable]{.underline}

- [Selling the property would be better to serve owner's
  interests]{.underline}

**Application:** Physical partition is feasible - subdivision is
possible even with the business present

- Owners interest - Delfinos might lose market value by having the
  business next door, so yes, physical partition is not in THEIR
  interest, but **you have to consider BOTH parties interests**

  - Vealencis home + livelihood depended on being on this property for
    several yrs

- **Only one set of owners interests would be served** by physical
  partition, so the second requirement isn't satisfied

  - The second requirement is that ALL owners interests would be better
    satisfied by partition by sale

- Court orders partition by kind

  - Trial court had divided up the property in kind - gave Vealencis
    only 1 acre out of 20.5 acres, and ordered her to pay an owelty

  - **Owelty =** payment to correct an imbalance that arises from
    partitioning in kind

  - Partitioning property in kind often cannot be done in exactly the
    way the parties interests are apportioned

    - Unique physical characteristics of property - like a ridge
      unevenly dividing the property

      - Person who gets smaller portion pays \$ to the other party (an
        owelty) to ensure the market value of what everyone gets is
        50/50

- Today, most states still favor partition in mind in some shape or form

- Might be a trend for support for partition by sale, which makes sense
  bc modern property is less likely to be easily divisible

  - Against partition by sale â†’ sentimental value to property that
    cannot be valued in dollars, social cost going to be lost in the
    sale bc no one is going to pay more for the property just bc it has
    an extra sentimental value for you
