**BIG IDEAS**

1)  **[COMPETING AIMS OF TORT LAW]{.mark}**

- Thinking about who should pay

  - Human argument -- the blameworthy party, who knew about the risk, who *can* pay / who is insured?

  - Policy arguments -- let losses lie where they fall, culture of safety, known risk, give incentives to people for how to act?

  - Legalistic arguments -- easy default rule /precedent?

I.  **CORRECTIVE JUSTICE** -- *D focused, ex post: Why DF is obligated to pay PL*

    a.  When one party wrongs another, correction of the wrong helps restore moral balance

    b.  Intentional w=Wrongdoer = most obvious occasion for Corrective Justice

    c.  *How* compensation happens changes the strength of the argument:

        i.  Strong when an individual compensates another individual face to face.

        ii. Weak when the connection between injurer and victim becomes less direct. (*Insurance, corporation -- no longer face to face)*

II. **CIVIL RECOURSE** -- *P focused, ex post: Why P is entitled to Receive*

    a.  Tort law gives victims a chance to have the fact that they've been wronged recognized.

    b.  Vindicates this need in a manner proportional to the seriousness of both the wrong and the injury (\$)

III. **OPTIMAL DETERRENCE** -- *Community focused, ex ante*

     a.  We want to deter excessively risky behavior -- only want to avoid those losses worth avoiding

         i.  The law *does* tolerate losses that result from some risky activities.

         ii. ðŸ¡ªNegligence requirement implies that some losses are worth avoiding and some are not

     b.  Nothing in the principle of Optimal Deterrence dictates what values should be taken into account in determining *which* losses are and and are not worth deterring.

         i.  Economic Calculus -- B \_\_ P\*L ignores human values

IV. **LOSS DISTRIBUTION**

    a.  DFs spread out their liability (e.g. through insurance, raising prices for their products, or through shareholders)

    b.  Having a larger \# of people bear small losses \> having a single person bear a large loss. This aim does not suggest a limit.

V.  **COMPENSATION**

    a.  Help to return the victims to the state they were in before the accident. Not to provide compensation for the victims.

    b.  Try to reduce the risk of socially productive activities (benefit, not goal); make us feel secure

VI. **REDRESS OF SOCIAL GRIEVANCES**

    a.  Right to sue promotes redress of social grievances especially against large, impersonal institutions. Ordinary people can put authority on trial.

**[RULE]{.mark}**

**Baltimore & Ohio RR v. Goodman** (1927). **F:** P's decedent killed when D's train hit his truck. P's view was obscured so he slowed down approaching tracks.

**H:** D not liable for injuries. **R:** P assumes risk of injury when danger is foreseeable and P does not take extra precaution (ordinary care is not sufficient here). Establishes a **rule** -- higher standard of care. P must take every possible precaution when crossing, including *getting out of the car and checking.*

Due care generally a question for Jury, but where standard of duty is clear, Court can decide.

- Holmes: Judges should use their experience to make decisions when it is clear from past cases what outcome should be; they should lay down guidelines rather than allow an oscillating jury to decide. Recurring fact pattern ðŸ¡ª make rule for consistency of results.

**[STANDARD]{.mark}**

**Pokora v. Wabash Railway** (1934). **F:** P drove truck across railroad crossing and had obscured view by boxcars so was hit by train. He stopped and listened before but heard no train coming. **H:** Ruled in favor of P. Reversed Goodman. **R:** Jury should decide whether P's action (relying on hearing and not sight) was reasonable given the circumstances. Cardozo cautions against making standards of behavior into rules of law and applying to every circumstance. (This limits *Goodman* which imposes standard application by the judge -- there, P may have been contributorily negligent as a matter of law given facts of the case, but that does not mean everyone who hasn't stopped, listened and gotten out is contributorily negligent!). Getting out of car to check is unnecessary & dangerous as it may *increase* risk.
