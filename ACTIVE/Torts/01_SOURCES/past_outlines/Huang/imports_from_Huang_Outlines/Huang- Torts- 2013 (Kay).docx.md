# II. Negligence

## A. History of Fault (injury is not enough)

*Brown v. Kendal (Mass Sup Ct 1850) 35: **When a Defendant unintentionally injures another while undertaking a lawful act, the Plaintiff must prove that the Defendant acted without due care as adapted to the exigencies of the circumstances.***

- Dogs are fighting and defendant backs up and pokes plaintiff in eye

- The requisite standard of ordinary care is the same for accidental injuries resulting from lawful actions, whether the actions are characterized as necessary, or merely permissible

## C. Reasonable Person 

![](media/image3.png){width="7.125in" height="4.208333333333333in"}

*Bethel v. NYC Transit Authority (NY Ct of Appeals 1998) 49***: *Court can redefine standard of care (here from utmost to reasonable) when old standard of care is no longer contemporarily applicable***

- Plaintiff is hurt on def's bus when wheelchair accessible seat collapsed and argues for "utmost" standard of care (past 100 years) which says that carriers must exercise utmost standard of care

- Court rejects this standard (because it was enacted in accident-prone RR time) in favor of reasonable person standard because it is now flexible enough for juries:

  - Takes in to account reasonably perceivable risk and gravity of harm to others

  - Provides leeway for due allowance to be made for particular circumstance

- Question of tailoring: how much tailoring do you want?

*Benefits of objective reasonable person standard*

- Costs/Efficiency, Incentives/Deterrence, and Fairness.

- Does not require jury to engage in psychology.

- Easier to communicate to the below-average individual the kind of actions he is accountable for.

- Policy Argument: Objective Standard gives the public assurance that those whose actions they are exposed to will either have predictable level of care, or actor will be liable for failure to do so

## D. Judges and Juries, Rules vs. Standards

- Reasonable vs. Rational

  - Rational -- instrumental rationality (it is rational for me to steal from you if you have something I want and I think I won't get caught)

    - Doesn't take the interests of others into account

  - Reasonable goes beyond instrumental rationality -- it may be rational for me to steal from you, but unreasonable to do so

    - Takes interests of others into account

    - Reasonable person (objective) vs. reasonably intoxicated/disabled person (subjective)

*RULES VS. STANDARDS*

- **Rules**Â are the most constraining and rigid. Once a rule has been interpreted and the facts have been found, then the application of the rule to the facts decides the issue to which it is relevant (unless the case falls within the penumbra of a rule with vague content). (ex, 4 year olds can be sued for negligence reasonable for her age and intellect)

- **Standards**Â provide an intermediate level of constraint. Standards guide decisions but provide a greater range of choice or discretion; standards define a set of mandatory considerations.

- **Principles**Â are even less constraining. Principles provide mandatory considerations for judges. Whereas, standards identify an exhaustive set of considerations for adjudication or policy making, a principle identifies a nonexhaustive set, leaving open the possibility that other considerations may be relevant to the decision.

- Cardozo believes this situation is too tailored for a clear cut rule, so you have to ask a jury what a reasonable person would do in those circumstances

- Barrels don't just fall out of shop windows by themselves so the fact that the accident took place is enough to show negligence.

*Negligence Per Se -- an act is considered negligent because it violates a statute (speed limit, building code)*

- You cannot just be negligent -- you need to show that conduct led to injury incurred

- We'll respect legislature's rule of contributory negligence

- Brother and sister junk collectors could not walk on grass median because of wheels of cart, so they walked on road instead, in violation of stay to the center line statute, and were struck by car

# III. Causation

## Overview of Types of Causation

- ALWAYS LOOK OUT FOR **CAUSATION IN FACT**, AND SEE IF THAT IS **DEBATABLE** (but for the negligent act, you would not have injury -- negligent act related to injury, not SL idea of having the dangerous thing in the first place)

- Factual Causation

<!-- -->

- 1\) but-for causation

  - counterfactual: if def had not done x, injury would not have occurred

  - reason for: we don't wan to punish people who didn't cause the injury

    - counter: butterfly effect, the chain goes back way far

- 2\) multiple sufficient causes

  - two fires scenario -- two people both cause an injury, and both of the their negligence would have been sufficient to cause the injury, they are both liable

  - not but for causation

  - reason for: intuitive -- you don't want a world where there is more negligence piling on and plaintiff gets less compensation -- just because here are two people doesn't negate plaintiff's need to be negated

  - If only one fire is negligently set (but arrive at the same time), some jurisdictions impose liability on the negligent actor only, while some do not (to give parties incentive to take precautions against harms that will occasionally occur anyway because of a simultaneous non-negligent cause)

  - *Restatement*: with MSC, which alone would have been the factual cause of harm at the same time in the absence of the other acts, then they are each the factual cause of the harm

- 3\) multiple necessary causes

  - a but-for test (both causes are but-for)

  - if 4 people all spill poison into their food while cooking, but eating one person's dish isn't sufficient to kill, but a plaintiff decides to sample all 4 dishes, and dies, there is multiple necessary causes

  - reason against: when you're doing actions you're not taking into account other people's negligence (this regime does not do anything)

  - Related to moral luck

- Funky Stuff outside of causation

  - Alternative Liability (not a causal test) -- Summers v. Tice, even if we know that only one def caused the harm, we'll hold both liable so plaintiff is not screwed

    - Imposing liability on negligent defendants, each of whom is equally likely ("as likely as not") to have harmed the plaintiff.

  - Market Share Liability

    - Requirements:

      - 1\) Identical Produce

      - 2\) "Relevant Market" (national, regional, local)?

      - Signature disease

    - Then All you need to show is that you've been harmed (shifts burden to defs)

  - Loss Chance

    - Usually limited to malpractice

    - Same as risk-creation suit --you cannot generally sue someone just for creating risk

    - When but-for doesn't work, you calculate loss chance by subtracting chance of surviving before negligence from chance of surviving after negligence

But for, sine qua non -- absent def's action, harm would not have occurred

- Counterfactual and facts together -- you have to prive them together

- You can have dual but for causes, but not unlimited as a means of distracting from essential but-for cause (aka "a" cause vs. "the" cause)

## Cause in Fact

*Stubbs v. City of Rochester (NY Ct of Appeals 1919) 334: **If more than one possible causes of harm exist, and the defendant would be liable for one of them, the plaintiff must establish with reasonable certainty that the cause of the injury was the one for which the defendant was liable***

- Dirty water in drinking system and plaintiff contracted Typhoid

- Court found for plaintiff and ordered a new trial since Plaintiff presented enough evidence to make a reasonable inference that the cause of the illness was due to contaminated water:

  - 57 other people ill on same street; plaintiff only drank that water; expert opinion says contaminated water is a very frequent cause of typhoid)

## Funky Liability outside of direct causation

*Zuchowicz v. US (2nd Circ 1998) 343: **when you cannot use a causal direct but-for test, you can use the substantial factor test: if several causes could have caused the harm, any cause that was a substantial factor is held liable (shifts burden to def) -- DON'T USE***

- Def doctors gave nearly double maximum amount of danocrine; plaintiff contract PPH and died after giving birth

- Expert testimony can be sufficient to prove causation in fact

- Defs fail test, so court affirms for plaintiff:

  - 1\) Def's negligent act or omission was a but-for cause of injury (FDA said it was unacceptable at that use -- since they've never tested at those levels, you can assume it's unsafe)

  - 2\) Negligence was causally linked to the harm (experts say yes with a reasonable degree of certainty)

  - 3\) Def's negligent act or omission was proximate cause to resulting injury

- Court said they weren't going to instruct jury to make the jump, but would inform them of the jump ðŸ¡ª Shifts burden to def (similar to Cardozo's inferences in Herzog for the jury to decide)

*Matsuyama v. Birnbaum (Mass Sup Ct 2008) 355: **when you cannot use but-for test with a terminally ill patient, you can apply a lost chance doctrine, which calculates decrease in plaintiff's lost chance of living absent defendant's negligence***

- Def doctor negligently did not order proper tests (even though he knew he was a smoker and of Japanese ancestry) that would have discovered gastric cancer

- Court employs loss of chance doctrine since we can now estimate patient's chance of survival (theory of injury, not a causation; limited to medical cases)

- Jury decided it was a 37.5% chance

- But for your negligence, I would have had a higher chance at living

If D1 and D2 each negligently cause an accident, Plaintiff could recover in full from D2, and then D1 must sue D2 for his share (usually happens in insolvency cases -- to protect plaintiff) \[not to be confused with joint/MNC)

*Ravo v. Rogatnick (NY 1987) 365: **when tortfeasors' actions cause a joint harm (even if you cannot tell who caused what harm), then there is "joint enterprise" liability among them for the damages***

- Unclear whether obs. or pediatrician's negligence caused injury

- Unfair possibly to def 1 who is only 25% liable and may need to pay 100% of damages if def 2 is insolvent

- This is replaced by comparative negligence rules in some states

*Summers v. Tice (Calif Sup ct 1948) 367: **in order to get around but-for loophole with two possible joint tortfeasors, courts will hold both defendants liable and put burden on defendants for liability between themselves***

- Both defendants were quail hunting when they shot at plaintiff, one striking his eye and another his lip -- unable to tell which gun harmed in eye

- Courts will treat both defendants as though they are joint tortfeasors so that plaintiff does not screwed with exonerated liability; defs can then apportion liability between themselves after case with plaintiff

- Encourages silent defs to speak

- Not a MSC case -- only one act here, not that both shots hit him in the eye

*Dillon v. Twin State Gas - notes 370: **Court will sometimes apply but-for test and find for defendant and reduce awards; with boy who slips off landing and then gets electrocuted by exposed wire, jury found he would be killed anyway***

Key aspects of [Market Share Liability]{.underline}: which market, absent def's share, how exactly to exculpate, who pays what

*Hymowitz v. Eli Lylly & Co (NY Ct of Appeals 1989) 372: **especially when injury is dormant, court will employ a Market Share Liability determination to defendant companies (national); shifts burden to def***

- Plaintiffs allege harm when mother ingested dangerous DES drug during pregnancy; problems in determining exact manufacturer (over 300)

- In order to address such an injury that remains dormant and to prevent manufacturers from hiding behind a curtain, Court adopts National Market Share apportionment of damages to manufacture

- Even if not all plaintiffs are present -- it will prevent some plaintiffs from recovering 100% of their problems

- Summers precedent does not work because we have a def ID problem

- No industry wide enterprise liability

- Dissent: National Market share approach except those who were able to prove that their product caused the injury; preponderance of the evidence standard

- Analysis

  - No more link between plaintiff and def -- its presumed

  - Not an all or nothing standard -- far on the proportional spectrum side of things

  - Legislature asked Court to "legislate" by lifting statute of limitations

## Proximate Cause / Scope of Liability

- "An actor's liability is limited to those harms that result from the risks that made the actor's conduct tortious"

- BEFORE CONSIDERING FORESEEABILITY, ASK WHETHER DEFENDANT WAS [NEGLIGENT]{.underline}

- After all elements (breach of duty, damages, cause in fact), prox cause limits liability for def

- We often ask juries why do we think that a certain type of action was negligent in the first place

  - Why is speeding bad? You might crash into other people, animals, you might leave someone stuck on a highway

- Foreseeable vs. unforeseeable (make arguments on both sides)

- Very fact intensive -- Very nebulous

- *Abraham on Prox Cause*

  - Foreseeable Type of Harm

    - Varies on a case-by-case basis

    - Polemis (wood plank fire ship case) -- different types of harm -- falling plank farm and catastrophic harm to ship

      - Def;'s negligence was proximate cause of even unforeseeable harm, so long as harm was a direct consequence of def's negligence

    - Wagon Mound -- oil harms (polluting a bay) vs. fire harms

      - Foreseeability not important because plaintiff didn't want to admit contributory negligence

    - Palsgraf (box falling on someone's foot vs. box of explosives injuring Palsgraff -- changes if box was labeled)

  - Foreseeable Manner and Mechanism (does not matter)

    - General rule: unless in retrospect the manner in which foreseeable P suffered foreseeable type of harm appears to be so extraordinary as to be completely unforeseeable, the fact that the harm occurred in unforeseeable manner does **not** bar recovery

    - Didn't break the chain unless it's unforeseeable

    - One person negligently hits another car, is uninjured, but as he exists, is hit by a 3^rd^ car -- type of harm is the same but manner is different -- foreseeability in getting hit by a different car does not matter

    - What if injury occurs in a bizarre manner?

      - Plaintiff is struck by a third car after pulling over after accident -- as long as plaintiff was injured at roughly the same time and roughly same place as was foreseeable, it is not necessary that precise manner injury took place have been foreseeable

  - *Foreseeable Extent of Harm (thin skull rule) -- does not matter*

    - This has fallen by the way side for egg-shell, thin skull rule -- you take your victim as he comes, and you are liable for full extent of harm, even if you didn't realize he had a heart condition

      - Reasons: too many practical problems: what are the normal amount of injuries under circumstances? Most people are in good health (requiring expert testimony complicating cases); this in effect imposes strict liability (and could deter defendants from doing things altogether, such as driving which would not be good)

      - Exception: intervening cause (man is struck by lightening on stretcher after being assaulted)

  - Foreseeable Victim/Plaintiff? -- Matters a lot

    - Passing through a red light, it's foreseeable you would collide with someone else

    - Since injury to Palsgraf was not even remotely one of the risks to be taken into account by RR employee helping a 3^rd^ passenger, injury was not foreseeable and highly improbable

    - For Andrews, RR company could have foreseen because of proximity in space and time -- prox cause is malleable; once RR was negligent, it's enough

      - Cardozo said before question of prox could even be reached, def must have breached a duty to exercise reasonable care toward plaintiff vs. Andrews who said that it was about prox cause

      - Cardozo is about a breach of duty (not an existence of duty)

      - Policy:

        - Andrews liability protects plaintiffs (like strict liability); Cardozo doesn't hold people always liable (liability without fault?)

        - Maybe it makes more sense for negligent def to bear more costs

  - [Harm within the Risk Test]{.underline} 143

    - Is the risk of injury plaintiff suffered one of the risks that makes the def negligent for act?

    - Plaintiff, driving by blocked hydrant where def is parked, skids on road and collides with def's parked vehicle

    - Is the risk of injury the plaintiff suffered one of the risks that makes the def negligent for blocking access to the fire hydrant? (no)

    - Def's negligence did not foreseeably *increase* risk of harm that materialized

  - Intervention -- with 2 actors

    - Does def owe a duty to plaintiff? How foreseeable to def injury to parties in plaintiff's position in, which party is in a better position

      - Ex, bartender owes a duty to 3^rd^ parties harmed

    - If you have a duty, then you consider prox cause of injury -- [foreseeability]{.underline} of injury to plaintiff as a risk that is taken into account in deciding whether def's actions were negligent

    - Intervention [does not break]{.underline} chain of causation

  - Superseding Causes

    - You can use it as a limiting principle, intervening cause that breaks the causal change

    - Yes this thing happened, but it was still within the scope of risk

  - Purpose of Proximate Cause

    - Liability-limiting doctrine for defs

    - Withholding a case from jury on prox cause grounds limits considerations of many factors

    - Need for jury instruction that acquaints jury with its responsibility not for a mechanical decision, in tort law, but to use its judgment in deciding whether a def was sufficiently blameworthy under all circumstances

*In re Polemis (British Court of Appeal KB 1921) 399: **a defendant's negligence can still be the proximate cause of an unforeseeable harm, so long as harm was a "direct" consequence of def's negligence; negligence can be enough***

- Actual anticipations of negligent party in falling of plank are irrelevant when considering whether resulting damage is remote

- Fire was a direct result of the negligent act, so defs are liable

- Damage that might result when a plank falls is a foreseeable consequence of negligence, whatever damage might be

*The Wagon Mound Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v. Mort's Dock & Engineering Co. LTD (UK Privy Council 1961) 402: **overturns Polemis limits negligence only standard with foreseeability: even if plaintiff's property is injured as a direct result of def's negligence, when it is an unforeseen consequence, you may not have liability***

- Def's ship negligently spilled oil, but then oil ignited later on and fire spread, causing extensive damage to property

- Overturns Polemis: not consistent with current ideas of justice that an act of negligence, however slight, which results in some unforeseen damage should make actor liable for all consequences simply because they are direct

- Minimum standard of behavior (though every case is different and should be judged as such

- Defendants liable for injuries defendant should have anticipated at time it released oil into the water (for fouling slips on dock, but not fire which destroyed the dock itself)

- Appearance of the package could not have warned the guard to take extra care

## Negligence Regimes

### Contributory negligence

- Complete bar recovery to any plaintiff who is contributorily negligent

- Most states now put burden on Defendant to prove (previously in Brown v. Kendall, plaintiff had burden)

- Very harsh regime -- MD and CA grapple with this idea

- Limitations:

  - Recklessness (or willful misconduct) -- contributory neg is irrelevant

  - Statutory protected class (school bus driver cannot rely on contrib. neg. of children)

  - [Last clear chance]{.underline} - plaintiff behaved carelessly and got into a dangerous situation that led to injury; def had failed to utilize his last clear chance to avoid injury to the plaintiff (despite plaintiff's negligence) (Prox. Cause questions?)

    - In order to prove this, plaintiff need not prove that def appreciated position of danger in relation to plaintiff; rather, only that def had reason to appreciate danger

    - Transitional doctrine that melded into comp neg

  - Refusal to impute contrib. negligence (respondeat superior) -- this has been largely eliminated except in wrongful death actions

  - Jury's role -- jury would sometimes reduce plaintiff's damages by some amount rather than return a defense verdict (ignore judge's instructions)

  - Avoidable consequences (mitigation) -- even if accident was entirely def's fault, plaintiff's recovery might be reduced to the extent he failed to exercise due care to mitigate harm dome (medical advice)

### Comparative Negligence

- Pure Comp. Neg

  - Negligent plaintiff's recovery depended on how serious plaintiff's negligence was compared to the def's

  - The more plaintiff is negligent, the less plaintiff gets with no cutoff

- Modified Comp Neg

  - 50% cutoff (or sometimes 51% cutoff)

  - The more you're negligent, the less you get up to 50%; once you're at 51%, you get nothing

- Inherently related to causation -- though it seeks not to be (difficult for juries to separate out)

- What kind of negligence? Stop sign vs. speeding -- difficult to compute

- With joint/several, solvent def and plaintiff bear risk of other defendant's insolvency, in proportion to amount of negligence attributable to them

- *Comp Neg and Joint and Several Liability Possibilities*

  - Hypo: P=40%; D1=35% and D2 = 25%:

1.  Hold Ds J&S liable for full amount of the judgment (60% of P's losses), even though P was also negligent (only works with joint tort-feasors)

2.  Hold each Defendant liable for his own proportional share of the reduced judgment (effectively revoking J&S liability)

    a.  D1 pays 35/60 of judgment. \[& if 2nd D can't pay their 25/60 share, burden falls on P/P goes uncompensated for that amount. \*\* And the point of J&S liability is that burden should not have to fall on P if one of the neg Ds can't pay.\]

    - If one of the Ds is insolvent, then the injurers as a group (the 2 Ds plus P) are responsible for the extra burden, and should the burden of the 2nd D's amount in proportion to their own responsibility (since plaintiff was negligent too, we are not concerned about protecting him from insolvency

# V. Duty

General

- Negligence referred to as a breach of duty (move away from privity doctrine); breach of duty to exercise reasonable care

- Duty is a question of law for the **court**, whereas breach is a question for **jury**

- Affirmative duty -- defendant's conduct does not negligently create a risk of physical harm to plaintiff (and has therefore not breached)

  - But def might eliminate or reduce risks of independent harms to plaintiff -- def has an affirmative duty to reduce (duty to rescue)

  - There is rarely a duty to rescue, but sometimes there is if there is a special relationship

  - Gratuitous service exception -- you cannot see a victim, agree to help, and then take action to further his injury

  - Duty to warm: duty of stalled truck to warn approaching drivers of danger created in some states)

- Limited Duty - def's conduct foreseeably creates an unreasonable risk of haring the plaintiff

  - Def has committed misfeasance rather than nonfeasance

  - Some cases place limit on duty t exercise reasonable care not to risk such harm (limited duty)

  - Liability of owners for injuries to those who have entered property to *warn* the licensee, though not necessarily remove the harm

  - Social host has no duty in serving a drunken guests

  - Trespassers --only a duty to refrain from wantonly and willfully injuring them (hidden traps)

- List of factors courts take in to account

  - 1\) foreseeability of harm

  - 2\) closeness of connection b/w defs conduct and plaintiff's harm

  - 3\) moral blame of def's conduct

  - 4\) extend of burden on def and consequences for community if duty is imposed

  - 5\) policy of preventing future harm

  - 6\) availability and prevalence of insurance that is or would be available if duty is imposed

- [Misfeasance]{.underline} -- def who negligently created a risk of harm (**duty**) (re-roofer drops his hammer on a pedestrian)

- [Nonfeasance]{.underline} -- def who did not remove a risk of harm created by someone or something else (**no duty**/liability) (sunbather has no duty to save drowning child or notify)

  - Policy reasons for nonfeasance exception: nonfeasance made the plaintiff's situation no worse; infringement on individual liberty on unwilling bystanders; difficulty on defining duty to be imposed (what if bystanders could not swim; whole beach of bystanders?)In

- Factors for considering imposition of a duty: morality, burden of new duty, alternative ways of protecting plaintiff, effect of imposing duty on def's conduct, administrative concerns, foreseeability and avoidance, legislature

- Duty v. Proximate Cause

  - Courts use proximate cause to limit liability to foreseeable consequences of a negligent act

    - Driver leaves key in car and terrorist steals car and blows up building -- driver owes duty not to leave care open to theft, but result is so idiosyncratic and beyond the cope of harm that def is not liable for bombing damages

  - Duty concept denies liability for consequences that *are* foreseeable -- courts refuse to impose liability for policy reasons (not foreseeable that you could injure a fetus)

    - Though Palsgraf is the exception because it considers foreseeability

- Plaintiffs property is damaged from water from defs who had a reservoir (and inspected it by engineers)

- Court found for plaintiff deeming def strictly liable; similar to if cattle escapes and damages others' property

- No negligence is required because no one is to blame

- defendant brings reservoir at his own peril - he "assumes risk" by actively putting reservoir on his land; he had a **non-natural** use of the land (artificial)

- Other cases

  - Losee -- applied same standard to steam boiler in industrializing society

  - Texas case refused to apply same standard because storage of water is a natural use of land in arid Texas

*Indiana Harbor Belt RR v. American Cyanmid (7th Circ 1990) 521: **Posner: when negligence is possible and you can affect the "care levels" of a material, you do not apply strict liability***

- Def is manufacturer of acrylonitrile and liquid leaks while in a train car; RR company seeks decontamination costs

- Posner does not apply SL to defs, since leak was caused by carelessness, not the chemical itself

  - You can affect the care levels (part C of test below is most important)

  - This couldn't have been prevented at a reasonable cost, so not a case for strict liability

  - This is not Rylands because it is not the actors here; passive shipper vs. active shipper

- Huang: Posner does not examine how SL can affect care levels too (ie, whether transportation of flammable toxic material is abnormally dangerous); it asked only whether there is SL when particular means of escape of such material from the RR care could have been prevented by reasonable care

*2^nd^ Restatement SL test: (BE SURE TO STILL ADDRESS CAUSATION)*

- A\) risk of harm was great

- B\) harm that would ensue if risk materialized could be great (though didn't have to be

- C\) such accidents could not be prevented by exercise of due care \*\*

- D\) activity was not a matter of common usage

- E\) activity was inappropriate to the place which it took place (i.e. densely populated)

- F\) reinforcing d, value to community of activity is not great enough to offset unavoidable risks

- Example: explosion case -- no proximate cause requirement if you are transferring explosives

- Disadvantages - Ultrahazardous previous approach was simpler for court and jury

- Causation is still important

  - 2^nd^ allows for recovery in SL cases even if an unexpected act intervened

  - Madsen case where mother mink ate young after blasting; def was not SL because possibility that this would occur is not even one of the secondary risks that enters into the decision to impose SL for blasting (Mink owners are in a better position than blasters to make activity-level and research calculations that strict liability induces)

*3^rd^ Restatement SL: "abnormally dangerous" if activity creates a risk even when care does not mitigate risk; it is not common*

- Whether activity poses a foreseeable and highly significant risk of harm

- Activity is not common usage

- Victim is usually passive, third party

- SL should still be limited to the type of harm that makes the conduct dangerous

- You can still get nominal damages if no demonstrable harm was showed (nominal)

- No "intent" needed for regular trespass -- all you need that you were intending to use land

  - Unintentional trespass, when you do not intend to use property, is basically regular negligence according to restatement; Here, no damage/harm, no liability

- Necessity exceptions (discussed below)

*Martin v. Reynolds Metals Co (Oregon Sup Ct 1959) 676: **there was a trespass on plaintiff's land for the reason the actual deposit of "unseen things" caused his land to be unfit for the cattle he was trying to raise there; trespass and nuisance can exist concurrently; all you need is a feeling of exclusive possession***

- Court found that with advancements in science, we can define trespass as any intrusion which invades possessor's protected interest in exclusive possession, whether invisible or not (so fluoride counts as trespass)

  - This was originally a nuisance case but plaintiffs wanted trespass because of SOL

- Court said that all you need is a violation of a [FEELING]{.underline} of exclusive possession

  - What is the degree of feeling? Abuse?

  - This is an extreme form of nuisance and trespass standard; this is about protecting the safety of land ownership

- Probably an [objective standard]{.underline} -- whether a reasonable person would view vibrations as infringements on ownership

*Private necessity*

- Suppose private party uses or destroys the property of another in order to preserve his person or property of greater value (plaintiff mores ship at a dock of def's private island to avoid storm; def cut loose boat, and boat was harmed -- court awarded damages to plaintiff, recognizing privilege, born of necessity, to use def's property") -- as long as harm you're preventing is not greater than harm you are causing to land

- Important to note that the harm is not caused by the def himself here

*Defenses to Trespass (Abraham)*

- Public/private necessity, defense of property, recapture of chattels, lands

- Necessity is reserved for those cases in which def has acted reasonably in destroying plaintiff's property in order to avoid harm to himself or his property, but risk of harm to plaintiff has not been created by plaintiff

  - *Ploof v. Putnam, **when def's servant untied a family's boat from a dock -- court found there was necessity to use the dock and did not constitute trespass, def did not have right to exclude from dock***

- Public -- when risk property of a sufficiently large number of people to make risk public (tearing plaintiff's house down to prevent spread of fire to remainder of town) -- privilege is absolute, a complete defense

- Private -- risk to one party or his property only, and party can reduce risk by damaging or destroying someone else's property -- qualified or conditional -- def may be liable for damage done to plaintiff's property

*Vincent v. Lake Erie Transportation Co (Minn sup Ct 1910) 958: **you can trespass on to another person's property out of private necessity, but you are still liable for damages you cause to the dock (as destruction was not necessary)***

- Def mores ship to plaintiff's dock with upcoming storm

- Court finds no trespass because given the character of the storm, it would have been highly imprudent to attempt to leave (boat is more valuable than dock)

- But Court does award damages for injury to dock -- not a total act of god -- def advisedly took advantage of plaintiff's property for the purposes of preserving its own more valuable property

- Dissent: def should not be liable for damages because is was the result of an accident

- Abraham:

  - Between two innocent parties, **the one whom nature put at risk to bear the cost** rather than owner

  - Dock owner chose to have a dock where ships might tie up -- if we did not have this system, docks would be worth less (more risk) and ships would be worth more (more right)

*Policy -- Who should be liable in Trespass?*

- If dock owners can recover from non-negligent defendants during storms, then dock owners have less incentive to take measures to reduce risk of damage to their docks (and vice versa)

  - At the same time, if ship owners are not liable, then they will increasingly moor up on docks and risk damage

  - But with the Ploof and Vincent cases, threatening a party with liability gives this party incentive to compare the cost of liability with the cost of alternatives that may avoid liability (the threat induces more careful behavior)

  - Then it becomes a question of which party is in a better position to compare risk to the dock with risk to the ship (cheapest cost avoider)

  - If no party knows more, and no legal privilege, then maybe shipowners will be willing to pay for docking in storms (then balance costs)

    - Coase theorem -- Wrong-doer will pay as much as is profitable to continue to do the wrong

      - when parties themselves can identify the most cost-effective approach and can inexpensively set aside otherwise-applicable tort liability rule, then cost of courts' adopting the "wrong" rule is simply the cost of setting the rule aside in advance by contract

      - The same levels of production are achieved whether the perpetrator of the negative externalities is legally liable for the externality costs or is the victims of the negative externalities make a payment to the perpetrator that is reduced by the amounts of the externalities

## Nuisance

- A *[substantial]{.underline}* and *[unreasonable]{.underline}* interference with the right of an owner or occupier of land to the use and enjoyment of the land (usually pollution)

  - Public nuisance -- not a tort, but a species of low-level quasi criminal offense that affects general public in common (blocking public access to a highway)

  - Intentional Nuisance -- interference itself must be unreasonable

  - Unintentional -- like negligence -- something an owner would be able to do like selling an item

- Differences between trespass

  - Trespasser is liable for even trivial intrusions (a single act)

  - Granting an injunction is usually easier in trespass cases

*Private Nuisance*

- Courts must consider context and balance the equities to consider substantial (you don't want people "coming to the nuisance", like building a house next to a power plant)

  - But first person deserves active use is not the rule -- it would encourage development race

- Unintentional nuisance (arising out of negligent, reckless conduct, or abnormally dangerous conditions or activities (A "SL" tort)

- Injury must also be related to an invasion of interests in use and enjoyment of land

- Remedies

  - Factory may be required to pay for past and future harms, but may still be able to continue nuisance activity (Boomer)

- Unreasonableness requirement

  - A\) if gravity of harm outweighs utility of actor's conduct

  - B\) harm caused by conduct is serious and financial burden of compensating for this and similar harm to others would not make continuation of conduct feasible

- Gravity of Harm

  - Extent of harm

  - Character of harm

  - Social value that law attaches to type of use or enjoyment invaded

  - Suitability of use invaded to character of locality

  - Burden on person harmed of avoiding the ham

- Utility of Conduct:

  - Social value that law attaches to primary purpose of conduct

  - Suitability of conduct to character of locality

  - Impracticability of preventing or avoiding invasion

*Boomer v. Atlantic Cement Co (NY Ct of Appeals 1970) 683: **When a nuisance is of such a permanent and uneatable nature that a single recovery can be had, the Court will allow for damages (future), but not grant an injunction because of the public policy reasons against granting it; cost of discontinuing nuisance (in jobs and dollars) is grossly disproportionate to amount of harm nuisance was harming***

- Def operates cement plant and is sued by owners of neighboring land to injury from dirt, smoke, vibration

- Court grants payments that compensate for present and future loss, but refuses injunction

- Court is hesitant to address overall problem of pollution; does not want to put everyone out of work (Cos theorem -- service of factory is too great in shutting down)

- Dissent: this violates a rule about granting injunctions; licenses a wrong; private interest with no public benefit

- Usually pollution, contamination or noise; substantial harm

- Formally a SL tort, but some negligence calculations come in to play on the Gravity vs. Utility prong, or on whether Plaintiff should bear ham overall

## Conversion

- Conversion -- tort against personal property rather than land

  - You have effectively become the owner because you do something only the owner can do (ex, stealing and selling a car) -- usually for a period of time

- Trespass of chattels -- mere minor damage to property that is not yours (stealing a car and denting it) (chattels are your personal property)

- Factor Test for Conversion: (1) plaintiffs\' ownership or right to possession of the property at the time of the conversion; (2) defendants\' conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of plaintiffs\' property rights; and (3) damages.

- Factor Test for Property Rights (Kremen): (1) an interest capable of precise definition, (2) it must be capable of exclusive control, and (3) the putative owner must have established a legitimate claim to exclusivity

- Remedies are different b/w conversion and trespass to chattels

  - Damages for complete value to property (conversion)

  - Trespass to chattels -- damages for whatever actual harm you caused

- Look at remedies to determine whether to apply conversion to trespass chattels (partial or continued use after harm done)

*Moore v. Regents of UC (Calif ct of appeal/sup ct) Handout**: Calif Sup Ct failed to find conversion when UC profited on discarded cells from surgery because Moore discarded his cells afterwards and valued relevant policy considerations of finding conversion***

- Without plaintiff's knowledge or consent, doctors used plaintiffs cells and patented them

- Appellate division found trespass because you can derive property benefits from cells, so they are plaintiff's personal property and therefore have conversion (no abandonment because you didn't have intent to discard)

- Supreme Court reverses, finding no property rights because cells were abandoned; finds a breach of physician's disclosure obligations instead of conversion

- Policy considerations

  - protection of a competent patient\'s right to make autonomous medical decisions vs. motivating researchers (hindering research)

  - Liability based upon existing disclosure obligations, rather than an unprecedented extension of the conversion theory, protects patients\' rights of privacy and autonomy without unnecessarily hindering research.

  - This is legislature's roles not ours

*Kremen v. Cohen (9th circ 2003): **Court found that an internet domain name (intangible property) satisfied property criteria primarily because registering a domain name is exclusive, and because registering a domain name informs others that domain name is no one else's, therefore conversion***

- Sex.com case where plaintiff owner sues theif and domain name services

- Court found that domain name is property because:

  - \(1\) a domain registrant decides where on the internet those who invoke that particular name are sent

  - \(2\) ownership is exclusive because the registrant alone makes that decision

  - \(3\) registering a domain name informs others that the domain name is the registrant's and no one else's

- Court was careful about making an extension -- domain fit already into definition, not extended

- California common law should not be extended to cover, as a trespass to chattels, an otherwise harmless e-mail whose contents are objectionable

- Court refuses to grant injunction, because no irreparable injury

  - You cannot just show you were harmed in an abstract intentional way- you have to show harm in an intentional way, such as slowing down the server or damaging the property in some way

- Dissent - The majority refused to distinguish the Internet from unauthorized intermeddling on a private Intranet

*EBay v. Merchexchange LLC (Sup Ct 2006) Handout: **Court establishes factor test for granting injunction***

- Plaintiff must show

  - **(1) that it has suffered an irreparable injury;**

  - **(2) that remedies available at law are inadequate to compensate for that injury;**

  - **(3) that considering the balance of hardships between the plaintiff and defendant, a remedy in equity is warranted;**

  - **(4) that the public interest would not be disserved by a permanent injunction**

- Concurring Opinion Roberts: long standing tradition of granting injunctions -- it's not an entitlement but a discretionary function

- Concurring opinion (Kennedy): injunctions sometimes have consequences (not in public interest, leverage in pressuring, patent trolls etc.), but we still should grant it

- Effect now is that courts apply this for all injunctions, not just patents; They will stretch it and especially focus on gov't interests in protecting land

## Compensatory Damages

- To return plaintiff as closely as possible to his condition before accident -- measure harm in past and future terms

- Pain and Suffering -- depends on how you *frame* the question to the jury: how much would you pay not to have this happen to you (before) vs. how much would you pay to go back (afterwards)

  - Some states have caps and maximums, but some courts have found that they violate Constitutional as an impermissible intrusion into jury's assessment of damages

- Factors to be considered: proportionality, consistency with past awards, who bears the loss (the public at large?)

- Principle of Single recovery (past and future losses)

  - Advantages: legal closure as case does not go on forever; legal incentive for plaintiff to stay sick disappears; def's financial exposure ends; lower administrative with one proceeding

  - Disadvantages: less accuracy; present value problem with inflation

*Seffert v. Los Angeles Transit Lines (Calif Sup Ct 1961) 711: **example of high compensatory damages that were upheld on appeal because of severity of injury; did not shock the conscience of the court***

- Plaintiff was severely injured for life and required multiple surgeries after being dragged along by a bus; Trial court issued 188k for plaintiff

- Court upheld damages as appropriate and not excessive: valid pecuniary lost calculation (medical bills, lost income) and high pain and suffering calculation (Trial Court is entitled to deference)

- Traynor Dissent:

  - Not proportional to low salary

  - Emphasis shift from ad hoc punishing in a mechanized society to orderly distribution of losses through insurance and the prices of goods -- loses are borne by a public free of fault

  - Consistency: highest award in history

## Punitive Damages

- Huang: we still may want to deter negligence with punitive damages because it is still something we want to deter, especially when it has serious ramifications

- Reasons defendants might not self-deter before PD:

  - Def knows potential plaintiffs face difficulty in proving causation and does not expect them to sue

  - Def wishes to harm plaintiff and is willing to pay compensatory damages to be able to do so

  - Defendant has been paying insufficient attention to the threat of liability for compensatory damages

- Gore Guideposts

  - 1\) Degree of reprehensibility of def's misconduct

  - 2\) Disparity b/w actual or potential harm suffered by plaintiff and punitive damage award

  - 3\) Difference between punitive damages awarded by jury and civil penalties authorized or imposed in comparable cases

*Exonn Shipping v. Baker (Sup Ct 2008) Handout**: in federal maritime cases, Souter believes it is appropriate to cap punitive damages at a 1:1 ratio with compensatory damages***

- Original award by district court was 500m compensatory and 2.5b punitive (9^th^ circuit later reduced)

- Souter found that a 1:1 ratio was appropriate as upper limit:

  - The Court needs to be especially conscientious of highly punitive verdicts as they may be unfair

  - Juries are inconsistent and you need a system for courts to reach an optimal level of penalty and deterrence (like criminal system where you don't want to have unequal sentences)

  - Cap is untenable because court will not be able to modify routinely (arbitrary)

- Dissent: no state courts have adopted this common multiplier rule

- Exonn wanted a reputation of litigating aggressively

*State Farm v. Campbell (US Sup Ct 2003) 760: **single digit ratio is most usually appropriate when considering punitive damages; 145:1 is a DP violation***

- Campbell and former plaintiffs got together to try and show that state farm engaged in nation-wide practice of forcing people to litigate rather than settle (because they have a liability maximum, they want to see if they do not have to pay) Jury awarded 1m compensatory and 145m punitive

- Kennedy held the ratio was too high under DP and applied the 3 Gore guideposts

  - 1\) Degree of reprehensibility of def's misconduct

    - Defs deserve no praise in their conduct, but nationwide activities are not really applicable since they bear no relation to Campbell's harm (DP

  - 2\) Disparity b/w actual or potential harm suffered by plaintiff and punitive damage award

    - No bright-line ratio, but 145:1 is too high; single-digit ratio is appropriate

    - Compensatory damages contain a punitive element as well

  - 3\) Difference between punitive damages awarded by jury and civil penalties authorized or imposed in comparable cases

    - not really applicable -- loss of business license and disgorgement of profits

- Scalia Dissent: DP clause offers no substantive protections against excessive or unreasonable awards of punitive damages

- Ginsberg Dissent: damage capping legislation available; state sovereignty when federal courts instate a single-digit ratio

*WR Grace & Co v. Waters -- **repetitive awards and asbestos -- court refused to bar multiple awards of punitive damages and put a limit on punitive damage claims just because the company would be repeatedly charged for the same claims by various plaintiffs because of the company\'s own negligence***

*Philip Morris USA v. Williams (Sup Ct 2006) Handout: **when considering punitive damages under DP, you cannot consider \[alleged\] harm to 3^rd^ parties ("Strangers to the litigation") as its own prong (though you may consider it peripherally in relation to the degree of reprehensibility)***

- Widow of heavy smoker sues Phillip Morris and gets 821 in compensatory and 80m in punitive damages

  - Oregon Court tried to say that this is distinguished from State Farm by saying that harm is similar to others

- Breyer ordered a new trial on DP grounds with new instructions to the jury:

  - DP prohibits a State to use punitive damages to punish a def for injury it inflicts to non parties (strangers to the litigation), since there is no authority and we would have a standard-less determination

  - Jury can consider harm to others in considering the degree of reprehensibility, but cannot go any father than that

  - State Court cannot create confusion

- Stevens dissent: novel limit on State's power to impose punishment on civil litigation (harming others should not) be taken into account

  - Distinction between 3^rd^ parties in reprehensibility and punishing def directly eludes Stevens

- Ginsberg Dissent: what is the point of punitive damages then? Oregon court followed BMW appropriately; plus, Phillip never objected to jury instructions at trial

- Kicker: jury instructions were crazy after this ruling and unclear

*Grimshaw v. Ford (Pinto case 1972): **Court upheld very high punitive damage ratio (1:75) because Ford distributed a memo which put value on human life in relation to lower safety standards***

- Kicker was that this memo was not about pinto but in response to a proposed safety rule to later manufactured cars; and 200k/human life was from gov't

- But reprehensibility came from that they deliberately included a dangers feature in design because of cost

### Litigation Classes vs. Settlement Classes

- Litigation Class

  - Must meet requirements of Rule 23

  - 23e -- Court has to approve any settlement to ensure class' interests are best met (Fairness, no kickbacks)

- Settlement classes -- certifying a class only for settlement

  - Kind of "contracting around the law"

  - Are we worried about there being a **fair fight**? Removing adversity, judge does not hear arguments against class; parties want same outcome, to bind parties

  - What about plaintiffs who aren't present too and their interests?

  - Court might not be doing DD because they just have to approve settlements on the dotted line

  - Issues of notice, variations in harm

- Subclasses -- generic solution to some problems with aggregating large, diverse classes

  - Tradeoff is that some of the efficiency brining it all together is compromised

- Litigation Class vs. Settlement Class

  - \[File -- cert -- settle\] vs. \[settle -- file -- cert for settlement -- one package\]

*Amchem Products v. Windsor: **asbestos settlement class: you still need to do a robust Rule 23 analysis for a mass tort settlement class, with "common questions of law and fact" among class members***

- In asbestos CA, question was whether to include all plaintiffs who were or would become injured as a result of exposure

- Court rejected settlement class because it did not meet the Rule 23 criteria in lack of commonality -- they should have divided it into subclasses

- Concerns over:

  - Adequacy of representation

  - Inter-class conflict (people injured now vs. future) -- they will have different opinions/incentives of how fund is used (invested or doled out now)

    - Variations in harms

    - Future plaintiffs

  - Adequacy of notice

- Breyer Dissent: settlement is important here; Differences in state law or uncommon questions should not make a difference; Sup Ct is too distant to decide on commonality here

- Facially, it is not harder to get a settlement class cert; Tidmarsh thinks it is actually easier

- Court did not articulate circumstances under which mass tort class actions would be appropriate - single calamitous event?

### Punitive Damages Classes

*Sharkey Article*

- PD are important because they have an important societal element and avoid the DP issues of damages for harm done to non-present plaintiffs

- Viability of the punitive damages class depends upon the persuasiveness of the societal conception of punitive damages

- Phillip Morris did not present a problem for PD classes since Court left untouched the non-retributive deterrence conception of punitive damages as a legitimate state interest

*In re Simon (Tobacco Certification case): **PD class cert does not work here because it does not bear sufficient nexus to actual and potential harm to plaintiff class***

- Weinstein tries to create a PD class for this mass tort in a mandatory limited fund (using the constitutional ceiling on PD ration)

  - Advocates for an advisory jury that would make a class-wide determination of harm and PD to whole class (actual jury would then determine whether conduct warranted damages)

  - This is an innovation with an "advisory" jury

- Surgeons do not support this PD class cert because of preclusive effects on unrepresented parties and DP concerns:

  - No futures -- people who develop disease later

  - Excludes second-hand smokers as well

- Chamber of commerce do not support cert because of jurisdictional concerns: New York has no legitimate interest in punishing conduct that took place outside its borders; that's what federalism is all about

- Holding: Weinstein's PD Class Cert Denied

  - There is no evidence to ascertain the limits of either the fund or the aggregate value of punitive claims against it, such that the postulated fund could be deemed inadequate to pay all legitimate claims.

  - Certification Order would fail to ensure that a jury will be able to assess an award that, in the first instance, will bear a sufficient nexus to the actual and potential harm to the plaintiff class, and that will be reasonable and proportionate to those harms

*"Repeat" Punitive Damages*

- Should PD decisions be awarded for successive plaintiffs?

- Problems

  - Each jury is not aware of other suits and awards high PDs, so you over-punish defendant

  - Problematic for deterrence in that you are over-punishing for societal/other ham repeatedly

  - Why should 1^st^ plaintiff get the windfall over other plaintiffs?

  - When you have a "core" bad to everyone you should not punish def more than once with PDs

    - Counter: you can include core badness piecemeal for each plaintiff

  - Tension between deterrence and punishment

  - With Weinstein's theory of PD class, you avoid the problem of over punishment with juries thinking they are the only case punishing defs, so we do not run out of money from the "PD fund"

- Pros

  - Repetition is ok because harm is worse with more plaintiffs coming forward and def should be punished accordingly

  - With the SF 1:10 ratio, you are only punishing for 10 people of extra harm, so you need repetition to reach optimal level of deterrence (for 100 people for example)

  - You should allow repeat PDs if you cannot consider harm to 3^rd^ parties

### Alternatives to Class Actions (MDL and Compensation Funds)

*MDL*

- Essentially consolidation across districts

- Bellwether trials:

  - Consolidates all trials to one jurisdiction for fact-finding/discovery

  - In theory, transferee district sends back for individualized trials, but parties usually end up settling

- Judge decides during settlement what is an acceptable way of contracting around the law (preclusive effect; similar to CA) (only transferee judge authorizes settlement)

- Problems: opt-out, are plaintiffs required to accept settlements?

- Pros: not binding, you get an idea of the merits of the whole case where you could not from an individual case

- *Vioxx* showed that Bellwethers might not be that informative, and parallel litigation might still be important (Vioxx settled despite favorable Bellwether trails because they did not want people to opt-out or have cases get sent back to original jurisdictions after MDL)
