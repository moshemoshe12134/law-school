> Economic Analysis of SL by Richard Posner:

- Whenever the damages exceed the cost of the deterring injury the D is likely to choose the precaution, even in a SL regime. But when the cost of damages is less than the precaution the SL tortfeasor will probably just pay the damages (not exactly the aim of the SL regime to deter activity levels)

- Courts usually don't say that an activity, even taken with all possible care is negligent. Ex: court won't assess values for driving to the grocery store vs going on a weekend roadtrip...the court looks at the level of care assoicated with your activity not the activity prima facie vis-Ã -vis the amount or reasoning.

  - This represents a pitfall of negligence

- SL however works to reduce activity levels deemed dangerous and therefore more activity would definitely necessiate more risk

  - Howver SL doesn't take into account plaintiffs here and their activity levels that bring forth injury

- Novel activities are often deemed dangerous until we learn about them (think Bethel) so attaching SL will presumably allow for cautious development of expertise

  - However some activities like explosives and wild animals will likely always be in the SL regime.

- Judicial Efficiency:

  - Will make tort claims easier through eliinating Negligence *but* will also encourage more claims. So while easier, may have more volume

A.  **Foreseeability Standard\*:**

> D's N is a proximate cause of P's harm if causing that harm was a foreseeable result of action. (Ex: accident after running red light.)

\* **Note:** sometimes P claims harm was unforeseeable in order to dodge contributory N.

- Did something unexpected contribute to the occurrence or severity of harm?

- If you couldn't foresee it, how could you prevent it, or balance B \>PL?

- **[Traditional Rule]{.underline}:** D is liable for **all** harm "directly" caused by N, even if it wasn't the foreseeable harm (even the explosion, **[In re Polemis]{.underline}**)

<!-- -->

- **Rationale:**

  1.  Act was N b/c harmful. Even if not *expected* harm, you're still N (**[Kinsman]{.underline}**).

  2.  Act is N in itself.

<!-- -->

- **[Modern Rule]{.underline}:** D only liable for Foreseeable consequences of his N (slippery, not fire, **[WagonMound]{.underline}**).

<!-- -->

- **Rationale:**

1.  If D anticipated this harm, might not have done it. Couldn't do B \> PL.

2.  N is a term of relation, only liable when you breach a duty you owed

3.  In life there are unavoidable risks and some of them are unforeseen; the charge of the court is not to dodge every risk, but to not create risks. The court cannot ask you to avert that which is unknown.

> Foreseeability is the hallmark of proximate cause.
>
> 2 step analysis

a)  was the D negligent

    a.  all risks considered inquiry

b)  whether the harm or injury was forseeable result of the negligence

    a.  asks about the nature and relation if the relatin between the defendant's negligence and what actually happened to the plaintiff

Harm within the Risk analysis demands the question: is the risk of the injury the plaintiff one of the risks that makes the defendant negligent? Did the defendant's negligence increase the risk of the injury

This analysis becomes more murky when you get to the secondary risks associated with negligence and these should go to juries to determine.

      - Normally will weigh using questions like in Andrews dissent below.

In order to limit the otherwise potential infinite liability which would follow every negligent act, the law of torts holds defendant amenable only for injuries to others which to defendant at the time were reasonably foreseeable.

In the absence of 'overriding policy considerations \* \* \* foreseeability of risk (is) of \* \* \* primary importance in establishing the element of duty.' (Grafton v. Mollica (1965) 231 Cal.App.2d 860, 865, 42 Cal.Rptr. 306, 310. See also McEvoy v. American Pool Corp. (1948) 32 Cal.2d 295, 195 P.2d 783; Hergenrether v. (See Keeton, Legal Cause in the Law of Torts (1963) 18---20; Seavey, Mr. Justice Cardozo and the Law of Torts (1939) 52 Harv.L.Rev. 372; Seavey, Principles of Torts (1942) 56 Harv.L.Rev. 72.)
