# 1. Big Concepts 

## 1.1 Nature and Functions of Tort Law

1)  **Torts** -- concerns the rights of private parties to obtain monetary compensation from those who have caused with injury or damage. Kind of: "A civil wrong not arising out of a contract"

    a)  **Accident law:** "Standard of Care" whose breach results in liability

        i)  **Strict liability:** imposed without regard to the degree of care that the defendant exercised.

        ii) **Negligence liability:** imposed only upon proof of some kind of carelessness

    b)  **Intentional torts:** liability is not imposed for negligence, but only upon proof of the defendant's intention to invade the legally protected interest of another.

2)  Four elements of any cause of action:

    a)  Duty

    b)  Breach of Duty

    c)  Causation

    d)  Damages/Harm

3)  **Four features of tort law**:

    a)  **Jury trial** -- Right to trial by jury in almost all tort cases

    b)  **Liability insurance** -- Availability of insurance influences the decision whether to sue and whom to sue

        i)  The more tort liability expanded, the more liability insurance became available

        ii) Often tort law expanded because liability insurance was or could be expected to become available

    c)  **Contingent fee system**

        i)  A plaintiff's attorney receives a percentage of the plaintiff's recovery if the suit is successful, and charges nothing if the suit is unsuccessful.

        ii) Affects the selection of cases in which suit is brought.

    d)  **"American Rule" for costs**

        i)  Each party pays its own costs, including counsel fees, win or lose

        ii) Plaintiffs are less discouraged from bringing suit because they do not risk incurring sizeable personal cost if they lose their suits.

## 1.2 Aims of Tort Law

1)  **Corrective justice & Civil Recourse (Fairness):** Individual injurer directly compensates individual victim with injurer's own money.

    a)  **Corrective justice:** restoring the moral balance between wrong-doer and the victim (injustice corrected)

        i)  **Critique:** less applicable in modern times with faceless corporations and liability insurance

        ii) Various opinions on importance, largely because defendants are typically large corporations or individuals with insurance

            (1) Large corporations: shareholders, employees, or customers may bear cost of compensating victims

            (2) Liability insurance: injurer's paid premiums are what she pays in compensation to the victim.

    b)  **Civil recourse:** tort law is a way of providing victims with recourse for civil wrongs

        i)  Some argue that tort liability is better understood not as ensuring injustice is corrected, but as providing redress for civil wrongdoings.

        ii) Under this view, the imposition of liability is a complex social practice that recognizes the occurrence of a wrongfully caused injury and vindicates victims' need for recognition that they have been wronged.

    c)  Both corrective justice & civil recourse conceptions understand tort law as mainly concerned with the moral relationship with between the plaintiff and defendant (ex-post) rather than that of future actors (ex-ante).

    d)  **Compensation**: principal function sometimes considered promotion of compensation for sufferer

        i)  *Why not:* Desirability of providing compensation to a particular class of injury victims rarely explains the lines drawn to distinguish those who are and those who are not entitled to prevail in a tort claim.

        ii) *Instead:* Liability is not imposed in order to provide compensation to victims; rather, they are provided compensation to serve other goals of tort law, corrective justice and deterrence.

        iii) **Pros:** Makes people feel more secure that they will be compensated

        iv) **Cons:** We need injury to justify liability, so there needs to be more than just one goal of compensation

2)  **Instrumental, Ex-Ante Framework**

    a)  **Optimal deterrence:** deter *excessively* risky activity so that only those losses worth avoiding are avoided.

        i)  Deterring certain losses is not worth what it would take to deter them.

            (1) Virtually every activity poses some risk, and up to a point, tort law threatens liability for risky activity, but beyond that point it does not, and the law tolerates the losses that result.

            (2) Some losses are worth avoiding (those caused by negligence) and some (those caused without negligence) are not worth avoiding.

        ii) Nothing in the principle of optimal deterrence dictates what values should be taken into account in determining which losses are and which losses are not worth deterring.

        iii) **Pros:** Economic solution, just enough deterrence to prevent risks not worth taking, but not too much to require foregoing some of the benefit of that activity.

        iv) **Cons:** Only takes into consideration economic values and ignores human values; pain & suffering from accident shouldn't be reduced to economic value of what it takes to prevent it.

    b)  **Loss distribution:** Inclination to develop rules of law that permit imposition of tort liability on businesses and institutions that can distribute their liabilities through the purchase of insurance or by raising the price of their products or services.

        i)  **Pros:** Large people bearing small cost \> single person bearing all of it

        ii) **Cons:** (1) does not satisfy sense of moral blame; (2) tort law expensive mechanism for distributing losses

    c)  **Redress of social grievances**: view that right to sue in tort promotes redress of social grievances, particularly against large, impersonal institutions.

        i)  In this view, tort law is a populist mechanism that permits ordinary people to put authority on trial.

3)  **Competing Aims:**

    a)  Deterrence (defendant-based) v. Compensation (plaintiff-based)

    b)  Blame-worthiness (defendant-based) v. Fairness (plaintiff-based)

    c)  Risk-spreading (defendant-based) v. Social welfare (plaintiff-based)

4)  **Summary: A mixed system**

    a)  Tort law operates to serve a set of goals whose strength is likely to vary with the situation. In some cases, concern for corrective justice will dominate, but in others, deterrence or concern for loss distribution will be the key.

\*\*\*The justice of tort law is at its heart corrective, rather than punitive.

**[Extra Recovery?]{.underline}**

\*\*\*Tort law does not aim to deter all harmful or risky behavior. Rather, it accepts that some injury or risk is necessary in society. But to maintain this principle, the level of deterrence must be proportional to the risk created. Imposing the full recovery against each defendant, regardless of their particular degree of fault, would likely cause society to be overly cautious, and deter behaviors that are necessary to society.

\*\*\*Similarly, a disproportionate recovery scheme would provide adverse incentives to plaintiffs.

The windfall possible in such cases could potentially cause a floodgate of litigation for plaintiffs

who have potential claims against multiple defendants. Thus, such a policy would cause society

to not only be overly cautious, but also overly litigious.

#### [Hammontree v. Jenner]{.underline} (CA 1971): Seizure; Sudden illness inappropriate for strict liability

**Gist**: Jenner (D) has epileptic seizure and runs car into Hammontree's (P) shop. He had a long history of seizures, but his doctor approved his driver's license. Last seizure 1953, now 1967 though\... **Proc. Hist:** Judge issued jury instruction on *res ipsa*, and jury found for D. P appealed asking for an instruction on strict liability.

**Rule:** Sudden illness rendering a driver unconscious will not be grounds for an action in [negligence]{.underline} or [strict liability]{.underline}.

**Issue:** Is strict liability in tort a proper theory to apply to sudden illnesses that renders a driver unconscious? *No.*

**Court:** held that it was proper not to offer an instruction on absolute liability, and thus affirmed judgment for Jenner (D) because he had taken his seizure medications and behaved in a reasonable manner.

**Analysis:** If he had constructive or actual notice of onset of serious illness, negligence may be found.

**Notes:**

1)  Holmes

    a)  Critical of strict liability. Believes in requirement that the defendant should have made a choice or action.

    b)  "State interference is an evil, where it cannot be shown to be a good."

    c)  Believes in private insurance and only imposing liability due to a fault based on action/choice

2)  Posner

    a)  Looks for the most cost-justified levels of accidents and safety

    b)  Where the measures necessary to avert the accidents would have consumed excessive resources, there is no occasion to condemn the defendant for not having taken them.

3)  *Maloney v. Rath*

    a)  Court refused to hold defendant strictly liable for her non-negligent violation of the traffic law (brake failure due to her garage mechanic's negligence)

    b)  However, she was held criminally liable because statutorily it was her duty to maintain operable car

- Three types of arguments & when they are effective:

  - **Human** (may be more attractive to jury):

    - Who is more blameworthy? Who can pay? Bear the losses with the least trouble?

    - Shared blame or innocent victim?

  - **Policy** (good for judge & jury)?

    - What is easy, less costly to administer? What activities, precautions do we want to encourage, discourage?

  - **Legalistic** (attractive to judge)

    - Easy default rule? Follow precedent.

### 1.4.ii Rules v. Standards (see also below)

1)  How much tailoring do you do? And how do you tailor?

2)  Rules: constraining and rigid.

    a)  Once interrupted and the facts are found, application of rule to the facts decides the issue to which it is relevant (e.g. 5 year old children are not negligent).

3)  Standards: intermediate level of constraint

    a)  Guide decisions but provide range of choice or discretion

    b)  Learned Hand: Standards define a set of mandatory considerations.

4)  Example: Railroad crossings for drivers

    a)  Holmes: argues that drivers must stop and look (rule, get out of car)

    b)  Cardozo: argues for standard (drivers must be reasonable)

## 1.5 Economic Theories of Torts

### 1.5.i Calebresi Aims of Tort Law (Cheapest Cost Avoider)

1)  Goal of tort law is to find the [cheapest cost avoider]{.underline}, and to make that person pay the costs.

    a)  Aim is optimal deterrence ðŸ¡ª minimize cost to society

    b)  Injury cost (cost to victim) + precaution cost (cost of activity)

    c)  Try to internalize all of the externalities

2)  Who is the cheapest cost avoider

    a)  **Cheapest Cost Avoider #1: [Best knowledge \*]{.underline}**

        i)  Stick liability on person who (1) knows risks/harm (who will be hurt and to what extent); (2) knows precautions available; (3) knows the tort law (needs to know that they are liable)

        ii) Problems with CCA 1: Morality, blameworthiness, social norms, distribution, risk spreading are not considered by CCA (school shouldn't pay for factory's pollution, almost seems like you are sticking liability on the more responsible party) AND no stability because who gets stuck with liability depends completely on volatile factors like technology

    b)  **Cheapest Cost Avoider #2:** **[Best bargainer]{.underline}**

        i)  Best briber is one who faces lowest transaction costs, based on Coase Theorem

        ii) Factors: (1) who knows the risks; (2) who knows how to reduce the risks?; (3) who knows where they will be liable?

    c)  ["But for" causation]{.underline} sweeps all candidates in, [proximate cause]{.underline} is the tool to implement the idea of CCA\< assigns liability to the CCA

        i)  BP Case (no causation) -- we want causation in fact for optimal deterrence b/c causation makes sure that precaution taken would have made a difference, don't want ppl just spending precaution money that makes no difference in other ppl's harms (waste) ðŸ¡ª many tortfeasors have limited resources, so only spend \$ on things that make a difference to people

        ii) Proximate Cause/HWR -- we have tort law for certain types of harm, that is what we assign liability for, we can't prevent freak accidents, harm has to be foreseeable, people can't take precautions they don't know about

    d)  **Calabresi** [Compensation goals]{.underline} (backward looking -- ex-post)

        i)  Spreading of losses/costs via insurance and mutual insurance to promote societal distribution of losses

        ii) Wealth distribution -- deep pockets should pay:

            (1) Better able to bear the costs (form of thinking about insurance)

            (2) Diminishing marginal utility of money ðŸ¡ª better to tax rich

            (3) More \$ means more power to control things = more responsibility

        iii) We are still uncomfortable with a disconnect with the physical connection; **causation doesn't matter** when we are just concerned with distribution or deep pockets (same as taxes)

        iv) **Fairness/Deterrence:** Person should only pay if at fault. Need to deter people form dangerous conduct, rich people can't just pay.

    e)  **Calabresi** [Deterrence goals]{.underline} (forward looking -- ex-ante) (negligence is CCA, not bad fault creator)

        i)  Efficient deterrence: minimize the sum of injury costs and safety (precaution) costs as well

        ii) **Specific deterrence** (regulation): regulations to control behavior (bans)

            (1) We do not need to know the exact causation

            (2) Not as economically efficient compared to general because it does not account for different situations such as changing technology and circumstances

        iii) **General deterrence** (price/tax): increases the price of certain activities such as carbon tax

             (1) **Works through causation**: A link between behavior and the harm -- we can generate the proper price and tax to achieve the right amount of deterrence.

             (2) **Forseeability matters more for this** because we can measure the right price that leads to the proper amount of deterrence (and not lead to over-deterrence or under-deterrence)

                 (a) You need to foresee the amount of harm in order to impose a proper amount of price or taxation to deter that behavior in an efficient manner

                 (b) Internalize the costs

             (3) Price mechanism makes it more efficient to adopt cheapest choice

                 (a) People can adopt a more efficient (cheaper) choice when there's no regulation

        iv) Controls both [*care levels* and *activity levels*]{.underline} ðŸ¡ª if the harm costs \$100 and the business is \$90, then it will shut down. *Care level* affects both the harm causer and the potential victim; *activity level* only controls the harm causer's behavior

**[Aims of CCA:]{.underline}**

1.  Minimize sum of accident costs and precaution costs.

2.  Assign loss to those who can most cheaply manage the risk.

    a.  Those who have information best know the risk

    b.  CCA forces information sharing â†’ incentivizes Trolley Co. to share dangers with pedestrians.

3.  What's missing?

    a.  For incentives to work, parties must know that tort law places liability on CCA.

    b.  Idea of blameworthiness/morality

    c.  Risk spreading (maybe Trolley Co., can absorb financial burden of precaution better)

    d.  Combinations of precautions

    e.  Punitive damages

### 1.5.ii Coase Theory -- Function of Law

1)  Coase Theory:

    a)  Definition: When there are no transaction costs, regardless of where liability is placed, the parties will bargain to get the cheapest, most optimal solution (school with giant fan and factory with scrubbers)

    b)  Questions the **purpose of law**? Do we need law at all if society will achieve most cost efficient result?

2)  Critiques and Limitations of Coase Theorem

    a)  Assumes that there are no transaction costs, which is unrealistic (and difficult with multiple parties)

        i)  Coase works when there is good bargaining ability:

            (1) Sophisticated parties who know how to negotiate

            (2) Parties have pre-existing relationships

            (3) Fewer parties

            (4) Harms are easily monetized

        ii) Role of Law: If transaction costs are too high, law is very important

    b)  Also have problem when there is **information asymmetry**

    c)  Ignores distributional consequences: fairness, fault, etc.

        i)  Coase works when bargaining power is equal: 2 big businesses

        ii) Role of law: where we stick liability, that party must bribe or pay, so law attempts to place liability on CCA (either party that knows more, or best briber), which creates an incentive for person stuck with liability to actually find the CCA.

#### [Brown v. Kendall]{.underline} (MA 1851): Dog-fight, stick in eye; Apply ordinary care to both 'case' and 'trespass'

**Facts:** Kendall (D), while attempting to separate his dog from Brown's (P) dog when the two dogs were fighting, accidentally struck Brown (P) in the eye with a stick. P brought action of *trespass*.

**PH:** TC required extraordinary care from Kendall (D) to escape liability, and ordinary care on part of Brown (P)

**Court (Shaw):** changes standard of behavior to **ordinary care** for both case and trespass.

- Burden is on the plaintiff to prove lack of ordinary care.

**Dicta**: plaintiff must also take ordinary care, or no recovery. Only recovery if plaintiff took care AND defendant did not take care (*Contributory negligence*)

**Analysis:** established that some form of fault, negligent or intentional, must form the basis of liability.

**Note** (Gregory): had incentive to make risk-creating less hazardous to investors than it had been at common law for the purpose of the industrial revolution.

- *Contributory negligence*: behavior on part of injured plaintiff falling below standard of ordinary care that contributes to the defendant's negligence, resulting in the plaintiff's injury.

**KA Notes**: Creates separate actions based on plaintiff proving that there was either intent to injure or negligence, whether the action is brought in trespass or in case (so intent was in addition to direct-ness). Further, there is possibility for situations where standard should be extraordinary care.

## 2.8 Rules v. Standards & Judge v. Jury

#### [Baltimore & Ohio RR v. Goodman]{.underline} (1927): Holmes-RULE; less deference to jury; contributory negligence; DV

**Facts:** Goodman (P) was killed. The truck he was driving was struck by the railroad's (D) train at a grade crossing. He slowed down while approaching and crossing and listened for the train, but his view was blocked and he could not stop once he saw the train.

**Railroad's defense**: Goodman was negligent as a matte of law; [contributory negligence]{.underline} meant not for jury.

**DC:** Denied railroad's motion for a directed verdict and submitted it to the jury, which found for Goodman.

**Court (Holmes):** Declares that there is a clear standard of conduct (leave car and look for train)

- "Nothing is suggested by the evidence to relieve Goodman of responsibility for his won death." (I.e. directed verdict should have been granted because plaintiff was also negligent as a matter of law)

**Reasoning**:

(1) Led to less suits during this time of diversity jurisdiction pre-[Erie]{.underline};

(2) Holmes hated the potential for inconsistent jury results for the same facts;

(3) \*Judges develop expertise from similar cases over time, and should be able to create and apply a standard, uniform, rule. Otherwise, juries oscillate and are unpredictable, where like cases are not decided similarly.

#### [Pokora v. Wabash Railway]{.underline} (1934): Cardozo-STANDARD; Changes from [Goodman]{.underline}; defer to jury; no DV

**DC**: directed verdict for defendant on authority of [Goodman]{.underline} (contributory negligence)

**[Schlag, Rules and Standards]{.underline}**

1)  Holmes v. Cardozo

    a)  Holmes -- bright line rule

    b)  Cardozo -- standard

2)  Defining rules and standards

    a)  Trigger -- empirical or evaluative

    b)  Response -- determined or guided

        i)  Rule -- hard empirical trigger and a hard determinate response

        ii) Standard -- soft empirical trigger and a soft modulated response.

3)  Rules v. Standards dialectic

    a)  Deterrence -- deter selected activities or conduct (can argue either way)

        i)  Rules:

            (1) Pros: sharp line between forbidden and permissible, so people can adjust.

                (a) Sharp lines assure no desirable or permissible conduct will be chilled.

            (2) Cons: Permit and encourage activity up to boundary of permissible conduct.

                (a) Application fails to distinguish between flagrant and technical violations (*does it though?)*

                (b) Allow Holmes' 'bad man' to treat deterrent as fixed cost of doing business

        ii) Standards:

            (1) Pros: allow addressees to make individualized judgments about how offensive conduct was

                (a) Deterrence from engaging in borderline conduct and to substitute less offensive activity.

            (2) Con: Risk-averse people will be chilled from conduct that is desirable or permissible.

                (a) Risk-preferring people will be encouraged to try antisocial conduct

                (b) Boundary is not preset, so there may be erratic results.

    b)  Delegation -- agencies, administrators\...

        i)  Rules

            (1) Pros: give subordinate complete authority and responsibility for factually defined tasks.

                (a) Need for tests of authority are minimized, and time is saved.

#### [Martin v. Herzog]{.underline} (NY 1920): Per se statute; car lights were off, so there is contributory negligence

**Facts:** Mrs. Martin's (P) husband was killed when the buggy he was driving at night without lights, in violation of a statute requiring them, collided with the car Herzog (D) was driving somewhat over the center line.

**Rule:** Failure to perform a statutory duty constitutes negligence per se, which can be prima facie evidence of contributory negligence (which must be overcome by proof in the opposite direction).

**Court (Cardozo):** Plaintiff is *contributorily negligent* because they violated statute by not having their lights on, so they were negligent per se.

**Alternative: (minority rule?)** Could have remanded for jury question deciding whether the statutory violations contributed to the accident.

- Cardozo follows rule/statute/precondition and does not submit issues to the jury; bends statutes to fit the facts of the case.

- [Alternative Argument:]{.underline} Violation of a statute proves negligence because a reasonable person would not violate a statute; BUT because statutory violations require civil penalties, this form of argument would make the defendant susceptible to double damages

- [Compliance]{.underline}*:* Compliance with a statue does not vindicate the violator; statutes set a *minimum standard of care,* and court may find that reasonable care exists above this minimum standard (see **Edwards**, **Hubbard-Hall,** and **Alvarado** on pg. 85)

#### [Tedla v. Ellman]{.underline} (NY 1939): No contrib. negligence per se following statute (walking next to road) because less safe

**Facts:** Ellman (D), the driver of a car which injured Tedla (P), and killed Bachek, defended a suit against himself by asserting the fact that the victims were struck while walking on the right side of the roadway was a violation of a statute. (Plaintiff was struck from behind by traffic while walking with back to traffic, even though statute said you have to walk facing traffic)

**Plaintiff** **contended:** due to heavy traffic on opposite side, it would have been more dangerous and risky to walk facing traffic.

**Defendant argued:** they were contributorily negligent per se because of the statute violation

**Rule:** Where a statutory general rule of conduct fixes no definite standard of care, but merely codifies or supplements a common law rule, which has been subject to exceptions; or where the statute is intended to promote public convenience or safety, then in the absence of clear language to the contrary, it is not negligence as a matter of law for one to violate the statute, if by so doing he is likely to prevent---rather than cause---the accident which it is the purpose of the statute to avoid.

**Court:** No negligence per se because following statutes would have been more dangerous.

- "We cannot assume reasonably that the legislature intended that a statute enacted for the preservation of the life and limb of pedestrians must be observed when observance would subject them to more imminent danger"

- Custom often **correlates** to what ought to be done

- If factor is compliant with custom, **cautions the jury** that finding actor negligent implies that the entire industry or grouping of peoples is also behaving unreasonably.

    a)  **The Safety Statute Exception** - When defendant's negligence consisted of breach of a statute designed specifically to protect a class of persons unable to protect themselves, the contributory negligence of a member of the protected class does not bar recovery. But if the plaintiff was also willfully or wantonly negligent, this cancelled out the defendant's blameworthy behavior.

        i)  If the negligence defendant had the last clear chance to avoid harming the plaintiff, then the plaintiff's contributory negligence was not a bar to recovery.

    b)  **Joint and Several Liability** -- under comparative negligence, what to do when there is joint liability\...pay just for your own share, or should you be jointly and severally liable for combined share of defendants in the case of insolvency?

        i)  Option 1: Hold defendants jointly and severally liable for the amount of the judgment (this has less appeal if defendants are just concurrent defendants, as opposed to joint tortfeasors)

        ii) Option 2: Hold each defendant liable for only his or her own proportional share of the reduced judgment. If one is not able to pay, the loss rests with the plaintiff.

        iii) Option 3: Plaintiff and solvent defendant are proportionally responsible for the insolvent defendant's share. (Negotiation between options 1 & 2).

4)  **Effect on Other Common Law Doctrines** -- Comparative negligence statutes and cases do not outline effect on other doctrines such as last clear chance, assumption of risk or greater-degree-of-blame exceptions to contributory negligence

    a)  **Last clear chance** -- most courts have ruled that this is no longer dispositive and these cases should follow the comparative negligence scheme. Jury can take the last clear chance into account when dividing negligence.

    b)  **Greater-degree-of-blame** -- largely unnecessary once jury is already dividing degree of negligence. But some courts have said that plaintiff's negligence is no defense to an intentional or similar tort but same-degree-of-blame can be a defense in these same torts.

    c)  **Assumption of risk** still completely bars recovery when plaintiff knowingly assumes the risk that defendant will injure. Usually this only works if plaintiff's assumption was unreasonable and therefore negligent, which the jury when dividing negligence can take into account.

5)  **Strict Liability** -- contributory negligence is only a defense against strict liability in some jurisdictions.

    a)  Easiest to apply comparative negligence whenever contributory negligence used to apply but some still bar recovery based on plaintiff negligence in strict liability cases.

    b)  Some say plaintiff's behavior is irrelevant

6)  **Setoffs** -- defendant counterclaims and both are given a judgment

    a)  Should the party owing more just pay the difference or should both parties be required to pay out the whole amount? Seems to be that when insurance is involved, both insurers must pay the entire amount to the opposing party.

## 4.1. Causation-in-Fact (But for)

![](media/image17.png){width="7.527777777777778in" height="5.054861111111111in"}

'But-for' cause is a **COUNTERFACTUAL** -- it requires imagination of what would have happened had defendant not been negligent

- Asks jury to make a decision about what would have happened

- Requires assumptions about the way the word typically works and would include jury biases about what is normal, including capacities of different groups of people (age, gender, etc.)

- Places a burden on the plaintiff to provide some concrete experiential basis for making a judgment on the way the world works in situations like their own, then court determines whether plaintiff has made out a prima facie case on the causation issue.

There is no rule about how much narrative detail a court will require before ruling the plaintiff's claims

If there are **[multiple necessary causes]{.underline}**, then both need to cause injury, so but-for works and is cause

If there are **[multiple sufficient causes]{.underline},** then the 'but for' test is under-inclusive, and does not work.

- E.g. 2 cars, car accident only avoided if BOTH cars have lights on ðŸ¡ª no 'but for'

- But for Car 1 lights off, car accident would still have occurred.

- But for Car 2 lights off, car accident would still have occurred.

#### [Aegis Insurance v. 7 World Trade Center]{.underline} (2c 2013): construction of WTC not cause-in-fact of collapse

**Facts:** ConEd sues World Trade Center for destruction to its building, arguing that the World Trade Center fell because of negligent construction.

- There was a lot of fire/damage to building with limited water supply; firefighters decided not to risk more lives to fight fire and established a collapse zone and let it collapse---caused damage to Con Ed substation that was built under the WTC 7 building, and they sued WTC 7 for negligence.

**Holding:** Given the unprecedented nature and sheer magnitude of the events of 9/11, the alleged negligence on the part of the defendants was not the cause-in-fact of the collapse of the World Trade Center (unprecedented constellation of events, falling burning debris triggering multiple fires on multiple noncontiguous floors, mortally wounded firefighters).

**Counter:** Reasonable jury could conclude that firemen would not have abandoned building if it were properly constructed.

**Dissent:** Could have been preventable, even if event was not foreseeable.

## 4.2. Multiple Sufficient Causes (MSC)

![](media/image14.png){width="7.527777777777778in" height="7.775in"}

**[Example: two fires converge to burn down a house]{.underline}**

**Case 1: Both fires are started by negligence**

- \'But-for\' test fails because the house would still have been destroyed, but courts have held that neither defendant can escape liability, both are held to have caused plaintiff\'s injury

- Two Simultaneous Causes: Both are Negligent

**Case 2: Only one of the fires is started negligently**

- \'But-for\' test fails: Should the one negligent party be held liable for the entire amount, even if damage would have occurred without their negligence?

- Imposing this liability is problematic because it will give such parties an incentive to take precautions against harms that will occasionally occur anyways because of a simultaneous non-negligent cause

- On the other hand, imposing it could still prevent negligent conduct.

- Many courts DO NOT hold the negligent defendant liable, but some courts DO.

- It is critical for courts that DO impose liability that they happen at exact same time.

**[Sequential Causes: Pre-emptive Causation]{.underline}**

One fire burns down the house, then another fire comes after. The first fire is '**pre-emptive'** cause.

- If the harms occur sequentially, then neither fire can be said to be a 'but-for' cause of the harm because the house would have still burned down.

- The first fire is not a 'but-for' cause, but caused the damage.

- **Defendant (of first fire) is liable if negligent**

- Second fire risked harm, but simply was not cause of harm.

- **Defendant (of second fire) NOT liable, even if negligent**

- This result is somewhat unfair because the later fire is just lucky and escapes liability even if he is blameworthy; but key because we only have liability for [ACTUAL HARM, not risk creation]{.underline}.

- Alternative? We could divide damages to get both fire starters, causation counterfactual could be that first fire is only responsible for the one second of house life, before 2^nd^ fire would have burnt down the house, not whole house.

### 4.4.1 Alternative Liability

- Defendants are more likely to be able to figure out who did what. So impose liability and let them bring proof and sort damages out

#### [Summers v. Trice]{.underline} (CA 1948): Two hunters shoot; man shot in eye; alternatively liable; burden shifts

**Facts:** Two hunters shoot towards the same quail; plaintiff gets shot in the eye, but they cannot figure out which hunter actually hit the plaintiff.

**Holding:** Under these circumstances, the **burden shifted** to each of the defendants to disprove that he had caused the harm; the defendants were not held to be each liable for the full amount of damages, but rather that the burden fell on them to prove that the other was at fault.

- Distinct from multiple sufficient causes because only ONE cause exists, but they cannot figure out which defendant.

- There is 50/50 chance that one defendant is guilty of injury, no preponderance.

- Not "joint" because 2 different shotguns, and they did not help each other with their shots

- Unfortunately, limited group is important, if there are more defendants, they are less likely to impose liability, even if plaintiff is even less likely to be able to prove anything.

**Hypo:** Same fact pattern as [Summers]{.underline}, but only one of the shooters was negligent, so the innocent Simonson could not be liable to the plaintiff even if his pellet had done the harm.

![](media/image30.png){width="7.527777777777778in" height="2.2381944444444444in"}

##### [Sindell v. Abbott Laboratories]{.underline} (CA 1980): Bad drug, many manufacturers; Solution: pay market share

**Facts:** Plaintiffs (women whose mothers had taken DES drug while pregnant and got a signature disease) sued DES drug manufacturers. However, no individual plaintiff could identify which manufacturer had made the DES that her mother had taken.

**Court:** Circumvented this by holding that the plaintiffs need not prove individual causation. Instead, each plaintiff recovered from each defendant a portion of her damages equal to the **defendant's share of the market** for DES.

- DES is chemically identical, so market share is an accurate surrogate for the amount of harm each defendant caused. Therefore, imposing liability in proportion to market share forces each defendant to pay compensation for amount of injury caused. Optimal deterrence is achieved.

##### [Hall v. Du Pont]{.underline} (EDNY 1972): Industry-wide liability for blasting-caps

**Gist:** 6 manufacturers of blasting caps sued by 13 children from separate incidents.

**Court**: if more likely than not that any child can prove that more likely than not a manufactures caused injury, burden shifted to defendant.

**Possible reasons:** (1) they made it intentionally difficult to know whose cap was whose; (2) the relatively small number of plaintiffs.

#### [Hymowitz v. Eli Lilly]{.underline} (NY 1989): Market share liability for DES during pregnancy

**Facts:** Plaintiffs allege that they were injured by the drug DES, which was ingested by their mothers during pregnancy; they seek relief against DES manufacturers.

**Statute of Limitations issue:** Resolved by statute that reignited 1 year SOL when latent effects are discovered.

**Court (Wachtler):** Limiting a defendant's liability to its market share will result, over the run of cases, in liability on the part of a defendant roughly equal to the injuries the defendant caused.

**Approach:** DES plaintiffs only are required to establish (all required):

(1) That the plaintiff's mother ingested DES during pregnancy

(2) That the plaintiff's injuries were caused by DES, AND

(3) The defendant or defendants produced and marketed DES for pregnancy purposes.

Subsequently, the burden of proof shifts to the defendants to exculpate themselves by establishing, **by a preponderance of evidence**, that the plaintiff's mother could not have ingested their particular pill (because the manufacturer did not market the pill for pregnancy purposes).

- Of those defendants who are unable to exculpate themselves from liability, their **respective share** of the plaintiff's damages would be measured by their **share of the national market** of DES produced and marketed for pregnancy purposes during the period in question. (Absolute-market share, and may lead to \<100%)

**Concurrence:** Want to do relative-market share, so it adds up to 100%, and want to exculpate defendants with unique pills that can prove they were not at fault.

## 4.5. Loss of Chance

![](media/image31.png){width="7.527777777777778in" height="1.2763888888888888in"}

**[Example]{.underline}:** Patient diagnosed late and had only 25% survival rate, but had he been diagnosed correctly, he would have had a 39% survival rate ðŸ¡ª Physician is liable for the 14% reduction in the survival rate.

- Note, they are 100% responsible for a decrease in survival rate of 14%.

#### [Matsuyama v. Birnbaum]{.underline} (MA 2008): Doctor misdiagnosis step-cancer; court recognizes Lost Chance theory

**Facts:** Doctor diagnoses patient with stomach cancer late in the illness (misdiagnosed for 3 years). By the time the doctor makes the diagnosis, the prognosis has fallen by 37.5%. The plaintiff dies.

**Old Theory:** A plaintiff may recover damages only by showing that the defendant's negligence more likely than not caused the ultimate outcome, in this case the patient's death (i.e. if patient had a 51% chance of survival, and the negligent misdiagnosis caused that chance to drop to zero, the estate is awarded *full* wrongful death damages.

- Problems with old theory: lets doctor off no matter what if someone has \<50% chance of survival.

- Inconsistent with aims of tort law -- allocating costs and risks of human injuries; fails to deter; immunizes whole areas of medical practice from liability.

**New Theory/Court (Marshall):** Recognizes loss of chance as a theory of injury.

- In order to prove loss of chance, a plaintiff must prove by a **preponderance of the evidence** that the physician's negligence caused the plaintiff's likelihood of achieving a more favorable outcome to be diminished.

- Limited this to medical malpractice actions.

**Damages:**

- P&S in two parts: (1) delay in treatment caused P&S-pay in full; (2) P&S in death-pay share.

- Death under wrongful death statute/medical malpractice, then take share.

- Share: chance of survival immediately preceding malpractice -- chance of survival as a result of medical malpractice.

**Limiting principles:** Medical malpractice

**Can someone who survived sue?** No, (under-deterrent results---need to show harm)

**Can only sue for limited % change, not 100% windfall at 51%** (Concept: Harm within the risk)

#### [Palsgraf v. Long Island Railway]{.underline} (NY 1928): Fireworks not foreseeable consequence of negligent act

**Facts**: Palsgraf (P) was standing on a platform of LIRR (D). - Factors: (1) Natural and continuous sequence; (2) Not too many intervening causes; (3) Is negligence likely to create that effect; (4) foreseeable post-negligence; (5) Not too remote time and place

##### [In re Polemis]{.underline} (UK 1921): Sailors-unforeseen explosion; type /extent of damage is irrelevant; is it direct?

**Facts:** Sailors drop a wooden board while transporting petrol from one hold to another, causing a massive explosion.

**TC:** Employees were negligent in causing plank to fall and caused explosion from a spark. Spark could not have been reasonably anticipated from falling plank, only some damage to ship would have been foreseeable.

**Issue:** Was DF's negligence a proximate cause of the unforeseeable harm?

**Court (Bankes):** The anticipations (re: **extent of damage** and **type of damage**) of the person whose negligent act has produced damage are irrelevant; the damages claimed are not too remote.

- Rejects distinction between unanticipated *extent of harm* and *type of harm*. Says anticipation is irrelevant.

- Injury was a '**[direct']{.underline}** consequence of negligence, so hold him liable.

**Opinion (Scrutton):** It is relevant to determine whether any reasonable person would foresee that the act would cause damage; if he would not, then the act is not negligent; **once the act is negligent, the fact that is exact operation was not foreseen is immaterial.**

##### [The Wagon Mound]{.underline} (NY 1928): Overruled [In re Polemis]{.underline}; Is damage kind that reasonable man should foresee

**Facts:** A ship chartered by defendants spilled oil into a bay; defendants made no effort to disperse the oil. Then, the oil ignited and a fire spread, causing extensive damage to the plaintiff's equipment/dock.

**Defendant:** Argues that type of harm matters; Foreseeability is key to determining whether there are different types of harm.

**Plaintiff** fails to argue that it was foreseeable that oil would ignite and burn the dock; (probably because plaintiff is worried about contributory negligence.)

**Court:** The essential factor in determining liability is whether the damage **is of a kind such that a reasonable man should have foreseen.** A man should not be held liable for damage **unpredictably** **caused** by a reasonable man because it was direct or natural.

- Limited by [Smith v. Leech Brain]{.underline} (UK 1982), which holds that the defendant must take his victim as he finds him.

**Rejects:** Direct test from [In re Polemis]{.underline}; says that direct test creates a never-ending problem of causation.

- But there is still liability to an unforeseen extent of harm. In this case, jury found fire was unforeseeable type of harm, because the defendant did not know fire was possible.

#### [Rylands v. Fletcher]{.underline} (UK 1866): Mill owner held strictly liable for water damage from reservoir

**Facts:** Fletcher's (coal mines, P) property was flooded by water, which broke out of a reservoir constructed and maintained by the Ryland (D, cotton mill) on their land. Rylands was not negligent (contractors were).\
**Court:** Neighbor is strictly liable for any damage caused by his non-natural use of land ("takes the risk upon himself")

- Cannot impose negligence, but uses strict liability based on the principle of 'escape'

- Traditional rule: if you bring something and it escapes, you are liable for anything that occurs during escape (usually animals), but extends to water, filth, stenches, etc.

**Rationales:** (1) Rylands is cheapest cost avoider; (2) Rylands benefits from building reservoir, so he should pay; (3) Want to deter reservoir building altogether?

**[House of Lords]{.underline}** (1868): Add the [natural v. unnatural]{.underline} distinction

- People had no way to take care if plaintiff brings unnatural condition-bringer of unnatural condition is CCA

- If it was natural, then others could expect that danger and take precautions (PL would be CCA in that case)

#### [Doerr v. Goldsmith]{.underline} (NY 2013): No SL for dog because no viscous propensities

**Facts:** Plaintiff rides a bike and gets injured when defendants let their dog run across the road to the other defendant; plaintiff could not have avoided dog and collided with it.

**Court:** There exists no evidence that the defendant had knowledge that her dog had a propensity to interfere with traffic, and her motion for summary judgment was properly dismissed.

- If owner knew or should have known about animals viscous propensities ðŸ¡ª strict liability

- For domestic animals that owner does not know or have reason to know to be abnormally dangerous ðŸ¡ª negligence (owners must take reasonable steps to prevent domestic animal from causing \[foreseeable?\] injuries.

**Rule:** NY common law does not allow negligence liability for injures caused by animals. Only imposes SL on cases where injury results from a **vicious animal** and owner is aware of vicious-character of animal.

- If animals escaped there could also be strict liability.

- Plaintiff wants to sue for negligence, court allows this by saying that in this case, the fact that it was an animal was irrelevant, and the animal's behavior did NOT cause the accident. Instead it was the owner's negligence for 'throwing animal' across the road. Likens it to throwing a football across a yard.

#### [Indiana Harbor Belt RR v. American Cyanamid]{.underline} (7c 1990): Negligence with greater care is adequate; no SL

**Facts:** Plaintiff operates a railroad that transports a toxic chemical manufactured by the defendants; plaintiff sues for damages arising out of a chemical leak (train company had to pay clean up costs of \$1M).

- Should leak of dangerous chemicals be under negligence or strict liability? Apply R2T factor test: (1) Negligence is enough to prevent accidental spill; (2) No substitute route to transport dangerous chemicals -- cost too much to re-route; (3) Danger is not from substance, but from mishandling of chemicals; (4) Residents fault for being closet to rail yard, shouldn't live in dangerous area

- State court applies Restatement, so Posner applies it here

**Court (Posner):** Negligence regime (greater care) is adequate to remedy and deter the accidental spillage of chemicals from railroad cars.

**Rule:** Manufacturer of a product is not considered to be engaged in an abnormally dangerous activity merely because the product becomes dangerous when it is handled or used in some way after it leaves his premises.

## 6.2 Strict Liability v. Negligence

5)  Rights-based theories:

    a)  "**Benefit**" ðŸ¡ª Those who benefit from engaging from an activity should rightly bear the costs associated with that activity

    b)  "**Reciprocity**" ðŸ¡ª Strict liability should be imposed when an activity poses risks for potential victims that the victims do not also pose for others.

**Comparison:**

If you can take (more) care to eliminate the risk you are in the world of negligence.

If you cannot eliminate the risk with care, then you are in the world of strict liability.

### 8.3.1 Compensatory Damages

#### [The Equipoise Effect]{.underline}, Bert Huang

**Disgorgement:** allows person to break even.

**Substituting disgorgement for any other remedy**, part of the time, can emulate the incentive effect of using that other remedy all of the time. Therefore, in theory, courts can sometimes substitute disgorgement for compensatory or expectation damages without undoing the benefits of harm internalization. This can be especially useful when harm is hard to measure.

1)  Introduction

    a)  **Deterring polluters:** better to disgorge profits or pay damages based on harm?

    b)  **Typical view:** More disgorgement means more deterrence; many courts have found that to be a "strong medicine" that should be used "sparingly"; Viewed as OVER-Deterrence

    c)  **BUT:** if you know that you will disgorge, your act is not costly or gainful, it is just a wash. Therefore, it is not a complete deterrence.

2)  **Huang: Substituting disgorgement for any other remedy, part of the time, can emulate the incentive effect of using that other remedy all of the time.**

    a)  Example: patent infringer disgorges net profits (breaking even) or pays compensation for harm to patentee (thus keeping profits while also paying for patentee's loss). Anticipating uncertain mix of potential remedies, start-up founder should be expected to make the *same* decision as if she faced only the harm-based damages with certainty. If her net gains \> compensation, then she will infringe and with some probability disgorge. Likewise, if profits \< compensation, then it is not worthwhile for her to infringe.

        i)  **Logic**: some chance of breaking even, but otherwise enjoying a net gain, is still a net gain overall.

    b)  **My problem**: **I don\'t think the probability you won't receive that profit is a 0-sum game if you only can dedicate so much time to different activities with profit...If you use the technical profit terminology, it is nearly impossible to estimate...**

    c)  **Benefits:** provides flexibility in switching between remedies easing tradeoffs with non-deterrence concerns in a given case, such as distributional effects or certain notions of justice.

3)  **Conditions**

    a)  **Not required:** nothing requires harms and gains be similar in value; logic does not rely on the use of a liability test for predetermining which acts should be deterred.

    b)  **Particular application:** common problem for courts and regulators---harm based damages are often hard to measure (particularly compensatory damages, with resulting systematic underdeterrence). **Huang wants to substitute disgorgement when harm-based damages are biased or hard to assess**.

    c)  **Factors pushing actor from equipoise to complete deterrence:** litigation costs, reputation costs, opportunity costs.

        i)  **Complications with determining harm-based or gain-based award**.

    a)  **Limitations:** (1) actor's gains may be hard to measure; (2) substitution plainly sacrifices the aim of accurate compensation for those injured parties who do end up with a disgorgement award.

    b)  **Problems of measuring harm:** inchoate and future harms are particularly difficult; also-subjective or emotional harms; even economic harms, such as lost profits or market prices when no market exists...

        i)  **CL approach:** ranges from guesswork to no damages at all. If a court decides that the amount of damages cannot be shown with **"reasonable certainty"** then the award may end up being zero-even when the harms are very real.

### 8.3.2 Punitive Damages

**[Purpose]{.underline}:**

**Old reasons:** exemplary (set example); compensate for intangible damages (P&S weren't allowed yet)

    1.  **"Absolute Deterrence" --** **gains-based (e.g. disgorgement)** eliminates the defendant's gains and profits; tries to **stop** the bad behavior

2.  **Punishment**

    1.  **Justice & retribution** for the defendant, plaintiff, and society

**[Why constrain punitive damages]{.underline}?**

- **Burden: preponderance \< reasonable doubt:** damages begin to resemble criminal punishments (criminal trials have higher burden of proof; the same punishment shouldn't be imposed in civil trials with lower burden)

- **DPC requires fair notice**

#### [Exxon v. Baker]{.underline} (2008): permits punitive damages; \$2.5B capped at 1:1; oil tanker spill case

## 8.4 Statutory Damages

**[Framing important]{.underline}: willing to accept is higher than willing to pay**

**[Statutory damages]{.underline}** are set by legislatures.

**[Why statutory damages?]{.underline}**

Criminal-like punishment; Fair notice & deterrence; Predictability; Incentive to sue; Hard to calculate actual damages

- *Examples*: Copyright, cyper-squatting, hacking, spam, littering, disclosure/fraud, employment

**[Constitutional limit]{.underline}:** whether the penalty prescribed is **so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportionate to the offense and obviously unreasonable.**

- Statutory damages are not to be confined or proportioned to the loss of the private party.

- The legislature may adjust damages for the **public wrong** rather than the **private injury**.

- Usually, statutory damages work linearly: statute gives a \$ range for each violation, and jury will total up the cost per violation.

**[Critique of Statutory damages]{.underline} (Linear v. Concurrent damages):**

- Awards are linear, why not use concurrent damages?

- **Concurrent damages** are awarded in criminal sentencing -- allows judges to use **discretion to collapse multiple, redundant counts** into one count.

- Counting linearly is usually **arbitrary**: it is like a lottery---can over or underdeter.

- E.g. email hacking over a week, depending on whether hacker logs out frequently or not; can range 1-100s

- Normal judicial remedies (DPC, excessive fine constraints, CL remitter) don\'t work well in context of statutory damages and are overly blunt.

- Fudging, where you artificially lower each count award when counting is high also creates inaccuracy because in the next similar case, the fictionally low price per violation may be seen as a benchmark and lead to under-deterrence for next violator.

- Flexibility is needed to allow **case-by-case tailoring** of the structure of how damages accumulate.

- **Danger of concurrent damages:** introduces **judicial freedom** (more risk of bias or variability in outcomes)

**[Copyright Infringement and Remedies]{.underline}:**

**Statutory damages option** (instead of actual damages and profits); strict limits on payout per infringement;

- Actual damages are difficult in patent suits.

**[Sony v. Tenenbaum]{.underline}** (CA 2013, packet 4, page 110):

- *Facts*: Defendant illegally downloaded and distributed copy-written music against the warnings of his parents and school

- *Issues*: (1) What is the correct standard for evaluating the constitutionality of an award of statutory damages under the Copyright Act? (2) Did an award of \$675k violate defendant's due process?

- *Holding*: (1) *Gore* cannot logically apply to an award statutory damages under the Copyright Act (under second prong, plaintiff need not prove actual damages and under third prong, court would have to compare award against itself); instead, under *Williams,* **to determine whether the penalty prescribed is so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportioned to the offense and obviously unreasonable,** Court must examine the **legislative purpose of the statutory damages and defendant's behavior**; (2) award was not unreasonable

#### [In re Deepwater Horizon]{.underline} (2014): January class certification & March contract dispute

**Facts:** started as MDL, but BP made Settlement Agreement and Class Certification for all cases. **DC certified** the class and settlement, emphasizing the 'uncapped compensation' that would be available so benefits to one member of class would not reduce or interfere with benefit for another class member.

- **BP in rush to settle:** feared looking bad in criminal trial against USG, etc.

- **Settlement agreement:** No cap on fund; replaced the voluntary fund (GCCF) run by Feinberg (\$20B); formula does not require proof of causation (just economic evidence)---inevitable problem.

- **BP regrets this:** because there are claimants who didn't actually get injured now collecting compensation

**[January Class Certification Challenge (FRCP 23):]{.underline}**

**Gist:** BP does not object because they agreed to class certification, but it is challenged by 3^rd^ parties

- **[Commonality]{.underline}:** D says commonality requires the plaintiff to demonstrate that class members have suffered the same injury ðŸ¡ª preclude all plaintiffs that suffered an injury.

- Does not need to relate to damages component of claim; same injury can refer to the same conduct related to defendant's liability;

- **A single common question is sufficient**; long list of common questions; court does not need to determine whether class contains individuals who have not suffered injury, because that is part of merits

- **Adequacy:** BP argues that because class includes people not injured, the inclusion of non-injured members, makes injured members worse off because less \$ available, and hurts their bargaining power during negotiations.

- **DC:** named plaintiffs adequate with category of loss and adequate counsel.

- Uncapped compensation available under agreement ensuring adequate remedy

- **Subclass:** Cobb Objectors wanted this for states and members better off under GCCF claims process

- BUT: doesn't need to accommodate every possible weighted interest; claim under federal law, so no need for state-by-state; incorporated to agreement because sub-class based on geography, level of harm based on proximity to coast.

- **[Predominance]{.underline}:** wide disparity in damages doesn't preclude class certification with common issue

**[March Contract dispute---causation problem:]{.underline}**

**Issue:** settlement agreement didn't require an extrinsic causation analysis in paying damages. Is this ok if class certification did require causation?

**Contract has explicit language disclaiming need for proof of causation**

- Only required V or U profit curve, so after spill, business down then up (can be under *and* over-inclusive)

- However, the contract did make claimants **certify** that they were injured because of oil spill ðŸ¡ª gets around class certification problem because there is an **affidavit of causation, and subject to perjury and investigation**

**BP challenges:** "*BP, in essence is arguing that only if a claimant can prove its injuries are traceable to BP's conduct will Exhibit 4B's forswearing of the need for proof of traceability to BP's conduct apply*"

- Says there will be a chilling effect for future class actions and settlements

- Unfair to those actually injured if uninjured gets monies ðŸ¡ª hurts legitimacy

- BP just wants administrator to screen claims that are super obviously fraudulent

**Why is this settlement good?**

- Proof of causation may be expensive; this helps people who deserve it but are disadvantaged

- Screening takes too much time and money

- Administrator can make mistakes, better to have false positives

- Larger deterrent effect on oil companies to take more care
