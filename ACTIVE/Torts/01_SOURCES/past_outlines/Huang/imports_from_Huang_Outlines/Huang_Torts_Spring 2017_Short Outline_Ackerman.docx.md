[**Abnormally dangerous activity**:]{.underline}

1)  Foreseeability

2)  Highly significant risk

3)  Common usage (can be framed -- to people/area/industry)

    a)  Also consider: location appropriateness and value to community

**[Animal]{.underline}:**

1)  **Highly vicious?** If yes, then strict liability

2)  **Non-vicious animal?** If yes, then no negligence (one-bight rule)

    a)  Consider if animal is causing injury as animal (or as football thrown across the street)

**[Benefits of SL v. Negligence]{.underline}**

1)  Reduces information costs (costly to determine proof of negligence)

2)  Reduces error costs (more predictable, less fact-intensive)

3)  Influences activity levels and research decisions

4)  Distributes loss

5)  Rights-based theories:

    a)  Benefit: those benefiting from activity should rightly bear costs associated

    b)  Reciprocity: SL should be imposed when activity poses risks for potential victims that victims do not pose for others

**Choosing:**

- If you can take (more care) to eliminate risk you are in the world of negligence

- If you cannot eliminate the risk with more care, you are in the world of SL

# (5) Intentional Torts

**[[Trespass to land]{.underline}]{.mark}: (STRICT LIABILITY)**

1)  **Elements:**

    a)  **Entry** ðŸ¡ª anything fits, including intangibles (bacteria?)

    b)  **Harm** ðŸ¡ª not necessary (presumptive invasion of ownership feelings); BUT if no injuryðŸ¡ªnominal damages

    c)  **Intent** ðŸ¡ª only need intent to enter land; NOT intent to enter someone else's land (no intent ðŸ¡ª negligence)

2)  **Defenses to trespass: Necessity Exceptions**

    a)  **Private necessity: qualified/conditional defense (must compensate)**

        i)  **When:** risk to one party's property only, who can reduce/eliminate risk by damaging someone else's

        ii) **Liability:** D liable to P for damage to P's property; privilege conditioned on P later compensating

        iii) **Incentivizes cheapest cost avoidance calculation** (deterrence)

    b)  **Public necessity: absolute defense (no compensation)**

        i)  **When:** risk to property of many ('public') can be reduced by damaging property of D

**[[Conversion]{.underline}]{.mark} (like trespass, but with [personal property]{.mark}):**

1)  **Elements:**

    a)  **P owns real property** ([Kremen]{.underline} provides 3 part test; all property meets it)

        i)  Interest capable of precise defintion

        ii) Capable of exclusive possession or control

        iii) Putative owner must have legitimate claim to exclusivity

        iv) *See also*: Tangibility requirement (physical item) or Merger requirement (tangible document merged)

    b)  **Wrongful deprivation** (D deprives P or property; must be **permanent/indefinite**; not giving back)

    c)  **Unauthorized conduct by D** (did not have P's permission)

    d)  **Harm or injury** (damages = full value of good converted + loss of extra profits OR return of property)

**[[Trespass to chattels]{.underline}]{.mark} (interference with possession of [personal property]{.mark}):** recovery for *interference* with possession of personal property, but *not severe or important enough* to be conversion.

1)  **Impaired use of chattel v. Conversion**:

    a)  **Duration of interference** (forever gone or just a few days?)

    b)  **Type of possession** (stole care or sat in back seat?)

    c)  **Profit/benefit to defendant** (used machine to make \$ or just blocked usage of machine?)

2)  **Elements: (1) Intentionally (2) interfered with the (3) P's right of possession in chattel; (4) injury**

    a)  **No requirement for D's ownership** (only current right of possession)

    b)  **Intent: to touch or use** (not intent to invade legal right or harm chattel

    c)  **[Actual injury]{.underline}**: BIGGEST DIFFERENCE FROM TRESPASS TO LAND; For liability, need (1) actual damage; (2) actual dispossession f the chattel; (3) loss of use of the chattel for some appreciable amount of time, OR (4) harm to P or to something or someone in whom P had legal interest, because of D's action.

**[Legal remedies]{.underline}:**

1)  Compensatory damages ðŸ¡ª includes pain and suffering for physical injury

    a)  Pain and suffering: for physical damage only; cap? Purpose?

    b)  Single recovery: only one recovery including past & future losses

    c)  Inchoate and future loss: no independent COA for losses yet to occur, must wait for actual occurrence

    d)  Reasonably certain through preponderance of the evidence

    e)  Disgorgement: Huang-Equipoise effect (part of time doesn\'t change incentives of former remedy all time)

2)  Punitive damages (still ongoing)

    a)  Purposes: Deterrence (absolute or optimal) and Punishment (justice and retribution)

    b)  When: *small incentive for many little suits*, *hard to detect*

    c)  Problems: bankruptcy, uncertainty, encourages vindictiveness and revenge-seeking state of mind, DPC

    d)  Procedures: *Bifurcation*-liability for PD at all? If yes, jury sets PD award; *Special verdict*-jury gives findings on factual issues not necessarily stating which party should win, judge decides Q's and draws legal implications

    e)  Repetitive awards: punitive damages multiple times over, may violate DPC

3)  Nominal damages

**[Equitable remedies]{.underline}:**

1)  Permanent injunctions ([eBay]{.underline} test)

    a)  4-factors:

        i)  Suffered irreparable harm

        ii) Remedies at law are inadequate to compensate

        iii) Balance of hardships between P & D (is injunction warranted)

        iv) Public interest (is it disserved by granting injunction?)

2)  TRO/Preliminary Injunctions

**[Statutory remedies]{.underline}:**

1)  Set by legislatures (may be criminal-like)

2)  Pro: fair notice/deterrence, predictable, incentivize suit, actual damages may be difficult to calculate

3)  Problems: inflexible, may be arbitrary, tailoring & judicial freedom

**[Limitations on damages]{.underline}:**

1)  **Partial defenses**

    a)  Proximate cause?

    b)  Contributory negligence?

2)  **Full defenses to injury**

    a)  **"Pure" economic harm**: no liability; must have property or personal damage to recover economic loss

        i)  **Exceptions**: NJ allowed pure economic harm if it is foreseeable

        ii) **Pros if allowed**: easily calculable, recognizes compensatory aim of tort law, added deterrence

        iii) **Cons if allowed**: causation difficult to determine without injury, chilling effect

    b)  **"Pure" emotional harm**: no liability without physical injury

        i)  **Why**: would open floodgates (anything can be emotional loss); potential for fraud; require standards of reasonable care that don't exist; legal resources limited, save for people needing it more.

        ii) **Exceptions**: mishandle corpse of loved one, telegram incorrectly announcing death (STD?)

3)  **Judicial Review**

    a)  **Too speculative: must be 'reasonably certain'**

        i)  **Appeals: "Shocks the conscience"**

    b)  **Additur:** jury award unduly low, must add or retrial

    c)  **Remittitur:** jury award unduly high, must lower or retrial

4)  **Alternative Limits:**

    a)  **DPC for punitive damages**

    b)  **Statutory Ceiling/cap**

# (7) Mass torts

**[Class actions]{.underline} (FRCP 23):**

1)  **Requirements for all classes (a)**

    a)  **Numerosity** (joinder impracticable)

    b)  **Commonality** (common question of law or fact); Posner-use subclasses or sub-issues

    c)  **Typicality** (claims or defenses of representative parties typical of claims or defenses of the class)

    d)  **Adequate representation** (representative party will fairly and adequately protect class' interest)

2)  **Types of classes (b); Need AT LEAST ONE of:**

    a)  **Limited fund/Inconsistency**: limited pool for recovery or incompatible legal standards

        i)  **Notice mandatory & cannot drop out**

    b)  **Injunctive class:** injunction or declaratory relief; remedy would itself affect entire class

        i)  **Must show:** D acted or refused to act on ground applying generally to class (common practice)

        ii) **Notice not mandatory**

    c)  **Damages class** (most common and controversial; most difficult to certify)

        i)  **Requirements:**

            (1) **Common issues [predominate]{.underline}**

            (2) **Class action is superior to alternative methods** for fairly & efficiently adjudicating controversy

            (3) **Need notice & opt-out mechanism**

3)  Sub-classes and issue classes?

4)  **Mass Settlements (e):** Non-adversarial class action settlement requiring judge's approval.

    a)  **Requires:** notice and Fairness Hearing, where 3^rd^ parties can object, etc.
