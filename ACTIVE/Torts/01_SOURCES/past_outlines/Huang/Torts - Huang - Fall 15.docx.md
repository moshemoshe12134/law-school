THEMES OF TORT LAW

**Types of arguments**

AIMS OF TORT LAW

I.  **Deterrence vs. compensation**

II. **Fairness vs. blameworthiness**

III. **Risk spreading vs. social welfare**

CONFLICTING AIMS OF TORT LAW

I.  **Corrective justice and civil redress**

    - tort law to restore moral balance between wrong-doer and victim

    - way of providing victims with recourse for civil wrongs

    - PROS: justifies sense of moral blame; works well for individuals; vindicates victim; disincentives wrong doer

    - CONS: less applicable when DF is faceless corporation; liability insurance covers individuals

II. **Optimal deterrence**

    - Deter excessively risky activity so losses are worth it (BPL)

    - PROS: most economic solution, doesn't require foregoing activity benefits

    - CONS: ignores human values like pain & suffering and reduces to economic value

III. **Loss distribution**

     - Promotes the broad distribution of losses due to accident/injury

     - PROS: Among all other things equal, having large number of people bear small loss is better than having single person bear all of it

     - CONS: Does not satisfy sense of moral blame; tort law system is an expensive mechanism for distributing losses (insurance, social security are cheaper)

IV. **Compensation**

    - Promote compensation to those who have suffered injury

    - PROS: Makes people feel more secure that they will be compensated for the harm they suffered

    - CONS: We need more than injury to justify liability, so there needs to be more than just the goal of compensation

CHEAPEST COST AVOIDER -- Calabresi's aim for tort law

Assigns liability to the party that best knows the risks/harms, the precautions possible, and the law

- Aims: optimal deterrence, minimize costs

  - Compensation goals: promote societal distribution of losses

    - Fairness/deterrence issue: can't just get richest person to pay if the person at fault gets off free; need to deter the person doing the dangerous activity

  - Deterrence goals: minimize precaution costs

    - Regulation (specific deterrence) -- no need for causation; or general deterrence (e.g. tax) -- works through causation

      - Controls care and activity levels

- Policy concerns: is the cheapest cost avoider always fair?

- CONS:

  - What if there is incomplete knowledge or a 3^rd^ party has the best knowledge?

  - No consideration of morality or blameworthiness; trading minimal costs with other aims of tort law

  - Deep costs/most able to bear cost has no relation with causation

  - Can be inconsistent as technology/circumstances change (CCA doesn't care who was liable before; will shift liability as long as technology shifts)

*Brown v. Kendall* (MA 1850)

- [Facts]{.underline}: Dog fight--- in attempting to break up the fight with a stick, the DF accidentally struck the PL in the eye. Claim of trespass (direct harm)

- [Principles]{.underline}:

  - Judgment standard is "did DF take ordinary care in the circumstances?"

  - Recognition that PL contributed to accident in some way -- "takes 2 to tort"

  - Judge Shaw -- changes standard to ordinary care, with burden on PL to prove lack of ordinary care

*Adams v. Bullock* (NY 1919)

- [Facts]{.underline}: Boy swings huge wire around which catches onto the wiring of trolley and electrocutes him; sues trolley company for negligence in preventing accident

- [Holding]{.underline}: not negligent

  - Trolley company was operating lawfully; PL did not show evidence of failure to take reasonable care; unreasonable to ask the company to prevent unreasonable accidents (expensive, impractical)

- Policy consideration: would deter industrial growth; not worth preventing

**"...in the circumstances"**

*Daniels v. Clegg*

- [Facts]{.underline}: daughter driving buggy; Daniels on wrong side of the road hits her, daughter's dad sues & Daniels claims negligence

- [Holding]{.underline}: not negligent -- given instructions to compare Clegg to "woman of her age"

*Railway Co. v. Tankersley*

- [Facts]{.underline}: woman injured trying to get on train in skirt

- [Holding]{.underline}: negligent -- should not have tried to get on knowing it was dangerous for her condition

\*compare *Daniels* and *Tankersley*; personal circumstances of each PL are used differently by the court. The 'ordinary reasonable person' can still be very broad

**When to into account special characteristics**

- **physical infirmity**: usually considered part of the "circumstances"; so would be taken into account if reasonable person would take it into account; subjective because physical disabilities are easier to see/prove than mental; can raise standard of care for disabled DFs

- **mental infirmity**: no (objective standard)

- **expertise/superiority**: yes; help---e.g. novice could not have made the turn but he was better and still could not avoid, so circumstances; hurt -- expert had information to know better

- **kids**: semi-objective; expected to exercise care for a reasonable child of similar age/experience, unless engaged in "adult activity" (depends on state---cf. *Goss v. Allen*, where skiing was not adult activity because it's recreational, but driving is since it requires a license)

- **emergencies**: part of the 'circumstances' (cf. *Levey*; "honest exercise of judgment")

- **sex**: no; ordinary reasonable standard

Taking characteristics into account can help lower the standard by lowering expectations; this cuts both ways, and can also be used to heighten standard

- if not a category that allows special consideration, consider the circumstances/use a proxy (e.g. height, physical composition vs. sex, mental ability)

- In judging contributory negligence, DF's internal circumstances become part of PL's external circumstances (cf. *Clegg*)

**Res ipsa loquitor** -- when circumstantial evidence shows DF was negligent without needing proof of exactly how (e.g. car hitting sidewalk). Requirements:

1.  event causing injury must be one that ordinarily occurs because of DF negligence

2.  causes other than negligence are sufficiently eliminated

3.  (instrumentality in DF's control & no voluntary action/contribution by PL)

- forces DF to defend rather than motion for directed verdict/piggyback off PL's failure for direct evidence

BPL TEST -- Learned Hand's Economic Theory of Tort Law

*United States v. Carroll Towing Co.* (1947)

- [Facts]{.underline}: negligently secured barge breaks off and hits tanker; barge sinks with cargo

- [Holding]{.underline}: (Judge Hand) negligent for not having bargee on board during workday (low burden of increased precaution); reversed and remanded for damage allocation.

- [Principle]{.underline}: responsibility is a function of BPL:

  1)  **P: Probability the harm occurs**

  2)  **L: Amount of injury in case of harm**

  3)  **B: Burden of taking more precaution**

<!-- -->

- If B \> PL, society is better off lettering the accident happen

- Unclear whose harms count---DF/world/proximate cause & foreseeable harms?

Aims of tort law: deterrence/incentives, fairness in terms of foreseeability, distribute losses

Foreseeability vs. probability -- tort law can only make people liable for foreseeable things, but foreseeability needs to be cut off somewhere (e.g. loss of flour can't be attributed to possibly losing the war)

- Exam: note the scope of a BPL argument---everything can be construed as very big or very small; where do you cut it off Is it a planning tool or just an articulated judicial justification?

**Criticisms & counterarguments of BPL**

- Difficult to measure P and L; there are costs to calculating costs

  - Relative measure, only need for comparison to B

- Fairness vs. economic theory

  - Makes you weigh other peoples' benefits equally

- Comparability/commensurability -- e.g. losing a leg; requires DF to value cost of actions

  - Things must be valued anyways, no reason not to do it here

- Juries may ignore BPL

**Restatement 3^rd^ on Hand Formula**

- **Negligence Defined**: A **person acts negligently** if the person does not exercise **reasonable care under all the circumstances**. Primary factors to consider in ascertaining whether the person's conduct lacks reasonable care are the **foreseeable likelihood that the person's conduct will result in harm**, the **foreseeable severity of any harm that may ensue**, and the **burden of precautions to eliminate or reduce the risk of harm.**

- Correctly articulates the difference between probability and foreseeability (BPL is about foreseeable probability, not actual)

**STRICT LIABILITY**

Liability without finding fault (does not need to prove negligence or intent) -- usually for inherently dangerous activities (like explosives)

- Aiming to deter activity, not just taking precautions

- Judge chooses whether to apply strict liability or negligence standard

- The courts/juries decide negligence standard behaviour, but individuals decide their own activity levels under strict liability

- Strict liability doesn't judge blameworthiness -- maybe not fair to make someone pay unless they did something wrong

- **Often applied when DF is the cheapest cost avoider**

- Pros:

  - Reduce costs where cost of fact finding is high (e.g. destroyed evidence)

  - Less error than negligence evaluation because that requires more fact assessment

  - Incentivizes research into cost-effective safety measures

  - Internalizes externalities and regulates dangerous activity

  - Large institutions are better able to absorb cost of non-negligent accident

- Cons:

  - More claims when B \> PL

  - Consequences of mistake are worse; person would be sanctioned for efficient conduct

  - Could be cheaper to pay damages than change activity levels

  - Some activities have no alternative, or current activity level is already desirable

  - Tort law is more expensive than insurance market

*Rylands v. Fletcher* (Exchequer Chamber 1866, House of Lords 1868)

- [Facts]{.underline}: PL's property damaged by flooding from DF's reservoir

  - What duty does DF have? Absolute or reasonable care?

- [Holding]{.underline}: strict liability for escaping things for unnatural use unless it was the PL's fault

  - if natural/common, PL is in better position to protect self

*Indiana Harbour Belt Railroad Co. v. American Cyanamid Co.*

- [Facts]{.underline}: DF contracts rail car to ship chemical to NJ; switches through PL's rail line; chemicals leak

- [Holding]{.underline}: no strict liability

  - Strict liability would apply for explosions because evidence would be destroyed -- here it's just a leak

  - No alternative method to transport -- strict liability can't help regulate activity levels

  - Could avert risk by increased precautions/not being negligent -- no need for strict liability?

**Restatement 1^st^** "ultra-hazardous" instead of abnormally dangerous -- high risk of harm that cannot be eliminated with due care and not in common usage

**Restatement 2^nd^** factors to consider to decide whether something should use strict liability

- Can't avoid using due care

- Uncommon use

- Use not appropriate to place

**Restatement 3^rd^** two requirements to be *abnormally dangerous*

**SL v. N: Institutional Competence and Aims of Tort Law**

Individual Decisions and Role of Courts:

- SL ðŸ¡ª Liable for harm for certain types of activities despite level of care. Activity level itself is the liability, even though care level can still be affected. Individual figures out activity level & care level. Pay your way.

- Institutional competence question: Do actors make better determinations about who the CCA is or does jury/court? [Institutional argument for SL:]{.underline}

  - Individuals are better at determining CCA.

**DUTY**

To consider: did you create the risk? Are you increasing the relative risk? (more duty the more responsible you are for the risk); in general, there is no affirmative duty to rescue others from 3^rd^ party harms unrelated to the DF (nonfeasance)

*Moch Case* (NY 1928)

1)  [Facts]{.underline}: PLs warehouse caught fire, suing waterworks DF for failing to provide adequate water as per contract

2)  [Holding]{.underline}: no tort action available to users of city water supply

**Medical rescue**: if you start rescue, you have duty to rescue non-negligently (would deter other people from attempting rescue) (even if no one is there)

**Special relationship**: (e.g. parents) -- where you have an affirmative duty to step in

**Informed consent**: patient can sue if doctor was not negligent but physical harm occurred such that PL would not have consented to the procedure if he had been adequately informed

- **reasonable patient standard**: requires doctor to disclose what a reasonable person in the patient's situation would want to know (takes into account idiosyncrasies)

- **reasonable physician/standard practice**: treats informed consent like malpractice; PL must prove doctor failed to comply with standards of the position

**VICARIOUS LIABILITY**

**Respondeat superior**: employer is liable for certain torts committed by employees; SL tort because employer does not need to be negligent (just employee while in scope of employment); since employer is in better position to make decisions on activity level and etc.; saves info cost because no need to prove negligent employer

- Who is an employee?

  - Seeks to distinguish between situations in which strict liability will have a desirable effect and when it will not

  - Does not include independent contractors (e.g. homeowner hires an electrician)

- When is a tort committed within the scope of employment?

  - Limits the effects of strict liability on employers

  - Employers do not have infinite capacity to control the conduct of their employees

  - Distinguishes between a \"detour\" and a \"frolic\" -- NOT a bright-line rule

  - Employer liable for tortious acts committed on a \"mere\" detour but not on a frolic

- Respondeat superior re: Exxon -- court was split 4:4 on vicarious liability for company

  - BP may or may not be liable for actions of employees (although politically too risky to litigate)

**CAUSATION**

Causation can help promote optimal deterrence

- e.g. BP had no causation requirement; PROS: more deterrence for very reprehensible acts; but CONS: deterring useful activities for society, and over deterrence doesn't help anything

- you want to deter the actual risk you're trying to prevent, not freak accidents (cf. child dropping guns)

- harm has to be foreseeable for the deterrence to work---people aren't deterred from taking risks they don't know are risks

**SCOPE OF LIABILITY / PROXIMATE CAUSE**

**PROXIMATE CAUSE**

Even after it is determined that DF is under a duty to exercise reasonable care, and even after PL has proved the other elements of his case, liability isn't imposed unless DF's action also were the "proximate cause" (legal cause) of PL's injury. (Note: very mushy concept---concepts blend together and it's a fact-specific inquiry)

*Berry v. Borough of Sugar North*

I.  PL speeding in car; hit by falling tree ðŸ¡ª speeding was not a proximate cause because it was pure chance

**Traditional Tests**

- **Foreseeability:** DF's negligence is proximate cause of PL's harm if causing that harm was a foreseeable result of DF's conduct.

- **Indirect vs. Direct cause:** Is there an intervening/superseding cause?

**Unforeseeability** (note: these are blurry categories!)

Traditional case law distinguishes:

- Unforeseeable plaintiffs (e.g. *Palsgraf*) -- yes; bars recovery

- Unforeseeable extent of harm ('thin-skull' plaintiffs) -- no; does not bar recovery, you take your victim as they come

- Unforeseeable type of harm (usually considered case-by-case) -- yes, bars recovery (as per *Wagon Mound*, overruling *Polemis*)

- Unforeseeable manner of harm -- no; does not bar recovery

**Modern "scope of liability"** -- courts are somewhere in between unforeseeability and the HWR test ðŸ¡ª argue whichever to help your case; e.g. how you could win under both tests

**Harm within the (foreseeable increase in) risk test**: DF's negligence is not a proximate cause if the harm or risk of harm is not the same risk that made DF negligent (a further limitation on scope of liability)

- Was the DF negligent under the negligence standard?

- Was harm to the PL a foreseeable result?

- **Restatement 3^rd^ Â§ 29**: liability is limited to harms resulting from the risk that made the conduct tortious

- **Â§ 36**: If negligent conduct is **only a trivial contribution** to the cause of harm, it is not within the scope of liability (e.g. Harriet example--- Â§ 36 is designed to exclude Harriet)

- e.g. kid dropping gun is foreseeable but not the harm within the risk ðŸ¡ª no liability

*Polemis* (UK 1921)

- [Facts]{.underline}: Accident of negligently falling plank sparks and sets off explosion of ship

- [Holding]{.underline}: yes liability -- negligent tortfeasor will be held liable for unforeseeable damages/type of harm

*Wagon Mound* (UK 1961)

- [Facts]{.underline}: ship leaking oil into harbour; oil catches fire and dock burns down

- [Holding]{.underline}: no liability for unforeseeable consequences -- based on reasonable foreseeability

*Palsgraf v. LIRR* (Ct. App. NY 1928)

- [Facts]{.underline}: woman hit by scale after train conductor pushes man holding fireworks onto train

- [Holding]{.underline}: no liability

  - (Cardozo): Palsgraf is outside the "orbit of danger" / reasonably perceived risk; negligent act wasn't connected to Palsgraf -- nature of negligence should be relational between PL & DF (no 'liability in the air'); liability limit is at duty

  - (Andrews, dissent): negligent action creates responsibility for the harm created by it, even outside reasonably foreseeable danger zone; use intuitive cutoff of space and time (note: this is now the rule)

*Davis v. United States* (2012)

- [Facts]{.underline}: FBI protects gang members, who kill PL's family member; circuit ct. affirms cause-in-fact and says government is liable

- [Holding]{.underline}: foreseeable that the men would murder someone; no need to prove exactly these victims

- [Principles]{.underline}: could argue 'but for' causation; but we don't know counterfactual; FBI increased a foreseeable risk

**Calabresi's Cheapest Cost Avoider + Causation**

- Cause-in-fact: need for causation because there is no deterrence unless you stick the proper defendant with the cost

- Proximate cause: the party with info knows the risks/harms and knows the possible precautions, so cost should go to them

**Policy for/against proximate cause**

- **Pros:** Normatively don't want negligent persons to have to pay for distant harms, Deterrence is not served bc unforeseen harms can't be deterred, impossible to administer a system where any cause is allowed to become a source of liability (we need to break the chain at some point)

- **Cons:** Important aim of tort law is compensation (negligent + injury should lead to compensation), moral luck -- there is no difference between tortfeasors who do same thing but only one results in harm.

**REMEDIES FOR NUISANCE**

Types of Remedies:

- **Injunctions (Property Rule)** -- D must stop its activities and the nuisance.

- **Damages (Liability Rule)** -- D pays P for all past and future losses but could be permitted to continue the activity

- **Damage + Injunction** -- D may have to pay the plaintiff for damages for all past losses and also be enjoined from continuing the nuisance thereafter

Courts balance the equities at the liability stage to determine whether DF has engaged in nuisance. But also at the remedial stage in determining appropriate remedies:

- Whether to enjoin (plus past harm perhaps) the DF, OR

- Permit the activity and hold DF liable for damages for past and future harm

**Factors for deciding nuisance remedies**

- Parties' respective faults

- Hardship to the parties in respect to continuance or discontinuance of the nuisance

- Value of the activity that constitutes the nuisance to the surrounding community

  - Usually in tort law, injury has already happened & ceased, so remedy of liabilities is appropriate, but for nuisance, the injury is often continuing so injunction is also available

**REMEDIES**

**Types of remedies**

I.  **Damages** (law)

    a.  **Compensatory**

        i.  Loss to property, diminished profits, "out of pocket" losses

        ii. "general" damages for **pain and suffering** -- must prove by preponderance of evidence that there is suffering; not available for property damage

        iii. Policy: also allows PL extra money that aren't going to attorney's fees; uncertainty for DF

        iv. Courts can lower/raise award (remittitur etc.); some states have absolute limits

        v.  **Pure emotional loss** (no negligence-related physical injyr) -- limited;

    b.  **Punitive**

    c.  Nominal

II. **Injunction** (equity)

    a.  Preliminary & permanent

    b.  4-factor test for injunction:

        - Irreparable harm

        - Remedies at law (damages) are inadequate

        - Balance interests of PL and DF

        - Public interest

**Purpose of remedies**

1)  **Compensation**: achieving justice or providing civil recourse by compensating the PL for losses resulting from DF's tortious act

2)  **Deterrence**: promoting deterrence via threatening DFs with liability with the costs resulting from their tortious acts

3)  **Redistribution & Accountability**: recognizing the social and individual significance of PL's loss and confirming the weight of DF's responsibility for that loss, redistributing money to balance out the scales

4)  PLs receive **single recovery**: compensation for both past and future harm

    a.  Pros: case doesn't go on forever; no incentive to stay suffering for more recovery; DFs end liability and avoids multiple judicial proceedings

    b.  Cons: hard to estimate future costs; PL must save that money for long term; future value is discounted in award; no recovery for likely future harm except medical monitoring

**PURE ECONOMIC LOSS**

Harms not directly damage to property or personal injury -- common law says no recovery for pure economic or emotional loss (because liability is limited by no duty)

- PROS:

  - Prevents high opportunity for fraud; floodgates of litigation

  - Remedying things beyond actual harm is beyond what we want to put tort law energy into

  - Cuts off liability for the DF at a certain manageable point; DF doesn't face ridiculous damages

  - Deterrence still exists for injuries

  - Third party economic losses are widely distributed/spread out enough

- CONS:

  - Means tort law doesn't take into account third parties

  - cf. construction cases; harms like lost income cannot get recovery

  - deterrence is undervalued

- type of tort (negligence, gross negligence, recklessness)

- limited damages amount (e.g. limited fund class action)

- size of economic loss

II. Bankrupting DFs

**Policy considerations for punitive damages**

- Deterrence -- makes behaviour costly for DFs/forces changes

- Corrective justice -- corrects for victims who don't come forward

- Legitimacy and consistency -- PD can be unpredictable and due process is questionable; however, unpredictability also helps deterrence/avoids cost of business calculation

- Role of court -- accounting for non-parties, setting multipliers, etc.; what should be within courts' role?

- Efficiency -- don't want to overpunish/overdeter DF

- Fairness -- due process concerns, overcompensation to PL, overpunish in compensatory because juries account for it subconsciously; funding for state = incentive for more PD

- Pinto paradox -- DFs calculate value of life vs cost of increased precautions; fact-finders are biased against valuing life economically

- **Repeating punitive damages**

  - Overlapping punishment to DF

  - Incentive for other PLs to sue

    - Some states only allow large PD recovery for first PL -- encourages other PLs to come forward in exchange for certainty of compensatory damages, but they don't need PD incentive anymore

    - Over-deterrence is unproductive and unfair to DF

    - However, unfair that other PLs don't get the large award (favours PL with resources to sue first)

  - Depends on whether DF hurt someone personally or exposed society to poor behaviour

- Class action -- could be used instead of repeated punishment; establishes reprehensibility in one shot and has one payment split between PLs

  - Should punitive damages be allowed in class actions?

    - Yes: there are other PLs outside of class that were affected but aren't suing

    - No: PLs are all represented so compensatory is enough deterrence

  - Exxon -- got a mandatory PD class action (solution?)

    b.  Court goes with *Williams* test which says that statutory damages follows due process as long as not severe and oppressive to be obviously unreasonable (highly deferential)

**MASS TORTS & CLASS ACTIONS**

Advantages of class actions

- **PL**: small claims can be aggregated, pool (legal) resources, improve leverage for settlement, establish pattern

- **DF**: world peace, preclusion effects, save resources

- **Societal**: judicial efficiency, consistent results, substitute for PD, distributive benefits

**Rule 23(a):** 4 conditions that must be met for all class actions (in order to satisfy DP)

4)  **Numerosity**: joinder of members impractical

5)  **Commonality**: "questions of law or fact common to the class"

6)  **Typicality**: claims/defenses of representative are typical to the class

7)  **Adequacy**: representatives will fairly and adequately represent interests of members

**Rule 23(b)**: types of class actions -- pick 1

- **(a) Inconsistency**

**(b) "limited fund"** (e.g. if one PL would soak up available funds)

- **injunctive class**

- **predominance & superiority** (damages class) -- common question has to predominate and class action method has to be superior to alternative

special protections:

opt-out (chance to bring your own case)

mandatory notice (to people who may be part of the class)

*Walmart Stores v. Dukes*

- [Facts]{.underline}: Title VII action for discrimination against women across different individual stores; tries to certify 23(b)(2) "commonality" and 23(b)(2) injunctive class

- Scalia (majority) -- common question not good enough; there must be "same injury' & "common answer," from resolution of lawsuit; not just pleading standard

- Ginsburg (dissent) -- that makes 23(a)(2) into 23(b)(3)'s "predominance" requirement, which shouldn't need to apply to (b)(1)/(2) classes

- [Holding]{.underline}: class did not meet 23(a)(2) (common question of law or fact)

  - Claim for back pay improperly certified because monetary claims can't be certified under 23(b)(2); must be single remedy for each class member

*Parko v. Shell* (2014)

- [Facts]{.underline}: property owners vs. Shell & current owner ConocoPhillips, operating oil refinery near a village; claiming benzene leaks into groundwater; claiming lost property value

  - DFs argue some class members weren't injured so fail numerosity

- [Holding]{.underline}: too much work to prove injury before certifying; predominance requirement is quality \> quantity; not just pleading evidence

  - However, PLs fail predominance evidence because no evidence water was drunk etc.; damages could be done individually

  - Comparing *Metcoil* case: had same source of pollution in one storage container and there was proof it entered water supply; therefore meets predominance and commonality

- Principle: courts are making it harder to bring class actions

Rule 23(c)(4): "issue class" -- certifying only the issues that are the same; can de-class after determining common issue and decide others separately

Rule 23(c)(5): can divide class into subclasses

Rule 23(e): Settlement class -- certifying and settlement at the same time

- "contracting around the law"

- Pros: saves time and cost of litigations.

- Cons:

  - Absent PLs interest are less visible/represented (settlement binds them too)

  - Mismatched bargaining power may lead to unfair settlements

  - May reflect inaccurate facts if there was no adversarial process

  - Collusion between PL and DF attorneys to make money

- is it helping the people class actions were meant to help?

- Court needs to approve terms of the settlement; need to give notice out; hearing and fair & reasonable determination

- Note you still need to satisfy 23(a) and (b)

**Multi-district litigation** (MDL)

*temporary* transfer of single actions pending in different districts which involve one or more common questions of law or fact to a single district for consolidated trial under single judge.

- Differs from class actions: (1) No absent class members (unless one of the consolidated action is a class action), (2) Does not bind, and (3) Cannot opt-out

  - Does not provide a remedy

- When MDL becomes untenable/inefficient, sample PLs are selected for trial that represent range of harms

  - Most parties settle after consolidation ends; if no settlement, not precluded from litigation

- Pros: facilitates settlement; efficiency

- Cons: can't do the whole thing in an MDL; no global peace like class actions

- *BP* went from MDL to class action

**Gulf Coast Claims Facility (GCCF) & 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund (VCF) -- Pros & Cons**

- Questions to ask: *how they are created, who creates them and how are they managed?*

- **9/11 VCF**: Congress set it up as part of package through legislation (contracting out of tort law because it restricts litigation, but legislature *adds legitimacy*)

  - Allows PLs to recover for mass tort with no culpable DF

  - No cap on funds

  - Provides mechanism to appeal individual claims, due process values

  - Easier to administer: centralized admission, but difficult to decide who could receive damages on behalf of decedents

  - How much of tort principles do we want to use?

    - Pain and suffering: Did not resort to tort law & provided equal amount to everyone

    - Lost future earnings calculation -- used tort principles.

    - Think BP's no causation -- there they waived tort principles.

- **GCCF**: BP set it up themselves & appointed Feinburg (same person as 9/11), but failed due to legitimacy issues -- he was paid by BP.
