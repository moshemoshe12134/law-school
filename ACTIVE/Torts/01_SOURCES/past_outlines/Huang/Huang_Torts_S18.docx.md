Themes:

- Corrective Justice and Civil Recourse: Ex post framework

  - Corrective Justice- fixing the wrong to restore moral balance. Focus on the defendant's obligation to pay

    - Critique: immeasurable, and less applicable in our times when we have faceless corporations and liability insurance

  - Civil Recourse- focus on what Ï€Â is entitled to receive. providing victims for a recourse for civil wrongs. Recognition of the wrong, not even aspiring to be fully compensatory or fully correcting a wrong.

- Ex ante Framework

  - Optimal deterrence- prevent future tortious actions that *are excessively risky*

    - *Pro:* Economic solution- just enough deterrence to prevent risks not worth taking

    - *cons:* only takes economic values into account- what about human values- P &S

  - Loss distribution- inclination to develop rules of law that permit imposition of tort liability on business and institutions that can distribute their liabilities through the purchase of insurance or raising the cost of their products.

    - Con- does not satisfy moral blame, tort law is expensive mechanism for distributing losses

- Redress of social grievances (reason to sue even when other goals of tort law won't be satisfied)

  - Pros: Opportunity to put authority on trial

- Compensation- victims are provided compensation to serve the other goals of tort law, not its own law Holmes "loss from accident must lie where it falls"

  - Pros: sense of security

- Competing aims of tort law:

  - Deterrence (Defendant based) v compensation (plaintiff based)

  - Blame-worthiness (Defendant based) v fairness (plaintiff based)

  - Risk spreading (Defendant based) v social welfare (plaintiff based)

- Mixed system

- Recovery: only try to deter extremely risky behavior. don't want over deterrence, or for Ï€ to be overly litigious

- **Objective standard:** Generally the **reasonable person standard** is an **[external, objective]{.underline}** standard and is not based on the defendant's unique capacity of care.

- **Subjective standard:** would be if jury were asked if the **individual defendant** exercised reasonable care under their particular set of circumstances. Would create a different rule for every person.

  **Pro's of Objective standard**

- Cost and ease of adjudication -- no mind reading, easy fact finding

- Subjective standard encourages fraud and deception ðŸ¡ª incentive to understate strengths

- Encourages more care and caution -- might push defendants that are incapable of complying with objective standard to reduce activity levels.

- Fairness: treats everyone equally.

**Con's of Objective Standard:**

- Not impossible to inquire into 'state of mind' - Juries determine intent in criminal cases

- Only increases safety if the objective standard is understood by the 'below-average individual' ðŸ¡ª this is unlikely because they are below-average...

- Not fair, treating people equally in this case might remove people from activity that is needed or desirable. Society might prefer allowing some people to be able to engage in some activities, despite increased risk (disabled people should be able to drive)

**[Physical Disability]{.underline}: Ordinary care for a person with the same disability.**

If a person has a distinct physical defect that is recognizable to everyone around, others can take reasonable precautions based on his/her defect.

- Physical disability is visible, measurable, and verifiable.

- Jury may consider whether physical limitation should induce the person to take extra precautions or not engage in activity at all. (E.g. blind person shouldn't drive\...)

**[Mental disability:]{.underline} No special standard**

- Can't know if mental infirmity is constant or if the person was lucid when causing injury.

- Incentives to guardians to control mentally ill charge.

- They are often invisible and hard to measure.

**[Children]{.underline}**: **Ordinary care for a child of the same age, intelligence, and experience.**

- **Below 5: no negligence**

- **5-18: Semi-Objective Standard**

- Adults around a child are expected to take self-protective care around children.

- **Dangerous adult activity:** employ ordinary, adult standard

- Don't want kids doing adult activities, people won't expect kids to be doing adult activity, no social value, and could be necessarily dangerous.

- *Adams v. Bullock*: No contributory negligence because jury instructed that the "degree of care required by the child is the same\...as boys of that age, intelligence, and judgment would exercise"

- *IL*: No negligence for children under 7; dangerous activities = operating a vehicle, mini-bike, motorcycle, powerboat, airplane, or the like; not dangerous = driving a farm vehicle or riding a bicycle.

- *NY*: No negligence for children under the age of four

**[Superior Attributes]{.underline}/[Expertise]{.underline}: Ordinary care for a person with the same level of expertise.**

- Other people rely on person's expertise and act accordingly.

- They are more qualified to act at higher standard -- they know better.

- Could disincentivize people from gaining expertise or punishes people for having expertise.

- **Restatement, Section 289(b):** Defendant, in addition to exercising the attention, perception of the circumstances, memory, knowledge of other pertinent matters, intelligence and judgment as would a reasonable person, must also exercise ***such superior attributes*** as the actor himself has

**Emergency Doctrine**

In the law, when in an emergency, not of your own making, you only need to show **honest exercise of judgment** (aka you tried your best in an emergency). Lesser standard of care.

- Jury should imagine what a reasonable person would do only with the given amount of time to prepare (we should not imagine what a reasonable person would do in similar circumstance had they foreseen the circumstance and had adequate preparation time).

Questions to consider:

- Why carve out emergency doctrine at all? Isn't an emergency part of the circumstances?

- What constitutes an emergency? There is little uniformity in the law.

- Who is incentivized by this lower standard of care? There is no reason for incentives because emergencies are by definition unpredictable.

**MI Sudden Emergency Doctrine --** "If a driver acts according to his or her best judgment, or who, because of lack of time in which to form a judgment omits to act in the most judicious manner is not chargeable with negligence if the emergency was not brought about by the party's own negligence."

> Sudden emergency must have been unusual or totally unexpected in order for a jury to receive the Sudden Emergency Instruction.

[**Defense of limited statutory purpose** (statute is irrelevant)]{.underline}:

- Plaintiff was not **class of persons** or did **not suffer type of harm** intended to be protected by statute.

- This defense rarely prevails in practice because purpose if not usually clear.

- For this defense to work, the plaintiff could not by **any stretch of the imagination** have been an intended beneficiary of the statute's protection. (Exception case: [DiCaprio v. NY Central RR]{.underline})

- Modern courts likely to hold that at least a "secondary purpose" of statute was to protect individuals in the plaintiff's position:

- \(1\) If there is a legislative history or preamble referencing all of those in the plaintiff's situation, then courts are likely to find the plaintiff to be an intended beneficiary

- \(2\) If violation of the statute does not increase the risk of harm to those in the plaintiff's situation, the courts are unlikely to hold the plaintiff to be an intended beneficiary

  **[Courts read statute's protection broadly to include indirect injuries]{.underline}**

- Courts are likely to read statute's protection broadly to include **indirect injuries**

- E.g. Statute prohibits sale of alcohol to drunks; a shop violates the statute, and a drunk person is served and gets into a car accident, injuring a third-party; the shop is liable.
