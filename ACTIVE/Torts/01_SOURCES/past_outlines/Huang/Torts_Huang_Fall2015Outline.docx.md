1.  **Social Welfare** -- Tort law recognizes that sometimes society needs activities that have some risk of causing harm. We don't want to impose liability for things that cannot be foreseen or when precaution costs would be too high to stop the activity altogether.

2.  **Moral Luck**

3.  **Corrective Justice & Civil Recourse (Fairness):** Popular moral blame

    a.  Corrective Justice: restoring the **moral balance** between wrong-doer and victim

        i.  Good for individuals against intentional wrong-doer

    b.  Civil Recourse: Tort law is a way of providing victims with **recourse** for civil wrongs.

        i.  Vindicates victims' need for recognition they have been wronged

    c.  **Critique**: Less applicable in modern day where DF is often a faceless corporation or individuals covered by liability insurance.

1.  **Optimal Deterrence:**

    a.  Deter excessively risky activity so that only those losses worth avoiding are avoided. Liability is based on HARM caused (compensatory damages)

    b.  Sometimes used for Punitive Damages when wrong is not caught frequently, or for intangible harms

    c.  **Pros**: Produces the most economic solution -- just enough deterrence to prevent risks not worth taking, but not too much to require foregoing some of the benefit of that activity.

    d.  **Cons**: Only takes into consideration economic values and ignores other important human values; pain & suffering from accident shouldn't be reduced to economic value of what it takes to prevent it.

    e.  Complete OR Absolute Deterrence ðŸ¡ª Based on the gains to defendant. Take away ALL profits of bad activity ðŸ¡ª shut down the activity altogether, make it so there is NO gain from activity, so no incentive to do the activity ðŸ¡ª a lot of juries think this way (juries often seem to want to take away profits) ðŸ¡ª Completely independent from harm. ðŸ¡ª maybe this makes sense for punitive damages b/c usually it is DF focused idea, not PL focused

2.  **Absolute** **Deterrence (Mostly used for Punitive Damages)**

    a.  Based on the gains to defendant. Take away ALL profits of bad activity ðŸ¡ª no gain from activity, so no incentives to engage in the activity.

    b.  Some activities are so bad we should shut it down.

    c.  A lot of juries think this way for punishment when negligence is egregious.

3.  **Loss Distribution (Spreading Risk):**

    a.  Promotes the broad distribution of losses due to accident/injury. Who should bear costs? Who is best person to spread the cost through society?

    b.  **Pros**: Among all other things equal, having a large number of people bear a small loss is better than having single person bear all of it.

    c.  **Cons**: (1) Does not satisfy sense of moral blame (2) Tort law system is an expensive mechanism for distributing losses (insurance, social security are cheaper ways).

4.  **Compensation**:

    a.  Promote compensation to those who have suffered injury. Return PL to original state.

    b.  **Pros**: Makes people feel more secure that they will be compensated**.**

    c.  **Cons**: We need more than injury to justify liability, so there needs to be more than just the goal of compensation.

[]{#_Toc311118623 .anchor}Economic Theories of Torts

**CALEBRESI AIMS OF TORT LAW (Cheapest Cost Avoider)**

1.  Goal of tort law is to find the **[cheapest cost avoider]{.underline}**, make him pay the costs.

    i.  Aim is optimal deterrence ðŸ¡ª minimize COST TO SOCIETY

    ii. [Injury cost]{.underline} (cost to victim) + [precaution cost]{.underline} (cost of activity)

    <!-- -->

    a.  Try to internalize ALL the externalities

2.  Who is Cheapest Cost Avoider?

    a.  **CCA v. 1** -- Stick liability on the person who has the best knowledge of:

        i.  Knows the risks/harm (who will be hurt, what extent)

        ii. Knows precautions possible

        iii. Knows the tort law ðŸ¡ª needs to know if they would be liable

    b.  Problems of CCA v. 1: Morality, blameworthiness, social norms, distribution, risk spreading are not considered by CCA (school shouldn't pay for factory's pollution, almost seems like you are sticking liability on the more responsible party) AND no stability b/c who gets stuck w/ liability depends completely on volatile factors like technology

    c.  **CCA v. 2** -- Who is the cheapest transactor? "Best briber"?

        i.  Best briber is one who faces the lowest transaction costs, based on Coase Theorem. Factors:

            1.  Who knows the risks?

            2.  Who knows how to reduce the risks?

            3.  Who knows whether they will be liable?

3.  ["But for" causation]{.underline} sweeps all candidates in, [proximate cause]{.underline} is the tool to implement the idea of CCA, assigns liability to the CCA

    a.  BP Case (no causation) -- we want causation in fact for optimal deterrence b/c causation makes sure that precaution taken would have made a difference, don't want ppl just spending precaution money that makes no difference in other ppl's harms (waste) ðŸ¡ª many tortfeasors have limited resources, so only spend \$ on things that make a difference to people

    b.  Proximate Cause/HWR -- we have tort law for certain types of harm, that is what we assign liability for, we can't prevent freak accidents, harm has to be foreseeable, people can't take precautions they don't know about

4.  **Calebresi** *[Compensation Goals]{.underline}* (backward looking -- ex post)

    a.  Spreading of losses and costs via insurance and mutual insurance to promote societal distribution of losses

    b.  Wealth distribution -- deep pockets should pay:

        i.  Better able to bear the costs (form of thinking about insurance)

        ii. Diminishing marginal utility of money ðŸ¡ª better to tax rich

        iii. More \$ means more power to control things = more responsibility

    c.  We are still uncomfortable with a disconnect with the physical connection; **causation doesn't matter** when we are just concerned with distribution or deep pockets (same as taxes)

    d.  **Fairness/Deterrence:** Person should only pay if at fault. Need to deter people form dangerous conduct, rich ppl can't just pay.

5.  **Calebresi** *[Deterrence Goals]{.underline}* (forward looking -- ex ante) (negligence is the person that is CCA, not the bad, fault creator)

    a.  Efficient deterrence: minimize the sum of injury costs and safety (precaution) costs as well

    b.  **Specific deterrence** (regulation): regulations to control behavior (bans)

        i.  We do not need to know the exact causation

        ii. Not as economically efficient compared to general because it does not account for different situations such as changing technology and circumstances

    c.  **General deterrence** (price/tax): increases the price of certain activities such as carbon tax

        i.  **Works through causation**: A link between behavior and the harm -- we can generate the proper price and tax to achieve the right amount of deterrence.

        ii. **Forseeability matters more for this** because we can measure the right price that leads to the proper amount of deterrence (and not lead to over-deterrence or under-deterrence)

            1.  You need to foresee the amount of harm in order to impose a proper amount of price or taxation to deter that behavior in an efficient manner

            2.  Internalize the costs

        iii. Price mechanism makes it more efficient to adopt cheapest choice

             1.  People can adopt a more efficient (cheaper) choice when there's no regulation

    d.  Controls both [*care levels* and *activity levels*]{.underline} ðŸ¡ª if the harm costs \$100 and the business is \$90, then it will shut down. *Care level* affects both the harm causer and the potential victim; *activity level* only controls the harm causer's behavior

**COASE THEORY -- FUNCTION OF LAW**

3.  [*Coase Theorem*:]{.underline} when there is no transactions costs, regardless of where liability is placed, the parties will bargain to get the cheapest, most optimal solution (school w/ giant fan and factory w/ scrubbers)

    a.  Questions the **purpose of the law?** Do we need law at all if society will achieve most cost efficient result?

4.  [Critiques and Limitations of Coase Theorem]{.underline}

    a.  Assumes there is no transaction costs ðŸ¡ª unrealistic and sometimes bargaining is impossible (e.g. too many parties, unknown parties)

        i.  Coase works when there is good bargaining ability:

            1.  Sophisticated parties who know how to negotiate

            2.  Parties have pre-existing relationship

            3.  Fewer parties

            4.  Harms are easily monetized

        ii. Role of Law: if TC costs are high, law is important.

    b.  Ignores distributional consequences: fairness, fault, etc.

        i.  Coase works when bargaining power is equal:

            1.  2 big businesses

        ii. Role of Law: Where we stick liability, that party must bribe or pay, so law attempts to place liability on CCA (either party that knows more, or best briber) ðŸ¡ª creates incentive for person stuck with liability to actually find the CCA.

[Brown v. Kendall]{.underline} (MA, 1851) Defendant dog owner tries to separate dogs with a stick. Unintentionally hits plaintiff in the eye. Case brought an action of **trespass**, trial court required extraordinary care from defendant to escape liability.

VI. []{#_Toc311118631 .anchor}Standards of Care

**Ordinary Care**

[Bethel v. NYC Transit]{.underline} (1998) (Wheelchair seat collapses in bus. PL sues transit authority for negligence in keeping chair in repair because they are held to standard of utmost, extraordinary care as a 'common carrier.' DF asks for standard to change back to ordinary care.)

Court allowed the change from extraordinary care to ordinary care for common carriers.

- Single ordinary care standard is flexible enough for juries to take into account the ultrahazardous nature of tortfeasor's activity.

  - Con: Jury is variable. People might not think bus/common carrier should be expected to take more care. We would not have as high level of safety as before.

- Times have changed, other regulatory standards, we don't need torts to make buses safe.

- We want to incentivize bus companies to keep running, social good. But maybe less people will ride buses with lower care b/c they fear for their own safety.

**Extraordinary Care**

[Wood v. Groh:]{.underline} (plaintiff shot accidentally by defendant\'s son with a gun that he had taken out of the locked case with a screwdriver and ammunition was kept in same cabinet.)

- The appeals court found defendant liable because he had taken ordinary care, but that there should be \"the highest degree of care in safekeeping the handgun.\"

- Gun is on object of **dangerous instrumentality,** requires more than reasonable care.

- Ordinary care is not enough to deter the bad behavior; trial court found that dad was reasonable. And sends a message to other gun owners to be extra careful. And don't want to incentivize gun ownership and cost of more precautions is not prohibitive.

- Con: Shouldn't move standard to extraordinary care because gun owners shouldn't be liable for non-predictive circumstances, making them pay more is unfair and imposes too high a cost for safety.

**Emergency Doctrine**

In the law, when in an emergency, not of your own making, you only need to show **honest exercise of judgment** (aka you tried your best in an emergency). Lesser standard of care.

- Jury should imagine what a reasonable person would do only with the given amount of time to prepare (we should not imagine what a reasonable person would do in similar circumstance had they foreseen the circumstance and had adequate preparation time).

Questions to consider:

- Why carve out emergency doctrine at all? Isn't an emergency part of the circumstances?

- What constitutes an emergency? There is little uniformity in the law.

- Who is incentivized by this lower standard of care? There is no reason for incentives because emergencies are by definition unpredictable.

**Custom**

Custom is not dispositive in proving negligence or proving reasonable care, it is **only persuasive**.

- In order for a practice to be custom, it does not have to be universal, but most people.

- Consider WHY a custom exists. Is it to save money? Or is it to improve safety?

The current rule of custom is a strong rejection of 2 other possible interpretations:

- Safe-harbor Rule: compliance w/ custom = no liability

- Irrelevance Rule: compliance w/ custom is not relevant and should not be admissible

Benefits of Current Custom Rule

- Custom often **correlates** to what ought to be done

- If actor is complaint w/ custom, **cautions the jury** that finding the actor negligent implies that the entire industry or grouping of peoples is also behaving unreasonably.

- If actor is non-compliant to custom, **makes jury** **feel better** about finding them negligent

- Coordinating function -- potential victims can take **self-protective care** based on custom

- Some departures of custom may be **equally safe or more safe** than custom dictates

- Promotes reliable and accurate decisions - custom is an important way to **inform juries** on how reasonable actors should act, especially if jury is unfamiliar w/ circumstances

[T.J. Hooper]{.underline} (1932) (Tug towed 3 barges but they sank in a storm. Tugboats did not have receiving sets to get weather reports, otherwise could have avoided storm. Not customary for industry to provide them.)

- No statute. Custom to have radio (90% of boats have them), but NOT custom for company to provide radios. ðŸ¡ª **No custom to have radios**, because liability of owner.

- Tug owners are still negligent because compliance to custom is not reasonable care.

- Custom, in this case, is lagging behind ordinary care for safety.

- Courts must push safety customs for industry because industry has incentive to keep costs down and to choose not to implement more safety standards.

**Medical Malpractice**

Compliance w/ medical custom provides **safe-harbor** for defendant from liability.

Failure to comply w/ professional custom is malpractice (negligence per se).

- **Alternative schools of thought**: No negligence per se if practice complies w/ a school of thought or practice followed by a respectable minority of practitioners.

- Special standard is based on **necessity** -- juries can't be expected to be knowledgeable enough to make medical or other profession judgments.

- If anything, this special standard should stretch to cover all non-professional, but highly technical/complex problems, but this has not happened yet.

Strict Locality Rule: Old requirement that expert witnesses, usually for plaintiff, had to come from the defendant\'s own community and have same/similar practice or qualifications.

- Needed because community medical standards varied widely.

- But \"conspiracy of silence\" - professionals would not testify against each other

With the increased standardization of medicine, many courts have **rejected strict locality rule**.

- National standards now govern and any expert familiar with standards can testify.

- Caused explosion of medical malpractice suits, some doctors only work as expert witnesses.

- In response, some courts only allow practicing doctors to testify, or require higher qualifications.

[Sheeley v. Memorial Hospital]{.underline} (1998) (Patient filed suit for medical malpractice during child birth. Lower court excluded expert testimony because witness was over-qualified and had not practiced medicine in a while.)

- Rejects strict locality rule -- doesn't matter if expert is more qualified than defendant physician or had not practiced in recent years.

- Issue is a medical procedure that had not changed in 30 years.

- Jury gets to decide what is national standard and which expert to believe now.

IV. []{#_Toc311118649 .anchor}Question of Proof -- Alternative Liability

+-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+
| Problem of **indeterminate** **causes**:                                                                                                                                                                                |

|                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |

|                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| - Another factor in invoking alternative liability is the connection between or among defendants.                                                                                                                       |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
|   - Pure strangers are less likely to be subject to alternative liability than defendants that are acting in the same \'arena\'                                                                                         |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| Policy Reasons:                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| - Defendants are more likely to be able to figure out who did what. So impose liability and let them bring proof and sort damages out.                                                                                  |

[Summers v. Tice]{.underline} (1948) (2 hunters shoot towards same quail, plaintiff gets shot in the eye, but can't figure out which hunter actually hit the plaintiff)

- Distinct from MSC b/c only ONE cause exists, just can't figure out which defendant.

- There is 50/50 chance that one defendant is guilty of injury, no preponderance

- Not "joint" b/c 2 different shot guns, they didn't help each other w/ their shots

- Unfortunately, limited group is important, if there are more defendants, less likely to impose liability, even if plaintiff is even less likely to be able to prove anything

V.  []{#_Toc311118650 .anchor}Joint and Several Liability

+--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+
| When two or more defendants are liable to plaintiff for the **same** harm, damages are apportioned using \"joint and several liability\"                                                                                                                           |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - Plaintiff can sue any of the defendants can recover 100% of damages from any defendant that has liability, but PL can **never** recover more than 100% of the amount of damages                                                                                  |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| <!-- -->                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - Imposing joint and several liability places the risk that any defendant\'s insolvency on the other defendant(s), instead of the plaintiff - this is more fair then forcing plaintiff to bear the cost, if one of the defendants is unable to pay for some reason |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - "Several" liability ðŸ¡ª Plaintiff must sue each defendant separately to get full amount of damages, can be based on comparative negligence percentages                                                                                                             |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
|   - DF only responsible for own share of fault                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |

|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| Olde system of Joint and Several Liability (no contribution)                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - If plaintiff sues one defendant for 100%, then others pay nothing                                                                                                                                                                                                |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - If plaintiff sues one defendant and recovers 70% of costs, can sue other DF for 30%                                                                                                                                                                              |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| This unfair result in common law has led to the development of *contribution*                                                                                                                                                                                      |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - A right of **contribution** is the right to be reimbursed by another defendant for a share of the amount paid to plaintiff - traditionally this has been straightforward fractions (2 defendants, both pay 50%; 3 defendants, each pay 33.3%; etc)               |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| - With **comparative** **negligence**, many states now assess contribution shares of co-defendants in proportion to the amount of negligence attributable to each defendant, rather than straightforward fractions.                                                |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| Joint and Several liability is imposed in 3 main situations:                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| 1.  Defendants are **joint tortfeasors** - both defendants are involved in the same joint enterprise                                                                                                                                                               |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| 2.  Independent tortfeasors of a single **indivisible harm** - defendants are both necessary (but-for) causes for plaintiff\'s harm, but no way to pinpoint defendant\'s causal responsibility                                                                     |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| 3.  Independent tortfeasors for a single, theoretically divisible, but **practically indivisible harm** -- BOP is on defendants to show how much damage they each caused, otherwise J&S                                                                            |

VI. []{#_Toc311118651 .anchor}Market Share Liability

+----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+
| Problem of **indeterminate** **causes**:                                                                                                                                                                             |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Defendant has clearly injured some but not all plaintiffs, and plaintiffs cannot prove which of them the defendant injured                                                                                         |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| In some situations (drug defect), court may impose liability based on defendant's share of the market for the product that caused harm, especially when there are lots of DFs and PLs.                               |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Idea is that once it is clear that defendant's product injured some plaintiffs, they shouldn't escape liability just b/c plaintiff's can't show which defendants harmed which plaintiffs                           |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Achieves optimal deterrence b/c defendant must pay for exact amount of harm caused based on market share                                                                                                           |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| Problems/Limitations                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Not used often b/c there are few signature diseases                                                                                                                                                                |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Limitations of market-share informationðŸ¡ªhard to determine proper share at time of harm                                                                                                                             |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Same product by manufacturers might not be 100% identical or fungible                                                                                                                                              |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Over-imposition of liability ðŸ¡ª some manufacturers are out of business, so others must overcompensate if using relative market share or there is under-compensation for plaintiff is imposing absolute market share |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| - Does not serve corrective justice                                                                                                                                                                                  |
+======================================================================================================================================================================================================================+

[Sindell v. Abbott Labortories]{.underline} (plaintiffs (women whose mothers had taken DES drug while pregnant and got a signature disease) sued DES drug manufacturers. But no individual plaintiff could identify which manufacturer had made the DES that her mother had taken.)

- Court circumvented this by holding that the plaintiffs need not prove individual causation. Instead, each plaintiff recovered from each defendant a portion of her damages equal to the **defendant\'s share of the market** for the DES.

- DES is chemically identical, so market share is accurate surrogate for the amount of harm each defendant caused. So imposing liability in proportion to market share forces each defendant to pay compensation for amount of injury caused. Optimal deterrence is achieved.

[Hymowitz v. Eli Lily & Co.]{.underline} (1989) (PL sued makers of DES)

- **Proof:** Market share could reflect how much drugs you put out and how probably one is for causing the disease.

- Requirements for Market Share

  - Identical product

  - Signature disease

- Which market? ðŸ¡ª court uses national market, narrower markets would be good, but national data is better and could bar recovery for PLs

- What if a particular PL can prove a specific DF's liability? ðŸ¡ª court allows PL to use normal product liability to sue them

  - BUT DF's can't be freed from liability based on particular injury caused to particular PL ðŸ¡ª market share is about how much RISK DF put out into market relative to other DFs

  - Contradictory b/c DF can't defend based on particular proof that their pill was distinguishable from other pills

- Possible defense: manufacturer did not target pregnant women

XVIII. []{#_Toc311118653 .anchor}Scope of Liability -- Modern View

+--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------+
| Once damages, breach of duty, and cause in fact have been established, liability is not imposed until \'proximate cause\' is also established.                                                                                                                                                                                 |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - Doctrine of proximate cause limits the scope of the defendant\'s liability and is required both for negligence and strict liability cases.                                                                                                                                                                                   |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - No single formulation of rules governing scope of liability. Often used as a \'fudge-factor\' to permit judges and juries to decide whether liability should be imposed.                                                                                                                                                     |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Modern View** -- **Harm w/in the Risk**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **R3T 29** -- liability is limited to harms that result from the risks that made the actor's conduct tortious                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - Jury must go back to reasons they decided that an action was negligent ðŸ¡ª Does the risk the occurred one of the risks they used to determine if action was negligent?                                                                                                                                                         |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - Asks juries to consider reasons for creating tort liability in the first place, look at competing aims of tort law.                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - Permissive standard -- even if harm is not *principle* risk of statute, you can still be HwR if it was a foreseeable, secondary, and relevant risk.                                                                                                                                                                          |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - Very similar to Foreseeability, which is used in negligence calculation.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **R3T 36 Trivial Contributions to Multiple Sufficient Causes**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| - When an actor's negligent conduct constitutes only a trivial contribution to a causal set that is a factual cause of harm under **R3T 27**, the **harm is not within the scope of the actor's liability.**                                                                                                                   |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| <!-- -->                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |

| **Intervening/Superceding Cause - Jury Instruction**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| When an intervening cause comes between the PL's harm and DF's negligence, than the DF is not a contributing factor and not liable.                                                                                                                                                                                            |
|                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |

[Berry v. Borough of Sugar Notch]{.underline} (trolley was speeding, gets hit by a tree.)

- No liability b/c a harm within the risk of speeding is not getting hit by a falling tree.

[Gorris v. Scott]{.underline} **(**Sheep washed overboard b/c not kept in a pen on the ship.)

- Statutory violation b/c require sheep be kept in the pen, but purpose of statute is to protect sheep from contagious disease, not being washed overboard. So no HwR.

[Example of Secondary Risk]{.underline}: Defendant violates statute, requiring railroads to fence their rights of way. A 2-year old child gets injured from wandering onto the tracks. Main purpose is injury to livestock. Nonetheless, courts hold a secondary purpose includes risk to people, jury can decide.

[Wagner v. International Railway Co]{.underline}*.* (defendant was negligent in failing to close the door to one of its rail cars, plaintiff\'s cousin is thrown out of car. Plaintiff was injured while attempting to rescue cousin. Principle risk was falling out. Also proximate cause to plaintiff/rescuer's harm?

- Cardozo said it could be proximate cause. Says, \"Danger invites rescue.\"

- It is foreseeable that others will rescue someone whose safety the DF's actions put at risk.

- So harm to the rescuer can be within the cluster of risks that make DF's actions negligent.

- Jury must decide proximate cause.

[Marshall v. Nugent]{.underline} (defendant\'s driving forced car w/ plaintiff to run off the road. Plaintiff was unharmed when run off road, but suffered injury after he walked down the road and was struck by 3rd vehicle as he was attempting to warn oncoming traffic of the danger ahead.)

- Despite unusual manner of injury, did not preclude liability on part of defendant.

- As long as plaintiff was injured roughly the same time and in roughly same place as was foreseeable, through same general forces as were foreseeable, it is not necessary that precise manner in which injury took pace has to be foreseeable.

[Smith v. Leech Brain and Co.]{.underline} (1962) (DF's negligence in providing inadequate shielding caused a worker to burn his lip by a piece of molten metal. The burn was treated but developed into cancer that killed plaintiff later. Worker was pre-disposed to cancer by 10 years of work in gas)

- DF must take his victim as he finds him. Not different **type** of harm ðŸ¡ª burn v. cancer.

- The injury was the same (burn) but **extent** of harm was cancer. So DF is liable.

[Palsgraf v. Long Island Railway]{.underline} (Railroad employees help passenger onto train, dislodged package of fireworks passenger was carrying. Package falls to tracks and explodes, causes penny scale to tip over and injure Mrs. Palsgraf)

- **Cardozo**: No liability, should not go to jury. No N unless there is duty owed to PL.

- Reasonable person could not foresee plaintiff, duty only to passenger and package.

- There is no duty of care to anyone outside of zone of danger and boundaries determined by foreseeability of negligence.

- NO negligence in the air. PL and DF must be connected, negligence arises from relationship, not from act of negligence.

- **Andrews** (dissent): when act is negligent, then DF should be liable for damage that results to world. Only cut off proximate cause by jury determination based on time/space, stream, directness, intervening/superseding, and common sense.

[Davis v. US (1st Circuit 2012)]{.underline} (Plaintiffs are suing for death of 2 women caused by 2 members (Flemmi and Bulger) of a Boston gang. Suing FBI for protecting gang members due to their status as informants, even after the FBI was aware of their violent history & dangerousness)

- **But-for**: Had FBI not tipped Flemmi off so he could flee charged for bombing, he would be in jail and not have murdered women.

  - Proof: Flemmi's co-defendant was jailed for bombing, so would have been jailed. And many instances in which he would have been jailed w/out FBI tip-off

- **Proximate Cause**: FBI could foresee that men were violent and just b/c women were not mob targets is not enough to make unforeseeable. The women had relationship w/ men.

  - **Counter**: convicted murders, but only before killed biz associates, so unforeseeable plaintiffs! And intervening cause was sour personal relationship.

- Also FBI was **reckless** ðŸ¡ª court allows jury to use recklessness to help determine if FBI's conduct bears sufficient causal relationship to the murders. Given FBI was reckless, even though victim was unspecified, the FBI made outcome eminently predictable

**Policy for/against proximate cause**

- Pros: Normatively don't want negligent persons to have to pay for distant harms, Deterrence is not served b/c unforeseen harms can't be deterred, impossible to administer a system where any cause is allowed to become a source of liability (we need to break the chain at some point)

- Cons: Important aim of tort law is compensation (negligent + injury should lead to compensation), moral luck -- there is no difference between tortfeasors who do same thing but only one results in harm. Is it just the same as negligence?

**Cheapest Cost Avoider (v.1)**

- But-for: We want to make sure precautions would make a difference in avoiding the injury ðŸ¡ª if precaution doesn\'t actually prevent injury, no need to incentivize it

- Proximate Cause (HwR): party that foresees harms should be liable, but even if not foreseeable, there are some cases where liability will incentivize better future choices. And extent of harm is irrelevant to CCA calculation, so makes sense it doesn't matter.

II. []{#_Toc311118658 .anchor}Strict Liability v. Negligence

If you can take (more) care to eliminate the risk you are in the world of negligence.

If you cannot eliminate the risk with care, then you are in the world of strict liability.

- **Strict liability can modulate both care and activity levels**

  - SL can obviously modulate activity level

    - Might be too expensive to continue activity

  - But can also modulate care level

    - If it is worthwhile activity, an actor would choose to be more careful to avoid total harm that would cost them each time harm occurred.

    - Actors *internalization the risks* under SL to change care or activity levels.

- **Negligence can modulate care and activity levels too (but less effective)**

  - If reasonable person standard of care is high, then PL may lower activity level

  - Court can technically declare an entire activity negligent, but this is rare

  - Negligence might also prevent deterrence b/c if you take enough care, you have a safe harbor from liability and so no incentive to regulate activity levels regardless of number of injuries or dangerousness of activities

  - Negligence also places costs on victims (SL for plaintiffs)

- **Strict Liability places decisions of activity and care on individual (better at CCA)**

- **Negligence ðŸ¡ª court/jury determines if care level is sufficient (court decides CCA)**

  - Courts rely on customary evidence for N analysis, but custom may be wrong

**If SL works so well, why do we need N? Is SL better for finding the cheapest cost avoider?**

1.  Plaintiff's role? SL creates free harbor for victims so they don't need to take care

2.  Fairness ðŸ¡ª better to leave accident on victim, unless actor is blameworthy?

3.  Tradition ðŸ¡ª negligence is default, why change?

[Ploof v. Putnam]{.underline} (1908) (plaintiffs sailed in lake and sudden storm forced them to tie up in defendant\'s dock. Defendant untied ship from his dock and the ship was lost and the plaintiff\'s were injured.)

- DF argued he was protecting private property.

- Court held that plaintiffs had **privilege of necessity** that entitled them to use dock, so there was no trespass and the defendant had **no right to exclude** them from dock.

- Untying the ship can even be trespass on ship. So court awarded damages to PL.

- Private necessity afforded plaintiff limited property interest in the dock, which the defendant interfered with, so defendant is liable for harm ensued from untying.

[Vincent v. Lake Erie Transportation]{.underline} (1910) (defendant\'s ship was unloading at plaintiff\'s dock when storm arose so had to stay docked. Plaintiff sued for damage that resulted from ship hitting the dock as it was tied up.)

- Defendant said it would be negligent to untie ship, so not liable for damage to dock.

- But court held that **defendant\'s privilege was conditional only** and though they had done no wrong, it had saved ship at expense of dock, so are liable for the damages.

- Because ship would w/out dock have been lost, they only were saved from using dock and preserved their property at dock's expense, so only **fair** that ship owner is responsible for damage.

- Conditional privilege means the dock owner is **less likely to exclude** from property.

- **Dissent: (1)** Argues that the damage to the dock was the result of an inevitable accident. There is no fault on the shipmaster, so cost of damage should like where it falls. **(2)** Assumed the risk by being a dock owner.

**[Reasonable Versus Unreasonable]{.underline}:**

- Looks like **SL for the trespasser**, but **negligence regime** for owner.

- Private necessity ONLY applies if the threatened harm to ship is greater than the harm to the dock. If harm is not greater, you are just a trespasser, no private necessity.

  - This matters b/c if you are just trespassing, the owner can exclude you.

[Boomer v. Atlantic]{.underline} (1970)(DF operates cement plant. Neighboring land owners allege injury to property from dirt, smoke, and vibration. PLs seek both damages and injunctive relief)

- Default NY law: if there is nuisance, court imposes both damages and injunction.

- Court declines injunction (changes NY law) because

  - Cost-benefit analysis -- cost of injunction is much higher than harm caused

  - Cement plant is important to economy of area

  - Saving nuisance law! Must pay for future damages resulting from nuisance.

- Court does not enjoin because cement plant is important to economy of area AND also the cost of discontinuing the nuisance was grossly disproportionate to the amount of harm the nuisance was causing.

- **Dissent**: thinks that cement company should not be able to pollute indefinitely upon paying permanent damages. Court is essentially licensing the pollution. Burden should be on DF to find a way to fix air pollution problem.

**Mistakes & Institutional Competence**

- If court gets **damages too high (liability rules)**, then it functions as an injunction.

  - Parties can still bargain.

- If court gets **damages too low (liability rules)**, then it functions like no liability, but parties can still bargain & work it out

  - Here, if bargaining won't work (conditions are bad for bargaining), court should be extra concerned about getting damages right.

- If court gets **injunction wrong,** parties can still bargain it out, but since PL & DF can demand a lot of money, may lead to strategic holdouts, **extortion**, etc.

- Even if court makes a mistake, doesn't foreclose bargaining for more efficient outcome (Coase), assuming limitations of Coase theorem

[Spurs v. Del Webb:]{.underline} PL is property developer, found to have come to the nuisance b/c he moves to place where there is a feed-lot & sues for nuisance due to flies and odor. Court rules that PL may enforce his property right (get injunction), but must pay DF for the cost of moving the feed lot.

- Justification: PL could have foreseen this nuisance & took advantage of cheaper land values of rural area to build, so should pay for those forced to leave.

- Holding is limited to where the developer foreseeably came to land and made it necessary to grant an injunction against a lawful business, and that business has no adequate relief.

[Exxon Shipping v. Grant Baker]{.underline} (2008) (Supertanker recklessly grounded on reef in Alaska and spilled lots of oil. Case consolidates all classes of plaintiffs for one big PD decision.)

- Compensatory damages were about \$500 million, Alaska District Court set PD at \$5 billion. Court of Appeals remanded twice saying PD was too high, eventually it cut damages to \$2.5 billion.

- Generally there is DP limitation to control PD ðŸ¡ª single-digit ratio is best and when CD is large, better to have smaller ratio.

- Key goal is to set federal common law (maritime) penalties to be reasonably predictable in severity and increase consistency.

- Rejects verbal approach and hard dollar cap.

- Yay ratio approach! More predictable b/c pegged to CD.

- State usually us 3:1 ratio, but better to use actual media ratio which is actually 0.65:1.

- So hold that 1:1 is a fair upper limit.

[Pinto Case]{.underline} (Pinto struck in rear by another car, results in gas tank rupture and fire. Driver killed and passenger burnt. Jury: CD is \$2.5 mill. and PD is \$125 mill. Trial court reduces to \$3.5 mill.)

- DP clause does not allow punishing of non-party victims

  - can't punish w/out giving DF opportunity to present every available defense

  - Must use non-party harms to explain how bad the harm was, need to punish them.

  - If there is a discrete harm (direct-letter campaign), you can divide up the badness of the harm for each piece of mail. This is the harm, and that is what PD is for.

**[Repetitive Awards]{.underline}** -- Courts can award both compensatory and punitive damages repetitively

- Too much: no deterrence after a while, what if company goes bankrupt.

- Too little: even total of all CDs + PDs might not be enough to stop tortious action.

- And sometimes too little if only fraction of victims actually bring the case.

- Offset punitive damages for future plaintiffs?

  - Requires a guessing game, you can try to estimate total amount of PD and divide it based on anticipated defendants, but what if estimate is wrong? And how do we decide upon a fixed punitive damage number, especially if not pegged to CD

[W.R. Grace v. Waters]{.underline} (mass tort involving asbestos products, court refused to bar multiple awards of punitive damages)

- Generally courts have declined to bar multiple punitive damages.

- They say that it is unfair to plaintiffs to bar latter ones from recovering punitive damages.

- It might be somewhat unfair to make defendants pay multiple punitive damage awards, but no good system is in place.

- Multiple damages on same conduct is not per se unconstitutional, but might be challenged if the total sum becomes too high or defendant cannot pay the damages.

- One suggestion is to advise jury of previous punitive damage awards so that they award smaller punitive damages.

- Might hurt DF because it tells jury that the DF was guilty in previous trials.

- Solution is bifurcated proceeding where first step is one liability, second step is amount of compensatory damages, and last step is liability for punitive damages.

  - This would allow defendant to build a record for due process argument based on cumulative effect of prior awards
