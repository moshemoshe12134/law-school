    a.  Q: When do you switch regimes?

        i.  **Rules** - are the most constraining and rigid. Once a rule has been interpreted and the facts have been found, then the application of the rule to the facts decides the issue to which it is relevant. (Five year children are not negligent)

        ii. **Standards** - provide an intermediate level of constraint. Standards guide decisions but provide a greater range of choice or discretion; standards define a set of mandatory considerations (Learned Hand)

        ii. **Standards**

            1.  P: Ex post fairness

            2.  P: Permits ex post flexibility & allows judicial discretion

            3.  P: Easier to allow flexible exceptions based on circumstances

            4.  C: Unclear from ex ante guidance

            5.  C: Inconsistent results in courts

# CONFLICTING AIMS OF TORT LAW

## Rights-oriented aims: Corrective Justice & Civil Recourse:

a.  Corrective Justice: Tort law as a way of restoring the moral balance between wrong-doer and victim

b.  Civil Recourse: Tort law is a way of providing victims with recourse for civil wrongs.

c.  **Pro**: Justifies popular sense of moral blame (both), works well for individuals, intentional wrong-doer (corrective justice), vindicates victims' need for recognition that they have been wronged (civil recourse).

d.  **Con**: Less applicable in modern day where DF is often a faceless corporation or individuals covered by liability insurance.

## Optimal Deterrence:

e.  Promote optimal deterrence -- to deter excessively risky activity so that only those losses worth avoiding are avoided. (BPL)

f.  **Pros**: Produces the most economic solution -- just enough deterrence to prevent risks not worth taking, but not too much to require foregoing some of the benefit of that activity.

g.  **Cons**: Only takes into consideration economic values and ignores other important human values; pain & suffering from accident shouldn't be reduced to economic value of what it takes to prevent it.

## Loss Distribution:

h.  Promotes the broad distribution of losses due to accident/injury.

i.  **Pros**: Among all other things equal, having a large number of people bear a small loss is better than having single person bear all of it.

j.  **Cons**: (1) Does not satisfy sense of moral blame (2) Tort law system is an expensive mechanism for distributing losses (insurance, social security are cheaper ways).

## Compensation (LIMITED):

k.  Promote compensation to those who have suffered injury.

l.  **Pros**: Makes people feel more secure that they will be compensated for the harm they suffered.

m.  **Cons**: We need more than injury to justify liability, so there needs to be more than just the goal of compensation.

## NEGLIGENCE STANDARD

### Articulation of the Standard

1.  N.B. Distinguish from tort of negligence (claim for negligence), which has four elements: (1) a duty of reasonable care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) causation, and (4) resulting damages. Here we deal with SECOND element (breach of duty) because to say that DF "was negligent" is to say that he breached the standard of due care.

2.  **Negligence is failure to exercise the care that would have been exercised by the reasonably prudent person under the circumstances.**

    a.  [A]{.smallcaps}n unintentional act can be tortious if it is negligent.

**[Brown v. Kendall (MA 1850)]{.underline}** -- PL and DF owned dogs that were fighting, and in attempting to break up the fight with a stick, the DF accidentally struck the PL in the eye.

- **(Factor 1)** No liability if DF did not intent to injure the PL **or** had negligently injured him & negligence results from *[lack of ordinary care, which varies with the circumstances.]{.underline}*

- **(Factor 2)** *[PL has burden of proving either intent to injure or negligence]{.underline}*; DF need not prove due care.

- **(Factor 3)** Early recognition that *[PL has contributed to the accident in some way]{.underline}*. Case turns attention to the person making the claim as well as the DF bc it takes two to tort.

**[Adams v. Bullock (NY 1919)]{.underline}** (Cardozo)-- Boy swings huge wire around which catches onto the wiring of trolley and electrocutes him.

- **Holding:** Negligence may not be imputed where reasonable care was taken (untaken precautions are not prohibitively expensive -- just ordinary care would suffice) & conduct was lawful (operating trolley line is legal & certain risks come along with it that society must accept), but an extraordinary injury happened (unforeseeable).

- **(Factor 4)** Accident was *[not foreseeable]{.underline}*. Cardozo claims that only a freak accident would've made the accident happen.

  - Cardozo doesn't go into the details of the situation.

- **(Factor 5)** The *[untaken precautions]{.underline}* of the trolley company are so expensive.

  - Party arguing for negligence: Point to all the untaken precautions (e.g. court doesn't mention the unusual configuration, place wire further away from people, insulate it, put up a sign, etc.)

    - Burden on PL to show that there were alternative precautions that could have been taken but weren't

  - Party arguing for no negligence: (1) Too expensive (2) Untaken precautions would've have worked.

**[Learned Hand's Economic Formulation of Negligence (BPL)]{.underline}**

Conduct is negligent where the *expected* 'cost' of the accident outweighs the cost of precaution. This is seen sometimes as the intuitive process we do anyway in deciding what the reasonable person would do.

- **[U.S. v. Carroll Towing Co (NY 1947)]{.underline}**

<!-- -->

- Appellant owned a barge chartered by a RR company. The barge was moored to the end of the pier and had a large cargo of flour

<!-- -->

- Appellant hired a tug company (Carroll Towing Co.) to drill out one of the barges. Carroll readjusted mooring lines and barge broke free and sank.

<!-- -->

- The barge wouldn't have sunk if someone were aboard when the barge broke free. The "untaken precaution" here was that the guy was not there on the boat during the day with no excuse --- it was his job!

<!-- -->

- **Holding**: An owner has a duty to take care, and will be deemed negligent otherwise, when the **probability of an accident** multiplied by the **magnitude of the resulting injury** outweighs the **cost of taking adequate precautions**.

- **Negligence if B \< P\*L**; no negligence otherwise

  - **B = burden of taking adequate precautions** (precautions that a reasonable person would've taken)

  - **P = probability of accident (if the precaution is untaken)**

    - P means foreseeable probability, NOT actual probability

      - rarely does a defendant escape liability purely because a risk was unforeseeable

      - foreseeability needs to be applied to the "reasonable person", not the actual person

**[Bethel v. New York City Transit Authority (NY 1998)]{.underline}** -- PL was hurt on DF's bus when the "wheelchair accessible seat" collapsed under him.

### Objective & Subjective Standard

Objectivity or subjectivity of standard of care depends on the degree to which a person's shortcoming or disability is **evident** to others. Some shortcomings will not be taken into account at all.

#### Objective Standard 

**[Vaughan v. Menlove (1837)]{.underline}** -- DF should be held to the standard of ordinary prudence in spite of the variances in judgment and ability; stupidity & lack of intelligence is not part of the "circumstances", hence no excuse.

- Facts: DF landowner piled hay in a way that created a fire hazard to neighbors, including the PL.

- ***Mental Ability** -- usually not part of the "circumstances"*

- **Policy for objective standard for mental ability**: (1) Incentivizes less capable people to bring themselves up to the standard of conduct of ordinary people. (2) Subjective standard would result in basically a different rule for everyone, which would be difficult to manage (administrative argument).

  - **Against**: Fairness argument -- It's not fair to hold people with differential mental ability to the same standard as everyone else.

#### Exceptions to Objective Standard:

- **[[Children]{.underline}]{.mark}** -- **Semi-objective standard**: Expected to exercise the degree of care that would be reasonable in a child of similar age, intelligence, and experience except when children engage in "adult activities".

  - NY: Under 4 -- no negligence/IL: Under 7 -- no negligence. (RULE)

    - **Pros**: With this rule, PL can save time & \$\$ that would've be spent trying to sue kids.

    - **Cons**: No deterrence effect to regulate the kids' behavior.

    - **What if neuroscience** tells us that 19-year-olds are just as immature as 17-year-olds? The line has to be drawn somewhere -- dose custom/culture dictate this?

  - "Adult activity" -- depends on state.

    - **[Goss v. Allen (1976) --]{.underline}** skiing is not an adult activity because it's **recreational** & unlike driving, operating a motorboat, does not require (at least by NJ) that the minor be **licensed**.

- **[[Infirm Adults]{.mark}:]{.underline}** Typically, physical infirmities are taken into account in judging the reasonableness of behavior (semi-objective standard), but mental infirmities are not.

  - ***Physical Disability** -- usually part of the "circumstances"*

    - **Subjective Standard -- [Roberts v. Ramsbottom (1980)]{.underline} --** DF liable bc (1) he failed to realized, after one or two problems on the road, that he was physically unfit to continue driving for the day. Physical disabilities are considered part of the "circumstances" & given these circumstances, a "reasonable person" would've stopped driving. (2) he had some (although limited) control over his actions; loss of consciousness might've sufficed.

    - Facts: Old man suffered stroke a few minutes before setting out on a drive & caused accident.

    - **Policy for subjective standard for physical disability**: (1) Ease of proof (easier to prove physical disability than a mental one).

    - **Against**: Loses some deterrence. If physical disabilities are subject to objective standard, those with disabilities will take heighted precautions. (**Counter**: See *[Ramsbottom]{.underline}* -- subjective standard works against DF by raising the standard of care due to the physical disability).

    - *[Hammontree]{.underline}* -- are Jenner's epileptic seizures a mental or physical disability? [On EXAM, argue that something is like a mental ability if you don't want it to count, argue as physical if you want it to count as "circumstances"]{.mark}

- **[[Experts]{.underline}]{.mark}**

  - ***Superior Attributes***

    - **R2T 289(b):** The DF, in addition to exercising the "attention, perception of the circumstances, memory, knowledge of other pertinent matters, intelligence, and judgment as would a reasonable person, must also exercise such "superior" attributes on the listed items as the "actor himself has".

      - **So subjective standard for superior mental ability, but objective for inferior mental ability.**

      - Tension with *[Bethel]{.underline}*?

    - Expertise can cut both ways (*[Goss]{.underline}*):

      - HELP: (1) Expert was not negligent bc his expertise -- it might've been negligent for novice to make the turn, but not him. #### Personal Characteristics That Become Part of the Circumstances -- HURT OR HELP?

- ***[Little Rock v. Tankersley (1890)]{.underline}*** -- To determine whether an actor is negligent, actor must be judged by comparison with acts of a person of ordinary prudence under similar circumstances -- age, sex, physical condition. **Characteristics HURT the party.**

  - Facts**:** A woman jumps off a moving train.

  - **Counter** -- so characteristic would HELP: (1) train should've known it has all kinds of passengers on it & should've taken steps so a woman can alight safely (**CLEGG COURT**), (2) women don't know how trains work bc they stay home & shouldn't be held to the same standard as a man.

- ***[Daniels v. Clegg (1873)]{.underline}*** -- Degree of diligence required of incompetent persons should take account of their incompetence; persons encountering an obviously incompetent person have a correspondingly higher duty of care to avoid injury. **Characteristics HELP the party.**

  - Facts: Young female buggy driver crashes with wagon.

  - **[The characteristics have an effect on both PL's and DF's standards of care.]{.mark}**

  - **Counter** -- so characteristic would HURT: She should've have been driving anyway (**TANKERSLEY COURT**).

**[4 lessons from *[Tankersley]{.underline}* and *[Clegg]{.underline}*]{.mark}**

1.  Taking characteristics into account can help to lower the standard by lowering expectations. (*[Clegg]{.underline}*). Note that this cuts both ways and can work to heighted the standard (*[Ramsbottom, Tankersley]{.underline}*).

2.  If you're not allowed to use something as part of the circumstances (e.g. sex, mental ability), use a proxy that would work better (e.g. height, physical composition).

3.  On a spectrum that runs from the internal/subjective circumstance to external/objective circumstance, where do these circumstances (e.g. sex, inexperience, weather, age) go? People (jury and judge) have different instincts about where things go on the spectrum & this is where lawyers fight out the battles.

#### Negligence Per Se (Statutory Violations)

Violation of a safety statute command can be negligence *per se* if purpose of statute is to set a safety standard, but not necessarily if it just codifies existing common law.

- **[Martin v. Herzog (1920, Cardozo)]{.underline}** -- the unexcused omission of a statutory obligation is negligence *per se*.

  - Facts: Buggy driver sues wagon driver & wagon driver argues for contributory negligence bc buggy driver didn't have lights on.

  - Arguments for: (1) Legislature provided us with a standard of reasonable care. (2) A reasonable person wouldn't violate a statute.

**Defenses:**

A.  **Legally cognizable excuse**

- **[Tedla v. Ellman (1939)]{.underline}** -- Non-adherence to a statute that **merely codifies** a **general rule of conduct**, rather than affixing a standard of care, does not necessarily constitute negligence *per se*; There is an implied exception for cases in which compliance with the statute would involve a greater risk of harm than violation.

  - Facts: PL was struck by DF's vehicle while she was walking on the wrong side of traffic (violation of statute). PL argued traffic was much heavier on the side that the statute required her to walk on.

- Non-legally cognizable excuses: Excuse does not get to jury and DF will be found negligence as a matter of law (e.g. speeding bc he was driving his son to school and he was late).

B.  **Purpose of statute**

- [On Exam: argue for both widening and narrowing of statute's scope by looking to (1) legislative history or preamble, and (2) whether the violation does or does not increase the risk of harm to those in the PL's situation.]{.mark}

  - Statute that has it's own penalty -- cuts towards no negligence per se?

- **[Di Ponzio v. Riordan (1997)]{.underline}** -- Violation of ordinance requiring that car engine be turned off while car is being filled with gas. Car rolled back and pinned PL against his car, but court ruled for DF because ordinance was the city's fire code.

- **R2T 286 -- Exception to Negligence Per Se for Statutory Violations:**

  - The court may adopt as the **standard of conduct of a reasonable man the requirements of a legislative enactment or an administrative regulation** whose **purpose** is found to be exclusively or in part

# Role of Plaintiff - Contributory & Comparative Negligence

- All forms of these negligence regimes exist in different states.

- **Affirmative defense** of **contributory negligence** is a complete bar to recovery for PL.

  - DF has burden of proof to establish PL's negligence.

  - Maryland still has contributory negligence regime

  - Modern trend is for comparative negligence regimes

- Comparative negligence regimes apportion the damages to % of fault of the parties.

1.  **Pure comparative negligence:** damages apportioned proportionally to % of fault

2.  **Modified comparative negligence:** damages apportioned proportionally to percentage of fault, up until plaintiff is 50% or \>50% responsible, depending on jurisdiction, in which case plaintiff recovers nothing.

- **Pro**: (1) Moral blame concept (if PL is more responsible than DF, he shouldn't be able to recover -- think of a situation where DF is 1% responsible) (2) Incentivizes PL to game the system if we allow recovery when PL is mostly responsible & Does not provide enough incentives to be careful. (3) Administrative ease -- at a certain point, we don't have to make the determination.

- **Con**: (1) Not fair & bad distribution: same harsh applications as in contributory negligence results; there's no meaningful difference between PL being 49% and 51% to blame. (**[Li v. Yellow Cab]{.underline}**) (2) Doesn't do enough to incentivize DF to be careful. (note that this cuts bothways -- pure comparative neg. might depress DF's activity level)

  - Some "exceptions":

    - Duty to rescue: if you started rescue, you have duty to rescue non-negligently.

      - Policy: By attempting rescue, others did not attempt rescue that might've been more successful bc you induced reliance.

      - **[BUT! What if you were the only one there? (THINK ABOUT THIS FOR EXAM)]{.mark}**

    - If you non-negligently create a danger to another person, you may have duty to warn the other person of that danger, and perhaps even to undertake a more active rescue. (**[Montgomery]{.underline}**)

  - **Look for** in fact pattern: **[Affirmative duty]{.underline}** created by **statute, omission, special relationship, or duty to warn.** Then, you should discuss duty a bit more.

  - Generally, there is **no [affirmative duty]{.underline} to others**; no good Samaritan law.

**Misfeasance**: DF negligently created a risk of harm.

**Nonfeasance**: DF who did not remove a risk of harm created by someone or something else.

- No affirmative duty for nonfeasance (bright-line rule) unless there is exception.

## Act v. Omission

**[The Moch Case (Cardozo) (NY, 1928)]{.underline}** - **Withdrawal of a benefit is an act of omission (nonfeasance), rather than act of commission (misfeasance);** **[This is negligent nonfeasance unaccompanied by malice & therefore no affirmative duty.]{.underline}**

- **Facts**: DF water works had a K with city to supply water for various purposes, including service at fire hydrants. Building caught fire & destroyed PL's warehouse & PL claimed that water company's failure to supply adequate water permitted the spread of fire.

- **HOLDING**: No CL tort action available to users of the water supplied to city.

## Duty v. Breach

**Institutional Competence & Standard v. Rules**:

- Duty is a question of law & decision is made by judge, meaning it's more of a bright-line rule

- Breach (Due Care) is a question of fact & decision is made by jury, meaning it's more of a standard.

- Both CL and Statue can create both.

**[Cuyler v. U.S. (Posner) (7^th^ cir., 2004)]{.underline}**

- Facts: Crazy nanny on military base abused children & that abuse ended up killing one of the kids. Parents sue U.S. through FTCA.

- Laws:

  - **Federal Torts Claim Act (FTCA):** The federal government is liable for the torts of its employees to the same extent under the law of the place where the tort took place.

  - **IL Statute (Abused and Neglected Child Reporting Act)**: Variety of people (ranging from physicians to foster parents & childcare workers) having reasonable cause to believe a child known to them in their professional or official capacity may be an abused child or a neglected child shall immediately report to the IL Department of Children and Family Services.

    - U.S. concedes that they violated this statute.

  - **IL Common Law**: (1) Violation of statutory standard of care is prima facie evidence of negligence rather than negligence per se. **[(2) Statutory STANDARD OF CARE does not come into play unless the PL establishes that the DF owes a DUTY OF CARE to the person he injured.]{.underline}**

- **Issue**: Did IL CL or statute establish the hospital employee's duty of care to the child?

  - **CL**: No affirmative duty to rescue child from 3^rd^ party bc IL has no Good Samaritan law.

  - **Statute**: Not expressly. Only sanctions provided are criminal & disciplinary sanctions for willful violations. *If there were a provision for private right to obtain damages for the violation, there WOULD be an affirmative duty to the child.*

    - **Factors to consider** when considering whether to read damages remedy into a statute (implication of a private right of action): Whether

1.  PL is a member of the class for whose benefit the statute was enacted,

2.  It is consistent with the underlying purpose of the statute,

3.  PL's injury is one the statute was designed to prevent, and

4.  It is necessary to provide an adequate remedy for violations of the statute.

- **HOLDING**: No CL & statutory affirmative duty bc (1) statute already has its own sanctions, suggesting that it's meant to be separate from tort law, not an add on, and (2) those sanctions are imposed only for willful violation, suggesting reluctance to impose liability for mere negligence, and (3) statute had been amended multiple times & legislature had lots of opportunities to plug in damages remedy, and (4) the wide range of people covered by the statute cuts against imposing affirmative duty.

**Possible argument not taken by state regarding application of statute:**

- [Statutory purpose]{.underline} is just meant to keep a record, collect info, not to protect children ex ante. Therefore, negligence per se (or in this case, prima facie case) not applicable.

## Special Relationships

- An exception to the bright-line no duty rule for nonfeasance is when PL & DF are in a special relationship. (e.g. common carriers & innkeepers, parents & child-care professionals).

- In Tarasoff, CA SC takes this CL special relationship exception and **turns it into an affirmative duty between DF and a third party.**

**[Tarasoff v. Regents of the University of California (1976)]{.underline}** -- Existence of a special relationship between a therapist and a patient creates **duty to warn the foreseeable victim to whom the patient poses a danger.**

- Court reasoning: Doctors already control patient's action by medication & tell others to prevent patient from harming others.

  - Counter/Criticism: It's very difficult to predict mental disease & behavior (not as concrete as physical disease).

- Tarasoff factors for when determining whether affirmative duty should exist:

  - Foreseeability of harm

  - Closeness of connection between D's conduct and harm suffered by P

  - Moral blame attached to D's conduct

  - Extent of burden on D and consequences for community if duty is imposed

  - Policy of preventing future harm

  - Availability, prevalence of insurance that is/would be available if duty is imposed.

- **CA Legislature's Reaction**: Immune from liability for failure to warn unless patient communicated **serious threat of physical violence against a reasonably identifiable victim.** **Reasonable efforts to communicate the treat to victim & police satisfies duty to warn**.

- **Most states** have accepted, some have extended, **few** have rejected the CA statute's take on Tarasoff.

### Product Liability

- In most jurisdictions, there is a CL duty to **reasonable care to warn of risks** posed by product design.

- Jury decides the reasonable care taken to warn and takes into account **evidence of custom, statutory regulations.**

- Theory underlying liability for the failure to warn is that, once an adequate warning has been given, the purchaser rather than the product seller is the superior bearer of the risk of injury.

- **Adequacy of warning:**

  - **What is required is an "adequate" warning or instruction, typically of foreseeable risks**. Jury, or judge on directed verdicts, must make this call.

    - **Too much = label clutter & won't be read (Hood court's reasoning)**

      - **[Hood v. Ryobi America Corp. (4^th^ Cir, 1999)]{.underline} -- Using BPL in deciding whether a warning is adequate**, court finds that warnings to not detach the blade guards is adequate, and warnings of dangers of using the saw without the blade guards in place is not necessary.

        - **Court:** In BPL -- there has been only one accident in 15 years, so the PL is low, while the B is high bc resulting label cluttering.

        - **Counter:** (1) Just four words would suffice "blade may cut off" (2) the fact that the blade may do this is NOT common knowledge.

    - **Causation issue** like in informed consent: PL must prove that he would not have used the product (or in a manner that he did), if an adequate warning had been given.

      - **Heeding presumption**: presumption that PL would have heeded an adequate warning if it had been given. Probably not true, but (1) w/o PL may not able to prove causation, and (2) creating insurance protection for the unlucky few who suffer side effects (counter: insurance through tort is expensive)

- **The warning/design defect connection**:

  - When **a reasonable alternative design** can eliminate a product defect, mere warning about these risks does not insulate the DF from liability.

  - When there is no reasonable alternative design, there used to be **patent danger rule** (adequate warning shields from liability), but **overruled in many jurisdictions**, implying that jury may find that certain products are too dangerous to be on the market even when their risks are known and understood.

  - **In this area involving the intersection between design defect & warnings law, the [tension between consumer sovereignty]{.underline} and the [protective goals of tort law]{.underline} continues.**

## STRICT LIABILITY -- Restatement Standards

A.  **Third Restatement** -- Latest Approach to the Law

    a.  [Sets out TWO requirements to be *abnormally dangerous*:]{.mark}

        i.  Creates a foreseeable and highly significant risk of harm and injury despite the exercise of reasonable care (most believe this embodies **proximate cause**)

            1.  Taking more caution when under SL will not have much effect because of the inherent danger. So controls both the *activity level* and the *care level*.

        ii. Activity is not of common usage

            1.  Frame "common" to make different arguments -- common to normal people? Common to an area? Common industrial practice?

        iii. Comments suggest considering R2T's factors as well.

B.  **First Restatement** -- Uses *ultra-hazardous* instead of *abnormally dangerous* but achieves the same purpose

    a.  High risk of harm ðŸ¡ª cannot eliminate even with care exercised and requires that it is not common (also called un-natural per *Rylands*)

C.  [**Second Restatement** -- More nuanced in the factors. Sort of a balancing test when deciding whether the court should use SL for the *abnormal activities*.]{.mark}

    a.  [Existence of a high degree of risk of some harm]{.mark}

    b.  [Likelihood that the harm that results from it will be great]{.mark}

    c.  **[[Inability to eliminate the risk by the exercise of reasonable care]{.underline}]{.mark}**

    d.  [Extent to which the activity is not a matter of common usage]{.mark}

    e.  [Inappropriateness of the activity to the place where it is carried on]{.mark}

    f.  **[[Extent to which its value to the community is outweighed by its dangerous attributes]{.underline}]{.mark}**

        i.  [SL is a tool for building policies: the sixth factor is what the legislature does in terms of weighing the societal costs and benefits]{.mark}

D.  [D**O NOT FORGET ALL OTHER ELEMENTS OF TORTS ARE NEEDED: DUTY, CAUSATION (CIF & PC/SOL), DAMAGES**]{.mark}

    1.  [Also, don't forget the **role of PL (defenses of contributory, comparative negligence apply)!!!**]{.mark}

    2.  **[SCOPE OF LIABILITY: There is only SL if engaging in the relevant activity is not only the CiF, but also the PC of the harm that PL suffered.]{.mark}**

        a.  **[Harm must fall within the bucket of reasons why we made the activity SL.]{.mark}**

        b.  [**NO SL if** harm results from Act of God, unforeseeable intervention, or PL's knowledge of danger.]{.mark}

    a.  **[Doerr v. Goldsmith (2013)]{.underline}** -- accident occurred when PL was riding bike and DF's dog ran across in front of him.

        i.  Old law: For animals, if there are vicious & had dangerous propensity + was known, SL applied. If not, N. For escape, if animal escapes + natural known mischief occurs, SL.

        ii. **Holding**: Court bypasses by saying that dog running across were the actions of the DFs (like throwing a football), so N applies.

    b.  **[Connecticut Horse Case]{.underline}** -- horse bites boy.

            2.  LESSON: You can shape negligence so that it looks like SL.

    c.  **[Rylands v. Fletcher (England, 1868)]{.underline}** -- SL for damages caused by "non-natural use" of land.

# Causation In Fact

## Types of Causation in Fact (CiF)

### But-for Causation

a.  Requires a determination whether "but for" the DF's negligence (or breach of another applicable standard of care) the PL would have suffered injury or damage? No -- liable if also proximate cause; Yes -- not liable.

b.  **DF's argument**: Construct a counter-factual (e.g. had the lights been on...would still have crashed into the tree) to say that the outcome would not be changed.

c.  **[7WTC (2^nd^ Cir, 2012)]{.underline}** -- PL claims that negligent building was a CiF of the destruction of the building during 9/11, but court implies that the terrorist attack was the dominant cause, so DF's arguendo "negligent" building was not CiF & grants summary judgment to DF.

    i.  "It is simply incompatible with **common sense and experience (judge's common sense)** to hold that DF were required to design & construct a building that would survive the events of 9/11."

    ii. **Counter**: (1) Reasonable jury could conclude that the falling rocks due to the bad building contributed to the collapse or something else, and (2) judge ignores evidence on a very factual issue of what may have caused the destruction, etc., just says terrorist attacks trumps any other reason, and therefore other reason (neg. building) is not CiF.

d.  **Informed Consent Cases (Watford Concurrence in [Stengel]{.underline}, [Cuyler]{.underline})**

    i.  Even after PL has proved the failure of the DF to make proper disclosure, proof of causal connection between that failure & PL's injury is required, meaning that PL must show that he would've declined the treatment/use if he had been given the info about risks.

    ii. [**Two Standards** -- a difference of how much subjectivity (the circumstances of the patient) are taken into account.]{.mark}

        1.  [**Fully objective standard:** Whether an objectively reasonable person would've declined treatment had info been disclosed.]{.mark}

        2.  [**Semi-objective**: Whether a reasonable person **[in the PL's position]{.underline}** would have declined treatment had info been disclosed.]{.mark}

            a.  [Protects interest of idiosyncratic patients]{.mark}

    iii. **Watford Concurrence in [Stengel]{.underline}**: To prove CiF, PL must prove that disclosure to FDA would've reached him, prompting him to not use equipment. THIS IS HARD TO PROVE.

         1.  Original claim: DF failed to warn doctors -- but that would've been pre-empted by MDA.

    iv. **[Cuyler]{.underline}**: Even if duty had been established by statute & DF had been found to have neglected it, PL still must've proved CiF. **Relevant evidence:**

        1.  Show how fast the news spreads in the community/community alert from reporting?

        2.  When reported, not allowed to babysit?

        3.  Cuylers screened potential babysitters by checking database?

### Multiple Sufficient Causation (MSC)

a.  Multiple acts, each of which would have been sufficient to cause the harm in question. Either tortfeasor's negligence would have been sufficient to be but-for cause of P's harm. (e.g., two fires, separately set, reach house at the same time)

    i.  There is no "but for" cause in this situation bc even if one act did not occur, accident still would've occurred.

    ii. Here, law choses over-imposition of liability (over-inclusion)

    <!-- -->

    a.  **[One negligent, one non-negligent sufficient causes (CLOSE CALL & EXAM Q)]{.mark}**

        i.  Many states **impose liability** on the tortious actor

            1.  **Pros**: Serves deterrence function

        ii. Some states **don't impose liability** on tortious actor

            1.  **Pros**: Unfair to DF bc injury would've happened absent DF's actions.

            2.  **Cons**: Moral luck -- tortious action that does not turn into injury is equally blameworthy, Unfair to make it harder for PL to get relief

        iii. *Under **comparative negligence**, many states reduce the liability of the tortious actor in accordance with the non-tortious cause*

    b.  **[Sequential Causes: Pre-emptive Causation (CLOSE CALL & EXAM Q)]{.mark}**

        i.  First tortious conduct (e.g. fire) causes injury (burns house), then second tortious actor (e.g. second fire) is not liable had the first tortious conduct completely pre-empted the later probable cause.

        ii. **There** is a between when cause is **determined already, internal (e.g. cancer patient is killed by bike) & partially pre-empted** and when **cause has not been determined, external & completely pre-empted (e.g. misses plane that crashes bc some ran into you with bike).**

            1.  **External, not-yet-determined/full pre-emption:** Damages owned are not affected (law of causation).

                a.  If house would've stood on ground 2 min. longer w/o 2^nd^ fire, then damages are 2 minutes of rent.

                    i.  Absurd results: If both fires got there the same time (MSC), then can't you say that both parties are only liable for 0 sec. of rent (ZERO) bc they are burning it at the same time?

            2.  **Internal, already determined/partial pre-emption**: Damages are affected by life expectancy (law of damages)

    c.  **[Strategy: Argue to shift MSC to MNC if you're PL:]{.mark}**

        i.  One cause was actually NOT sufficient (e.g. two sources of fires were actually necessary, not sufficient bc one fire would not have been able to overwhelm the sprinkler).

        ii. DF would attempt to **shift MNC to MSC, then maybe to argue one cause was non-tortious.**

    d.  **[3^rd^ Restatement on "but for" test for MSC]{.mark}**

        i.  **R3T 26 (Factual Cause):**

            1.  **Tortious conduct** must be a factual cause of harm for liability to be imposed. Conduct is a factual cause of harm when the harm would not have occurred absent the conduct. Tortious conduct may also be a factual cause of harm under R3T 27.

        ii. **R3T 27 (Multiple Sufficient Causes):**

            1.  If multiple acts occur, each of which under R3T 26 alone would have been a factual cause of the physical harm at the same time in the absence of the other act(s), each act is regarded as a **factual cause of the harm.**

        iii. Regarding MSC when one SC is non-tortious:

             1.  *R3T assigns CiF & then leaves to the law of damages to sort out who really ends up paying.*

             2.  **R3T 27 Cmt d. One cause tortious, the other innocent**. [This Section applies in a case of MSC, regardless of whether the competing cause involves tortious conduct or consists only of innocent conduct]{.underline}. Conduct is a factual cause of harm regardless of any other cause with which it concurs to produce overdetermined harm. Question of damages is different matter.

### [Lost Chance]{.mark}

[**[Matsuyama v. Birnbaum (Mass. 2008)]{.underline} --** PL can recover for loss of chance in a **[medical malpractice claim]{.underline}, where DF's negligence deprived PL of a less than even chance of survival (even if PL initially had less than 50% odds).**]{.mark}

- **Loss-of-chance** is majority rule.

- **Facts:** Doctor's malpractice caused delay in detection of cancer, which deprived cancer patient chance of survival that was already below 50%.

- **Court's move:** Redefine injury; death is not the injury, it's the loss of chance of survival that is the injury done to the PL.

  - [**What if the PL survived? Fennel** thinks recovery is still possible under the doctrine, but Matsuyama seems limited to situations where death actually resulted.]{.mark}

- **Damages:** (Total damages allowed by statute) \* (Survival odds pre-malpractice -- Survival odds post-malpractice)

- **N.B.** Not about BOP -- PL must establish by a preponderance of evidence that DF caused his injury just like in any other negligence context.

- **Significant minority** has **refused to adopt lost chance theory**.

  - **[Fennel v. Southern Maryland Hospital Center (Md. 1990)]{.underline} --** Court denied all recovery to a PL who had shown that the hospital's negligence deprived 40% chance of survival.

a.  Court interpreted loss of chance doctrine dictated some recovery if a DF's negligence reduced a PL's chance of survival from 40% to 10% - **even if patient survived.**

b.  **Concern about the conduct of trials:** stats are unreliable and confuse jury.

c.  **Fairness Concern:** No real difference between patient who had a 49% chance of survival to one that had 51%, but 49% is limited to 49% value of life while 51% is entitled to full recovery for life.

- **[Reasons to limit to medical context & why it shouldn't extend to general risk creation:]{.mark}**

  - [In medical context, there is more hard statistical evidence.]{.mark}

  - [Requires party more capable of preventing harm (doctor) to bear the consequences of his negligence; doctor is the cheapest cost avoider.]{.mark}

**[At the frontier (EXAM!!!!)]{.mark}**

- What if patient had a [genetic condition (non-tortious cause)]{.mark} that also decreased chance of survival along with malpractice?

- **[Survival]{.mark}**: If people were exposed to risk that deprived them of a 30% chance of survival (from 40% to 10%), should DF pay for (1) 30% of the value of lives of those who died or (2) 30% of value of lives of all exposed to the risk?

  - **(2)** would provide full deterrence & gets rid of issue of moral luck, risk creation is just as morally reprehensible regardless of result

  - **(1)** would result in optimal deterrence (bc (2) would be too much deterrence, might affect activity level of socially beneficial conduct -- surgeons)).

- [**Beyond medical context:** What if DF burned PL's lottery ticket, stock certificates?]{.mark}

  - [Courts currently use pain & suffering damages & infliction of emotional distress to deal with this.]{.mark}

  - **[Would loss of chance doctrine be a better alternative?]{.mark}**

# Proximate Cause

Even after it is determined that DF is under a duty to exercise reasonable care, and even after PL has proved the other elements of his case, liability isn't imposed unless DF's action also were the "proximate cause" (legal cause) of PL's injury.

Very mushy concept. All the approaches below blend together. Very fact-intensive inquiry.

## Elements of the doctrine

- **Modern Approaches:**

  - Restatement (Third) Approach: **SCOPE OF LIABILITY**

    - **R3T 36 Trivial Contributions to Multiple Sufficient Causes**

      - When an actor's negligent conduct constitutes only a trivial contribution to a causal set that is a factual cause of harm under **R3T 27**, the **harm is not within the scope of the actor's liability.**

        - **"Harriet's case"** -- one girl among a 7 male car-tippers: Any four of 8 of the car-tippers required to tip car over. **Anyone necessary to a possible sufficient cause has created CiF**, including Harriet. R3T 36 & proximate cause designed to exclude Harriet.

  - **HARM-WITHIN-THE-RISK TEST:** DF's negligence was not the proximate cause of PL's injury if risk of injury PL suffered was not the risk that made DF negligent.

    - [Harm-within-the-**foreseeable increased risk ([Palsgraff]{.underline}) --** see below chart for results of the type of unforeseeability.]{.mark}

    - **R3T 29 Limitations on Liability for Tortious Conduct**

      - An actor's liability is limited to those harms that result from the risks that made the actor's conduct tortious.

- **Traditional:**

  - **FORESEEABILITY:** DF's negligence is proximate cause of PL's harm if causing that harm was a foreseeable result of DF's conduct.

  - **INDIRECT v. DIRECT CAUSE:** Is there an intervening/superseding cause?

### Policy for/against proximate cause

- **Pros:** Normatively don't want negligent persons to have to pay for distant harms, Deterrence is not served bc unforeseen harms can't be deterred, impossible to administer a system where any cause is allowed to become a source of liability (we need to break the chain at some point)

- **Cons:** Important aim of tort law is compensation (negligent + injury should lead to compensation), moral luck -- there is no difference between tortfeasors who do same thing but only one results in harm.

- **"Proximate cause" is a test for negligence.**

##  BP Oil Spill: When ordinary negligence becomes gross negligence and willful misconduct (intentional torts)

**Basic Facts of BP Oil Spill:**

- On April 20, 2010, Deepwater Horizon, a state-of-the-art, offshore oilrig operated by BP in the Gulf of Mexico exploded. Blast killed 11 crew members. Then Deepwater Horizon sank, causing the LARGEST offshore oil spill in American history.

- During trial, oil-drilling expert testified that the rig's crew misinterpreted results of the negative pressure tests done the day of the explosion. U.S. contends that the botched test led to the blast.

  - Handling of the check "was a gross and extreme departure" from accepted standards in the oil industry

  - "Bladder effect": condition occurs when pressure on valves may produce false readings -- BP & Transocean content the rig's crew accepted the anomalous test results because of this. Experts agree this can't be an acceptable excuse.

- Government and spill victims say BP was over budget and behind schedule, prompting the company to cut corners and ignore safety tests showing the well was unstable.

- BP sued contractors (e.g. Transocean employees -- disabled safety system, failed to maintain the installation properly, Halliburton -- provided shoddy cement materials)

**Legal Issue:**

- Are BP, Haliburton, and Transocean guilty of gross negligence and/or willful misconduct?

- What's at stake is Clean Water Act (CWA)'s penalty, which quadruples the amount of damages if **GN OR WM** are found.

- GN and WN are not defined by CWA, so court must look to tort law (common law)

**Gross Negligence:**

- U.S.: GN is just really bad negligence (objective element), something far less than ordinary care.

- BP: GN requires finding of recklessness (subjective element) -- which is a culpable mental state -- along with negligence (objective element) that far less than ordinary care.

- **Judge Barbier:** (1) Agrees with U.S. (Objective standard of a reasonable company man under the circumstances.) (2) Even if we use BP's standard, still guilty of GN because the facts make you look so bad/imply culpable mental state & even if we use WN standard that requires subjective element as well:

a)  Inherently dangerous activity (foreseeable that risks were high so they knew the dangers) -- subjective

b)  Guy in Houston told the operator that something was wrong with negative pressure test, but operator continued without stopping & Houston didn't pursuit the matter further. -- subjective

c)  Bladder effect excuse: (1) could go to mental state because it's a BS excuse (2) could be objective bc no one would've misread test results due to this.

d)  Area drilling had been difficult -- objective bc a reasonable company person would've taken more precautions then due to the foreseeable risks of the area.

e)  Untaken precautions/BPL analysis: Burden was so low for untaken precautions like the Sheen test that required operator's approval after the negative pressure test (he could've just not approved & accident would've been prevented). -- objective

f)  Motivated by money!! -- subjective

- BP's counters:

  - Subjective info can be used to support BP:

    - The two employees were nice, responsible people.

**Willful Misconduct:**

- U.S. & BP agree: Some subjective element is required.

- Court: Only need GN OR WM, but see above for why facts show that BP is guilty of both WM and BP's standard of GN.

## COMPENSATORY DAMAGES

A.  Policy Considerations

    a.  **Goal**: make the plaintiff whole & restore the plaintiff to the position before the accident

    b.  Functions to satisfy aims of tort law: (1) achieve corrective justice & provide civil recourse, (2) promote deterrence by threatening DFs with liability, (3) recognizes social & individual significance of PF's loss & confirming the weight of the DF's responsibilities for the loss.

<!-- -->

1.  **Mechanism -- two types of compensatory Ds: (1) pecuniary (medical expenses, lost wages) and (2) non-pecuniary (pain & suffering)**

    a.  In ordinary cases, PF gets a **single (untaxed) recovery**, providing compensation not only for past but also future losses, if any.

        i.  **Pros**: (1) Closure -- PL has incentive to recovery as soon as possible after & get on with life, repose for DF & can plan for economic future w/o uncertainty of being liable to PL again. (2) Efficiency for legal system -- avoidance of multiple suits & their costs

        ii. **Cons**: (1) inaccurate (e.g. prediction of how injured PL will be in future, lost future earnings, how much & far into the future pain & suffering will continue, etc.) (2) PL must be a good investor or she will not have the award when she really needs it in the future bc award is discounted to present value (i.e. reduced to a sum which, if invested, will equal the amount of the future loss at the time it occurs.)

        iii. Recovery is virtually never permitted today for damages resulting from even a reasonable fear that one will suffer harm in the future; liability for future loss must await the actual occurrence of present physical injury.

    b.  **Pain and Suffering Awards**

        i.  **Function:** (1) acknowledges intangible losses from pain & suffering (compensation for just out-of-pocket expenses would rarely make the PL "whole"), (2) can use money for alternative enjoyments to compensate for what was taken away (e.g. can't use legs to play soccer, buy an awesome TV to watch soccer), (3) optimal deterrence (here, it doesn't matter to who DF pays, he just has to pay), (4) payment for attorneys (many tort suits are paid via contingent-fee arrangement)

            1.  **Regarding deterrence in contract settings** (e.g. medical malpractice or products liability): If people aren't willing to pay higher fee as insurance for P&S, then P&S damages shouldn't be awarded. COUNTER: Rational to assume that people want to purchase P&S insurance and tort law is more than just an insurance system.

        ii. **Limitations on Awards:** (1) remittitur & additur (2) absolute dollar ceiling (3) "schedule" of P&S awards like workers comp

        iii. **Pain and Suffering for Property Damage:** Black-letter limitation on recovery of P&S damages for property damages may be weakening (e.g. State statutes for deaths of pets).

        iv. See *Seffert* below

[**Bishop's justification for no recovery for pure economic losses**: Many economic loss situations involve **no net social costs**. Although these situations involve **private costs to the victims**, the net effect is a **mere redistribution of wealth with no consequent loss to society**.]{.mark}

**Other rationales:**

- **Loss-distributing:** Economic losses resulting from negligently caused bodily injuries/property damage are distributed among *large numbers of people*. Loss-distribution rationale for imposing liability on injurer is weak given *already-existing broad loss distribution*.

- **Deterrence:** No deterrence shortfall, since there would be liability for bodily injury/property damage.

## Vicarious Liability

**Respondeat superior = \"let the superior answer\"**

- Employer is liable for certain torts committed by employee

- Employer need not be negligent - just need an employee to be negligent \"within the scope of employment\" ðŸ¡º **So a SL TORT!**

- Theory is that employer is probably in better position to research and make decisions re: activity level and other changes to activities, saves info costs bc hard to prove employer to be neg. as well.

**Two questions about respondeat superior:**

- Who is an employee?

  - Seeks to distinguish between situations in which strict liability will have a desirable effect and when it will not

  - Does not include independent contractors (e.g. homeowner hires an electrician)

- When is a tort committed within the scope of employment?

  - Limits the effects of strict liability on employers

  - Employers do not have infinite capacity to control the conduct of their employees

  - Distinguishes between a \"detour\" and a \"frolic\" -- NOT a bright-line rule

  - Employer liable for tortious acts committed on a \"mere\" detour but not on a frolic

# Statutory Damages

## Definition

- Damages whose amount (or range) is set by law, usually without regard to the actual harm suffered by the PL.

  - For PL: simplifies proofs, awards damages w/o requiring them to proffer evidence of actual harm

  - For DF: become a multiplier of liability, esp. when claims or parties are aggregated. Often unpredictable bc wide range of statutory damages.

## Policy for Statutory Damages

- Statues are for crime-like activities, which makes people think of PD.

  - **Counter**: Then if it's criminal-like & punishment is purpose, shouldn't we afford DF the protections of criminal law?

  - **Counter-counter**: Courts have never come out & said that statutory damages are punishment. Would be problematic bc there will be overlap between federal civil penalties and crim penalties.

- Statues give fair notice to DFs & has deterrent effect & predictable

- **Makes litigation more attractive when damages are hard to calculate (traditional rationale for statutory damages)**

[**Possible alternative:** Floor-only statutory damages would preserve deterrent effect, incentive to litigate when damages are hard to calculate, punishment-like function. Counter is that that increases unpredictability.]{.mark}

B.  They really meant these numbers for any act prohibited by the statute.

        2.  Thousands of lawyers are put into a PL steering committee, resulting in some coordination problems.

    iii. PLs decide to set up a **settlement class action** & BP goes with this bc they want **global peace.**

         1.  Concern might've been binding missing PLs, but there's already a lot of them from MDL.

         2.  **Incentive to settle fast before trial against U.S. on allocating fault. That trial resulted in GN ruling for BP (opens up PD possibilities that were shut off by settlement)**

         3.  **[Settlement: (1) [no cap]{.underline} & payment starts right away (2) GCCF is gone & that money is used to pay PLs now (3) [no evidence of causation]{.underline} for certain PLs -- just need to show economic damages and say under threat of perjury that those injuries were caused by BP]{.mark}**

    iv. **Judge certifies, and objectors appeal resulting in BP II, an BP appeals resulting in BP IV. Court-appointed Juneau is fast & loose with \$\$ and BP starts to regret.**

        1.  **BP II** - no causation requirement causes (1) issues of commonality/predominance requirement of Rule 23 and (2) some PLs aren't even injured to be paid.

            a.  **[Court agrees with Posner on these points. THERE IS NO CIRCUIT SPLIT on CLASS ACTIONS, may have pushed back on Walmart.]{.mark}**

            b.  **Adds to Posner on situations where some PLs may not be harmed at all: no adequacy issue bc no cap = no intra-class conflict**

        2.  **BP IV --** matter of contract interpretation

            a.  Can't interpret as no causation bc then people who weren't injured will get paid.

            b.  **Court: (a) evidence you knew this was what you were agreeing to, and (b) claimers are under threat of perjury & therefore, not unreasonable.**

                i.  Court saying to BP: go after those people with perjury suit. But that's expensive for BP.

                ii. Court protects purpose of class actions (like Posner) by saying we don't have to worry about individual causation at the certification process.

        3.  [[However, circuits are split on **punitive damages in maritime law for respondeat superior. SCOTUS may take this up**]{.mark}**.**]{.underline}

d.  **Judges' role:**

    i.  Although they don't have technical authority, it's understood that judges have heightened equitable powers for MDL to ensure fairness (since Rule 23 protections are not available).

        1.  E.g. Post 9/11, Judge Hellerstein didn't have authority, but demanded revisions to the settlement agreement, etc. & 2^nd^ Cir. allowed this.

        2.  E.g. In government v. financial institutions, judges have been more active even when you don't have formal Rule 23 requirements.
