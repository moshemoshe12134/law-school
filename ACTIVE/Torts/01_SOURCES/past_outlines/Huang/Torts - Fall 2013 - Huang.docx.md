**NEGLIGENCE**

**Duty of Reasonable Care**

- [Brown v. Kendall]{.underline} (Kendall accidently injured Brown while trying to separate their fighting dogs with a stick)

  - Landmark case where negligence comes from (disputed).

  - A party is liable for damages caused by if it fails to use **ordinary care**. (This is when "fault" matters).

  - Dicta: Contributory negligence as a defense.

  - [Burden of proof:]{.underline} The plaintiff has the burden of proof to show that the defendant did not use ordinary care.

- [Adams v. Bullock]{.underline} (Bullock owned trolley line with overhead wire system. Boy (Adams) injures himself when a long wire he was swinging hit the overhead wire below a bridge)

  - Ordinary caution does not involve forethought of extraordinary peril.

  - Only **"reasonable" care** required ðŸ¡ª saves costly further (extraordinary) care.

  - Not mentioned by Cardozo: could have just put up a warning sign or made fence higher.

- [United States v. Carroll Towing Co.]{.underline} (Improperly secured barge drifts away from pier, damaging other boats and sinking itself (contained cargo of US Gov't. Issue is whether bargee had duty to be on boat (if he was on it, boat would not have sunk))

  - **Learned Hand Formula:** Duty to take precaution if **B \< P x L**

    - Breach of duty if the burden (cost) of taking adequate precautions is less than the probable marginal cost (to society) of not taking precautions (probability of loss x gravity of loss).

    - **Rationales:** Efficiency (Posner), Utilitarian

    - Negatives: **Incommensurability**

  - Facts of the case that showed lack of reasonable care: accident occurred during working hours, during a high traffic time and bad weather. Bargee didn't have an excuse.

- [Bethel v. New York City Transit Authority]{.underline} (Bethel injured in NYC bus when wheel chair seat collapsed)

  - Common carriers do not have a duty to increased standard of care ðŸ¡ª basic negligence standard of ordinary care applies.

**Objective/Subjective Standard**: It's a trade off.

- If you hold everyone to an objective standard, then you no longer would have duty to take into account other people' capacities when making your own decisions. ðŸ¡ª No incentive to increase care around people with decreased capacities.

- However, maybe people with lower capacities should decrease activity levels. Objective standards incentivize this.

**Mental Capacity**

- Mental ability is not a valid defense. Standard of negligence is objective.

**Physical Capacity**

- Held to same standard as reasonable person with same physical disability (Restatement).

**Children**

- [Dellwo v. Person]{.underline} (Defendant injured plaintiff while driving motorboat)

  - A child should be held to the standard of care of an adult when engaging in **adult activities** (exception).

  - People can anticipate that the conduct of a child engaged in children's activities may not reach an adult standard of care. However, much more difficult to establish that a driver of a vehicle is a minor so people can't protect themselves.

  - Children should not be participating in adult activities in the first place if they are incapable of adult standard of care.

  - Unanswered: What constitutes an adult activity (clues given).

**Emergencies:** One need only exercise basic reasonableness in judgment (lower standard)

- Issues: What constitutes an emergency? Should this be a rule or a standard (if there is an emergency doctrine)?

- FORK: Some states are getting rid of emergency doctrine and replacing it with general negligence standard (makes sense).

**Res Ipsa Loquitur**

- [Byrne v. Boadle]{.underline} (Byrne was hit by a barrel of flour but couldn't remember what happened)

  - If there is no direct evidence, liability for negligence can lie solely on account of that type of accident that occurred. As long as the plaintiff can show that accident was such that the plaintiff was probably negligent (**by a preponderance of the evidence**) then the burden of proof passes to the defendant.

- [McDougald v. Perry]{.underline} (McDougald was injured when a spare tire flew off Perry's triler and hit McDougald's windshield)

  - Res ipsa loquitur applies when the instrument which caused the injury was under the **exclusive control** of the defendant and some sort of **negligence was required** for the accident to occur.

**Custom**

- [The TJ Hooper]{.underline} (General practice was for tug boats not to have radios)

  - There are precautions so imperative that even their universal disregard (custom) will not excuse their omission.

  - **General practice doesn't necessarily connote lack of negligence**.

- [Timarco v. Klein]{.underline} (Glass door to bathtub shattered injuring plaintiff. Door was made of regular glass despite the common practice of using shatterproof glass.)

  - **Evidence of compliance/noncompliance with custom can be used as evidence but is not dispositive.**

- Certain professions, including the medical professions, are held to a higher standard of care ðŸ¡ª reasonable standard of care for someone in their field. Violation of professional custom by someone in one of these fields constitutes negligence.

**Negligence per se:** Unexcused violation of a statute designed to promote safety.

- FORK: A minority of states hold that violation of a statue is not negligent per se, but evidence of such violation is admissible and relevant.

- [Martin v. Herzog]{.underline} (Martin was killed when Herzog negligently crashed into his buggy (driving over center line). Martin's buggy had no lights in violation of a statute)

  - To omit, willfully or heedlessly (through thoughtlessness), the **safeguards** **prescribed by law for the benefit of others**, is to fall short of one's duty of care (negligent per se).

  - Jury not permitted to decide whether violation of statute was negligent (assumed unless âˆ† has excuse)

    - Per se = negligent as a matter of law.

  - Liable for harm caused if harm was the type the statute was meant to prevent (see defense of limited statutory purpose below).

- [Tedla v. Ellman]{.underline} (Two junk collectors were hit by a negligently driven car while walking on the right side of the road despite statute that says the walk on the left side. Left side had lots of traffic while the right side did not)

  - When a statutory rule of conduct fixes no definite standard of care, which would in all cases increase safety, **one is negligent only if he fails to observe the statute without good reason** (exception to negligence per se).

  - **Legally cognizable excuses** (opaque)

    - \(1\) necessity

    - \(2\) emergency

    - \(3\) incapacity

    - \*Also, defense of limited statutory purpose

      - Plaintiff wasn't in class of persons, or didn't suffer the type of harm, statute was meant to protect

      - Really hard to prove since statutes can be (and often are by modern courts) construed to be intended to protect unstated secondary beneficiaries.

- **Compliance:** All states hold that evidence of compliance with a statute is admissible, although such evidence usually does not have much weight, because statutes only prescribe minimum standards (often reasonable to exercise more care than minimum standard, e.g., drive slower in the snow).

- Law and economics explanation: Cost of precaution to follow statute in given situation is zero since [always]{.underline} supposed to follow statute, so should always at least take that much precaution, but often there are times where there are other precautions which have a cost/burden less than probable damage from accident (so it would be negligent not to take such precautions).

**CAUSATION**

**Cause in Fact**

- "But for" or *sina qua non* test: requires a determination whether if breach of duty had not occurred, then injury would not have occurred (if yes, then cause in fact).

  - Means that breach of duty must have been necessary for the injury to have occurred, but does not require that it be sufficient by itself.

  - "But for" the breach of duty, the plaintiff would have suffered injury or damage (if no, then cause in fact)

- [Stubbs v. City of Rochester]{.underline} (Stubbs got typhoid after drinking sewer water due to negligence of the City)

  - Don't have to rule out all alternatives ("differential etiology"), though ruling some out still helpful.

  - Instead, plaintiff must establish with **reasonable certainty** that the direct cause of the injury is one for which the defendant is liable.

    - Possible tools include: changes/timing, experts, statistical evidence, exposure, etc.

- [Zuchowitz v. United States]{.underline} (Zuchowitz got a rare and fatal disease after taking too much Danocrine due to the negligent instructions of naval hospital's doctors)

  - **If (1) a negligent act was deemed wrongful because that act increased the chances that a particular type of accident would occur, and (2) a mishap of that very sort did happen, this allows a trier of fact to find that the negligent behavior caused the harm.**

    - If plaintiff shows this, she satisfies her burden, and burden of proof shifts to defendant.

    - **Rationale:** Defendant likely has more access to relevant information regarding the case, but less incentive to reveal it (shifting burden gives him this incentive).

**Loss of Chance**

- [Matsuyama v. Birnbaum]{.underline} (Due to Birnbaum's negligence, Matsuyama's cancer was not discovered when he still would have had a chance for survival).

  - Loss of Chance Doctrine: A physician may be liable for damages when his negligence diminishes or destroys a patient's likelihood of achieving a more favorable medical outcome (injury = loss of chance).

  - Damages for loss of chance calculated based on proportional damages approach (proportional to full value of damages for death).

  - Loss of chance doctrine has so far been largely limited to medical malpractice cases (though Huang says you can imagine a lot of other possibilities).

    - Reliable expert/statistical evidence more likely

    - Common/widespread issue in medical cases

    - In the absence of the doctrine, the party who is the least capable of preventing the harm is forced to bear the consequences of the more competent party's negligence.

  - \*Court does not decide whether a plaintiff may recover on a loss of chance theory when the ultimate harm (e.g. death) has not yet come to pass.

    - If not including people who didn't die, gets incentives wrong for negligent actor (not enough internalization) ðŸ¡ª Even if you were allow plaintiffs to collect for loss of chance when they didn't die, many likely would not since they wouldn't even know they had experienced a loss of chance.

  - Huang: Everyone's actual chance of survival is ultimately zero. Court is really just correcting what the actual harm we are talking about is.

**Future Harm**

- Proportional liability often used due to statute of limitations and res judicata (though some states are beginning to lift these restrictions in certain cases).

  - Arguments against:

    - Plaintiff might not get the second disease and will receive money for nonexistent damages.

    - May deplete the defendant's coffers in the mass tort context so that there will be no money left for those who ultimately do get the disease.

    - Concern about giving a large sum to someone who does not yet need it---and who may spend it now and not have it when the disease does strike.

  - Arguments in favor:

    - Difficulty of proof if one must wait 20 or more years to sue.

    - Deterrent aspect is diminished.

- [Dillon v. Evanston Hospital]{.underline} (Piece of a catheter left in Dillon's body. 0-20% chance of adverse future consequences)

  - Court permitted plaintiff to recover for an increased risk of future injures even if not reasonably certain to occur, but the compensation should reflect the low probability.

- What about pure risk cases? Cases without legally compensable harm?

  - Probably not? Paz v. Brush Engineered Materials, Inc. (Miners beryllium lungs case) and asbestos cases.

**Alternative Liability**

- [Summers v. Tice]{.underline} (Summers was injured after âˆ†s simultaneously both negligently shot in his direction.)

  - Alternative Liability: If **negligent** defendants brought about a situation in which the negligence of one of them caused the injury to the plaintiff, the **burden of proof shifts** to the plaintiffs to absolve themselves.

  - **Rationale:** Defendants generally have better **access to information**, but less incentive to access/reveal it. Shifting burden gives them this incentive.

    - When there are few possible wrongdoers, there is a relatively high chance that anyone of them caused the injury (**less applicable if many**).

  - Questions unanswered: what if it is unclear whose negligence caused the injury but negligent acts weren't simultaneous but rather virtually simultaneous or not simultaneous at all? (pg. 370 #7,8)

  - What if source of one of the other causes is non-tortious?

    - In Rowe v. Munye, the court said burden shifting inapplicable (pg. 371 #9).

[Dillon v. Twin State Gas & Electric Co.]{.underline} (Boy slips and is going to fall off bridge to death, grabs negligently exposed wire and is electrocuted to death)

- Defendant's liability drastically reduced if injury that was caused by negligence was going to happen anyways (in the future).

**Market Share Liability**

- [Hymowitz v. Eli Lilly & Co.]{.underline} (Plaintiffs injured by drug (DES) mothers took made by many manufacturers)

  - Where **identification** of the manufacturer of a drug that injures a plaintiff is **impossible**, NY courts will apply a market share theory using the **national market**, to determine liability and apportionment of damages.

    - While we can't prove **but for causation** for particular harm, we can prove causation for **harm in general**.

    - Because liability is based on overall risk produced, and not causation in a single case, a defendant cannot exculpate itself by proving that it did not cause a particular plaintiff's injury.

      - [Huang: Does this apply to plaintiffs as well? (137-140 in Abraham)]{.mark}

    - **Not alternative liability / burden shifting**: Lots of defendants who are not necessarily in a better position to provide evidence.

  - **Requirements:**

    - \(1\) Unable to identify manufactures

    - \(2\) **Fungible** (identical products)

    - \(3\) **Clear causal relationship** between the product and the harm (signature disease)

    - \(4\) **Reliable market share data** (**"relevant" market**) ðŸ¡ª What a "relevant" market is still disputed by scholars.

  - Dissent ([Relative Market Share Approach]{.underline}):

    - Burden of proof should just shift (defendants should still be able to exculpate themselves)

    - Defendants unable to exculpate themselves should be joint and severally liable

    - Disadvantages: Imposing excessive liability on manufacturer (particularly if many of the manufactures are no long around), fairness concerns, overdeterrance, etc.

    - Partial solution (actually part of the theory): require that a substantial part of the market be joined as defendants.

**Moral Luck:** Even though you may have been exactly as negligent as other persons, you may get off without liability if their negligence causes harm while yours does not (Are we ok with this?)

- Reasons Alright:

  - Individuals should be free to take risks without paying for it if they don't hurt anyone else (Is putting others at risk really injuring them?)

  - If paid everyone for risk, those who were actually injured would be underpaid (unless maybe if they saved all payments from risks in which they were not injured but this is unlikely).

  - As a **practical matter**, too hard to measure risk normally until injury realized

  - **Deterrence:** Don't have to punish morally lucky people to get incentives right (can just punish people who actually cause injury). Best way for potential injurers to be able to anticipate liability is to link it to causation (risk-as-wrongs approach too amorphous).

- *Restatement Second* (old school): Used substantial factor test.

- **Two Simultaneous Causes: Both Negligent**

  - Courts universally hold that neither defendant can escape liability.

  - Choice is between over-imposition of liability (since harm will occur anyway even when one of the defendants is careful), and under-imposition of liability. The law chooses threatening "too much" liability rather than too little.

- **Two Simultaneous Causes: One Negligent, One Not**

  - Imposing liability on the negligent party will give such parties the incentive to take precautions against harms that will occasionally occur anyway because of a simultaneous non-negligent cause.

  - Also, here plaintiff has much less basis for objecting to denying his recovery, because injury would have occurred regardless of negligence.

  - Many courts do not hold the negligent defendant liable in this situation, but some courts do. It is essential to the courts that do impose liability in such cases that the negligent cause arrived no later than the non-negligent cause (see below).

- **Sequential Causes: Pre-emptive Causation**

  - First cause was the actual physical cause ("pre-emptive" cause). Therefore, party responsible for first cause is liable for injury while party for second "cause" is not.

  - Not entirely consistent with analogous rules (Abraham pg. 128)

    - Hypo 1: Negligently crash into car injuring plaintiff's leg, but cause plaintiff to miss plane which crashes (plaintiff would have died). ðŸ¡ª Still liable for broken leg.

    - Hypo 2: Negligently crash into car injuring plaintiff's leg, but plaintiff has terminal cancer and will soon die. ðŸ¡ª Damages will take into account the fact that only had a couple months to live.

    - Possible distinctions (though not clear why any of them should make a difference):

      - Hypo 1 treated under the law of causation, hypo 2 under law of damages.

      - Hypo 1 deals with outside forces not yet materialized, hypo 2 deals with internal forces that had already materialized.

**Proximate Cause** ("scope of liability" / "legal cause")

- Claim that negligence was not the proximate cause of injury is based on the fact that something quite unexpected has contributed either o the occurrence of the harm or its severity.

- Has little to do with proximity of time or geography, or with factual cause.

- **Harm-Within-The-Risk Test** (modern view -- restatement): Scope of liability is injuries suffered that are within the risks that makes the defendant negligent in the first place.

  - Things get difficult when injury was not part of the principal risk, but was perhaps a foreseeable (one may say secondary) and relevant risk (foreseeable â‰  imaginable).

- **Foreseeability Test** (old school) (foreseeable â‰  imaginable)

  - To discuss in terms of modern view, discuss in terms of whether a reasonable person would have foreseen and how this would affect reasonable person's determination of whether or not to take precaution.

  - [Unforeseeable Plaintiffs]{.underline} (never liable -- *Palsgraf*)

  - [Unforeseeable Extent of Harm]{.underline} (liable in most cases)

    - [Thin-Skull Rule]{.underline}: It is no defense that plaintiff had a "unforeseeable" weakness of infirmity that caused his/her injury, or much greater severity of injury.

      - Rejection of rule would create practical problems of adjudication

      - Rule imposes a desirable kind of strict liability.

      - Strong argument that Think-Skull plaintiffs should are foreseeable anyways.

    - Exceptions:

      - When it would make more sense for the plaintiff to bear cost of injury than for defendant to be held strictly liable for it

        - If plaintiff was negligent

        - Plaintiff failed to take reasonable steps to mitigate extent of loss due to injury

  - [Unforeseeable Type of Harm]{.underline} (Not liable, though many courts resistant to this and take into account other factors on a case by case basis)

    - *Polemis Case* (Negligence caused board to fall which caused spark which lit petrol, burning ship down)

      - Defendant's negligence is proximate cause of even unforeseeable type of harm, so long as the harm was a "direct" consequence of the defendant's negligence (overruled).

    - *The Wagon Mound* (âˆ† negligently discharged oil from ship, and two days later oil lit when molten metal from welding landed on cotton waste floating in bay, burning plaintiff's wharf)

      - No liability when a foreseeable plaintiff suffers an unforeseeable type of harm (even if the harm is a direct consequence of such negligence).

    - \^ This rule is broader than most courts accept. Most will make proximate cause decision on a case by case basis, taking into account factors including:

      - Directness of harm

      - Degree which harm resembles unforeseeable type vs. unforeseeable extent

      - Whether defendant's negligence caused other damage for which he will be held liable so that he won't be totally exonerated.

  - [Unforeseeable Manner (Mechanism) of Harm]{.underline} (Still liable if in roughly same time and place and through same general forces as was foreseeable)

    - E.g., Defendant's negligent driving forces plaintiff off the road, who is soon after hit by a third vehicle while trying to warn oncoming traffic.

- Restatement is trying to get as far away from discussion of unforeseeable categories as possible. Extent is the only one so entrenched that it hasn't really been able to get rid of it. However, these arguments are still relevant because courts are used to such arguments and such arguments still affect analysis of negligence (strict categories just aren't as important anymore).

**Superseding Causes:** Where some other cause makes the harm to plaintiff not be a harm-within-the-risk of defendant's negligence.

- A purported superseding cause has no independent significance; however, may make harm being within the risk much less likely, since the intervening factor/cause would have to have been one of the risks taken into account in deciding whether the defendant's actions were negligent.

**Joint and Severally Liable**

- Plaintiff is entitled to recover the full amount of his damages from either defendant.

- **Rights of contribution:** Right to be reimbursed by another defendant for a share of the amount paid to the plaintiff (traditionally such rights operated on a straight fractional-share basis).

- Why not simply hold each defendant liable to the plaintiff for its share in the first instance?

  - Places risk of one of the defendants being insolvent on the plaintiff (preferable to make defendants bear the risk).

- Imposed in **three main situations**:

  - \(1\) [Joint tortfeasors]{.underline}: If two people engage in a joint enterprise (e.g., business partnership).

  - \(2\) [Single, theoretically indivisible harm]{.underline} (by independent tortfeasors) (**MNC?**): If neither of two tortfeasors' negligence is sufficient but both are the necessary causes, and no way to pinpoint the extent of either defendant's causal responsibility (e.g., two negligent drivers crash into each other, killing pedestrian)

  - \(3\) [Single, theoretically divisible but practically indivisible harm]{.underline} (by independent tortfeasors): Each defendant is entitled to try to prove how much harm it caused, but if neither can, defendants will be held joint and severally liable (e.g., one defendant crashes into plaintiff, then another does, second defendant can try to prove to what extent the plaintiff was already injured, but if he can't he will be held joint and severally liable).

- In a way, all modern manipulations of joint and several liability are about who bears the risk that someone isn't going to be able to pay their share (only deal with this question if you are using contributory negligence scheme).

  - Several: Plaintiff bears the risk

  - Joint and Several: Defendants bear the risk

  - Reallocation: ALL bear the risk (including plaintiff)

**Contributory Negligence**

- Contributory Negligence

  - Pros: Administrative Ease (but juries were historically doing comparative negligence anyways by ignoring contributory negligence rule and simply reducing damages (could be seen as a pro-softening tool, or all the more reason to change it)

  - Cons: Gets incentives wrong, Unjust

- Other Softening Tools for Contributory Negligence

  - [Safety statutes]{.underline}: In some situations where a statute is understood to be an effort to protect some group against its own inability to protect itself the statue me be interpreted as barring a defense of contributory negligence. However, relatively few statutes have been given such effect.

  - [Last clear chance]{.underline}: Contributory negligence was also disregarded under circumstances of "last clear chance." Generally, the doctrine was regarded as having a chronological aspect.

  - Greater degree of blame exception (but Huang says this makes no sense)

  - [Willful, wanton]{.underline} (intent): Virtually all courts have decided that contributory negligence was a defense only in cases of negligence, and not in cases where the misconduct of the defendant was more serious---recklessness or willful misconduct.

  - [Avoidable consequences]{.underline}

  - [The Jury's Role]{.underline}: The most modern technique for ameliorating the perceived harshness of the all-or-nothing contributory negligence rule (proper to the adoption of comparative negligence) was the increased frequency with which courts found that reasonable persons could differ over the characterization of the plaintiff's conduct---so that the jury question was presented. Observers asserted that most juries rejected the judge's instruction to return a defense verdict if they found any contributory negligence, even the most minimal contributory fault, so long as it proximately related to the harm (deducted small amount from award instead).

**Comparative Negligence:** A negligent plaintiff's recovery depends on how serious plaintiff's negligence was compared to the defendant's.

- ["Pure" Comparative Negligence]{.underline}: Damages directly proportional to fault (the degree which the cause of the injury can be attributed to parties' negligence)

  - Negatives

    - Moral: Not morally right to permit one more at fault in an accident to recover from one less at fault.

    - Efficiency: Probability of increased insurance, administrative, and judicial costs (but this has been seriously questioned)

- ["Modified" System]{.underline}: Two versions.

  - \(1\) A plaintiff who is at fault can recover as under the pure system but only so long as that negligence is "not as great as" the defendants (can't be equal).

  - \(2\) A plaintiff can recover as under the pure system but only so long as that negligence is "no greater than" the defendant's (can be equal).

  - Negatives (of modified system as a whole)

    - Moral: Modified system simply shifts the lottery aspect of the contributory negligence rule to a different ground (slight difference in the proportionate fault may permit a recovery).

    - Efficiency: Modified system could actually lead to more administrative costs, because of increased number of appeals due to plaintiffs challenging the crucial issue of whether their negligence was equal to the defendants.

**Affirmative and Limited Duties** (Abraham)

  - The [foreseeability]{.underline} of harm

  - The [closeness]{.underline} of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the harm suffered by the plaintiff

  - The [moral blame]{.underline} attached the defendant's conduct

  - The [extent of the burden on the defendant and the consequences for the community]{.underline} if a duty is imposed

  - The policy of [preventing future harm]{.underline}

  - The [availability]{.underline} and the prevalence [of insurance]{.underline} that is or would be available if a duty is imposed.

**"Pure" Economic Loss**

- Concerns the cases in which physical injury to the person or property of **one party** also results in **purely** economic harm to **another party**. That is, the latter party's losses are not parasitic on any bodily injury or property damage that he suffered.

- Traditional answer ("[economic loss rule]{.underline}") has been to deny such recovery outright.

  - Unlike the law governing recovery for pure emotional loss, however, in this field there has been only very modest evolution away from the bright-line rule denying recovery.

  - Although economic loss is often highly foreseeable, the amount of liability that could result could be enormous and very difficult to predict.

    - This unpredictability would render against the sale of insurance against such liability extremely risky, and therefore likely unavailable.

    - Also, for individuals and small businesses, threatening liability whose potential scope far exceed their assets is pointless.

  - âˆ† ordinarily is not immunized from all liability as a result of the rule.

  - Third-party economic losses are normally distributed among large numbers of people. Given such already existing broad loss distribution, the loss-distribution rational for imposing liability on the injurer is weak.

  - A pure economic loss suffered by one party may then result in a pure economic loss suffered by someone with whom he deals, and so on *ad infinitum*. No logical intermediate rule seems available to limit liability. For practical purposes, therefore, the law has chosen to draw the line at no liability rather than to venture into an arena where there would be few if any guidelines for adjudication.

  - Exceptions include some cases involving natural-resource damage for which no party seems to have a cause of action (in such cases, liability for economic loss serves as a kind of substitute for a cause of action for physical damage to natural resources). Importantly, even the cases that have permitted common law recovery tend to share one characteristic: the defendant's negligence is determined by reference to the reasonableness of the defendant's also having risked some other kind of harm---usually physical harm---either the plaintiff or to a third party.

**Defenses to Property Torts** (trespass to land, trespass to property, and conversion)

- **Public Necessity:** When there is a risk to the property of a sufficiently large number of people to make the risk "public" and that risk can be reduced or eliminated by damaging or destroying the property of the plaintiff.

**Calabresi on Proximate Cause**

- Goal of deterrence

  - The requirement of proximity in causal relationships, unlike that of but for relationships, may help to select from the universe of causally linked action those actions worth deterring collectively, by selecting relatively good avoiders of accident costs.

  - Proximate cause implies some look at the relative susceptibility of various actions to modifications diminishing their riskiness---excludes from liability some relatively less risky actions.

  - The requirement of proximate cause does not mean that only one among many causes must be determined to be proximate, yet the more subtle student also learns that whether a causally linked party is held responsible as a proximate cause depends, in party, on what other parties were involved and how risky their behavior was in comparison to his.   - Past foreseeability should not be a prerequisite to liability if the goal is purely market deterrence. (Assuming unforeseeable risks may cluster around certain activities). (Can explain whether we assign liability in different foreseeability categories).

- **Error Costs** (Risk of Error)

  - There is a greater risk of error in making the negligence determination because it is potentially more fact-intensive.

  - Strict liability may be preferable in circumstances in which there is likely to be a high rate of error because hard to get evidence (e.g., transportation of highly flammable material that is likely to destroy evidence of negligence when it explodes).

  - However, the consequences of an error is worse under strict liability, because a mistaken ascription of causation, or an overestimation of damages could fully deter an activity rather than just over incentivize precaution (less likely to fully deter the activity).

- **Influencing Activity Level and Research Decisions**

  - In practice, negligence liability is rarely imposed of for engaging in an excessive level of activity.

  - A shift to strict liability gives parties more reason to consider alternative methods of conducting their activities, by making them internalize non-negligently-caused activities.

    - It may be that a partial rather than a complete substitution of one activity for another is the cost-minimizing approach.

  - Because actors are fully internalizing the cost of their activities (including non-negligent injuries), strict liability may create research incentives.

  - Reasons why strict liability not categorically preferable to negligence:

    - \(1\) There may be activities for which there are no readily available substitutes.

    - \(2\) Changes in activity level by victims are also a method of accident avoidance, and one that is encouraged by negligence liability but discouraged by strict liability. In a sense there is always strict liability, since the operative effect of denial of strict liability recovery by victims is that victims bear the cost of their non-negligently-caused injuries---they are strictly liable for them, to themselves. [Imposing strict liability is thus a policy decision by the courts about which activity-level should bear costs.]{.underline}

      - [Only when a class of activities can be identified in which activity-level changes by potential injurers are the most efficient method of accident prevention is there a compelling argument for imposing strict liability.]{.underline}

- **Promoting Insurance or Broad Distribution of Losses**

  - Strict liability might be imposed because a particular category of injurers, rather than a particular category of victims, is in the best position to insure or otherwise broadly distribute the costs of non-negligently caused accidents.

  - In general, injurers are more likely than victims to be large institutions that can spread the cost of liability directly in their prices or services, or to purchase liability insurance whose cost is included in these prices or services.

  - Even in the domain of loss distribution, however, injurers are not necessarily always superior to victims as loss bearers (i.e. health care insurance, most people have it and soon virtually everyone should have it due to affordable health care act). The choice therefore, is not between broad loss distribution though injurer strict liability or narrow distribution by declining to adopt such strict liability. Rather, the choice is between two different channels of broad distribution.

  - Posner: In this category, using strict liability to insure victims of unavoidable accidents is a gain only if the cost of insurance through the tort system is less than the cost to potential victim of buying accident insurance policies in the insurance market---almost certainly it is not (tort system is a very costly method of providing insurance).

**Enterprise Liability** (fairness) (two propositions at its core) (takes issue with restatement approach, note 2, pg. 534)

- \(1\) Activities should bear their characteristic accident costs.

  - Fault liability pins the costs of the nonnegligent accidents that are the long-run price of an activity's presence in the world on the random victims of the activity.

  - Enterprise liability pins those accident costs on the activity---the enterprise---which imposed the nonnegligent risks responsible for the injuries at issue.

- \(2\) Enterprise liability holds that an enterprise's accident costs should be distributed among the members of the enterprise.

  - The costs of an injury should be shared by those who profit from the activity responsible for the injury; they should not be concentrated on the injured party, or be dispersed across unrelated activities (self: both buyer and seller of product are beneficiaries)

- These two propositions are often linked to a particular conception of fairness that requires a just distribution of burdens and benefits. It therefore gives rise to a presumption that [the costs of the accidental physical injuries characteristic of an activity should be borne by those who benefit from the activity, whether or not they are culpably responsible for precipitating the injuries at issue]{.underline} (self: argument against social insurance /government picking up the tab).
